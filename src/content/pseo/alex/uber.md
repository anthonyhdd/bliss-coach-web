Uber's interview process typically moves from a recruiter screen through one or two functional screens to a virtual onsite of four to six interviews, with behavioral rounds explicitly mapped to the company's published cultural norms. Analytical rigor shows up everywhere: even non-technical candidates commonly report data-driven case questions or take-home exercises.

## The Uber interview process

The loop varies by function — engineering, operations, product, data science, sales — but candidates commonly report this structure:

1. **Recruiter screen.** A 20–30 minute call on background and motivation. For operations and analytics roles, recruiters often flag an upcoming **analytics exercise** at this stage.
2. **Functional screen(s).** Engineers report a coding interview (data structures and algorithms); operations and strategy candidates frequently report a timed analytics test or case study built around marketplace-style data; product managers get product sense and execution screens.
3. **Take-home or case round (role-dependent).** Some business roles include a short take-home analysis or a presentation built from a dataset — a long-standing and commonly reported feature of Uber's ops hiring.
4. **Virtual onsite.** Usually four to six interviews: role-specific depth (coding + system design for engineers; case and metrics rounds for business roles), plus one or more dedicated **behavioral interviews** tied to Uber's cultural norms.
5. **Hiring committee-style review and offer.** Feedback is consolidated before a decision; commonly reported timelines are two to five weeks.

The consistent thread across roles: Uber runs a marketplace business obsessed with metrics, so interviewers keep pulling conversations back to numbers. "How would you measure that?" and "what does the data say?" are the house follow-ups.

## Behavioral questions Uber asks

Commonly reported questions and what each probes:

1. **"Why Uber?"** — Tests whether you understand the actual business (a multi-sided marketplace across mobility, delivery, and freight) and have a reason beyond brand recognition.
2. **"Tell me about a time you used data to make a decision others disagreed with."** — Probes analytical backbone — arguing from evidence, not seniority.
3. **"Describe a time you had to move fast with incomplete information."** — Tests judgment under ambiguity, a daily reality in marketplace operations.
4. **"Tell me about the biggest ownership you've ever taken — something you drove end to end."** — Maps to Uber's published norm about acting like owners; probes follow-through.
5. **"Describe a time you persevered through repeated setbacks."** — Uber's cultural norms publicly include perseverance; interviewers want a story with real adversity, not a mild inconvenience.
6. **"Tell me about a time you did the right thing even though it was costly."** — Maps to the company's published "we do the right thing" norm; probes integrity under pressure.
7. **"Give an example of working effectively with people very different from you."** — Reflects Uber's public emphasis that "great minds don't think alike"; tests inclusive collaboration.
8. **"Tell me about a time a project's metrics moved the wrong way. What did you do?"** — Probes diagnostic thinking: hypothesis, data, iteration.
9. **"Describe a time you disagreed with your manager and how you handled it."** — Tests constructive dissent and communication upward.
10. **"What's the hardest trade-off you've had to make between speed and quality?"** — Probes prioritization in an environment that publicly values bold, fast execution.

## What Uber looks for

Uber has published its cultural norms openly since rewriting them in 2017, and behavioral interviews commonly reference them. From public materials, the themes to align with:

- **Customer obsession.** Uber publicly frames its norms around building for the people who use the platform — riders, drivers, eaters, couriers, merchants. Stories with an end-user payoff resonate.
- **Ownership.** "We act like owners" is one of the published norms: seeing problems through, sweating details, and not waiting for permission.
- **Doing the right thing.** After its well-documented 2017 culture reset, Uber publicly emphasizes integrity — "we do the right thing, period" — and interviewers probe it directly.
- **Perseverance and boldness.** The published norms celebrate bouncing back from setbacks and taking big swings; safe, low-stakes stories undersell you here.
- **Difference as strength.** "Great minds don't think alike" is a published norm; concrete examples of seeking out opposing views land well.
- **Data fluency.** Not a formal "norm," but omnipresent in candidate reports across every function: the ability to define a metric, interrogate it, and act on it.

## How to prepare

- **Tag your stories to the norms.** Build 6–8 STAR stories and explicitly map each to ownership, perseverance, integrity, customer impact, or collaboration across difference. Our guide to [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) shows how to structure each one so it survives three levels of follow-up.
- **Practice metrics thinking out loud.** For business roles, rehearse case questions like "a city's completed trips dropped 10% week over week — walk me through your diagnosis." Structure: clarify, segment, hypothesize, identify the data you'd pull, propose an action.
- **Prepare for the analytics test (ops/strategy roles).** Brush up on spreadsheet-level analysis and, if the job description mentions it, basic SQL. Candidates commonly report timed exercises, so practice working fast and narrating your assumptions.
- **Engineers: standard big-tech prep applies.** Data structures and algorithms for screens; distributed-systems design and a behavioral round for the onsite. Depth on your past projects matters as much as puzzle performance.
- **Do a timed, full-length mock.** Uber loops are long and energy management is real. Follow the structured plan in our guide on [how to prepare for an interview](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) and simulate the full day at least once.
- **Prepare marketplace-curious questions.** Asking about how the team balances the two sides of its marketplace signals you already think the way they do.

## FAQ

**Does Uber really test analytics for non-engineering roles?**
It's commonly reported for operations, strategy, and analyst roles — usually a timed exercise or case built on marketplace-style data. Ask your recruiter exactly what to expect; they typically tell you the format.

**How long does the Uber interview process take?**
Commonly reported: two to five weeks from recruiter screen to offer, depending on role and scheduling. Take-home rounds can add a week.

**Are Uber behavioral interviews really based on the cultural norms?**
Uber's norms are public, and candidates consistently report behavioral questions that map cleanly onto them (ownership, perseverance, doing the right thing). Preparing stories against the norms is the highest-leverage behavioral prep you can do.
