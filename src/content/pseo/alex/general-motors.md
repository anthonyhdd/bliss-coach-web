The General Motors (GM) interview process is structured and behavioral-first, with role-specific technical rounds for engineering, software, manufacturing, and data positions. Candidates commonly report a recruiter screen, one or more technical or hiring-manager interviews, and a final panel built around GM's behaviors and its shift toward electric and software-defined vehicles.

## The General Motors interview process

GM doesn't publish one fixed pipeline, and steps vary by function (engineering, software, manufacturing, corporate), level, and location. Candidates commonly report a sequence along these lines:

1. **Application and resume screen.** Recruiters filter for relevant experience and interest in the automotive, EV, or software space.
2. **Recruiter phone screen.** A 20–30 minute conversation on your background, motivation ("Why GM?"), and logistics.
3. **Technical or hiring-manager interview.** Engineering and software candidates commonly report technical questions on their domain (mechanical, electrical, controls, software/coding); other roles get a deeper behavioral conversation.
4. **Final panel / on-site or virtual loop.** A series of interviews with team members and managers, mixing behavioral questions with role-specific technical depth, and sometimes a presentation for senior roles.

Timelines vary; campus recruiting follows a seasonal calendar, while experienced hires commonly report a few weeks across rounds.

## Questions General Motors commonly asks

These reflect what candidates frequently report, with what each probes:

1. **"Why GM, and why this role?"** — Tests whether you understand GM's business and its EV/software transformation, not just want an automotive job.
2. **"Tell me about a technical problem you solved."** — Core for engineering roles; they want depth and your specific contribution.
3. **"Describe a time you worked on a cross-functional team."** — Vehicle programs are deeply cross-functional; they want evidence you collaborate.
4. **"Tell me about a time you dealt with a tight deadline or a program milestone."** — Probes delivery under pressure in a milestone-driven environment.
5. **"Describe a time you had a conflict with a teammate and how you resolved it."** — Tests maturity and communication.
6. **"Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete information."** — Probes judgment.
7. **"Give an example of when you focused on safety or quality."** — Maps directly to a safety-critical industry.
8. **"Tell me about a time you failed or a project that went wrong."** — Checks ownership and what you learned.
9. **"Walk me through your resume / a project on it."** — Sets the tone; they want a clear narrative and real depth.

## What General Motors looks for

GM publicly frames its vision around a future of "zero crashes, zero emissions, and zero congestion," and its stated behaviors emphasize putting the customer first, innovating now, thinking big, being bold, taking accountability, and winning together (safety and integrity run through all of it). In interviews, that translates into evidence that you focus on safety and the customer, collaborate across functions, take accountability, and can execute in a milestone-driven, transforming company.

Practically, interviewers are listening for technical depth (for engineering/software), collaboration, accountability, a safety and quality mindset, and genuine interest in where GM is headed.

### A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you prioritized safety or quality."**

- **Situation:** On a design project, testing revealed a component that met spec on paper but showed inconsistent behavior in edge-case conditions.
- **Task:** We were near a deadline, and the easy path was to ship since it technically passed.
- **Action:** I flagged the inconsistency, ran additional edge-case tests to characterize it, and presented the data to the team with a recommendation to revise rather than accept marginal behavior. I framed it around downstream risk, not perfectionism.
- **Result:** We adjusted the design, which added a short delay but removed a failure mode that would have been far costlier later. I learned that "passes spec" and "actually robust" aren't always the same, and it's worth saying so.

## FAQ

**How long does the GM interview process take?**
It varies by role and location. Campus processes follow a seasonal calendar; experienced hires commonly report a few weeks across recruiter, technical, and panel rounds.

**Are GM interviews technical?**
For engineering, software, and data roles, yes — candidates commonly report domain-specific technical questions and deep dives into resume projects. Non-technical roles lean more behavioral.

**What should I emphasize for a GM engineering interview?**
Technical depth in your domain, a safety and quality mindset, cross-functional collaboration, and genuine interest in GM's EV and software-defined-vehicle direction.

**What values does GM interview for?**
Publicly, GM emphasizes customer focus, innovation, accountability, boldness, safety, and winning together. Prepare stories that demonstrate these clearly.
