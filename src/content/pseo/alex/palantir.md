Palantir builds data-integration and analytics software deployed against messy, high-stakes real-world problems, and it hires across software engineering, the well-known forward-deployed engineer (FDE) track, product, and go-to-market roles. Its interview process has a distinctive reputation: strong on practical problem-solving and clear thinking, less about memorized algorithm trivia, with a heavy emphasis on how you reason through ambiguous, open-ended problems. Expect a recruiter screen, practical technical rounds, and behavioral conversations that probe curiosity and judgment.

## The Palantir interview process

Palantir recruits by role family and region, so treat this as the commonly reported shape rather than a fixed script:

1. **Application and resume screen.** Reviewed against the specific track — software engineer, forward-deployed engineer, product, or business. Referrals and demonstrable building experience are widely reported to help.
2. **Recruiter screen.** A 20–30 minute call on your background, motivation for Palantir, location expectations, and logistics.
3. **Technical screen.** Candidates commonly report a practical coding round — often more applied and problem-oriented than pure algorithm puzzles — sometimes with a take-home or a shared-editor exercise.
4. **Technical / problem-solving loop.** For software and FDE roles: coding plus open-ended problem decomposition, where interviewers care about how you break down an ambiguous problem, ask clarifying questions, and reason toward a workable solution. Some rounds probe how you'd model a real data problem.
5. **Behavioral / "decision" round.** Conversations about how you think, past projects, curiosity, and how you handle ambiguity and disagreement — Palantir places real weight on intellectual honesty and judgment.

Commonly reported timelines run from three to six weeks depending on track, level, and region. If you're comparing other technical data-software employers, our [Databricks interview guide](/alex/interviews/databricks/) covers a closely related process.

## Behavioral questions Palantir asks

These are the questions candidates most frequently report, with what each is really probing:

1. **"Why Palantir?"** — Tests whether you're drawn to the kind of hard, real-world problems Palantir works on, not just its reputation.
2. **"Tell me about a project you built because you wanted to, not because you were assigned it."** — Palantir prizes genuine builders and intellectual curiosity.
3. **"Describe a time you tackled a problem with no clear right answer."** — Ambiguity is the core of the work; they want to see your decision process.
4. **"Walk me through the most technically interesting thing you've done."** — Probes depth and how you communicate complex ideas.
5. **"Tell me about a time you changed your mind after seeing new evidence."** — Tests intellectual honesty over ego.
6. **"Describe a time you disagreed with a teammate about an approach."** — They want constructive conflict and the ability to commit once decided.
7. **"Tell me about a time you failed. What did you actually learn?"** — An ownership check: name it plainly, avoid the rehearsed non-answer.
8. **"How do you decide what to work on when everything seems important?"** — FDE and product work demand ruthless prioritization.
9. **"Explain something complicated you understand well to me simply."** — Tests clear thinking and communication under a little pressure.
10. **"What's a belief you hold that most people you work with disagree with?"** — Probes independent thinking and whether you can defend a view.

For software and FDE candidates, behavioral questions sit alongside practical coding and problem-decomposition rounds. Interviewers commonly care more about your reasoning than about a perfectly optimal answer.

## What Palantir looks for

Palantir publicly frames its work around solving important, hard problems and hiring people with genuine intellectual curiosity and independence. Interviewers commonly probe how you think — clarity, honesty, and comfort with ambiguity — more than whether you've grinded a specific problem set. Because forward-deployed work puts engineers directly in front of complex customer problems, adaptability and clear communication carry unusual weight.

Practically, that means they're evaluating: structured problem-solving under ambiguity; intellectual honesty and curiosity; the ability to explain complex things simply; sound prioritization; and the willingness to defend and revise a view based on evidence.

## How to prepare

- **Practice thinking out loud on open-ended problems.** Palantir cares how you decompose ambiguity — narrate your assumptions, ask clarifying questions, and reason toward a solution rather than jumping to code.
- **Sharpen practical coding.** Focus on writing clean, correct, working code for realistic problems. Our guide on [technical interview tips](/alex/blog/technical-interview-tips/) covers how to structure a strong technical round.
- **Build a STAR story bank.** Six to eight structured stories covering self-driven projects, ambiguity, changing your mind, disagreement, and failure will cover almost everything above. Our guide to [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) breaks the format down with worked answers.
- **Prepare a genuine "why this problem" answer.** Vague enthusiasm reads poorly here; connect a real interest to the kind of work Palantir does.
- **Rehearse explaining a complex project simply, and run a timed mock.** Practice under pressure so follow-up questions sharpen your answer rather than unsettle it.

## A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you tackled a problem with no clear right answer."**

- **Situation:** "A client's data came from five systems that all defined 'active customer' differently, and leadership wanted one trustworthy number."
- **Task:** "I had to produce a definition everyone could stand behind, knowing any choice would upset someone."
- **Action:** "I mapped how each system defined the term, met each owner to understand why, and proposed a single rule with the edge cases written down explicitly so disagreements were about the rule, not hidden assumptions."
- **Result:** "Leadership adopted the definition, and because the trade-offs were documented, later debates stayed short and factual. It taught me that with ambiguous problems, making the assumptions visible matters more than finding a 'correct' answer."

Notice the shape: one sentence of context, a clear personal task, three concrete actions, a result plus a lesson. That's the register Palantir interviewers commonly reward.

## FAQ

### Are Palantir interviews just LeetCode?
Not really — candidates commonly describe the coding as practical and problem-oriented rather than a pure algorithm gauntlet, with heavy weight on how you decompose an ambiguous problem. Clean, working code and clear reasoning matter more than exotic tricks.

### What is a forward-deployed engineer (FDE) interview like?
Candidates report a mix of coding, real-world problem decomposition, and behavioral conversations about working directly with customers on messy data problems. Adaptability and communication are central.

### How long does the Palantir hiring process take?
Commonly reported: three to six weeks, depending on track, level, and region. Campus cycles follow set calendars; experienced hires vary more.

### What should I wear to a Palantir interview?
Business-casual or even casual is the commonly reported norm. For video interviews, dress neatly and test your setup beforehand — the focus will be squarely on how you think.
