Johnson & Johnson is one of the few companies where the interview rubric is a public document. Its Credo — written in 1943 and displayed publicly ever since — states, in order, the company's responsibilities: first to patients, doctors, nurses, and customers; then to employees; then to communities; and last to shareholders. Candidates consistently report that interviews are built around exactly this: structured behavioral questions probing whether you'd make decisions in that order. If you interview at J&J without having read the Credo, you're walking in without the answer key.

## The Johnson & Johnson interview process

J&J hires across two large businesses — Innovative Medicine (pharmaceuticals) and MedTech (devices and surgical technology) — plus corporate functions, supply chain, and large university programs. Processes vary by business and region, but the commonly reported shape:

1. **Online application.** Resume screen against the requisition; university-program candidates commonly report an added online assessment.
2. **Recruiter screen or on-demand video interview.** Many candidates — especially early-career — report a recorded one-way video interview (preset questions, camera, timed answers) before speaking to a human. Others get a standard recruiter phone call.
3. **Hiring manager interview.** Video or on-site. Behavioral core plus a walkthrough of your background and motivation for the specific business (pharma vs. MedTech matters — know which one you applied to).
4. **Panel or sequential interviews.** The main event: several interviewers, commonly reported as working from a structured, standardized behavioral question set scored against defined criteria. Cross-functional partners often join. Our [panel interview tips](/alex/blog/panel-interview-tips/) cover how to perform when multiple people are scoring you at once.
5. **Role-specific components.** Sales roles (MedTech especially) commonly report role-play scenarios or territory business-plan discussions; technical and engineering roles get discipline questions and project walkthroughs; supply chain and quality roles get compliance and problem-solving scenarios.
6. **Offer and checks.** Background check and references. Multi-week timelines are commonly reported, longer for senior roles.

## Questions Johnson & Johnson asks

The commonly reported questions — notice how many trace straight back to the Credo:

1. **"What does our Credo mean to you?"** — The signature J&J question. Pick one line of the Credo and connect it to a real decision you've made.
2. **"Tell me about a time you put the customer or end user first, even at a cost."** — Credo line one. They want evidence you'd protect the patient/customer when it's inconvenient.
3. **"Describe a time you faced an ethical dilemma at work. What did you do?"** — Integrity is the hard gate in a healthcare company; the winning shape is naming the tension honestly and choosing the principled route through proper channels.
4. **"Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager."** — Tests respectful candor: did you voice it directly, with reasoning, and commit to the outcome?
5. **"Give an example of leading without formal authority."** — Maps to J&J's publicly described Leadership Imperatives (Connect, Shape, Grow); influence across a matrix is daily life there.
6. **"Tell me about a time you worked with a team across functions or regions."** — A global healthcare company runs on cross-functional, cross-border collaboration; friction-resolved stories score well.
7. **"Describe a time you had to deliver results under pressure without cutting corners."** — In regulated healthcare, how you delivered matters as much as whether you did.
8. **"Tell me about a failure and what you changed afterward."** — Checks ownership and learning; systemic fixes beat apologies.
9. **"Why Johnson & Johnson, and why this business?"** — Knowing whether you're interviewing with Innovative Medicine or MedTech — and naming why that side draws you — separates researched candidates from mass applicants.
10. **"Tell me about a time you improved a process or challenged the status quo."** — Probes initiative balanced with respect for regulated processes: improvement through legitimate channels.

Most of these follow standard behavioral patterns — our guide to [behavioral interview questions](/alex/blog/behavioral-interview-questions/) breaks down how structured interviewers score them.

## What Johnson & Johnson looks for

Anchor on the public documents, because J&J is unusually explicit. **The Credo** sets the decision hierarchy: patients, doctors, nurses, and the people who use J&J products come first; employees second; communities third; shareholders last — with the stated belief that serving the first three makes the fourth take care of itself. The company publicly describes "Credo-based decision making" as core to its culture, and candidates commonly report interview questions framed around it.

J&J's publicly described **Leadership Imperatives** — connect (collaborate internally and externally), shape (innovate and challenge the status quo), and grow (develop yourself and others) — commonly show up as the competency lens on top of the Credo.

Practically, interviewers are listening for: decisions reasoned from the end user's interest, integrity when no one is watching, collaborative instinct across functions, and initiative that respects a regulated environment. Candidates who frame wins purely in personal or financial terms are commonly reported to land poorly.

## How to prepare

- **Read the Credo — actually read it.** It's short, public, and on J&J's website. Pick the one line that genuinely resonates and prepare a real story where you acted consistently with it. That's your answer to the signature question.
- **Build 6 STAR stories mapped to the themes above:** customer-first at a cost, an ethical call, respectful disagreement, leading without authority, cross-functional delivery, and a failure you owned. Our [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) walk through the structure that scored panels expect.
- **Know which business you applied to.** Innovative Medicine and MedTech have different products, customers, and rhythms; mixing them up in an answer is a commonly reported credibility hit.
- **Practice the one-way video format.** Timed answers to a camera with no interviewer nodding along undoes unpracticed candidates. Record yourself; check your first 15 seconds especially.
- **Prepare Credo-flavored questions to ask.** "Can you share a time this team made a Credo-based call that cost something short-term?" shows you understand the culture at a level most candidates never reach.

## A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you put the customer first, even at a cost."**

- **Situation:** "At my last company, I discovered two days before a client deliverable that a data source we'd used was outdated — the conclusions were directionally fine, but two figures the client planned to present internally were off."
- **Task:** "As the analyst who built it, I had to choose between shipping on time and quietly fixing it later, or flagging it and delaying."
- **Action:** "I told my manager the same day, recommended we inform the client immediately rather than after their presentation, and worked out exactly which figures changed so the correction was surgical. I rebuilt those sections overnight."
- **Result:** "We delivered one day late with corrected numbers, and the client told my manager the proactive call increased their trust in us — they renewed the engagement that year. My takeaway: the customer's real interest was accurate numbers in front of their leadership, not our on-time metric."

End-user interest first, transparency over convenience, cost absorbed willingly — that's Credo-shaped reasoning, and it's what J&J's structured interviews are commonly reported to score highest.

## FAQ

**What is Credo-based interviewing at Johnson & Johnson?**
J&J publicly centers its culture on its Credo — a 1943 statement putting patients and customers first, then employees, communities, and shareholders last. Candidates commonly report behavioral questions built around it, including being asked directly what the Credo means to them.

**How long does the J&J hiring process take?**
Commonly reported: several weeks from application to offer across a recruiter or video screen, hiring manager interview, and a panel round. University programs and senior roles often run longer.

**Does Johnson & Johnson use one-way video interviews?**
Yes — a recorded on-demand video interview is a commonly reported early stage, especially for early-career and high-volume roles. You answer preset questions on camera within a time limit.

**Are J&J interviews behavioral or technical?**
The structured behavioral core is consistent across roles. Technical, engineering, and scientific roles layer discipline questions and project walkthroughs on top; MedTech sales roles commonly report role-play or business-plan components.

**What should I wear to a Johnson & Johnson interview?**
Business professional is the commonly reported norm for on-site and sales interviews; polished business casual is typical on video. Overdressing is the safer error.
