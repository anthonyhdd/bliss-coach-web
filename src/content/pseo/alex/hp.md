HP Inc. hires across hardware engineering, software, supply chain, sales, marketing, and corporate functions, and its interview process reflects a large, established company that values reliability, collaboration, and customer trust over flash. What you get depends on the track — a software role leans technical, a supply-chain or sales role leans scenario-based — but the behavioral spine is consistent: teamwork, ownership, and a real connection to HP's products and customers.

## The HP interview process

HP recruits by business unit and region, so treat this as the commonly reported shape rather than a guaranteed sequence:

1. **Application and resume screen.** Reviewed against the specific req and level. Internships, co-ops, and referrals are widely reported to help early-career candidates clear this stage.
2. **Recruiter screen.** A 20–30 minute call on your background, why HP, location and hybrid expectations, and compensation basics.
3. **Assessment or technical exercise.** Software and engineering roles commonly report an online coding or technical test; other tracks may get a case, presentation, or scenario exercise.
4. **First-round interviews.** Typically with a hiring manager and team members — behavioral questions plus role-specific depth (algorithms and design for software, fundamentals for hardware, pipeline and account scenarios for sales).
5. **Final round.** Often a panel or a set of back-to-back interviews including a senior manager or director, sometimes with a short presentation. Collaboration and culture-fit weigh heavily here.

Commonly reported timelines run from two to six weeks depending on level, team, and region. If you're also considering other large hardware-and-enterprise employers, our [Dell interview guide](/alex/interviews/dell/) covers a closely related process.

## Behavioral questions HP asks

These are the questions candidates most frequently report, with what each is really probing:

1. **"Why HP — and why this role?"** — Tests whether you understand the specific business (print, personal systems, or a corporate function) rather than "big tech company."
2. **"Tell me about a time you worked on a team to deliver something."** — HP work is deeply cross-functional; they want a concrete collaboration story with your specific contribution.
3. **"Describe a time you solved a difficult technical or operational problem."** — Probes structured problem-solving, not just the outcome.
4. **"Tell me about a time you handled a conflict with a colleague."** — Tests diplomacy and whether you can resolve friction without damaging the relationship.
5. **"Give an example of when you had to meet a tight deadline."** — They're listening for prioritization and communication, not heroics.
6. **"Tell me about a time you failed. What did you learn?"** — An ownership check: name it plainly, show the fix and the lesson.
7. **"Describe a time you improved a process or reduced waste."** — Operational efficiency is genuinely valued in a company of HP's scale.
8. **"How do you stay current with technology in your field?"** — Even non-engineering candidates commonly report a version of this; have a specific answer.
9. **"Walk me through a project on your resume."** — Tests clear, structured communication; rehearse a two-minute version.
10. **"What are your career goals over the next few years?"** — Checks alignment with HP's internal mobility and level structure.

For software candidates, the behavioral round usually sits alongside coding and design questions. For sales and supply-chain, expect scenario questions about qualifying, forecasting, or managing constraints.

## What HP looks for

HP's public values emphasize **meaningful innovation, trust, customer focus, and teamwork**, and interviewers commonly map behavioral questions onto those themes. The company positions itself around dependable products and long-term customer relationships, so interviewers listen for reliability, collaboration, and follow-through more than for bravado.

Practically, that means they're evaluating: how you work inside a team; structured problem-solving; genuine customer and product orientation; the ability to execute under real constraints; and integrity when something goes wrong.

## How to prepare

- **Learn the business unit.** Know whether you're joining print, personal systems, or a corporate function, what it builds or sells, and who its customers are. Connect two things from your background to it.
- **Build a STAR story bank.** Six to eight structured stories covering teamwork, hard problems, conflict, deadlines, efficiency, and failure will cover almost everything above. Our guide to [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) breaks the format down with worked answers.
- **Match technical prep to your track.** Software candidates should drill data structures, algorithms, and a clean system-design walkthrough; hardware candidates should refresh fundamentals; other tracks should rehearse a realistic scenario end to end.
- **Have a collaboration story ready.** Given how cross-functional HP is, a crisp teamwork example with a clear personal contribution pays off repeatedly.
- **Rehearse the resume walkthrough and run a timed mock.** Two minutes, outcome first, plain language — then practice under pressure so follow-ups don't throw you.

## A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you worked on a team to deliver something under a deadline."**

- **Situation:** "In my last role, our team had to ship a firmware update across three device models before a trade-show launch, and integration testing kept surfacing regressions."
- **Task:** "I owned the test coordination so we could hit the launch date without shipping something unstable."
- **Action:** "I set up a shared triage board, grouped the regressions by root cause instead of by report, and ran a daily 15-minute sync so engineering and QA fixed the highest-impact bugs first."
- **Result:** "We shipped on time with zero critical issues at launch, and the triage board became the team's default process for the next release. It taught me that visibility and prioritization beat raw hours when a deadline is fixed."

Notice the shape: one sentence of context, a clear personal task, three concrete actions, a result plus a lesson. That's the register HP interviewers commonly reward.

## FAQ

### Does HP ask coding questions?
For software and some hardware-engineering roles, yes — candidates commonly report an online assessment plus live data-structures and design questions. Supply-chain, sales, and corporate tracks are primarily behavioral and scenario-based. Confirm the format with your recruiter.

### How hard is the HP interview?
Most candidates describe it as fair and role-appropriate — technical depth matched to the level, with real weight on teamwork and customer focus. Clear communication and preparation carry a lot of the load.

### How long does the HP hiring process take?
Commonly reported: two to six weeks, depending on level, team, and region. Campus cycles follow a set calendar; experienced hires vary more.

### What should I wear to an HP interview?
Business-casual is the commonly reported norm, even though everyday dress is relaxed. For video interviews, dress as you would in person from the waist up and test your setup in advance.
