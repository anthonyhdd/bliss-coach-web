The McKinsey interview process combines two distinct tests in every session: the case interview, which measures structured problem-solving, and the Personal Experience Interview (PEI), a deep behavioral drill into one story at a time. Most candidates also face the McKinsey Solve assessment (a digital problem-solving game) before ever meeting an interviewer.

## The McKinsey interview process

McKinsey publishes more about its process than most employers, though details vary by office and role. The commonly reported flow for consulting roles:

1. **Application and resume screen.** McKinsey screens for evidence of achievement and leadership — not just grades or brand names, per its own recruiting guidance.
2. **McKinsey Solve.** A gamified digital assessment (historically the "Problem Solving Game") that tests reasoning through scenarios like ecosystem-building. There's no content to memorize; it measures how you process information and make decisions under time pressure.
3. **First-round interviews.** Typically two interviews, each combining a **PEI** segment (about 10–15 minutes on one personal story) and a **case interview** (about 25–40 minutes).
4. **Final-round interviews.** Usually two to three more interviews with senior colleagues (Associate Partners and Partners), same PEI + case structure, with cases that lean more on judgment and synthesis.

**The McKinsey case format is interviewer-led.** Unlike some firms where you drive the case, the McKinsey interviewer steers: they present a client situation, then ask specific questions — "structure this problem," "what factors would you consider," a quantitative exercise, an exhibit to interpret, and a final recommendation. You're graded on structured thinking, comfort with numbers, creativity, and communicating a clear answer under pressure, not on reaching one "correct" solution.

**The PEI is not a normal behavioral interview.** Instead of several quick stories, the interviewer picks one dimension — leadership, personal impact, entrepreneurial drive, or courageous change — and digs into a single story for ten-plus minutes, probing what *you specifically* did, said, and felt at each step. Thin stories collapse under this level of questioning.

## Behavioral questions McKinsey asks

PEI prompts candidates commonly report, and what each probes:

1. **"Tell me about a time you led a team through a difficult situation."** — Leadership: how you align people, not just direct them.
2. **"Describe a time you changed someone's mind who strongly disagreed with you."** — Personal impact: influence through reasoning and empathy, step by step.
3. **"Tell me about a goal you pursued against significant obstacles."** — Entrepreneurial drive: initiative and persistence when nothing is handed to you.
4. **"Describe a time you took an unpopular position."** — Courage: whether you'll speak up when it costs you something.
5. **"Tell me about your most significant accomplishment."** — Calibration: what you consider hard, and how honestly you assess your own role.
6. **"Tell me about a time you failed to convince someone."** — Self-awareness: what you learned about your own influencing style.
7. **"Describe working with someone whose style clashed with yours."** — Teamwork under friction — daily reality on client engagements.
8. **"Tell me about a time you had to deliver difficult feedback."** — Directness with care, a core consulting skill.
9. **"Why consulting, and why McKinsey?"** — Motivation: whether you understand the actual job, travel and ambiguity included.

Expect relentless follow-ups on whichever story you choose: "What exactly did you say?" "How did they react?" "What would you do differently?"

## What McKinsey looks for

McKinsey publicly describes what it assesses: **structured problem solving, personal impact, entrepreneurial drive, courageous change, and inclusive leadership**, and its stated values emphasize putting client interests first, upholding the obligation to dissent, and bringing the best of the firm to every client. In practice, interviewers are asking two questions: can this person break down a messy problem and communicate clearly, and would I trust them alone in front of a client? The firm also publicly emphasizes that it hires from many disciplines — advanced degrees, non-business backgrounds, and experienced professionals — with problem-solving aptitude as the common bar.

## How to prepare

- **Prepare five deep PEI stories, not fifteen shallow ones.** Pick stories with genuine complexity, map each to a PEI dimension, and rehearse the minute-by-minute detail: your actions, your words, others' reactions. Use the STAR skeleton as scaffolding — our [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) show how — then go two levels deeper than a normal behavioral answer.
- **Drill cases out loud, with a partner or a simulator.** Reading casebooks isn't enough; the skill is verbal. Practice structuring in 60–90 seconds, doing arithmetic aloud without a calculator, and closing with a one-sentence recommendation followed by supporting reasons.
- **Practice interviewer-led pacing.** In McKinsey cases you answer discrete questions rather than running the whole show. Practice being complete but concise on each segment, then stopping.
- **Sharpen mental math.** Percentages, break-evens, market sizing arithmetic — fast and error-free beats fancy.
- **Build a two-week runway.** Alternate case drills and PEI rehearsal daily; our guide on [how to prepare for an interview](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) covers the general structure you can adapt.

## FAQ

**How many interviews does McKinsey have?**
Commonly reported: two first-round and two to three final-round interviews, each containing both a PEI segment and a case, plus the Solve assessment beforehand. Offices vary.

**Can you prepare for the McKinsey Solve game?**
There's nothing to memorize, but familiarity helps: practice general logic games, read McKinsey's own description of the assessment, and go in rested. Strong case-style reasoning habits transfer.

**Is the PEI harder than the case?**
Many candidates underprepare for it, so effectively yes. The case has abundant prep material; the PEI's ten-minute drill into a single story exposes anyone who only prepared surface-level bullet points.
