The JPMorgan Chase interview process usually moves from an online application through assessments and a recorded video interview to final-round interviews with the hiring team. Behavioral questions dominate at every stage, with technical and market questions layered on for banking, markets, and technology roles.

## The JPMorgan Chase interview process

JPMorgan Chase is a huge organization — the investment bank, consumer bank (Chase), asset management, and technology org each run recruiting a bit differently. Still, candidates commonly report a pipeline like this:

1. **Online application.** Standard resume screen, with campus roles funneled through structured programs and deadlines.
2. **Online assessments (role-dependent).** Early-career candidates frequently report a pymetrics-style behavioral games assessment. Software engineering candidates commonly report a coding test (often HackerRank-based) before speaking to anyone.
3. **HireVue video interview.** A recorded one-way interview is a widely reported stage across divisions: a few behavioral and motivation questions, a short prep timer, and a fixed recording window per question.
4. **Final round.** For campus banking and markets roles this often looks like a Superday-style block of back-to-back interviews with team members of varying seniority. For corporate, operations, and tech roles, candidates more commonly report two to four separate interviews — a recruiter or hiring manager conversation followed by panel or one-on-one rounds mixing behavioral, situational, and role-specific technical questions.

Timelines are commonly reported as anywhere from two weeks to two months depending on the business line and whether it's a campus program with a fixed calendar.

## Behavioral questions JPMorgan Chase asks

Frequently reported questions, and what each one is checking:

1. **"Why JPMorgan, and why this specific team?"** — Tests whether you can distinguish the firm's businesses and articulate a real reason beyond brand name.
2. **"Tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult client or customer."** — Probes service instincts; customer focus shows up across the bank's public principles.
3. **"Describe a time you spotted an error others missed."** — Tests attention to detail and the willingness to raise a problem — critical in a controls-heavy business.
4. **"Tell me about a time you had to work with someone very different from you."** — Probes collaboration across teams and functions, which is daily life at a firm this size.
5. **"Give an example of when you took initiative without being asked."** — Looks for ownership rather than task-execution.
6. **"Tell me about your biggest failure."** — Checks accountability; interviewers want a real failure, a real lesson, and evidence you applied it.
7. **"Describe a time you had to make a decision with incomplete information."** — Probes judgment under uncertainty.
8. **"How do you prioritize when everything is urgent?"** — Tests whether you have an actual system, not just stamina.
9. **"Tell me about a time you persuaded someone to change their mind."** — Probes influence without authority.
10. **"What's happening in the economy or markets that interests you right now?"** — For markets-adjacent roles, checks genuine commercial awareness.

## What JPMorgan Chase looks for

JPMorgan Chase publicly frames its culture around its **Business Principles**, grouped under themes like exceptional client service, operational excellence, integrity and fairness, and building a great team and winning culture. In interview terms, that means they're listening for customer-first thinking, rigor and accuracy, honesty about mistakes, and evidence you make teams better. The firm also publicly emphasizes mobility — people building careers across multiple business lines — so curiosity about the broader organization plays well.

For technology roles, JPMorgan is one of the largest tech employers in finance, and interviews reflect standard software engineering bars: coding, system design at senior levels, and behavioral rounds.

## How to prepare

- **Map your stories to the principles.** Before interviewing, sketch six to eight STAR-format stories covering client service, catching an error, teamwork, failure, and initiative. Our [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) show how to structure each one so the result lands in under two minutes.
- **Practice the one-way video format.** HireVue punishes rambling because nobody redirects you. Record yourself answering common questions with a hard two-minute cap and review the tape.
- **Get specific about the team.** "Why JPMorgan" is a weak answer if it would work for any bank. Name the business line, what it does, and why your background maps to it.
- **Prepare role-appropriate technicals.** Bankers: accounting and valuation basics. Markets: a current trade idea or market view. Engineers: data structures, algorithms, and your past projects in depth.
- **Run a full simulation.** Do at least one complete mock covering behavioral plus technical in a single sitting — the endurance matters. Our guide on [how to prepare for an interview](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) lays out a step-by-step plan.

## FAQ

**Does JPMorgan Chase use HireVue for everyone?**
Not universally, but recorded video interviews are commonly reported across campus and early-career hiring, and appear in some experienced-hire processes too. Assume you'll face one and prepare for the format.

**Are JPMorgan interviews mostly behavioral?**
For most non-technical roles, yes — behavioral and situational questions carry the most weight, tied loosely to the firm's public Business Principles. Technical roles add coding or market questions on top, not instead.

**How many interview rounds does JPMorgan Chase have?**
Commonly reported: an assessment and/or video interview plus one final round of several interviews (campus), or two to four rounds spread over weeks (experienced hires). Your recruiter will confirm the exact structure for your role.
