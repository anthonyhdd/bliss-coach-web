Microsoft's interviews combine role-specific rounds with behavioral questions built around its publicly championed growth-mindset culture: learning from failure, collaborating across teams, and obsessing over customers. Walk in with stories about learning fast, helping others succeed, and turning setbacks into improvements, and you're aligned with exactly what interviewers are listening for.

## The Microsoft interview process

The commonly reported Microsoft process:

1. **Application and recruiter screen.** A call about your background, interests, and which team or discipline you're being considered for. University and early-career candidates may see an online assessment first.
2. **Phone/video screen.** One or two interviews, 30-60 minutes. Engineers get a coding exercise; PMs get product and behavioral questions; other roles get discipline-specific screens.
3. **Interview loop.** Typically four to five rounds, virtual or onsite, mixing technical or role-specific interviews with behavioral ones. Engineering loops commonly include coding, system design (level-dependent), and behavioral sessions woven throughout.
4. **"As appropriate" (as-ap) interview.** Candidates commonly report a final conversation with a senior leader or hiring manager who weighs the whole loop's feedback and often carries decisive influence. It usually blends behavioral depth with role fit and career discussion.
5. **Decision and team matching.** Feedback is consolidated and the hiring manager or committee decides. Some pipelines hire you into a specific team from the start; others match after the loop.

Microsoft's process varies noticeably between organizations (Azure, Office, Gaming, etc.), so use this as the typical shape and confirm details with your recruiter.

## Behavioral questions Microsoft asks

Commonly reported Microsoft behavioral questions and what each probes:

1. **"Tell me about a time you failed. What did you learn and apply later?"** — The signature growth-mindset question: they want evidence you actually changed behavior afterward.
2. **"Describe a time you had to learn a new skill or technology quickly."** — Probes "learn-it-all over know-it-all," the mindset Microsoft's leadership has promoted publicly for years.
3. **"Tell me about a time you helped a teammate or another team succeed."** — Probes collaboration and the publicly stated "One Microsoft" value of winning together.
4. **"Describe a time you turned an unhappy customer or stakeholder around."** — Probes customer obsession, one of Microsoft's stated culture attributes.
5. **"Tell me about a disagreement with a colleague and how you handled it."** — Probes respect and accountability — two of Microsoft's three published core values.
6. **"Describe a project where requirements changed midway. What did you do?"** — Probes adaptability and calm re-planning rather than frustration.
7. **"Tell me about a time you took accountability for a mistake that wasn't entirely yours."** — Probes ownership and integrity over blame-shifting.
8. **"How have you made a team or product more inclusive or accessible?"** — Probes alignment with Microsoft's public commitments to diversity, inclusion, and accessibility.
9. **"Tell me about the accomplishment you're most proud of and why."** — Probes motivation, scope of impact, and how you define success.
10. **"Where do you want to grow in the next few years?"** — Probes self-awareness and whether you frame your own development in growth-mindset terms.

Interviewers commonly follow the failure and learning threads deepest — rehearse those stories beyond the first answer.

## What Microsoft looks for

Microsoft is unusually public about its culture, which makes the behavioral rubric legible:

- **Growth mindset.** The centerpiece of Microsoft's publicly described culture transformation: preferring "learn-it-alls" over "know-it-alls," treating failure as data, and improving continuously.
- **Customer obsession.** A stated culture attribute — understanding and solving real customer problems rather than shipping features for their own sake.
- **Diversity and inclusion.** A public commitment Microsoft ties directly to building better products, including its well-known accessibility work.
- **One Microsoft.** Collaborating across team boundaries and optimizing for the company's success, not just your org's.
- **Making a difference.** Tying your work to impact, in the spirit of Microsoft's published mission: "to empower every person and every organization on the planet to achieve more."
- **Core values.** Microsoft publicly names respect, integrity, and accountability as its values — conflict and mistake stories are scored against them.

## How to prepare

- **Build a learning-centric story bank.** Prepare 8-10 STAR stories, and make sure at least three feature genuine failure, feedback, or rapid learning — that's the distinctive Microsoft emphasis. Our [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) show how to tell a failure story that demonstrates growth instead of dwelling on the mistake.
- **Add a "what I learned" beat to every answer.** Even success stories land better at Microsoft when they end with what you'd carry forward. Make it a reflex.
- **Prepare a customer story.** Have at least one example where you dug into what a user or customer actually needed and changed course because of it.
- **Know the team's products.** Microsoft's portfolio is vast; showing you understand the specific product area (and its customers) sets you apart from generic big-tech prep.
- **Practice the full loop format.** Four or five interviews in a day is a stamina game. Build up with timed mock sessions using the plan in our [interview preparation guide](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/).
- **Prepare growth-oriented questions.** Asking how the team handles postmortems, feedback, or career growth mirrors the culture back credibly.

## FAQ

**How long does the Microsoft interview process take?**
Candidates commonly report two to six weeks from recruiter screen to decision, with variation by organization and season (university hiring runs on its own calendar).

**What is the "as appropriate" interview?**
It's the commonly reported final-round conversation with a senior interviewer or hiring manager who has visibility into earlier feedback. Treat it as both a decision interview and a two-way fit conversation — bring real questions.

**Does Microsoft really ask about failure that much?**
Growth mindset is the most publicized element of Microsoft's culture, and candidates consistently report failure-and-learning questions across roles. Prepare at least two distinct failure stories so you're not reusing one.
