Lockheed Martin is one of the largest aerospace and defense contractors in the world, and its hiring reflects the seriousness of the work: rigorous technical interviews, a strong emphasis on teamwork and integrity, and a process shaped by the realities of security clearances and government programs. For engineering and program roles, expect discipline-specific technical questions alongside competency-based behavioral rounds. Because so much of the work is classified, many positions require a U.S. security clearance, and the process often runs longer than in commercial tech as a result. Lockheed screens hard for reliability, collaboration on large multi-year programs, and doing the right thing when it matters.

## The Lockheed Martin interview process

Lockheed Martin recruits by business area (Aeronautics, Missiles and Fire Control, Rotary and Mission Systems, and Space), function, and level, so treat this as the commonly reported shape rather than a fixed script:

1. **Online application and screen.** Resume screen against the specific program and level. **Clearance status matters early** — U.S. citizenship is required for many roles, and existing or eligible clearance can shape which openings you're routed to.
2. **Recruiter or HR screen.** A 20–30 minute call covering your background, motivation, work authorization and citizenship, clearance status, and logistics.
3. **Technical or hiring-manager interview.** Discipline-specific questions for your field — systems, software, mechanical, electrical, aerospace — plus behavioral questions tied to teamwork and program work. Software candidates commonly report practical coding or design questions rather than heavy algorithm puzzles.
4. **Panel interview.** Common at Lockheed: several engineers or managers together, mixing technical depth with competency-based behavioral questions. Some candidates report a short presentation for senior or specialized roles.
5. **Offer, then clearance processing.** For cleared roles, a conditional offer is commonly followed by the security-clearance investigation and adjudication, which can add weeks or months. Roles that don't require clearance move faster.

Commonly reported timelines run from a few weeks to several months, heavily dependent on clearance requirements. If you're also interviewing across aerospace and defense, our [Boeing interview guide](/alex/interviews/boeing/) covers a closely related process.

## Behavioral questions Lockheed Martin asks

These are the questions candidates most frequently report, with what each is really probing:

1. **"Why Lockheed Martin — and why this program?"** — Tests whether you understand the mission and the specific business area. A generic "big defense company" answer is the most commonly reported way to stumble.
2. **"Tell me about a time you worked on a large team or long project."** — Defense programs run for years with many stakeholders; they want evidence you thrive in that, not just fast startups.
3. **"Describe a time you caught or prevented a mistake before it became a problem."** — In aerospace, quality and safety are non-negotiable; this probes rigor and attention to detail.
4. **"Tell me about a time you handled a disagreement with a teammate."** — Collaboration on cross-functional programs; hold an opinion without damaging the team.
5. **"Give an example of when you followed a strict process or requirement."** — Defense work is heavily documented and standards-driven; they want someone who respects process, not routes around it.
6. **"Describe a technical problem you solved and how you approached it."** — Structured, methodical engineering reasoning over flashy shortcuts.
7. **"Tell me about a time you delivered under pressure or a tight schedule."** — Prioritization and communication on schedule-critical programs.
8. **"Give an example of when you demonstrated integrity in a difficult situation."** — Integrity is a stated value and matters intensely in a cleared, safety-critical environment.
9. **"Tell me about a time you had to learn a new system or domain quickly."** — Programs span many technologies; adaptability is valued.

For technical roles, expect discipline-specific depth — systems engineering lifecycle, requirements, safety and reliability, or software design — commonly weighted more toward sound engineering judgment and process than toward competitive-programming puzzles.

## What Lockheed Martin looks for

Stick to what the company states publicly. Lockheed Martin's stated values are **Do What's Right, Respect Others, and Perform With Excellence** — and interviewers are widely reported to map behavioral questions onto them. Because the work is safety-critical, long-cycle, and often classified, integrity, rigor, teamwork, and reliability carry real weight.

Practically, that means interviewers are listening for: integrity and sound judgment under pressure; attention to detail and respect for process in a safety-critical setting; collaboration across large, long-running programs; disciplined technical reasoning; and the reliability to be trusted with sensitive work.

## How to prepare

- **Understand the business area and mission.** Know what your program does — aeronautics, missiles, space, or mission systems — and connect two things from your background to it. Mission fit matters more here than at a typical tech company.
- **Know the clearance basics.** Be ready to discuss citizenship, current clearance status, and eligibility honestly. If you hold an active clearance, say so early; if not, understand the role may hinge on obtaining one.
- **Prepare discipline-specific technical answers.** Refresh the fundamentals of your field and the systems-engineering lifecycle; emphasize sound, safe, well-documented reasoning over clever hacks.
- **Build a STAR story bank.** Six to eight structured stories covering long-team collaboration, catching a mistake, following process, integrity, and delivering under pressure will cover almost everything above. ## A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you caught or prevented a mistake before it became a problem."**

- **Situation:** "During integration testing, I noticed a requirement in our interface spec had been interpreted two different ways by two subteams, and neither had flagged it."
- **Task:** "I owned the interface verification and needed to resolve it before the mismatch reached hardware testing."
- **Action:** "I traced both interpretations back to the ambiguous requirement, wrote up the discrepancy with the exact clause and both readings, and pulled the two leads plus systems engineering into a short review to agree on one definition — then I updated the spec and the test cases to match."
- **Result:** "We caught it before it cost us a hardware retest, and the clarified requirement prevented the same confusion downstream. It taught me that in a big program, surfacing an ambiguity early and in writing is worth far more than assuming everyone read it the same way."

Notice the shape: one sentence of context, a clear personal task, three concrete actions, a result plus a lesson. That's the register Lockheed Martin interviewers commonly reward.

## FAQ

### Do I need a security clearance to work at Lockheed Martin?
Many roles require a U.S. security clearance and U.S. citizenship, but not all. Some positions start uncleared and sponsor you for one. Be honest about your status early — it directly affects which roles you can be routed to and how long the process takes.

### How long does the Lockheed Martin hiring process take?
Commonly reported: a few weeks for uncleared roles, but potentially months when a clearance investigation is involved. The interview itself is quicker than the clearance adjudication that can follow a conditional offer.

### Are Lockheed Martin interviews heavy on coding puzzles?
Software roles report practical coding and design questions, but candidates commonly say the emphasis leans toward sound engineering judgment, requirements, safety, and process rather than competitive-programming-style puzzles.

### What should I emphasize in a Lockheed Martin interview?
