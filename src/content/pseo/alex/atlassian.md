Atlassian makes team-collaboration and developer tools — Jira, Confluence, and the rest — and it hires across software engineering, product and program management, design, and go-to-market roles. Its interview process is distinctive for one reason above all: a dedicated **values interview** that carries real weight alongside the technical rounds. Atlassian takes its company values seriously and screens for them explicitly. Expect a recruiter screen, role-specific technical or functional rounds, and a values round you should prepare for as deliberately as the coding.

## The Atlassian interview process

Atlassian recruits by function and region and runs a largely distributed process, so treat this as the commonly reported shape rather than a fixed script:

1. **Application and resume screen.** Reviewed against the specific track — software engineer, product manager, program manager, designer, or corporate. Referrals and internships are widely reported to help early-career candidates.
2. **Recruiter screen.** A 20–30 minute call on your background, motivation for Atlassian, remote/location expectations, and compensation basics.
3. **Technical or functional screen.** Engineering candidates commonly report a coding round (data structures and algorithms); PMs report product-sense and execution questions; designers present a portfolio.
4. **Onsite / virtual loop.** For engineers: additional coding plus system design. For PMs: product design, analytical/execution, and strategy questions. Most tracks include cross-functional and craft-specific interviews.
5. **Values interview.** A dedicated round mapped to Atlassian's published values — expect behavioral questions probing how you actually work, collaborate, and treat teammates and customers. This round genuinely influences the decision.

Commonly reported timelines run from three to six weeks depending on track, level, and region. If you're comparing other developer-and-enterprise-software employers, our [Microsoft interview guide](/alex/interviews/microsoft/) covers a related process.

## Behavioral questions Atlassian asks

These are the questions candidates most frequently report, with what each is really probing:

1. **"Why Atlassian — and why this role?"** — Tests whether you understand the products and the team-collaboration mission, not just that it's a strong tech company.
2. **"Tell me about a time you put the team's success ahead of your own."** — Maps directly to Atlassian's "play as a team" value; they want a concrete example.
3. **"Describe a time you delivered real value for a customer or user."** — Maps to "don't #@!% the customer"; specificity beats slogans.
4. **"Tell me about a time you gave or received difficult feedback."** — Maps to "open company, no bullshit"; probes candor and psychological safety.
5. **"Give an example of when you took initiative to improve something."** — Maps to "be the change you seek"; they want ownership, not permission-seeking.
6. **"Tell me about a time you failed. What did you learn?"** — An ownership check: name it plainly, then show the correction and the lesson.
7. **"Describe a disagreement with a teammate and how you resolved it."** — Tests constructive conflict and whether you can commit once decided.
8. **"How do you balance moving fast with getting it right?"** — Relevant to shipping software collaboratively at scale.
9. **"Walk me through a project on your resume end to end."** — Tests structured communication; rehearse a two-minute version out loud.
10. **"What does great teamwork look like to you?"** — A direct culture-fit probe; connect it to a real experience.

For engineers, the technical rounds sit alongside the values interview, and both matter. For PMs, expect product-sense, analytical, and execution questions in addition to values.

## What Atlassian looks for

Atlassian publishes its values explicitly and interviews against them: **open company, no bullshit; build with heart and balance; don't #@!% the customer; play as a team; be the change you seek.** Interviewers map the values round directly onto these, so vague answers stand out. Beyond values, the company looks for solid craft in your discipline and the ability to collaborate well in a largely distributed environment.

Practically, that means they're evaluating: genuine teamwork and candor; customer orientation; ownership and initiative; craft depth appropriate to the role; and clear communication across a distributed team.

## How to prepare

- **Prepare specifically for the values round.** Read Atlassian's five values and map at least one concrete STAR story to each. This is the round candidates most often underestimate. Structuring these answers well is essential — see our guide to [behavioral interview questions](/alex/blog/behavioral-interview-questions/).
- **Build a STAR story bank.** Six to eight structured stories covering teamwork, customer impact, candid feedback, initiative, and failure will cover the values round and most behavioral follow-ups. Our guide to [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) breaks the format down with worked answers.
- **Match technical prep to your track.** Engineers should drill data structures, algorithms, and a clean system-design walkthrough; PMs should practice product-sense and execution questions; designers should tighten the portfolio narrative.
- **Learn the products.** Be able to speak concretely about Jira, Confluence, or the relevant tool and how the team you're joining contributes.
- **Rehearse the resume walkthrough and run a timed mock.** Two minutes, outcome first — then practice under pressure so follow-ups don't rattle you.

## A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you put the team's success ahead of your own."**

- **Situation:** "Two of us were up for the same high-visibility feature, but a critical piece of shared infrastructure was quietly falling behind and nobody wanted it."
- **Task:** "I had to decide between the visible feature and the unglamorous work the whole team depended on."
- **Action:** "I took the infrastructure work, paired with the other engineer so they could ship the feature well, and set up the tooling so the next person wouldn't inherit the same mess."
- **Result:** "The feature shipped on time, the shared infrastructure stopped being a recurring fire, and the team started trusting me with the load-bearing work. It taught me that the least visible task is sometimes the highest-leverage one."

Notice the shape: one sentence of context, a clear personal task, three concrete actions, a result plus a lesson. That's the register Atlassian interviewers commonly reward.

## FAQ

### What is the Atlassian values interview?
It's a dedicated round mapped to Atlassian's five published values, with behavioral questions about how you actually collaborate, handle feedback, and treat customers and teammates. It genuinely affects the decision, so prepare a story for each value.

### Does Atlassian ask coding questions?
For software-engineering roles, yes — candidates commonly report coding rounds (data structures and algorithms) plus system design, in addition to the values interview. PM and design tracks are functional and portfolio-based. Confirm the loop with your recruiter.

### How long does the Atlassian hiring process take?
Commonly reported: three to six weeks, depending on track, level, and region. Campus cycles follow set calendars; experienced hires vary more.

### What should I wear to an Atlassian interview?
Casual to business-casual is the commonly reported norm, reflecting a relaxed culture. For video interviews, dress neatly from the waist up and test your setup beforehand.
