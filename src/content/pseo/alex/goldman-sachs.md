The Goldman Sachs interview process typically runs from an online application through a recorded video interview to a final round of back-to-back interviews with bankers and team members. Expect a heavy emphasis on behavioral questions built around the firm's stated values, plus role-specific technical questions for markets, banking, and engineering positions.

## The Goldman Sachs interview process

Goldman Sachs doesn't publish a single fixed pipeline, and the exact steps vary by division, level, and region. That said, candidates commonly report a process along these lines:

1. **Online application and resume screen.** Recruiters filter for relevant experience, academics, and demonstrated interest in the division you applied to.
2. **HireVue video interview.** Goldman is well known for using recorded one-way video interviews early in the process, especially for campus and early-career roles. You typically get a prompt on screen, a short prep window, and a fixed time to record your answer. Questions are mostly behavioral, with some motivation questions ("Why Goldman Sachs?", "Why this division?").
3. **Assessments (role-dependent).** Engineering candidates commonly report coding assessments (often on HackerRank); some markets and analytics roles report numerical or aptitude-style tests.
4. **Final round / "Superday."** The traditional final stage is a series of back-to-back interviews — often three to six sessions of roughly 30 minutes — with analysts, associates, VPs, and sometimes MDs from the team. Since the shift to hybrid recruiting, Superdays are sometimes run virtually. Expect a mix of behavioral questions, market or deal awareness, technical questions appropriate to the role, and fit conversations.

Timelines vary widely: campus recruiting follows a structured seasonal calendar, while experienced-hire processes can move in weeks or stretch longer depending on the team.

## Behavioral questions Goldman Sachs asks

These are questions candidates frequently report, along with what each one is really probing:

1. **"Why Goldman Sachs, and why this division?"** — Tests whether you understand the firm's businesses well enough to make a specific, non-generic case.
2. **"Tell me about a time you worked on a team with conflicting personalities."** — Probes collaboration under friction; the firm's culture leans heavily on teamwork.
3. **"Describe a time you had to deliver under a tight deadline."** — Tests composure and prioritization in a high-pressure environment.
4. **"Tell me about a time you failed. What did you do next?"** — Looks for honest self-assessment and recovery, not a humble-brag.
5. **"Give an example of when you went above and beyond for a client or stakeholder."** — Maps directly to the firm's client-service value.
6. **"Tell me about a time you had to learn something complex quickly."** — Probes learning speed, which matters more than existing knowledge for analyst roles.
7. **"Describe a situation where you disagreed with a supervisor or teammate."** — Tests whether you can push back with judgment and respect.
8. **"What's a recent market or business story that interested you?"** — Checks genuine commercial curiosity, not just interview prep.
9. **"Tell me about a time you juggled multiple competing commitments."** — Probes the time management the job will actually demand.
10. **"Walk me through your resume."** — The opener that decides the interview's tone; they're listening for a coherent narrative, not a list.

## What Goldman Sachs looks for

Goldman Sachs publicly describes its core values as **client service, excellence, integrity, and partnership**. In interviews, that translates into evidence that you put the client or stakeholder first, hold yourself to a high bar, are straight about mistakes, and genuinely work well in teams. The firm also publicly emphasizes that it hires from all majors and backgrounds — the common threads in its own recruiting materials are intellectual curiosity, drive, and teamwork rather than a specific pedigree.

For technical roles, demonstrated skill matters alongside values: engineers should expect data structures, algorithms, and system questions; markets candidates should be comfortable talking through current market dynamics.

## How to prepare

- **Build a story bank first.** Draft six to eight stories from work, school, or projects that cover teamwork, conflict, failure, leadership, and pressure. Structure each one using the STAR framework — our [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) walk through exactly how to shape them.
- **Rehearse for the camera.** HireVue answers reward concision and structure because there's no interviewer to redirect you. Practice answering out loud in 90 seconds to 2 minutes, on camera, until the format feels normal.
- **Prepare a sharp "why us."** Read the firm's own materials on the division you're targeting and be able to name what the group actually does. Generic prestige answers stand out — badly.
- **Know the news.** For any markets-adjacent role, be ready to discuss one or two current market themes with a point of view.
- **Do a full dry run.** Simulate the Superday format: several interviews in a row, mixed behavioral and technical, with no breaks in your energy level. ## FAQ

**Does Goldman Sachs still use HireVue?**
Candidates continue to report recorded video interviews as a standard early stage, particularly for campus and early-career hiring. Treat it as likely for your process, and prepare for the one-way format specifically — it feels different from a live conversation.

**How hard are Goldman Sachs behavioral interviews?**
The questions themselves are standard; the bar is in the delivery. Interviewers see thousands of candidates, so vague or rambling stories fail fast. Specific, structured, honest answers — with real numbers and outcomes where possible — are what pass.

**How long does the Goldman Sachs interview process take?**
It varies by role and season. Campus processes follow a set recruiting calendar; experienced hires commonly report a few weeks to a couple of months from application to offer, depending on team urgency.
