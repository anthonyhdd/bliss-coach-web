Meta's interview process pairs demanding role-specific rounds with a dedicated behavioral interview that tests how you operate: speed, directness, ownership of impact, and how you handle conflict. The candidates who do best arrive with crisp stories about shipping things fast, measuring their impact, and disagreeing productively.

## The Meta interview process

The commonly reported process at Meta looks like this, with variations by role and level:

1. **Recruiter screen.** A call on background, motivation, and logistics. Meta recruiters are commonly described as generous with prep material — read everything they send; it reflects the actual rubrics.
2. **Technical or role screen.** For engineers, one 45-minute coding interview; for product managers, a product-sense or execution screen; for design, a portfolio conversation. Some tracks use two screens.
3. **Full loop (onsite/virtual).** Typically three to five interviews. Engineering loops commonly include two coding rounds, a system design round (level-dependent), and a behavioral round. PM loops commonly include product sense, execution, and leadership/behavioral interviews.
4. **Behavioral interview.** A dedicated session on how you work with others, handle setbacks, and drive impact. Interviewers probe for specifics and follow up hard on vague claims.
5. **Debrief, calibration, and team matching.** Interviewers submit feedback, a hiring decision is calibrated across candidates, and for many roles you enter team matching after a successful loop — meaning you can pass the loop and then spend additional weeks finding a team.

As always, treat this as the typical reported shape; your recruiter's outline for your specific role wins.

## Behavioral questions Meta asks

Commonly reported Meta behavioral questions, and what each probes:

1. **"Tell me about a project you're most proud of. What was your specific contribution?"** — Probes ownership and whether you can isolate *your* impact from the team's.
2. **"Describe a time you received difficult feedback. What did you do?"** — Probes coachability and Meta's publicly stated norm of direct communication.
3. **"Tell me about a time you disagreed with a colleague or your manager."** — Probes whether you can be direct while respecting colleagues, and how you resolve rather than escalate.
4. **"Describe a time you had to move fast with incomplete information."** — Probes bias to action and how you manage risk when speed matters.
5. **"Tell me about a time a project failed or was cancelled. How did you respond?"** — Probes resilience and learning; Meta's culture materials openly celebrate intelligent risk-taking.
6. **"How have you handled competing priorities from multiple stakeholders?"** — Probes prioritization and communication under pressure.
7. **"Tell me about a time you improved something that wasn't your responsibility."** — Probes initiative and end-to-end ownership.
8. **"Describe the biggest conflict you've had on a team."** — Probes self-awareness and conflict style; interviewers often ask what the other person would say about you.
9. **"Tell me about a time you used data to make a product or technical decision."** — Probes the measurement-driven mindset Meta describes in its own engineering culture content.
10. **"Why Meta, and why now?"** — Probes motivation and whether you understand the company's actual product and mission beyond the brand.

Expect follow-ups drilling into scope ("how many people were affected?"), your exact role, and what you'd do differently.

## What Meta looks for

Meta publishes its company values, and they map directly onto the behavioral rubric:

- **Move fast** — acting with urgency, removing barriers, not waiting for perfect information.
- **Focus on long-term impact** — prioritizing work by durable impact rather than short-term wins.
- **Build awesome things** — a bar of shipping things people actually find valuable, not just adequate.
- **Live in the future** — willingness to use and build for what's next.
- **Be direct and respect your colleagues** — candid feedback delivered with respect; conflict handled openly.
- **Meta, Metamates, me** — a publicly stated ordering: company mission, then team, then self.

You don't need to recite these in interviews. You need stories where the behaviors are self-evident: shipped quickly, measured impact, gave or took hard feedback well, and put the team's outcome above personal credit.

## How to prepare

- **Anchor every story in measurable impact.** Meta's culture is explicit about impact, so quantify: users affected, revenue, latency, adoption, time saved. If your current role doesn't produce big numbers, show rigor with the numbers you have.
- **Prepare a genuine conflict story.** The behavioral round almost always goes here. Pick a real disagreement, show both sides fairly, and land on resolution and relationship repair. Structure it with STAR — see our worked [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) for how to keep conflict stories from sounding defensive.
- **Have a fast-decision story ready.** "We shipped in two weeks by cutting X and accepting Y risk" is the shape interviewers listen for.
- **For technical rounds, drill under time pressure.** Meta coding interviews are commonly reported as fast-paced — two problems in 45 minutes is a frequent expectation — so practice pacing, not just correctness.
- **Do full mock loops.** Simulate the back-to-back format at least once before the real loop; our [interview preparation guide](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) lays out a schedule that builds up to it.
- **Know the products.** Have a real opinion about a Meta product you use — what works, what you'd improve, and why.

## FAQ

**How long does Meta's interview process take?**
Commonly reported timelines are three to eight weeks from recruiter screen to decision, with team matching sometimes adding more time after a successful loop.

**Is the Meta behavioral interview really that important?**
Yes. Candidates and interviewers alike commonly report that a weak behavioral round can sink an otherwise strong loop, because it's the primary signal on collaboration and values.

**Does Meta still ask "Facebook" style questions?**
The company renamed itself, but the interview substance is the same: role-specific depth plus behavioral questions aligned to its published values. Older "Facebook interview" resources are still largely relevant.

To make sure your impact stories land under real follow-up pressure, rehearse them aloud with a mock-interview coach like Alex before your loop — it's the difference between knowing a story and delivering it.
