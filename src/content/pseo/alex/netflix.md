Netflix interviews are an extended test of fit with its famous public culture memo: high talent density, radical candor, and freedom paired with responsibility. More than at any other major tech company, you should read the primary source — the culture page Netflix publishes openly — because interviewers hire against it explicitly.

## The Netflix interview process

Netflix hires team by team, and hiring managers have unusual authority — the culture memo itself describes managers as owners of hiring decisions. The commonly reported flow:

1. **Recruiter screen.** A conversation about your background and, notably, about the culture — recruiters commonly gauge whether you've read and genuinely engaged with the memo.
2. **Hiring manager interview.** Earlier and weightier than at most companies. Expect a direct, substantive conversation about your craft, judgment, and how you operate.
3. **Interview loop.** Typically four to seven interviews with team members and cross-functional partners, mixing deep role-specific sessions (coding, system design, domain cases, portfolio) with culture-focused behavioral conversations. Some candidates report a second, shorter loop or follow-up round with senior leaders.
4. **Debrief and decision.** Feedback goes to the hiring manager, who makes the call. Candidates commonly describe the process as fast and decisive relative to peer companies — consistent with a culture that publicly prizes speed and judgment.

Netflix hires experienced practitioners into specific roles ("we're a team, not a family" is their own public phrasing, alongside the sports-team analogy), so expect depth over puzzles: interviews probe how you've actually operated at a high level.

## Behavioral questions Netflix asks

Commonly reported Netflix behavioral questions, each tied to the culture it probes:

1. **"What do you think of our culture memo? What would you push back on?"** — Probes whether you've done the reading and can practice the candor the memo demands — agreeing with everything is itself a red flag.
2. **"Tell me about the hardest feedback you've ever given someone."** — Probes willingness to be direct even when uncomfortable, a core memo behavior.
3. **"Tell me about the hardest feedback you've ever received."** — Probes openness and whether you treat candor as a gift rather than an attack.
4. **"Describe a significant decision you made without asking permission."** — Probes the memo's "freedom and responsibility": informed captains acting with context, not control.
5. **"Tell me about a time you disagreed with your leadership and said so."** — Probes courage and the memo's expectation that you dissent openly rather than comply silently.
6. **"Walk me through a judgment call you got wrong. What did it cost, and what changed?"** — Probes honest self-assessment and the memo's emphasis on judgment as the top skill.
7. **"How do you operate with minimal process or oversight?"** — Probes self-discipline; Netflix publicly describes minimizing rules and expecting responsible adults.
8. **"Tell me about a time you raised the bar on your team — or dealt with underperformance."** — Probes the "dream team" standard: adequate performance isn't enough, and they want people who care about talent density.
9. **"What would your last team say it was like to work with you — including the criticism?"** — Probes self-awareness and whether you can be candid about yourself, not just others.
10. **"Why Netflix, and why does this culture suit you?"** — Probes genuine fit versus brand attraction; interviewers listen for specifics from the memo, not platitudes.

## What Netflix looks for

Everything here is drawn from Netflix's own published culture materials:

- **High talent density / the dream team.** Netflix publicly describes itself as a professional sports team aiming for stars in every position, with the "keeper test" as the management standard it openly documents.
- **Judgment.** The memo puts wise, context-aware decision-making above process-following — making great decisions amid ambiguity is explicitly valued.
- **Candor.** Direct, constructive feedback in all directions ("only say things about fellow employees you say to their face" is the memo's own formulation).
- **Freedom and responsibility.** Context over control: leaders set context, individuals make informed decisions and own the outcomes.
- **Selflessness and curiosity.** The published values include seeking what's best for Netflix over your group, and learning voraciously.
- **People over process.** Netflix openly aims to keep rules minimal and hire people who don't need them.

## How to prepare

- **Read the actual culture memo — twice.** It's public on Netflix's jobs site. Form one genuine point of agreement, one genuine reservation, and be ready to defend both plainly. This single step separates prepared candidates from everyone else.
- **Build candor stories.** Prepare STAR stories for feedback given, feedback received, and open disagreement with authority. Keep them honest — sanitized versions collapse under Netflix-style follow-ups. Our [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) show how to structure uncomfortable stories without spinning them.
- **Prepare autonomy stories.** Document decisions you made independently, the context you gathered first, and the outcomes — including one that went wrong and what you owned afterward.
- **Bring senior-level depth in your craft.** Netflix hires experienced practitioners; expect your loop to go deeper into your real work than a standard big-tech loop. Refresh the details of your flagship projects.
- **Practice being direct in the room.** Hedged, corporate answers clash with the culture. Rehearse concise, plain-spoken delivery — the mock-interview cadence in our [interview preparation guide](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) is built for exactly this.
- **Interview them back.** Asking pointed questions about how the team practices candor or the keeper test demonstrates fit better than claiming it.

## FAQ

**How long does the Netflix interview process take?**
Candidates commonly report a comparatively fast process — often two to four weeks from screen to decision — reflecting the hiring manager's authority and the company's stated bias for speed.

**Do I really need to read the culture memo?**
Yes. It's the single most commonly reported interview topic, interviewers reference it directly, and being asked what you'd challenge in it is a standard question. It's public — there's no excuse to skip it.

**Is the "keeper test" used in interviews?**
The keeper test is a management practice Netflix documents publicly, not an interview stage — but interviewers commonly explore whether you'd thrive under that standard, so expect questions about performance bars and accountability.
