No large U.S. employer is more openly obsessed with culture fit than Southwest Airlines. The company built its public identity on the idea — often attributed to co-founder Herb Kelleher — of hiring for attitude and training for skill, and candidates consistently report that interviews are designed to surface personality, humor, and warmth as much as competence. Expect behavioral questions, group exercises for flight attendant roles, and evaluators watching how you treat other candidates when you think no one is scoring you.

## The Southwest Airlines interview process

Southwest doesn't publish one fixed pipeline, and stages shift between hiring waves. What follows is what candidates commonly report.

**Flight attendant track.** One of the most discussed hiring processes in the industry, usually described in these stages:

1. **Online application** — extremely high volume; customer-facing experience and open availability help you surface.
2. **Video interview** — commonly reported as a recorded or live video stage with behavioral and motivational prompts ("Tell me about a time you turned around someone's bad day," "Why Southwest?").
3. **Group interview or event day** — the famous stage. Candidates report group activities, short individual interviews, and sometimes an ask to share a story or introduce another candidate in front of the room. Evaluators are widely reported to watch behavior between activities — how you treat fellow applicants, staff, and downtime.
4. **One-on-one or panel interviews** — deeper behavioral conversation, often with recruiters and in-flight leaders.
5. **Conditional offer and training** — background checks, then several weeks of training in Dallas that candidates must pass to fly.

The full cycle commonly stretches across weeks or months, and many successful candidates report applying more than once.

**Customer service, ramp, and operations track.** Commonly reported as a video or phone screen followed by an in-person or panel interview, with the same personality-forward tone plus availability, physical requirements, and safety discussion.

**Corporate track.** Roles at the Dallas headquarters typically follow a recruiter screen, hiring manager interview, and panel round; technical roles may add a skills exercise. Culture questions persist all the way up.

## Behavioral questions Southwest asks

These are the questions candidates most often report, with what each is really probing:

1. **"Why Southwest?"** — The essential question, and generic airline answers fail. They're testing whether you get what makes this company different: the culture, the humor, the people-first reputation.
2. **"Tell me about a time you used humor to defuse a situation."** — A distinctly Southwest question. They want judgment plus levity — humor that lowered the temperature, not a stand-up routine.
3. **"Describe a time you turned a customer's bad experience around."** — The core service question; they're listening for empathy, initiative, and a real outcome.
4. **"Tell me about a time you went out of your way for a coworker."** — Southwest's culture language is famously employee-first; they want evidence you invest in the people beside you.
5. **"Describe a time you had to follow a rule you disagreed with."** — Aviation runs on compliance. This checks whether your fun-loving side coexists with respect for procedure.
6. **"Tell me about a time your plans fell apart. What did you do?"** — Weather and irregular operations are routine; flexibility is the trait under the microscope.
7. **"Have you ever had a conflict with a teammate? How did you handle it?"** — Tests direct, kind communication in a crew environment.
8. **"Tell me about a time you noticed a safety concern."** — Non-negotiable in aviation; proactive answers beat "I mentioned it to my manager."
9. **"What does being a good teammate mean to you? Give an example."** — An invitation to show the servant-hearted behavior the company publicly celebrates.
10. **"Tell me about yourself — and make it interesting."** — An open-ended prompt designed purely to see personality. Rehearsed monotone is the only wrong answer.

## What Southwest looks for

Stick to what the company says publicly. Southwest's published values are unusually concrete: a Warrior Spirit (work hard, persevere, strive to be the best), a Servant's Heart (follow the golden rule, treat others with respect, put others first), and a Fun-LUVing Attitude (don't take yourself too seriously, celebrate, be a passionate team player). The company's employees-first philosophy — happy employees make happy customers — is one of the most publicly documented cultural positions in American business, and its stock ticker, LUV, is part of the brand's identity.

Practically, that means evaluators are listening — and watching — for: genuine warmth rather than rehearsed enthusiasm, humor with good judgment, generosity toward other candidates during group stages, resilience under stress, and safety-mindedness underneath the fun.

## How to prepare

- **Build a story bank around service, teamwork, humor, and adaptability.** Five or six STAR-format stories cover nearly every question above. Our guide to [behavioral interview questions](/alex/blog/behavioral-interview-questions/) maps the themes, and [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) shows how to structure each one.
- **Prepare a humor story with judgment built in.** The strongest version shows you reading the room: a tense moment, a light touch, and a situation that got easier. Avoid stories where the joke was the point.
- **Make "why Southwest" personal and specific.** A flight you remember, the culture's public reputation, the employees-first philosophy — anything real, as long as the answer couldn't be copy-pasted to another airline.
- **Practice performing warmth in a group.** If you're headed for a flight attendant event day, rehearse introducing yourself — and someone else — to a room. Generosity toward other candidates is commonly reported as part of the evaluation.
- **Balance fun with procedure.** Have one story ready that shows you following a rule or safety protocol cheerfully — spirit and discipline together. For a contrast in style, our [Delta interview guide](/alex/interviews/delta/) covers a more formal airline process.

## A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you used humor to defuse a tense situation."**

- **Situation:** "I was working the register when our card system went down with a line of about fifteen people, and the frustration was building fast."
- **Task:** "I needed to keep the line calm and moving on cash while the system rebooted, without pretending nothing was wrong."
- **Action:** "I announced the outage honestly, then kept up a running bit — narrating the reboot like a sports commentator and taking guesses on how many seconds it would need. While people laughed, I moved cash payers through and wrote down orders for the card payers so nobody lost their place."
- **Result:** "Nobody left the line, two customers joined the countdown, and when the system came back we cleared the backlog in a few minutes. My manager asked me to handle the announcement the next time it happened."

Notice the shape: honesty first, humor as a tool rather than a distraction, and practical action running underneath the levity. That combination — Servant's Heart doing the work, Fun-LUVing Attitude setting the tone — is exactly what Southwest interviews are built to find.

## FAQ

**How hard is it to get hired at Southwest Airlines?**
Very competitive, especially for flight attendant roles — the airline is widely reported to receive far more applications than openings, and many successful candidates applied more than once. Specific, warm, structured answers are what separate similar resumes.

**What is the Southwest group interview like?**
Candidates commonly report group activities, short individual conversations, and prompts like introducing yourself or another candidate to the room. Evaluators are widely reported to observe behavior throughout the event — including how you treat other applicants between exercises.

**How long does the Southwest hiring process take?**
Candidates commonly report several weeks to a few months for flight attendant roles across the video, group, and panel stages. Corporate and operations roles typically move in a few weeks.

**What should I wear to a Southwest interview?**
Polished business attire is the commonly reported norm for flight attendant events, even though the airline's everyday culture is casual. Corporate interviews follow standard business or business casual.

**Do I need airline experience to work at Southwest?**
No. Customer service experience from any industry is the commonly cited foundation, and Southwest trains new hires on everything aviation-specific. The consistently reported bar is attitude: warmth, resilience, and humor with good judgment.
