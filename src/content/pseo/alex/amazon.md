Amazon interviews are built almost entirely around the company's publicly documented Leadership Principles: expect a structured process where nearly every question asks for a specific past example, told with data and told honestly. If you can walk in with 10-12 strong stories mapped to those principles and deliver them in STAR format, you've done the single highest-leverage piece of preparation.

## The Amazon interview process

Amazon's process is unusually consistent across roles, and the company itself describes much of it on its careers site. The commonly reported flow looks like this:

1. **Application and recruiter screen.** A short call covering your background, motivation, and logistics. Some roles add an online assessment (work simulations or role-specific tests) before you speak to anyone.
2. **Phone or video screen.** Usually one interview, 45-60 minutes, mixing behavioral questions with role-specific ones (coding for engineers, case-style questions for PMs and operations roles).
3. **The Loop.** Amazon's name for its onsite (now often virtual) round: typically four to five back-to-back interviews of about an hour each. Every interviewer is assigned specific Leadership Principles to probe, which is why behavioral questions dominate even for deeply technical roles.
4. **The Bar Raiser.** One interviewer in the Loop is a trained "Bar Raiser" from outside the hiring team. Their publicly stated job is to keep the hiring bar high and act as an objective voice; they carry significant weight in the debrief.
5. **Debrief and decision.** Interviewers submit written feedback with evidence, then meet to decide. Timelines vary, but candidates commonly report hearing back within a week or two of the Loop.

Exact stages differ by level and organization, so treat this as the typical shape rather than a guarantee — your recruiter will confirm the specifics.

## Behavioral questions Amazon asks

These are among the most commonly reported Amazon behavioral questions. Each maps to one or more Leadership Principles:

1. **"Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager and what you did."** — Probes Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit: can you push back respectfully, then commit?
2. **"Describe a time you had to make a decision without all the data you wanted."** — Probes Bias for Action and judgment under ambiguity.
3. **"Tell me about a time you failed. What did you learn?"** — Probes ownership and self-awareness; Amazon expects a real failure, not a humblebrag.
4. **"Give me an example of going above and beyond for a customer."** — Probes Customer Obsession, the first and most-cited principle.
5. **"Tell me about a time you took on something outside your job description."** — Probes Ownership: acting on behalf of the whole company, not just your team.
6. **"Describe a time you simplified a process or invented a better way."** — Probes Invent and Simplify; interviewers want measurable before/after impact.
7. **"Tell me about a time you had to deliver results under a tight deadline."** — Probes Deliver Results and how you prioritize when everything is urgent.
8. **"Describe a time you received tough feedback."** — Probes Earn Trust and whether you're genuinely coachable.
9. **"Tell me about a time you made a decision that was unpopular."** — Probes conviction, communication, and Are Right, A Lot.
10. **"Give an example of a time you dove deep to find the root cause of a problem."** — Probes Dive Deep: staying connected to details instead of delegating blindly.

Interviewers commonly follow up with "what were the exact numbers?" and "what would you do differently?" — vague answers stall quickly.

## What Amazon looks for

Amazon publishes its 16 Leadership Principles openly, and they are the explicit rubric for behavioral interviews: Customer Obsession; Ownership; Invent and Simplify; Are Right, A Lot; Learn and Be Curious; Hire and Develop the Best; Insist on the Highest Standards; Think Big; Bias for Action; Frugality; Earn Trust; Dive Deep; Have Backbone, Disagree and Commit; Deliver Results; Strive to be Earth's Best Employer; and Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility.

You don't need a story for all 16. Most candidates report that a core set — Customer Obsession, Ownership, Deliver Results, Dive Deep, Bias for Action, Earn Trust, and Have Backbone — covers the large majority of questions. What matters is evidence: specific situations, your individual actions ("I", not "we"), and quantified results.

## How to prepare

- **Build a story bank first.** Write out 10-12 real experiences — wins, failures, conflicts, deadlines — and tag each with the 2-3 Leadership Principles it demonstrates. One strong story can flex across several principles if you shift the emphasis.
- **Drill the STAR format until it's automatic.** Amazon interviewers are trained to listen for Situation, Task, Action, Result, and they will interrupt rambling answers. Work through concrete examples in our guide to [STAR method answers](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) so your structure holds up under follow-ups.
- **Quantify everything you can.** "Reduced processing time by 30%" beats "made things faster." If you don't have exact figures, use honest estimates and say so.
- **Prepare for the follow-up ladder.** Expect three to five probing follow-ups per story: metrics, trade-offs, what you'd change. Rehearse going deeper, not just delivering the opening answer.
- **Practice out loud, under time pressure.** Reading notes isn't rehearsal. Do full mock interviews and review your answers critically — our broader [interview preparation checklist](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) covers how to structure that practice week by week.
- **Prepare real questions for your interviewers.** Asking sharp questions about the team's customers and metrics signals the same principles they're testing you on.

## FAQ

**How long does the Amazon interview process take?**
Candidates commonly report two to six weeks from application to offer, though it varies by role, level, and hiring urgency. The Loop itself is usually completed in a single day.

**Does every Amazon interview include behavioral questions?**
For most roles, yes. Even technical interviews typically reserve time for one or two Leadership Principle questions, and the Loop dedicates entire sessions to them.

**What is a Bar Raiser and should I prepare differently?**
A Bar Raiser is a trained interviewer from outside the hiring team whose role — as Amazon describes publicly — is to keep hiring standards high. You won't always know which interviewer it is, so the practical answer is: prepare every session to the same standard.
