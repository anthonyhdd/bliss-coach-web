The BCG (Boston Consulting Group) interview process is built around the case interview. Across rounds, candidates commonly report a mix of interviewer-led cases, a personal-experience (behavioral) discussion, and — in some offices — an online problem-solving assessment. The case is the main event: you're given a business problem and expected to structure it, work through the analysis with the interviewer, and land a clear recommendation.

## The BCG interview process

BCG doesn't publish one fixed pipeline, and steps vary by office, level, and region. Candidates commonly report a sequence along these lines:

1. **Application and resume screen.** Recruiters filter for academics, leadership, and problem-solving signals; a referral or strong networking often helps.
2. **Online assessment (office-dependent).** BCG has used an online problem-solving / "Casey" chatbot-style test and other digital assessments in some markets. Treat it as possible and prepare for a timed, interactive case-style format.
3. **First-round interviews.** Usually two interviews, each combining a **case** and a shorter **personal-experience/behavioral** portion.
4. **Final round.** Typically two or three interviews with more senior consultants and partners, again mixing cases with fit conversations. Partners may bring looser, more judgment-driven cases.

Timelines vary; campus recruiting follows a seasonal calendar, while experienced hires depend on team needs.

## How the BCG case works

BCG cases are usually **interviewer-led**: the interviewer guides you through a business problem in steps rather than letting you run the whole thing. You'll commonly need to:

- **Clarify the objective** and restate the problem.
- **Structure the problem** into a logical, MECE framework tailored to the situation (not a memorized template).
- **Work through analysis**, including quick, accurate mental math and interpreting exhibits or data.
- **Draw insights**, not just calculations — what does the number mean for the client?
- **Recommend** a clear answer with reasoning and next steps.

Common case types include profitability, market entry, market sizing, pricing, and growth strategy.

## Behavioral questions BCG asks

In the personal-experience portion, candidates commonly report:

1. **"Why consulting, and why BCG?"** — Tests genuine interest and understanding of the firm.
2. **"Tell me about a time you led a team."** — Probes leadership and how you bring people along.
3. **"Describe a time you faced a major challenge or setback."** — Looks for resilience and what you learned.
4. **"Tell me about a time you persuaded someone to change their mind."** — Maps to the influence consulting requires.
5. **"Describe a time you worked with a difficult teammate."** — Probes collaboration under friction.
6. **"Tell me about an accomplishment you're proud of."** — Checks drive and impact.

## What BCG looks for

BCG publicly emphasizes values like integrity, respect for the individual, diversity, and a commitment to clients and impact, and its recruiting materials stress problem-solving, leadership, and the ability to work in teams. In interviews, that translates into structured thinking, comfort with quantitative analysis, strong communication, genuine curiosity, and a collaborative, coachable style — interviewers often nudge you mid-case and want to see how you use the input.

Practically, they're assessing whether you can break an ambiguous problem into a clear structure, stay accurate under pressure, and communicate a recommendation a client would trust.

## How to prepare

- **Do a lot of cases out loud.** Case skill is built by repetition. Practice profitability, market entry, sizing, and pricing cases until structuring feels natural, and drill mental math separately.
- **Build frameworks, don't memorize them.** Interviewers can tell when you're forcing a template. Learn to build a tailored structure for the specific problem.
- **Prepare your personal-experience stories.** Draft several STAR-structured stories on leadership, challenge, and persuasion — our [STAR method examples](/alex/blog/star-method-examples/) show exactly how, and our [behavioral interview questions](/alex/blog/behavioral-interview-questions/) guide covers common prompts.
- **Practice the online assessment format** if your office uses one — timed, interactive, data-interpretation heavy.
- **Simulate full rounds.** Do back-to-back case-plus-fit interviews to build stamina. Our guide on [how to prepare for an interview](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) lays out a compressed plan.

### A worked STAR answer

**Question: "Tell me about a time you persuaded someone to change their approach."**

- **Situation:** A teammate wanted to solve a data problem manually because they distrusted an automated approach.
- **Task:** The manual path would have blown our timeline, but I needed buy-in, not a fight.
- **Action:** Instead of arguing, I proposed a small side-by-side test: they'd do a sample manually while I ran the same sample through the automated method, and we'd compare. When the results matched and the automated path was far faster, I let the evidence make the case.
- **Result:** They adopted the faster method willingly, we hit the deadline, and the trust from handling it collaboratively made the rest of the project smoother. I learned that persuasion often means designing a way for someone to convince themselves.

## FAQ

**How many rounds are there at BCG?**
Candidates commonly report two first-round interviews and two to three final-round interviews, most combining a case with a personal-experience portion. It varies by office.

**Are BCG cases interviewer-led or candidate-led?**
BCG cases are commonly reported as interviewer-led — you're guided through the problem in steps — versus the more candidate-led style at some other firms. Practice engaging with the interviewer's prompts.

**Does BCG have an online test?**
Some offices use an online problem-solving or chatbot-style assessment. Treat it as possible for your market and practice the timed, interactive format.

**How important is mental math?**
Very. You'll do quick calculations live during cases. Practicing arithmetic and estimation until it's fast and accurate is essential.
