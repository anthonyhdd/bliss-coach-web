Yes — cashews are a nutritious, satisfying nut with a creamy texture that makes them unusually versatile in cooking. Like all nuts they're calorie-dense, so portioning is the whole game, but as a snack and ingredient they earn their place easily.

## What's in it

A serving is about a small handful, roughly 28–30 g (around 16–18 cashews). Per serving, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 155–160
- **Fat:** roughly 12–13 g, mostly the heart-friendly monounsaturated kind
- **Protein:** around 5 g
- **Carbohydrates:** about 8–9 g, a touch higher than most nuts, with some fiber
- **Micronutrients:** copper (very high), magnesium, manganese, phosphorus, zinc, and iron

Cashews stand out for their minerals — copper and magnesium in particular — and for being slightly softer and more carb-containing than nuts like almonds or walnuts, which is part of why they blend so well into sauces.

## Real benefits

**Healthy fats in a satisfying package.** The fat in cashews is mostly monounsaturated, the type highlighted in our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/), and it comes with protein and fiber that make a small handful genuinely filling. Regular nut eating is consistently linked with favorable heart outcomes.

**A real mineral contribution.** A single serving delivers a large share of a day's copper and a meaningful amount of magnesium — nutrients involved in energy metabolism, nerve function, and bone health that many people run a bit short on.

**Some plant protein.** At around 5 g per handful, cashews add protein to snacks and plant-based meals; blended, they even mimic creamy dairy, which is why they appear so often in dairy-free cooking. They pair naturally with other [plant proteins](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/) in a balanced day.

**Filling enough to steady snacking.** The fat-protein-fiber trio slows digestion, so cashews satisfy in a way that keeps you from grazing — provided you serve a portion rather than eating from the bag.

## Sensible portions

**One small handful (about 28–30 g)** is the sensible serving, and it's easy to eat far more without noticing, so the single best habit is to portion cashews into a bowl or bag rather than eating straight from a large container. Choose dry-roasted or raw unsalted where you can — heavily salted cashews add up on sodium, and honey-roasted or "cashew clusters" add sugar and turn a healthy snack into candy. Within a sensible portion, though, cashews are a smart, nutrient-dense choice for daily eating.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **A measured handful** — the simplest snack; portion it out and you're set.
2. **Trail mix** — with a few dried fruits and other nuts for a balanced, portable snack.
3. **Stir-fry finish** — cashew chicken or vegetable stir-fry gets crunch and richness from a scatter of cashews.
4. **Blended cream sauce** — soaked cashews blend into a silky dairy-free sauce for pasta or curry.
5. **Yogurt or oatmeal topping** — chopped over [Greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) or oats for crunch and staying power.
6. **Cashew butter** — on whole-grain toast or apple slices as an alternative to peanut butter.
7. **Salad crunch** — toasted cashews add texture to grain and green salads.
8. **Homemade energy bites** — with oats and dates for a no-bake snack.

Because cashews are calorie-dense, the smartest move is nearly always to pre-portion — a bowl beats the bag every time.

## Who should be mindful

- **Tree nut allergy:** cashews are tree nuts and among the more common triggers of serious reactions; a tree-nut allergy means strict avoidance.
- **People on sodium restriction:** salted and flavored cashews can carry a lot of salt; choose raw or dry-roasted unsalted.
- **People watching calories closely:** cashews are nutritious but energy-dense — the issue is portion size, not the nut; pre-portion to keep it sensible.
- **Anyone avoiding added sugar:** honey-roasted and "cluster" style cashews are closer to candy; plain versions are the everyday choice.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Are cashews good for weight loss?**
No food does that alone, but a portioned handful of cashews is filling thanks to its fat, protein, and fiber, which can steady snacking. The key is a real serving rather than eating from the bag.

**Are cashews healthier than other nuts?**
They're a strong choice, especially for copper and magnesium, though slightly higher in carbs and lower in fiber than almonds or walnuts. The best approach is variety — rotate your nuts rather than crowning one.

**How many cashews should I eat a day?**
About one small handful (28–30 g) is a sensible daily serving. There's no strict limit for a healthy person, but their calorie density rewards portioning.

**Are raw or roasted cashews better?**
Both are fine. Dry-roasted brings out flavor without added oil; raw is minimally processed. The main thing to check is added salt and sugar, not raw versus roasted.

**Do cashews have protein?**
Yes — around 5 g per handful. They add useful protein to snacks and plant-based meals, though they're best thought of as a healthy-fat food that also contributes protein.
