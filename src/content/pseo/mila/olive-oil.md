Yes — olive oil is one of the best-supported foods in all of nutrition science. It's the signature fat of the Mediterranean diet, the eating pattern with arguably the deepest research record behind it. The honest caveat is the same one that applies to every oil: it's pure fat, which makes it the most calorie-dense thing in your kitchen. That's a reason to pour it with a little intention, not to be afraid of it.

## What's in it

A typical serving is **1 tablespoon (about 14 g)**. That tablespoon contains roughly:

- **Calories:** around 120
- **Fat:** about 14 g — that's the whole tablespoon; olive oil is 100% fat
- **Fat breakdown:** roughly three-quarters monounsaturated (mainly oleic acid), with a modest share of polyunsaturated fat and a small amount of saturated fat
- **Carbohydrates and protein:** zero
- **Micronutrients:** vitamin E and a little vitamin K
- **Polyphenols:** plant compounds with antioxidant activity — this is where extra virgin stands out, since gentler processing preserves more of them

The "extra virgin vs regular" question matters less than the marketing implies but more than zero. Extra virgin olive oil (EVOO) is mechanically pressed without heat or solvents, which keeps the polyphenols and the peppery flavor. Refined "light" or "pure" olive oil has the same fat profile and the same calories — "light" refers to taste, not calories — but fewer of those minor compounds. If the price difference doesn't sting, extra virgin is the nicer choice, especially for anything you'll taste directly.

## Real benefits

**The best-studied fat swap in nutrition.** Replacing saturated fats (butter, cream) with unsaturated ones is one of the most consistent recommendations across dietary guidelines, and olive oil is the classic way to do it. Large trials of Mediterranean-style diets rich in olive oil have shown meaningful reductions in cardiovascular events — this is about as solid as nutrition evidence gets.

**Heart-friendly fat profile.** Oleic acid, the dominant fat in olive oil, is the same monounsaturated fat that makes [avocado](/mila/foods/avocado/) a nutrition darling. Diets built around it are consistently associated with better cholesterol profiles than diets built around saturated fat.

**Polyphenols with real activity.** Extra virgin olive oil carries compounds like oleocanthal — the one responsible for that peppery throat-tickle in a good EVOO — which have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties in lab and human studies. The effects are modest and come from regular use, not from any single pour.

**It makes vegetables taste good — and work better.** Vitamins A, D, E, and K plus carotenoids are fat-soluble, so a drizzle of olive oil on a salad or roasted vegetables measurably increases how much of those nutrients you absorb. It's also the single biggest reason people actually finish their vegetables.

**Yes, you can cook with it.** A persistent myth says olive oil "turns toxic" when heated. In reality, olive oil is quite stable at normal home-cooking temperatures — sautéing, roasting, pan-frying are all fine. Its monounsaturated fats and antioxidants make it more heat-stable than many polyunsaturated seed oils. Save the expensive peppery EVOO for finishing, use a mid-range one for the pan.

## Sensible portions

**One to three tablespoons a day** is the range most Mediterranean-style patterns land in, spread across cooking, dressing, and finishing. There's no need to measure every pour forever — but it's worth knowing that a "generous glug" from the bottle can easily be 2–3 tablespoons, or 250–350 calories, in about two seconds.

The practical move is the same one that works for every calorie-dense food: use olive oil **instead of** something (butter on bread, creamy dressing on salad, mayo in a marinade) rather than **on top of** everything. If you're actively managing your weight, measuring your salad dressing for a week is a genuinely useful calibration exercise — more on that in our [portion control tips](/mila/blog/portion-control-tips/).

## Easy ways to use it

1. **The default cooking fat** — sauté vegetables, sear chicken, roast anything at normal oven temperatures.
2. **Simplest salad dressing on earth** — 2–3 parts olive oil, 1 part acid (lemon or vinegar), salt, shake. Better than most bottles.
3. **Bread and olive oil** — with a little balsamic and flaky salt, instead of butter.
4. **Drizzled over finished dishes** — soups, hummus, grilled fish, pasta; a finishing drizzle of good EVOO adds flavor and the peppery polyphenol kick.
5. **Roasted vegetables** — toss broccoli, carrots, or peppers with a tablespoon and roast hot; this is the gateway to actually liking vegetables.
6. **In place of butter in cooking** — eggs, garlic for pasta sauce, sautéed greens.
7. **Marinades** — olive oil, garlic, lemon, herbs; works on chicken, fish, tofu, and vegetables alike.
8. **Yes, even some baking** — olive oil cakes are a legitimate classic.

Storage tip: heat, light, and air are olive oil's enemies. A dark bottle in a cupboard away from the stove keeps it fresh; a clear bottle on a sunny windowsill turns it stale months sooner.

## Who should be mindful

- **Anyone managing energy intake closely:** olive oil is the most calorie-dense item in the kitchen — about 120 calories per tablespoon with no fiber or protein to fill you up. It absolutely belongs in a weight-management diet; it just rewards being poured deliberately rather than freely.
- **Olive allergy:** rare, but people with olive fruit or olive pollen allergies should be attentive.
- **Anyone told to follow a specific fat-modified diet** for a medical condition should treat their care team's guidance as the final word.
- Olive oil is a fat *source*, not a fat *free pass* — its benefits come from replacing less-helpful fats, not from adding it to an already rich diet. For where it fits among other good fats, see our [healthy fats list](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/).

## FAQ

**Is olive oil fattening?**
No single food is fattening in isolation — total intake is what matters. Olive oil is calorie-dense, so quantities count, but the large trials showing heart benefits used diets with generous daily olive oil without weight gain, because it replaced other fats.

**Is extra virgin olive oil really worth the extra money?**
For flavor and polyphenols, yes — extra virgin retains more of both. But the core fat profile is the same across grades, so an affordable olive oil you use daily beats an expensive one you ration.

**Can you cook with extra virgin olive oil, or does heat ruin it?**
You can cook with it. Olive oil is stable at normal home-cooking temperatures, and the "it becomes toxic" claim isn't supported. High heat does gradually degrade the delicate flavors and some polyphenols, which is why many people cook with a standard olive oil and finish with a good EVOO.

**How much olive oil per day is healthy?**
Most Mediterranean-style research lands around 1–4 tablespoons daily, counting all uses. For most people, 1–3 tablespoons fits comfortably — the key is that it replaces butter, cream, or heavy dressings rather than stacking on top of them.

**Olive oil vs. butter — is olive oil really better?**
For everyday use, the evidence favors olive oil: swapping saturated fat (butter) for unsaturated fat (olive oil) is one of the most consistently supported moves in nutrition. Butter isn't poison — but as the default fat, olive oil is the stronger choice.
