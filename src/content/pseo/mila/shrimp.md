Yes — shrimp is a lean, high-protein food that cooks in minutes and fits almost any eating pattern. The one thing that scared people off for years — its cholesterol content — has been reframed by modern nutrition science, and shrimp comes out looking pretty good.

## What's in it

A serving is about 85–100 g cooked (roughly a small handful of shrimp). Per 100 g cooked, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 85–100 — very low for the protein it carries
- **Protein:** roughly 20–24 g, complete and highly digestible
- **Fat:** low, around 1 g, with a small amount of omega-3s
- **Carbohydrates:** essentially zero
- **Cholesterol:** relatively high (shrimp is naturally cholesterol-rich)
- **Micronutrients:** selenium (very high), vitamin B12, iodine, phosphorus, and some zinc and choline
- **Sodium:** naturally moderate, but often much higher in pre-cooked, brined, or breaded products

The standout numbers are the protein-to-calorie ratio and the selenium — shrimp is one of the leanest animal proteins you can eat.

## Real benefits

**A lot of protein for very few calories.** Gram for gram, shrimp is one of the most protein-dense, lowest-calorie foods available, which is why it sits comfortably in our [high-protein foods guide](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/). That combination is filling and useful for muscle maintenance without much energy cost.

**The cholesterol worry has eased.** Yes, shrimp is high in dietary cholesterol, but for most people dietary cholesterol has a modest effect on blood cholesterol — saturated fat and overall diet pattern matter far more. Shrimp is very low in saturated fat, so current guidance treats it as a reasonable choice for most people, including many managing heart health. The honest caveat: a subset of people are "hyper-responders," so individual advice still applies.

**Selenium and B12 in abundance.** A single serving covers a day's selenium (important for thyroid and antioxidant defense) and a large share of B12. These are nutrients that quietly matter and that shrimp delivers generously.

**Fast, which makes it get eaten.** Shrimp goes from raw to done in two to three minutes. That speed is a real nutrition advantage: an easy lean protein is one you'll actually cook on a busy night.

## Sensible portions

**One serving of about 85–120 g** is a normal protein portion, and eating shrimp a few times a week is completely reasonable. There's no special cap for healthy people — the practical variables are preparation and sourcing, not the shrimp itself. Watch for three things: heavy breading and deep-frying (turns a lean food into a heavy one), rich butter or cream sauces, and the sodium in pre-brined or pre-seasoned shrimp. Plain shrimp, cooked simply, is about as clean a protein as exists.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Garlic sauté** — 2–3 minutes in olive oil with garlic and a squeeze of lemon; done before your pasta water boils.
2. **Sheet-pan shrimp** — toss with vegetables and spices, roast at high heat for a fast one-tray dinner.
3. **Shrimp stir-fry** — with peppers, snap peas, and a light soy-ginger sauce over rice.
4. **Grilled skewers** — marinate briefly, grill 2 minutes a side; great for summer.
5. **Shrimp tacos** — spiced shrimp with cabbage slaw and a yogurt-lime drizzle.
6. **Cold shrimp cocktail** — a naturally lean, high-protein appetizer or snack.
7. **Tossed into pasta or grain bowls** — with [quinoa](/mila/foods/quinoa/), cherry tomatoes, and herbs.
8. **In soups** — added at the end of a broth or coconut-based soup, cooked just until pink.

Frozen shrimp is nutritionally identical to fresh and usually cheaper — keep a bag in the freezer and a fast dinner is always available.

## Who should be mindful

- **Shellfish allergy:** shellfish allergy is one of the most common and can be severe; it means strict avoidance of shrimp and related crustaceans.
- **Cholesterol hyper-responders / specific heart conditions:** most people are fine, but a minority see a larger blood-cholesterol response — if you've been told to watch dietary cholesterol, ask your clinician about your case.
- **People on sodium restriction:** raw shrimp is moderate, but pre-cooked, brined, and breaded products can be very salty; check labels and choose plain.
- **Gout:** shellfish contributes purines and may need moderating for those managing gout.
- **Pregnancy:** cooked shrimp is a low-mercury, encouraged choice; raw preparations should be avoided — confirm specifics with your provider.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Is shrimp bad for cholesterol?**
For most people, no. Shrimp is high in dietary cholesterol but very low in saturated fat, and dietary cholesterol has a smaller effect on blood cholesterol than once believed. A minority are more sensitive, so individual guidance applies.

**Is shrimp a good source of protein?**
Yes — around 20–24 g per 100 g cooked, for very few calories. It's one of the leanest, most protein-dense animal foods you can eat.

**Is frozen shrimp as healthy as fresh?**
Yes. Most "fresh" shrimp was previously frozen anyway. Frozen is nutritionally identical, convenient, and usually cheaper — just avoid pre-breaded versions if you want it lean.

**How much shrimp can I eat?**
A normal serving is 85–120 g, a few times a week is fine for most healthy people. Preparation (frying, rich sauces, added salt) matters more than the shrimp itself.

**Is shrimp safe during pregnancy?**
Cooked shrimp is low in mercury and generally encouraged in pregnancy. Avoid raw shrimp, and confirm the specifics with your healthcare provider.
