Yes — salmon is about as close to a consensus pick as nutrition science gets: high-quality protein plus omega-3 fats that are hard to obtain elsewhere in the diet. Most dietary guidelines around the world specifically recommend eating fatty fish like salmon regularly, which is not something they say about many individual foods.

## What's in it

A typical cooked portion is about 100–140 g (a standard fillet). Per 100 g cooked, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 180–210
- **Protein:** roughly 22–25 g, complete and highly digestible
- **Fat:** about 8–13 g depending on the cut and whether it's wild or farmed, mostly unsaturated
- **Omega-3s (EPA + DHA):** roughly 1–2 g — among the richest common food sources of these long-chain omega-3s
- **Carbohydrates:** 0 g
- **Micronutrients:** vitamin D (one of the few substantial natural food sources), vitamin B12 (well above daily needs per serving), selenium, B6, niacin, and potassium; canned salmon with bones adds meaningful calcium

Farmed salmon tends to be a bit fattier (including omega-3s) and milder; wild salmon is typically leaner, firmer, and pricier. Both are nutritious — the wild-vs-farmed debate matters far less than the eat-fish-vs-don't decision.

## Real benefits

**Omega-3s you can't easily get elsewhere.** EPA and DHA support heart and brain health, and regular fatty-fish intake — commonly one to two servings a week — is consistently associated with lower cardiovascular risk in large population studies. Plant omega-3s (flax, walnuts) convert to EPA/DHA poorly, so fish occupies a genuinely distinct slot in the diet. Honest framing: the strongest evidence is for eating fish regularly, not for dramatic effects from any single serving.

**Excellent protein with company.** Salmon delivers chicken-breast-level protein, but wrapped in beneficial fats rather than lean austerity — it earns its place near the top of our [high-protein foods guide](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/). That combination is filling and works for muscle maintenance just as well.

**Vitamin D and B12, where diets run thin.** Vitamin D shortfalls are common, especially at higher latitudes and in winter; salmon is one of the rare foods that contributes meaningfully. One serving also comfortably covers a day's B12.

**Brain and eye development nutrients.** DHA is a structural fat in the brain and retina, which is why low-mercury fish like salmon is specifically encouraged (in appropriate amounts) during pregnancy rather than discouraged.

## Sensible portions

**One fillet of about 100–150 g, one to two times per week**, matches what most dietary guidelines suggest for fatty fish. More is generally fine for healthy adults — some populations eat fish near-daily — but you don't need more than a couple of servings weekly to capture most of the observed benefit. On mercury: salmon is a *low*-mercury fish (it's short-lived and low on the food chain), so the caution that applies to swordfish or bigeye tuna largely doesn't apply here. Canned salmon counts fully and costs a fraction of fresh.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Roasted fillet** — 200°C / 400°F for 12–14 minutes with olive oil, salt, and lemon; nearly impossible to ruin.
2. **Sheet-pan dinner** — salmon plus broccoli and potatoes on one tray; the weeknight workhorse.
3. **Canned salmon cakes** — canned salmon, egg, breadcrumbs, herbs; pan-fry. Budget-friendly omega-3s.
4. **Salmon salad instead of tuna salad** — canned salmon with Greek yogurt, dill, and lemon in a sandwich or on crackers — a sturdy option among [healthy snacks for work](/mila/blog/healthy-snacks-for-work/) lunches.
5. **Honey-soy glazed** — a 2-minute glaze under the broiler makes it taste like takeout.
6. **Flaked over grain bowls** — rice or quinoa, avocado, cucumber, leftover salmon, and a soy-sesame drizzle.
7. **Smoked salmon breakfast** — on whole-grain toast with yogurt-based spread or a little cream cheese and capers (note: smoked is saltier, and pregnant readers should prefer cooked).
8. **Poached and gentle** — simmered in barely-bubbling water or broth for a soft texture; good with a mustard-dill sauce.

Frozen fillets are nutritionally equivalent to fresh and usually cheaper — keep a couple in the freezer and dinner is always 15 minutes away.

## Who should be mindful

- **Fish allergy:** finned-fish allergy is common in both children and adults and typically means avoiding salmon entirely — allergist guidance applies.
- **Pregnancy:** salmon is actually *encouraged* (low mercury, high DHA) at around 2–3 servings per week of cooked fish; raw preparations (sushi, gravlax) and cold-smoked salmon are the versions to discuss carefully with your provider.
- **People on sodium restriction:** smoked and cured salmon carry substantial salt; fresh or canned-in-water versions don't.
- **People on blood thinners or with bleeding disorders:** very high fish-oil intakes can matter; normal food amounts are rarely an issue, but it's worth mentioning to your doctor.
- **Gout:** fish contributes purines; individuals managing gout often get personalized fish guidance.
- For any medical condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits your actual situation.

## FAQ

**Is farmed salmon bad for you?**
No. Farmed salmon is nutritious, typically higher in total omega-3s, and contaminant levels in major farmed sources have fallen substantially over the years and sit within regulatory limits. Buy what your budget and taste prefer — eating salmon at all is the meaningful choice.

**Is canned salmon as healthy as fresh?**
Essentially yes — same protein and omega-3s, plus extra calcium if you eat the soft bones. It's one of the best value-for-nutrition items in the store.

**How often should I eat salmon?**
One to two servings a week aligns with most guidelines for fatty fish. More is fine for most healthy people.

**Does salmon help with weight loss?**
No single food drives weight loss, but salmon's protein-plus-fat combination is filling, which makes balanced eating easier — that's the realistic mechanism.
