Yes — pistachios are a nutritious, satisfying nut with a couple of genuinely useful quirks: they're among the higher-protein and higher-fiber nuts, and eating them in the shell tends to slow you down and help with portioning. They're a treat that also happens to be good for you, which is always a welcome combination — no hype required.

## What's in it

A serving is about a small handful, roughly 28–30 g (a generous portion of shelled pistachios, around 45–50 kernels). Per serving, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 155–160
- **Fat:** roughly 12–13 g, mostly the heart-friendly mono- and polyunsaturated kind
- **Protein:** around 6 g — high for a nut
- **Carbohydrates:** about 8 g, with a solid 3 g of fiber
- **Micronutrients:** vitamin B6, thiamine, potassium, copper, manganese, and some lutein

Pistachios stand out on two fronts worth stating honestly: protein (around 6 g, higher than most nuts) and fiber (around 3 g per serving, also toward the top for nuts). Both are real, useful contributions rather than dramatic ones — pistachios are still a healthy-fat food first, with protein and fiber as genuine bonuses.

## Real benefits

**Protein and fiber, both high for a nut.** This pairing makes a portion of pistachios more filling than you'd expect, and it's part of why they satisfy so well. They fit naturally among the [high-protein plant foods](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/) worth rotating in.

**Heart-friendly fats.** The fat is mostly unsaturated, the kind highlighted in our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/). Regular nut eating is consistently associated with favorable heart markers in large studies — a "regular habit" effect, not a single-serving one.

**Potassium, B6, and antioxidants.** Pistachios are a decent source of potassium and vitamin B6, and their green-purple color comes from lutein and other plant pigments also found in leafy greens. Nice extras rather than headline reasons.

**The shell as a built-in brake.** Eating pistachios in the shell physically slows your pace and leaves visible evidence of how many you've eaten — a small, genuinely helpful nudge toward a sensible portion.

## Sensible portions

**One small handful (about 28–30 g)** is the sensible serving, and pistachios reward portioning like all nuts because they're calorie-dense. The shell-on trick helps here — buying them in the shell naturally slows you down and shows you how much you've had. Choose unsalted or lightly salted where you can, since heavily salted pistachios add up on sodium. Within a sensible portion, they're a smart, nutrient-dense everyday choice, and swapping them in for a processed snack is where the visible win lives.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **In the shell, as a snack** — the classic; the shell slows you down and portions itself.
2. **Chopped over yogurt** — over [Greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) with fruit for crunch and staying power.
3. **On oatmeal** — scattered over [oatmeal](/mila/foods/oatmeal/) for texture and a little protein.
4. **Salad crunch** — toasted and chopped over grain and green salads.
5. **Pistachio crust** — crushed as a coating for salmon or chicken before baking.
6. **Trail mix** — with dried fruit and other nuts for a portable, balanced snack.
7. **Pesto twist** — blended in place of pine nuts for a greener, cheaper pesto.
8. **Baking and desserts** — chopped into energy bites, granola, or a sprinkle over dark chocolate.

## Who should be mindful

- **Tree nut allergy:** pistachios are tree nuts and can trigger serious reactions; a tree-nut allergy means strict avoidance.
- **People on sodium restriction:** salted pistachios can carry a lot of salt; choose unsalted or lightly salted.
- **People watching calories closely:** pistachios are nutritious but energy-dense — the issue is portion size, not the nut; the shell-on version helps you pace naturally.
- **People with certain digestive sensitivities:** in large amounts pistachios contain FODMAPs that can bother some sensitive stomachs; a normal handful is usually fine — test your own tolerance.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Are pistachios good for weight management?**
No food does that alone, but pistachios are filling thanks to their protein, fiber, and fat, and eating them in the shell naturally slows your pace and portions the snack. A real serving is the key rather than eating from a big bag.

**Do pistachios have a lot of protein?**
For a nut, yes — around 6 g per handful, higher than most. They add useful protein to snacks and plant-based meals, though they're still best thought of as a healthy-fat food that also contributes protein and fiber.

**Are salted pistachios unhealthy?**
Not unhealthy, but salt adds up if they're a frequent snack. Unsalted or lightly salted versions give you the same nutrition with less sodium.

**Are pistachios high in fiber?**
They're among the higher-fiber nuts, at around 3 g per serving. That's a genuine contribution toward a daily target most people miss, and part of why a portion feels satisfying.

**How many pistachios should I eat a day?**
About one small handful (28–30 g) is a sensible daily serving. There's no strict limit for a healthy person, but their calorie density rewards portioning — and the shell helps.
