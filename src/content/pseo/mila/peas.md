Yes — green peas are a healthy food, and one of the most underrated things in the freezer aisle. They tend to get treated as a boring plate-filler, but nutritionally they're a small overachiever: for a vegetable, they carry an unusually good amount of protein and fiber, along with a spread of vitamins and minerals. Peas sit at an interesting crossroads — sweet enough to feel like a vegetable, substantial enough to behave a little like a legume — and that dual nature is exactly what makes them useful.

## What's in it

A typical serving is about one cup (roughly 145 g). Per 100 g of cooked green peas, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 80–85 — a full cup lands near 115–120
- **Carbohydrates:** about 14 g, of which roughly 5 g is fiber and some is natural sugar
- **Protein:** around 5 g — genuinely high for a vegetable
- **Fat:** essentially none
- **Vitamin C:** a solid contribution
- **Vitamin K, thiamin, and folate:** strong amounts across the board
- **Iron and manganese:** useful plant-source minerals
- **Plant compounds:** including the pigments that keep them bright green

The honest framing here is about category, again. Peas are a legume (like [lentils](/mila/foods/lentils/) and beans), which is why they punch above the typical vegetable for protein and fiber — but as sweet green peas they're eaten young and treated as a vegetable, so they don't carry the same heavy, dense texture as dried pulses. That in-between status means they count more toward carbohydrates than a leafy green does, but they give you protein and fiber that most vegetables can't. It's a good trade.

## Real benefits

**Protein and fiber in one small package.** Around 5 g of each per 100 g is an excellent ratio for a plant food, and it's what makes peas so filling. That combination supports fullness and steadier energy, and it means a scoop of peas quietly upgrades the nutrition of whatever they're next to.

**Fiber that stacks easily.** Five grams of fiber per serving is a real contribution toward a target most people miss. Peas fold into grain dishes without fuss — stirred through [brown rice](/mila/foods/brown-rice/) or a pilaf, they add color, sweetness, and a nutrition boost at once.

**A broad vitamin and mineral spread.** Peas quietly deliver vitamin C, K, folate, thiamin, and iron — no single one dramatic, but together a genuinely useful package for a food this easy to keep on hand.

**Convenience that makes vegetables effortless.** Frozen peas cook from freezer to plate in a couple of minutes, keep for months, and cost very little. The easiest healthy habit is the one with no friction, and peas remove almost all of it.

## Sensible portions

**Half a cup to a cup and a half (about 70–220 g)** is a very reasonable everyday amount. Because peas carry more carbohydrate and protein than watery vegetables, they sit somewhere between a vegetable and a starchy side — so if you're managing carbs closely, count them a bit more like beans than like salad greens. For most people, though, there's no reason to ration them; they're a nutrient-dense, filling addition to almost any plate.

Frozen peas deserve a specific defense: they're picked at peak sweetness and frozen within hours, which means they're often *fresher* nutritionally than "fresh" peas that have spent days in transit. For everyday cooking they're not a compromise — they're arguably the smart default, always available and always good.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Straight from frozen into hot food** — stir a handful into pasta, curry, soup, or fried rice in the last minute; they thaw and cook instantly.
2. **Simple buttered peas** — warmed with a little butter, salt, and mint, a classic side that takes two minutes.
3. **Smashed on toast** — peas crushed with lemon, olive oil, and a little feta make a bright, protein-carrying topping.
4. **In a grain bowl** — peas over rice or quinoa with a protein and a dressing, colorful and filling.
5. **Blended into a green soup** — peas blitzed with stock and a little cream make a vivid, fast soup.
6. **Folded into fried rice** — the classic; they add sweetness and color to a whole-grain base.
7. **In a pasta** — with lemon, parmesan, and greens for a light spring dish.
8. **As a quick side salad** — cooled peas with mint, radish, and a lemony dressing.

## Who should be mindful

- **People managing blood sugar or carbohydrates closely:** peas carry more carbohydrate than leafy vegetables, so count them toward totals like other starchy or legume foods. They fit well — the protein and fiber help — but placement matters; your care team's plan wins.
- **People with IBS or FODMAP sensitivity:** peas contain fermentable carbohydrates that can cause gas or bloating in larger amounts for sensitive guts. Smaller portions are usually better tolerated; test your own.
- **Anyone treating peas as "just filler":** the only real risk here is underrating them — they're more nutritious than their reputation, so lean in rather than treating them as an afterthought.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

**Are peas a vegetable or a starch?**
They're a legume eaten young as a vegetable, which puts them in between. They carry more protein, fiber, and carbohydrate than watery vegetables — so nutritionally they behave a bit like a light starchy side with a protein bonus.

**Are frozen peas as healthy as fresh?**
Yes — often more so. Frozen peas are picked at peak sweetness and frozen within hours, while "fresh" peas may have traveled for days. For everyday cooking, frozen is a genuinely smart default.

**Are peas a good source of protein?**
For a vegetable, yes — around 5 g per 100 g, which is high. They won't replace a main protein source, but they meaningfully boost the protein and fiber of any plate they land on.

**Do peas cause bloating?**
They can in larger amounts for people sensitive to fermentable carbohydrates, since peas contain some. Most people tolerate normal portions fine; if you're sensitive, smaller servings usually help.

**How many peas should I eat?**
Half a cup to a cup and a half is a great everyday amount. If you watch carbohydrates, count them like a starchy or legume portion; otherwise, add them freely to boost almost any meal.
