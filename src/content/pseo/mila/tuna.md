Yes — tuna is one of the most affordable, convenient sources of high-quality protein you can buy, and it fits comfortably into almost any way of eating. The one thing worth knowing is mercury: tuna is perfectly fine to eat regularly, but it's a "a few times a week, not every single day" food, especially the albacore (white) variety.

## What's in it

Numbers vary by species, brand, and whether it's packed in water or oil, but a standard drained can of light tuna in water (about 100–120 g) gives you roughly:

- **Calories:** about 100–140
- **Protein:** around 22–28 g — a genuinely large amount for the price
- **Fat:** about 1–3 g for light tuna in water; oil-packed versions land higher depending on how well you drain them
- **Carbs:** essentially zero
- **Omega-3s (EPA and DHA):** present in all tuna, with albacore and fresh tuna steaks generally carrying more than light skipjack
- **Micronutrients:** selenium (a can often covers most of a day's needs), vitamin B12, vitamin D in modest amounts, and some potassium

Fresh tuna steak is similar on protein, with a bit more fat and omega-3 depending on the cut. None of these numbers need to be exact for tuna to do its job — the headline is simply "lots of protein, very few calories."

## Real benefits

**It makes hitting your protein target easy.** Protein is the nutrient most people quietly under-eat, and it's the one that keeps you full and protects muscle. One can of tuna delivers as much protein as three to four eggs with almost no prep. If you're working on eating more protein overall, our guide to [high-protein foods](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/) puts tuna in context with the rest of the list.

**It's one of the most filling foods per calorie.** Lean protein scores very high on satiety. A tuna-based lunch tends to hold you until dinner in a way a sandwich alone often doesn't.

**Omega-3 fats.** EPA and DHA are the fats consistently associated with heart and brain health, and fish is their main food source. Light tuna has less than salmon or sardines, but eaten regularly it still contributes meaningfully.

**It's cheap, shelf-stable, and instant.** No cooking, no spoilage anxiety, no excuses on busy days. That practical reliability matters more for long-term eating habits than any single nutrient.

## Sensible portions

A normal serving is one can (or a palm-sized portion of fresh tuna, about 100–150 g), and 2–3 servings of light tuna per week sits comfortably within official guidance from bodies like the FDA.

Where mercury matters:

- **Light/skipjack tuna** is the lower-mercury choice — fine 2–3 times a week.
- **Albacore (white) and yellowfin** carry more mercury — better kept to about once a week.
- **Bigeye tuna** (common in sushi) is among the higher-mercury tunas — an occasional food rather than a staple.

If you eat tuna most days, nothing dramatic happens overnight — mercury is about long-term accumulation. Simply rotating in other proteins (eggs, chicken, beans, salmon, sardines) solves it without any tracking.

## Easy ways to eat it

- **Five-minute tuna bowl:** tuna, rice or leftover grains, cucumber, a spoon of mayo or yogurt, soy sauce or lemon.
- **Upgraded tuna salad:** swap half the mayo for Greek yogurt, add celery, red onion, and mustard — more protein, same comfort.
- **Tuna melt or wrap** when you want something warm and fast.
- **Pasta with tuna, olive oil, garlic, and lemon** — a classic Mediterranean weeknight dinner.
- **Batch it:** tuna keeps beautifully in [meal-prepped lunches](/mila/blog/meal-prep-for-beginners/) — just store it separately and mix on the day for better texture.

Oil-packed tuna tastes richer and works better in pasta; water-packed is leaner and better for salads. Neither is "wrong" — pick by dish.

## Who should be mindful

- **Pregnant and breastfeeding women** should stick to lower-mercury light tuna and follow local guidance on weekly amounts (typically 2–3 servings of low-mercury fish per week, avoiding high-mercury species).
- **Young children** can absolutely eat tuna, but in smaller, less frequent portions for the same mercury reason.
- **People with fish allergies** should avoid it entirely.
- **Anyone watching sodium** may prefer no-salt-added cans — regular canned tuna carries a moderate amount.

If you're pregnant, managing a medical condition, or feeding tuna to small children regularly, a quick conversation with your doctor or a registered dietitian is worth more than any general article — they can tailor amounts to your situation.

## FAQ

**Is canned tuna as healthy as fresh?**
Nutritionally it's very close — protein is essentially identical, and canned light tuna is often lower in mercury than the species sold as steaks. Canned is not a downgrade.

**Is tuna in oil bad?**
No. It has more calories, but the drained difference is modest, and it tastes better in many dishes. Choose based on the meal, not on fear.

**Can I eat tuna every day?**
Occasionally a daily-tuna week happens and that's fine, but as a permanent habit it's smarter to rotate proteins — mainly for mercury with albacore, and simply for dietary variety with light tuna.

**Is tuna good for weight loss?**
It's one of the most filling, lowest-calorie protein sources available, so it makes eating fewer calories easier — no special "tuna diet" required (please skip those).
