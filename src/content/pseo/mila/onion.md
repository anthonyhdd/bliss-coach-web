Yes — onions are a healthy food, and one of the most quietly useful things in any kitchen. Nobody eats a bowl of onions the way they eat a bowl of berries, so they rarely make "superfood" lists, but that undersells them: as the flavor base of countless meals, they add real nutrition — fiber, vitamin C, quercetin, and prebiotic compounds — to food you were going to cook anyway. Their biggest health contribution might actually be indirect: they make vegetables and whole foods taste good enough to eat regularly.

## What's in it

A typical serving is about half a medium onion (roughly 70 g) or what you'd sauté as the base of a dish. Per 100 g of raw onion, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 40 — most cooked portions contribute little
- **Carbohydrates:** about 9 g, of which roughly 1.5–2 g is fiber and some is natural sugar
- **Protein:** around 1 g
- **Fat:** essentially none
- **Vitamin C:** a modest but real contribution
- **B6, folate, and potassium:** small amounts across the board
- **Quercetin:** a flavonoid onions are especially rich in, concentrated in the outer layers
- **Prebiotic fibers:** inulin and fructans that feed gut bacteria

Two honest notes. First, that sweetness when onions caramelize is their natural sugar concentrating as water cooks off — it's real sugar, but the amount in a normal portion is trivial and comes packaged with fiber and plant compounds. Second, onions are one of the richest everyday sources of quercetin, but you're eating grams of onion, not a supplement dose, so think of the benefit as a modest, steady contribution rather than a targeted treatment.

## Real benefits

**Quercetin, one of the more studied flavonoids.** Onions are among the top dietary sources of quercetin, a plant compound linked in research to antioxidant activity and modest cardiovascular associations. As with most single compounds in whole food, the honest framing is "helpful as a regular habit," not "one onion changes your health" — but it's a genuine, low-effort contribution.

**Prebiotic fiber that feeds your gut.** Onions contain inulin and related fibers that act as food for beneficial gut bacteria. Alongside beans like [black beans](/mila/foods/black-beans/) and [lentils](/mila/foods/lentils/), onions help build the kind of fiber-diverse diet that gut health research consistently favors.

**A flavor base that makes healthy food edible.** This is the underrated one. Onions, cooked down, are how a pot of vegetables, beans, or grains becomes something you actually want to eat — without needing much salt or fat. A food that reliably gets more whole plants onto your plate is doing real nutritional work, even if it's indirect.

**Vitamin C and antioxidants along for the ride.** Not dramatic in one portion, but onions turn up in so many meals that their small contributions add up steadily across a week.

## Sensible portions

**Anywhere from a couple of tablespoons to half an onion per meal** is a completely normal, healthy amount, and there's no practical upper limit for most people beyond taste and digestion. Because onions are usually a background ingredient rather than the star, portion size mostly takes care of itself — the useful move isn't rationing them but simply cooking with them often.

Raw vs cooked is worth a word. Raw onion is sharper and retains more of its vitamin C and some pungent sulfur compounds; cooking mellows the flavor, brings out sweetness, and makes onions easier on many stomachs. Both are healthy — pick by the dish and by what your gut tolerates. Different colors are largely interchangeable nutritionally, with red onions carrying extra pigment antioxidants and sweet onions being milder.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Sautéed as a base** — soften in oil at the start of almost any savory dish; the foundational move.
2. **Caramelized low and slow** — cooked gently until deep brown and jammy, they turn sweet and luxurious; fold into eggs, sandwiches, or grains.
3. **Raw and thinly sliced** — a few rings on tacos, salads, or sandwiches for crunch and bite; soak in cold water first to tame the sharpness.
4. **Roasted in wedges** — tossed with oil and roasted until the edges char and sweeten, a side in their own right.
5. **Pickled quick** — thin red onion in vinegar and salt for 20 minutes turns bright pink and tangy; brightens everything.
6. **In soups and stocks** — where their savory depth underpins the whole pot.
7. **Charred whole or halved** — on a grill or under a broiler for a smoky edge.

## Who should be mindful

- **People with IBS or sensitivity to FODMAPs:** onions are a well-known trigger for gas and bloating in sensitive guts because of their fructans. Cooking, smaller amounts, or using just the green tops of scallions can help — but if onions consistently bother you, that's worth respecting and discussing with a professional.
- **People with acid reflux:** raw onion in particular is a common heartburn trigger; cooked may be gentler.
- **Anyone whose eyes and stomach disagree with raw onion:** soaking slices in cold water, or simply cooking them, mellows both the tear factor and the digestive edge.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

**Are raw onions healthier than cooked?**
Raw onions keep more vitamin C and pungent sulfur compounds, while cooking mellows flavor and is easier on many stomachs. Both are healthy — the "best" one is whichever gets you eating onions regularly in food you enjoy.

**Do onions really help your heart?**
Onions are rich in quercetin, a flavonoid with favorable cardiovascular associations in research. The honest version is that it's a modest, regular-habit benefit as part of an overall good diet, not a single-food fix.

**Why do onions upset some people's stomachs?**
Onions contain fructans, a type of fermentable fiber that can cause gas and bloating in people sensitive to FODMAPs. Cooking helps, smaller portions help, and green scallion tops are usually better tolerated.

**Are red, white, and yellow onions nutritionally different?**
Broadly similar. Red onions carry extra pigment antioxidants, sweet varieties are milder, and yellow onions are the versatile all-rounder. Choose by flavor and dish — the differences are small.

**Can onions count toward my vegetable intake?**
Yes, though because portions are usually small and cooked-down, they're best thought of as a bonus rather than a main vegetable. Their real value is making other vegetables taste good enough to eat more of.
