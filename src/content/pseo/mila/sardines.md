Yes — sardines are one of the most nutritious foods for the money you can put on a plate, and they earn the praise without much hype needed. Small, oily, and usually eaten bones-and-all, they pack omega-3s, protein, calcium, vitamin D, and B12 into a cheap, shelf-stable tin. The honest footnotes are minor: they're higher in sodium when tinned, and the strong flavor isn't for everyone — but nutritionally, few foods deliver this much per calorie.

## What's in it

Sardines are a small, oily fish, and being eaten whole (with soft, edible bones) is central to their profile. Per 100 g of canned sardines, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 200
- **Protein:** roughly 24–25 g
- **Fat:** about 11 g, much of it heart-friendly omega-3s (EPA and DHA)
- **Carbohydrates:** none
- **Calcium:** unusually high for a protein food — thanks to the soft, edible bones
- **Vitamin D:** one of the better food sources, which are genuinely rare
- **Vitamin B12:** an excellent source
- **Selenium and phosphorus:** solid amounts of both

The omega-3 content is the headline and it's well earned — sardines sit alongside [salmon](/mila/foods/salmon/) as one of the reliable oily-fish sources, at a fraction of the price and with a much longer shelf life.

## Real benefits

**Omega-3s with a strong research record.** EPA and DHA support heart and brain health, and oily fish is one of the few foods that delivers them in meaningful amounts. Regular oily-fish intake is consistently associated with favorable cardiovascular markers — a "good habit, real evidence" story.

**Calcium from an unexpected place.** Because sardines are eaten with their soft bones, they're a rare non-dairy source of well-absorbed calcium — genuinely useful for bone health and for anyone who doesn't do much dairy.

**Vitamin D, which is hard to get from food.** Very few foods contain meaningful vitamin D; oily fish like sardines is one of them, supporting bone health and immune function.

**Cheap, shelf-stable, and zero-prep.** A tin costs little, keeps for years, and is ready to eat — which makes sardines one of the most practical ways to get oily fish into a real week, not just an ideal one.

**Low on the mercury scale.** As a small, short-lived fish low on the food chain, sardines accumulate little mercury — a meaningful advantage over larger predatory fish, and one reason they're often recommended as a fish you can eat regularly without much worry.

**A sustainability point that happens to be a nutrition one.** Because sardines are small, fast-reproducing, and eaten whole, they're often among the more sustainable fish choices — which, practically, means you can lean on them frequently without the cost or the guilt that pricier fish can carry.

## Sensible portions

**One tin (roughly 90–120 g drained) is a normal serving**, delivering a big hit of omega-3s, protein, and calcium. Health authorities suggest a couple of servings of fish per week with at least one being oily — sardines are an easy, affordable way to check that box.

The main variable is what's in the tin. Sardines packed in water or olive oil are the cleanest options; those in heavy sauces add sodium and sometimes sugar. Draining and, if you like, giving them a quick rinse trims some of the salt. Even so, sardines are a food where the nutrition is so dense that eating them a few times a week is an easy win rather than something to ration. If the flavor is the barrier, sardines in olive oil tend to taste milder and richer than those in water, and pairing them with acid — lemon, vinegar, tomato — cuts through the fishiness that puts some people off.

## Easy ways to eat them

1. **On toast** — mashed onto wholegrain toast with lemon and pepper; a two-minute classic.
2. **Straight from the tin** — genuinely fine as a fast, high-protein snack.
3. **Over greens** — flaked onto a salad with tomatoes and [olive oil](/mila/foods/olive-oil/).
4. **Sardine pasta** — broken into garlicky pasta with chili and lemon; a Mediterranean staple.
5. **On crackers** — with a squeeze of lemon and fresh herbs.
6. **In a grain bowl** — over rice or quinoa with vegetables.
7. **Mashed with avocado** — spread on toast for extra creaminess and healthy fat.
8. **Grilled fresh** — if you find fresh sardines, they're superb straight off a hot grill.

## Who should be mindful

- **People watching sodium (e.g., high blood pressure):** canned sardines carry added salt — choose no-added-salt or water-packed versions and drain them, and the concern largely resolves.
- **People with gout or on urate-lowering advice:** sardines are relatively high in purines, which can be a consideration for gout management — worth discussing portion and frequency with your doctor.
- **People with fish allergies:** sardines are a finfish; those with a diagnosed fish allergy should avoid them.
- **Anyone put off by the flavor:** that's taste, not nutrition — trying them mashed with lemon, on toast, or in pasta often wins skeptics over.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

### Are canned sardines as healthy as fresh?
Yes — canning preserves the omega-3s, protein, and calcium very well, and the soft, edible bones (the calcium source) come with the canned version. For most people, tinned sardines are the practical and nutritious default.

### Are sardines high in mercury?
No — they're low. As small, short-lived fish near the bottom of the food chain, sardines accumulate very little mercury, which makes them one of the safer oily fish to eat regularly, including for most during pregnancy (confirm specifics with your care team).

### Should I eat the bones?
Yes, and that's the point — the soft bones in canned sardines are edible and are exactly where the impressive calcium comes from. They're soft enough to eat without noticing, and they turn sardines into a rare non-dairy calcium source.

### How often can I eat sardines?
A few times a week fits comfortably within the "at least one oily-fish serving weekly" guidance, and their low mercury makes frequent eating easy. Just mind the sodium if you're watching salt.
