Yes — hummus is one of the better things you can keep in the fridge. Built from chickpeas, tahini, olive oil, lemon, and garlic, it combines plant protein, fiber, and healthy fats in a form that makes vegetables more appealing. It's calorie-dense from the oil and tahini, so the only real skill is portioning.

## What's in it

A serving is about 2 tablespoons, roughly 30 g (a generous dip's worth). Per 2 tablespoons, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 70–80
- **Fat:** roughly 5–6 g, mostly the unsaturated fats from olive oil and tahini
- **Carbohydrates:** about 4–6 g, including 1–2 g fiber
- **Protein:** around 2 g
- **Micronutrients:** folate, iron, magnesium, phosphorus, and some calcium from the tahini

The core ingredients are all whole foods, which is what sets hummus apart from most creamy dips. The trade-off is energy density: the same fats that make it nutritious also make it easy to overshoot if you eat it by the spoonful.

## Real benefits

**Plant protein and fiber together.** Chickpeas bring both, so hummus contributes to fullness and steady digestion in a way most dips don't — a tasty on-ramp to more legumes and a bit more [daily fiber](/mila/blog/how-much-fiber-per-day/). It's a natural fit for anyone eating more plants.

**Healthy fats from real sources.** The olive oil and tahini deliver mostly unsaturated fats, the kind featured in our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/). These fats also help you absorb the fat-soluble vitamins in the vegetables you dip.

**It makes vegetables get eaten.** This is hummus's quiet superpower: a bowl of it next to carrots, peppers, and cucumber turns "I should eat vegetables" into "I want to." A dip that increases vegetable intake is doing real work.

**Steady, not spiky.** The protein, fat, and fiber together make hummus a satisfying snack that doesn't send energy up and down the way a purely starchy or sugary snack can.

## Sensible portions

**Two to four tablespoons** is a sensible snack or meal portion. Because hummus is calorie-dense, the trick is to serve it *with* something bulky and low-calorie — raw vegetables, a small whole-grain pita — rather than eating it straight from the tub with chips on autopilot. A useful mental image: hummus is the flavorful, nutritious accent, and crunchy vegetables are the volume. Store-bought is perfectly fine; if you make it at home you can control the oil and salt, but there's nothing wrong with a good pre-made tub — check the label mainly for sodium.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Classic veggie dip** — with carrots, cucumber, peppers, and cherry tomatoes for an easy, balanced snack.
2. **Spread instead of mayo** — on sandwiches and wraps it adds flavor, protein, and fiber.
3. **Grain-bowl base** — a smear of hummus under [quinoa](/mila/foods/quinoa/), roasted vegetables, and greens ties the bowl together.
4. **Toast topping** — on whole-grain toast with sliced tomato, cucumber, or a soft egg.
5. **Protein pairing** — alongside grilled chicken or falafel as a plate rather than a dip.
6. **Thinned into dressing** — whisk with lemon and water for a creamy, no-mayo salad dressing.
7. **Dolloped on soup** — a spoonful stirred into a bean or vegetable soup adds richness.
8. **Snack plate** — with olives, cucumber, and a few whole-grain crackers for a mezze-style snack.

Pairing hummus with raw vegetables gives you the flavor and nutrition while keeping the portion sensible almost automatically.

## Who should be mindful

- **Sesame allergy:** tahini is ground sesame, a common and sometimes serious allergen — hummus is off the table unless made without it.
- **People on sodium restriction:** store-bought hummus can be fairly salty; compare labels or make it at home to control the salt.
- **Sensitive digestion / IBS:** chickpeas and garlic can cause gas or trigger symptoms in some people; smaller portions are often better tolerated.
- **People watching calories closely:** hummus is nutritious but energy-dense — the risk isn't the food, it's mindless portions with chips; pair with vegetables instead.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Is hummus good for weight loss?**
No single food drives weight loss, but hummus is filling thanks to its protein, fiber, and fat, and it makes vegetables more appealing. Portioned sensibly and paired with veg rather than chips, it's a genuinely useful snack.

**Is store-bought hummus as healthy as homemade?**
Broadly yes — the ingredients are similar. Homemade lets you control oil and salt, but a good store-bought tub is a perfectly healthy staple. Check the label mainly for sodium.

**Does hummus have a lot of protein?**
A moderate amount — around 2 g per 2 tablespoons. It's not a primary protein source, but it contributes, especially as part of a plant-forward plate with other legumes and grains.

**Why is hummus high in calories?**
The olive oil and tahini are calorie-dense (though the fats are the healthy kind). That's why portion and pairing matter — enjoy it with vegetables rather than by the spoonful.

**Is hummus keto-friendly?**
Not especially — chickpeas contain carbohydrates. A couple of tablespoons can fit a lower-carb day, but it isn't a strict-keto food.
