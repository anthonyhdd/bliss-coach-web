Yes — spinach is one of the most nutrient-dense foods you can put on a plate for almost no calories. The honest version of the story is slightly less cartoonish than Popeye suggested (the iron comes with an asterisk), but the overall verdict is not in question: leafy greens like spinach are recommended by essentially every dietary guideline in the world, and spinach is one of the easiest of them to actually use.

## What's in it

A big raw handful is about 30 g; a cooked side portion is closer to 100–150 g, because spinach collapses dramatically when heated. Per 100 g raw, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 25 — it's mostly water, which is exactly why you can eat a lot of it
- **Protein:** roughly 3 g, respectable for a leaf
- **Carbohydrates:** about 3–4 g, of which roughly 2 g is fiber
- **Fat:** essentially none
- **Vitamin K:** several times a full day's needs in a single serving — spinach is one of the richest common sources
- **Folate:** a large share of daily needs, which matters for everyone and especially before and during pregnancy
- **Other micronutrients:** vitamin A (as beta-carotene), vitamin C, magnesium, potassium, manganese, and iron (about 2.5–3 mg, with the caveat below)
- **Plant compounds:** lutein and zeaxanthin, carotenoids concentrated in the retina, plus dietary nitrates

The iron asterisk: spinach's iron is non-heme (plant-form) iron, and spinach also contains oxalates that bind some of it, so you absorb noticeably less than the label number implies. It still contributes — especially eaten with vitamin C — but it's a supporting player, not a substitute for beans, lentils, or meat if iron is a real concern for you.

## Real benefits

**Enormous nutrient return per calorie.** Very few foods deliver this much vitamin K, folate, and carotenoids for 25 calories per 100 g. If your goal is simply "get more good things in without rearranging your diet," spinach is close to the ideal vehicle — it disappears into dishes you already eat.

**Eye-supporting carotenoids.** Lutein and zeaxanthin accumulate in the macula of the eye, and higher intakes of these carotenoids are consistently associated with better long-term eye health in population studies. Leafy greens are the main dietary source — this is one of spinach's genuinely distinctive contributions.

**Folate where it counts.** Folate is essential for cell division and is specifically important before and during early pregnancy. Food folate from greens complements (not replaces) the supplement guidance given to people who are or may become pregnant.

**Fiber and bulk that make meals work.** Spinach adds volume, water, and fiber to meals without adding much energy, which supports fullness and regular digestion — part of the same logic behind our [best foods for gut health](/mila/blog/best-foods-for-gut-health/) guide. A meal with a pile of greens in it is simply more filling than the same meal without.

**Nitrates and blood-vessel function.** Leafy greens are naturally rich in dietary nitrates, which the body can convert into nitric oxide, a compound involved in blood-vessel relaxation. Regular leafy-green intake is associated with better cardiovascular markers; the honest framing is that this is one plausible mechanism among several, not a promise from any single salad.

## Sensible portions

**One to two cups raw (30–60 g), or about half a cup cooked, most days** is an entirely realistic target — dietary guidelines typically suggest several servings of vegetables daily with leafy greens featured regularly. Because spinach cooks down to a fraction of its raw volume, a whole bag wilted into a pan is a normal amount for two people, not an excess. There's no meaningful upper limit for most healthy adults eating normal food quantities; the caveats below apply to specific situations, not to spinach in general.

One practical tip: eat spinach with a little fat (olive oil, eggs, cheese, nuts) — the carotenoids are fat-soluble, so a dressed salad beats a dry one nutritionally as well as in taste.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Wilted into anything hot** — stir a few handfuls into pasta sauce, curry, soup, or chili in the last two minutes; it vanishes and the dish barely changes flavor.
2. **The invisible smoothie handful** — a cup of raw spinach in a fruit smoothie is genuinely undetectable; the color changes, the taste doesn't.
3. **Eggs and greens** — a spinach omelet or scramble is one of the fastest complete breakfasts there is; more combinations in our [healthy breakfast ideas](/mila/blog/healthy-breakfast-ideas/).
4. **Garlic-sautéed side** — olive oil, garlic, a whole bag of spinach, salt, lemon. Ninety seconds, done.
5. **Salad base with structure** — baby spinach with something crunchy, something creamy, and a real dressing so you actually enjoy it.
6. **Grain bowls** — a bed of spinach under warm rice or [quinoa](/mila/foods/quinoa/) wilts slightly and ties the bowl together.
7. **Frozen blocks in the freezer** — frozen spinach is cheap, already washed, nutritionally solid, and perfect for curries, lasagna, and soups.
8. **Blended into pesto or sauces** — half basil, half spinach in a pesto stretches it and softens the cost.

Frozen spinach deserves a special mention: it's picked and frozen quickly, keeps for months, and removes the classic fridge-drawer failure mode where the fresh bag turns to compost before Thursday.

## Who should be mindful

- **People prone to calcium-oxalate kidney stones:** spinach is one of the highest-oxalate foods there is. This doesn't mean zero spinach for everyone with a stone history, but it's a well-established consideration — portion guidance from your doctor or dietitian applies, and pairing spinach with calcium-containing foods can help bind oxalate in the gut.
- **People taking warfarin:** vitamin K interacts with this medication. The standard advice is *consistency*, not avoidance — keep your leafy-green intake steady rather than swinging between none and daily, and coordinate changes with your care team.
- **People with significant iron-deficiency concerns:** don't rely on spinach as a primary iron source given the absorption caveat; it's a contributor, not a cure.
- **Very young children:** purees and finely chopped preparations are the safe formats, as with any leafy green.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice for your actual situation.

## FAQ

**Is raw or cooked spinach healthier?**
Both, for different reasons. Cooking shrinks the volume so you eat more of it, softens oxalates somewhat (especially boiling), and makes some carotenoids easier to absorb; raw preserves more folate and vitamin C. The realistic answer: eat it however you'll actually eat it regularly.

**Is spinach a good source of iron?**
It contributes, but less than the raw numbers suggest — plant iron plus oxalates means modest absorption. Pair it with vitamin C (lemon, peppers, tomatoes) to improve uptake, and look to legumes, meat, or fortified foods as primary sources if iron is a genuine concern.

**Is baby spinach as nutritious as regular spinach?**
Essentially yes — the differences are minor and inconsistent. Baby spinach is more tender for salads; mature leaves stand up better to cooking. Choose by use, not by nutrition anxiety.

**Can you eat spinach every day?**
For most healthy people, yes — daily leafy greens are a pattern dietary guidelines actively encourage. The main exceptions are the kidney-stone and warfarin situations above, where individualized guidance matters.

**Does frozen spinach lose its nutrients?**
No meaningful loss for practical purposes — it's frozen shortly after harvest and holds its value well. It's one of the best cost-per-nutrition items in the freezer aisle.
