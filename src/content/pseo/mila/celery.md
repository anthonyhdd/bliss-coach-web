Yes — celery is a healthy food, though it's worth being clear-eyed about what it is and isn't. It's genuinely good: hydrating, high in fiber for its calories, a source of vitamin K, and one of the best low-effort vehicles for dips and snacks. What it isn't is a miracle. The wellness world has draped celery — especially celery juice — in claims it can't support, and the honest, celery-positive move is to enjoy it for what it actually offers rather than what an influencer promised.

## What's in it

A typical serving is a couple of stalks or about one cup chopped (roughly 100 g). Per 100 g of raw celery, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 14–16 — a stalk is roughly 6, which is genuinely tiny
- **Carbohydrates:** about 3 g, of which roughly 1.5 g is fiber
- **Protein:** under 1 g
- **Fat:** essentially none
- **Water:** around 95%, which is most of what celery is
- **Vitamin K:** a solid contribution for such a low-calorie food
- **Potassium and folate:** modest amounts
- **Vitamin C and A:** small but real
- **Plant compounds:** including apigenin and other flavonoids, plus natural nitrates

Two honest notes. First, the "negative-calorie" idea — that chewing celery burns more than it contains — is a myth. It's true celery is extremely low in calories and the digestion cost is proportionally higher than for most foods, but you don't end up in the negative. It's simply a very light food, which is useful enough without the tall tale. Second, celery is naturally somewhat high in sodium for a vegetable, though the amount is still small in normal portions.

## Real benefits

**Hydration and volume for almost nothing.** At roughly 95% water, celery adds crunch, bulk, and freshness to a plate or snack for a trivial calorie cost. If your goal is to feel satisfied while eating lightly, high-water vegetables like celery do real work — they fill space and time without adding much energy.

**A fiber-carrying crunch vehicle.** Celery's best practical role might be as the thing you dip. Sturdy, scoopable, and refreshing, it turns a spoonful of [hummus](/mila/foods/hummus/) or nut butter into a satisfying, fiber-forward snack — a far better default than crackers when you want crunch.

**Vitamin K and plant compounds along for the ride.** For something this light, celery delivers a respectable amount of vitamin K, plus flavonoids like apigenin that are of research interest. As always with single compounds in whole food, the honest framing is "a small, steady contribution," not a treatment.

**An easy way to build savory bases.** Chopped celery is part of the classic aromatic trio (with onion and carrot) behind soups, stews, and stocks — a quiet, flavor-building role that helps make whole-food cooking taste good.

## Sensible portions

**A few stalks to a couple of cups a day** is a completely reasonable amount, and there's no meaningful upper limit for healthy people beyond appetite. Celery is one of the genuine "eat freely" vegetables — the useful move isn't rationing it but pairing it with something more substantial, since on its own it's mostly water and won't keep you full for long. Add a protein or a fat-carrying dip and it becomes a real snack rather than a placeholder.

On celery juice specifically: the trendy claims — that it detoxes, cures conditions, or transforms health — aren't supported by good evidence. Juicing also strips out the fiber that's one of celery's better features. There's nothing wrong with enjoying celery juice if you like it, but treat it as a hydrating drink, not medicine, and don't let it replace eating actual vegetables. Whole celery, dipped and crunched, is the better everyday choice. It pairs nicely with [cucumber](/mila/foods/cucumber/) for a fresh, hydrating snack plate.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **With a dip** — the classic; celery sticks into hummus, guacamole, yogurt dip, or nut butter make crunch feel like a treat.
2. **"Ants on a log"** — celery filled with nut butter, an old-school snack that still works.
3. **Chopped into a chicken or tuna salad** — for crunch and freshness that lifts the whole thing.
4. **In the base of soups and stews** — sautéed with onion and carrot as the aromatic foundation.
5. **Sliced thin in a salad** — with apple and a lemony dressing for a crisp, refreshing side.
6. **Braised** — cooked gently until tender, celery becomes silky and mild, a surprising warm side.
7. **Juiced or blended** — if you enjoy it, as a hydrating drink rather than a health treatment.

## Who should be mindful

- **People on warfarin:** celery contains vitamin K, so the general "keep vitamin K intake consistent" guidance applies to your overall diet — not a reason to avoid it, but worth folding into a stable pattern.
- **People watching sodium very closely:** celery is a little higher in natural sodium than most vegetables. The amount is small in normal portions, but it's worth knowing if you're on a strict sodium limit.
- **People with a known celery allergy:** celery is a recognized allergen for a small number of people and can cause real reactions; if that's you, avoid it and check labels, since it hides in stocks and seasonings.
- **Anyone banking on celery juice claims:** the honest caution here is expectation, not safety — enjoy it as a drink, but don't rely on it for the health miracles it's been sold as.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

**Is celery really a negative-calorie food?**
No — that's a myth. Celery is extremely low in calories and does cost a bit to digest, but you don't burn more than it contains. It's simply a very light, hydrating food, which is useful on its own without the exaggeration.

**Is celery juice good for you?**
It's a hydrating drink, and if you enjoy it, fine — but the detox and cure claims aren't supported by good evidence, and juicing removes the fiber. Whole celery is the better everyday choice; don't rely on the juice as medicine.

**Does celery have any real nutrition?**
Yes — for such a light food it offers vitamin K, some potassium and folate, plant flavonoids, and a lot of water and fiber. It won't be the nutritional centerpiece of a meal, but it's a genuinely worthwhile vegetable.

**Why is celery good for snacking?**
It's crunchy, refreshing, sturdy enough to scoop dips, and almost calorie-free. Paired with a protein- or fiber-rich dip like hummus, it turns a craving for crunch into a satisfying, wholesome snack.

**How much celery can I eat?**
As much as you enjoy — it's one of the genuine "eat freely" vegetables. The only tweak worth making is pairing it with something more substantial, since on its own it won't keep you full for long.
