Yes — lentils are one of the best value-for-nutrition foods on the planet: serious protein, exceptional fiber, real iron and folate, all for pennies per serving. Legumes are one of the few food groups that essentially every dietary pattern with good long-term evidence — Mediterranean, DASH, traditional diets worldwide — agrees on. If lentils were expensive and rare, they'd be marketed as a superfood; because they're cheap, they get overlooked.

## What's in it

A typical cooked serving is about one cup (roughly 200 g); dry lentils roughly triple in volume when cooked. Per 100 g cooked, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 115
- **Protein:** roughly 9 g — about 18 g per cooked cup, among the highest of any plant food
- **Carbohydrates:** about 20 g, of which roughly 8 g is fiber — an unusually high share
- **Fat:** well under 1 g
- **Iron:** about 3 mg per 100 g — meaningful, in the plant (non-heme) form that absorbs better alongside vitamin C
- **Folate:** a large share of daily needs per serving; lentils are one of the richest common food sources
- **Other micronutrients:** potassium, magnesium, zinc, manganese, and B vitamins

One nuance worth knowing: lentil protein is slightly low in the amino acid methionine, which grains supply generously — so lentils plus rice, bread, or [quinoa](/mila/foods/quinoa/) over the course of a day forms complete protein without any careful combining at each meal. This happens automatically in almost every traditional lentil dish ever invented.

## Real benefits

**Plant protein that's actually substantial.** Eighteen grams per cooked cup puts lentils in genuinely useful territory — they hold their own in our [high-protein foods guide](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/) against far more expensive options. For anyone eating less meat, lentils are the workhorse, not the garnish.

**Fiber at a level almost nothing else matches.** Most people get roughly half the fiber they need; a single cup of lentils delivers around 15 g, a huge stride toward the target — see [how much fiber per day](/mila/blog/how-much-fiber-per-day/) for context. Much of it is soluble fiber and resistant starch, which feed gut bacteria and slow digestion.

**Steady blood sugar behavior.** Lentils digest slowly — the fiber-plus-intact-structure combination gives them a low glycemic impact, and trials show legumes eaten with or before other carbs blunt the overall glucose response of the meal. This is one of the better-documented individual-food effects in nutrition.

**Heart-friendly track record.** Regular legume intake is consistently associated with lower LDL cholesterol (soluble fiber is the plausible mechanism, supported by trials) and lower cardiovascular risk in population studies. Swapping some refined carbs or processed meat for lentils is one of the highest-confidence trades in nutrition.

**Iron and folate for the people who need them most.** Lentils are a standard recommendation for plant-based eaters and during pregnancy for good reason — the folate is outstanding and the iron is real, especially when eaten with vitamin C (tomatoes, lemon, peppers — conveniently, the things lentils are usually cooked with).

**Absurdly cheap.** A bag of dry lentils costs about as much as a coffee and yields many servings. No other food on this site delivers comparable protein and fiber per unit of money.

## Sensible portions

**Half a cup to one cup cooked (100–200 g), several times a week** is a great pattern — many dietary guidelines now explicitly encourage legumes multiple times weekly, and daily is fine. If you're new to lentils (or to fiber generally), start nearer the half-cup end and build up over a couple of weeks with plenty of water; your gut adapts, and the temporary gassiness that scares people off genuinely improves with regular intake.

Practical cooking notes: unlike beans, lentils need **no soaking**. Red/split lentils cook in about 15 minutes and dissolve into creamy soups; brown and green take 20–30 minutes and hold their shape; French/Puy lentils stay firmest for salads. Canned lentils are perfectly good — rinse them to cut sodium and gas-causing compounds.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Red lentil soup** — onion, garlic, cumin, red lentils, stock, 20 minutes; blend or don't. The gateway lentil dish.
2. **Dal** — the world's most-proven lentil format: spiced, gently simmered, served over rice for automatic complete protein.
3. **Lentil bolognese** — swap half (or all) of the ground meat in your pasta sauce for cooked brown lentils; the texture is remarkably convincing.
4. **Warm lentil salad** — firm green or Puy lentils, mustard vinaigrette, feta, whatever vegetables are around; eats like a meal.
5. **Canned-lentil rescue lunch** — a rinsed can, olive oil, lemon, salt, and a chopped tomato; three minutes, genuinely good.
6. **Lentil tacos or burrito bowls** — seasoned brown lentils do everything the meat was doing.
7. **Bulk out any soup or stew** — a handful of dry red lentils thrown into almost any simmering pot adds body, protein, and fiber invisibly.
8. **Curried lentils with rice** — a big batch on Sunday reheats better than it started, all week.

Lentils are also a batch-cooking dream: cooked lentils keep about four to five days in the fridge and freeze well in portions.

## Who should be mindful

- **People with IBS or FODMAP sensitivity:** lentils contain fermentable carbohydrates (GOS) that can trigger symptoms. Smaller portions of *canned, well-rinsed* lentils are the standard better-tolerated starting point — a dietitian can help calibrate.
- **Anyone jumping from low fiber to high fiber overnight:** ramp up gradually with water; the adjustment period is real but temporary.
- **People on potassium-restricted diets or with specific kidney conditions:** legume portions may need tailoring — individualized guidance applies.
- **Legume allergy:** less common than peanut allergy but exists; those with it avoid lentils entirely.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can fit lentils into *your* plan better than general notes can.

## FAQ

**Are lentils a complete protein?**
Not quite on their own — they're modest in methionine — but pairing with any grain across the day (rice, bread, quinoa) completes the picture. You don't need to combine at every meal; a normal varied day does it automatically.

**Do lentils cause gas, and does it get better?**
Often at first, and yes, it genuinely improves. Regular legume eaters report far less gas within a few weeks. Rinsing canned lentils, starting with small portions, and cooking lentils thoroughly all help.

**Are canned lentils as healthy as dried?**
Nutritionally very close. Rinse them to reduce sodium. Dried are cheaper and let you control texture; canned win on convenience. Both count fully.

**Which color lentil is healthiest?**
The differences are minor — all are excellent. Choose by texture: red for creamy soups and dal, brown/green for general use, French/Puy for salads.

**Can lentils help with weight management?**
No single food drives weight change, but lentils' protein-plus-fiber combination is among the most filling per calorie there is, which makes moderate eating feel easier — that's the realistic mechanism.
