Yes — feta can be a healthy part of your diet, and it has a quiet advantage most cheeses don't: it's so flavorful that a little goes a long way. It brings protein, calcium, and a satisfying tang, and it's often lower in calories than harder cheeses. The one honest caveat that genuinely matters here is sodium — feta is brined, so it's salty — but as a bold accent rather than a main event, it earns its place easily.

## What's in it

Feta is a brined cheese traditionally made from sheep's milk (or a sheep-goat blend), which gives it its crumbly texture and sharp flavor. Per 100 g of feta, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 260 — lower than many hard cheeses like cheddar
- **Protein:** roughly 14 g
- **Fat:** about 21 g, including saturated fat
- **Carbohydrates:** minimal, around 4 g
- **Calcium:** a strong, well-absorbed source
- **Sodium:** high — this is the number to keep an eye on, thanks to the brine
- **Other nutrients:** good B12, riboflavin, phosphorus, and some vitamin A

An honest note on portions: 100 g is a useful reference for the label, but nobody eats 100 g of feta in a sitting the way they might a chicken breast. A realistic crumble over a salad is more like 30 g — which keeps the calories, fat, and sodium modest while still delivering plenty of flavor. Feta's intensity is a feature: you need less of it.

## Real benefits

**Big flavor for a small amount.** This is feta's real nutritional strength — its sharp, salty tang means a modest crumble transforms a dish, so you get satisfaction without a large portion. That makes it easy to enjoy cheese while keeping the numbers sensible.

**Calcium and protein in a satisfying package.** Feta contributes well-absorbed calcium for bone health and a bit of protein, in a form that makes vegetables and grains far more appealing to actually eat.

**Often lighter than hard cheeses.** Because of its high moisture and brine content, feta tends to be lower in calories per gram than dense aged cheeses — a small edge if you're cheese-loving but calorie-aware.

**A gateway to eating more vegetables.** Feta's biggest real-world benefit may be indirect: it makes salads, roasted vegetables, and grain bowls genuinely crave-able, which nudges the whole meal in a better direction.

## Sensible portions

**A modest crumble — around 30 g, roughly a small handful — is a sensible everyday portion**, enough to flavor a full salad or bowl. Treated as a seasoning-with-benefits rather than a protein source, feta fits comfortably into a balanced pattern most days.

The sodium is the honest thing to manage. Feta is brined, so it's salty by design; if you're watching sodium, the move is simple — use it as an accent, not by the block, and go easy on additional salt in the dish since the feta is already doing that work. Draining and briefly rinsing feta trims some surface salt if you want. Full-fat feta is more satisfying than reduced-fat versions, and because you're using a small amount, the full-fat version is usually the better choice. Buying feta in a block rather than pre-crumbled is a small upgrade too — it keeps better in its brine, tastes fresher, and lets you crumble exactly as much as a dish needs rather than tipping in a whole tub.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Crumbled over salad** — the classic; especially good with tomato, cucumber, and [olive oil](/mila/foods/olive-oil/).
2. **On roasted vegetables** — scattered over warm roasted peppers, zucchini, or [broccoli](/mila/foods/broccoli/).
3. **Baked feta** — a block baked with tomatoes and olive oil, then mashed into a sauce.
4. **In grain bowls** — with quinoa, greens, and chickpeas for a Mediterranean plate.
5. **Whipped feta dip** — blended smooth with a little yogurt and lemon.
6. **In an omelet or scramble** — a little crumbled in melts into savory pockets.
7. **On watermelon** — the salty-sweet summer combo that surprises people.
8. **Stuffed into peppers or wrapped in filo** — a small amount adds a rich, savory core.

## Who should be mindful

- **People watching sodium (e.g., high blood pressure):** this is feta's main caveat — it's genuinely salty. Use it as a small accent, skip extra salt in the dish, and consider draining/rinsing it; the concern is manageable, not disqualifying.
- **People managing saturated fat or heart health:** like all cheese, feta contains saturated fat, so a modest portion is the sensible approach — easy here, since a little goes a long way.
- **Pregnant people:** traditional feta may be made from unpasteurized milk, which is generally advised against in pregnancy — choosing feta clearly labeled as made from pasteurized milk resolves this.
- **People with lactose intolerance:** aged and brined cheeses like feta are relatively low in lactose and often well tolerated, but individual tolerance varies.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

### Is feta healthier than other cheeses?
It has a couple of small edges: it's often lower in calories per gram than hard cheeses, and its strong flavor means you use less. The trade-off is higher sodium. It's not a "health cheese," but as a flavorful accent it fits a balanced diet nicely.

### Is feta high in salt?
Yes — feta is brined, so sodium is its main watch-point. That's not a reason to avoid it, but a reason to treat it as a seasoning: a modest crumble, and go light on other salt in the dish. Draining and rinsing trims some.

### Is feta good for weight loss?
It can fit well, because its intense flavor delivers satisfaction from a small, modest-calorie portion — and it makes vegetables genuinely appealing. The key is using it as an accent rather than eating it by the block.

### Can I eat feta during pregnancy?
Choose feta made from pasteurized milk, which is widely available and generally considered safe in pregnancy. Traditional unpasteurized versions are the ones usually advised against — check the label, and confirm specifics with your care team.
