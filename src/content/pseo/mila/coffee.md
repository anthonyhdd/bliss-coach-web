For most adults, coffee is not just harmless — large bodies of research associate moderate coffee drinking with neutral-to-positive health outcomes. The nuance is caffeine: coffee's benefits come with a stimulant attached, so the right amount depends on your sleep, your anxiety, your heart, and whether you're pregnant — and "coffee" stops being a near-zero-calorie drink once it becomes a large sweetened milkshake with an espresso inside.

## What's in it

A standard cup (about 240 ml / 8 oz) of black brewed coffee contains roughly:

- **Calories:** 2–5 — essentially nothing
- **Caffeine:** around 80–100 mg (espresso shots run about 60–75 mg each; cold brew and large mugs can be much higher)
- **Antioxidants:** chlorogenic acids and other polyphenols — coffee is one of the biggest antioxidant contributors in many people's diets, simply because we drink so much of it
- **Micronutrients:** small amounts of potassium, magnesium, and B3

What changes the math is everything added afterward. Milk adds modest, perfectly reasonable calories (a splash: ~10–20; a latte's worth: 80–150 depending on milk and size). Sugar, syrups, and whipped cream are where a coffee can quietly become a 300–500 calorie dessert. The coffee itself was never the issue.

## Real benefits

**Alertness and performance — the obvious one.** Caffeine reliably improves alertness, reaction time, and perceived energy, which is why humans have loved this drink for centuries.

**Long-term health associations.** Large observational studies repeatedly link moderate coffee consumption (roughly 2–4 cups a day) with lower risks of type 2 diabetes, liver disease, and Parkinson's disease, and overall mortality that's no worse — often slightly better — than in non-drinkers. These are associations, not guarantees, but the pattern is consistent enough that major health bodies treat moderate coffee as compatible with a healthy diet.

**Antioxidant load.** Those chlorogenic acids are the same family of compounds people pay for in supplements — delivered daily, for free, in your mug.

**Workout support.** Caffeine before exercise measurably improves endurance and perceived effort; it's one of the few legal performance aids with strong evidence.

**A near-zero-calorie pleasure ritual.** In a world of caloric drinks, black or lightly-milked coffee is one of the most satisfying things you can consume at almost no energy cost.

## Sensible portions

- **Up to about 400 mg of caffeine a day** — roughly 3–4 standard cups of brewed coffee — is the widely used guideline (FDA and others) for healthy adults.
- **Pregnancy:** guidance is typically **200 mg a day or less** — about one to two small cups. Check with your midwife or doctor.
- **Timing matters as much as quantity.** Caffeine lingers for hours (its half-life is commonly around five hours), so a 4 p.m. coffee is still partly in your system at bedtime. If sleep is a struggle, an early-afternoon cutoff often changes more than cutting the morning cups.
- **Calorie-wise,** black coffee and simple milk coffees fit anywhere. Daily large flavored drinks belong in the "treat" column — the same budgeting logic we walk through in [how many calories you need a day](/mila/blog/how-many-calories-a-day/).

## Easy ways to drink it

- **Black or with a splash of milk** — the everyday baseline that keeps coffee effectively free.
- **Cappuccino or flat white** — milk brings a little protein and calcium; a lovely mid-morning ritual at ~80–120 calories.
- **Homemade iced coffee:** cold brew or chilled filter coffee + milk + ice beats bottled versions that often carry more sugar than soda.
- **Upgrade gradually:** if you currently take two sugars, drop to one for a couple of weeks, then half. Taste adjusts remarkably fast, and the coffee starts tasting like coffee.
- **Pair it, don't replace with it:** coffee blunts appetite for some people — that's fine, but coffee-instead-of-breakfast until 2 p.m. often boomerangs into an over-hungry evening. A coffee alongside a real breakfast (even a quick [high-protein one](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/)) sets up a much steadier day.

## Who should be mindful

- **Pregnant or trying to conceive:** keep to the ~200 mg guidance and confirm specifics with your care provider.
- **Anxiety or panic-prone:** caffeine can amplify both; many people do better on half-caff or a lower ceiling.
- **Sleep problems:** before blaming life, audit caffeine timing — it's the cheapest sleep intervention there is.
- **Acid reflux or sensitive stomachs:** coffee (even decaf) can trigger symptoms; darker roasts or smaller amounts help some people.
- **Certain heart rhythm conditions, uncontrolled hypertension, or medications that interact with caffeine:** this is doctor territory — ask specifically about your caffeine ceiling.
- **Teens:** most pediatric guidance suggests considerably less caffeine than the adult 400 mg.

If any of these describe you, a quick conversation with your doctor or a registered dietitian will get you a personal answer — general limits are averages, and you're not an average.

## FAQ

**Is coffee dehydrating?**
Not meaningfully at normal intakes — the fluid in the cup more than offsets caffeine's mild diuretic effect. Coffee counts toward hydration.

**Is decaf healthy too?**
Yes — decaf keeps most of the antioxidants with only a trace of caffeine. It's a great tool for afternoon cups or caffeine-sensitive drinkers.

**Does coffee stunt growth?**
No — that's a myth with no supporting evidence.

**Is coffee good for weight loss?**
Black coffee is calorie-free and mildly appetite-suppressing, so it fits easily into a calorie-aware pattern — but sweetened coffee drinks can quietly work against the same goal.
