Dark chocolate is that rare food that's both a genuine treat and a source of interesting compounds — so yes, in modest amounts it earns its decent reputation. It's still an energy-dense food built on cocoa butter and sugar, though, so the honest framing is "a small daily pleasure with some perks," not "medicine that happens to be delicious."

## What's in it

A typical portion — about 30 g, or 3–4 squares of a standard bar of 70–85% dark chocolate — comes to roughly:

- **Calories:** about 160–180
- **Fat:** around 12–14 g, mostly from cocoa butter (a mix of saturated and monounsaturated fats)
- **Sugar:** about 5–9 g at 70–85% cocoa; milk chocolate carries two to three times more
- **Fiber:** a surprising 3–4 g
- **Minerals:** meaningful amounts of magnesium, iron, and copper
- **Flavanols:** the cocoa polyphenols behind most of the health research — higher-cocoa bars generally contain more
- **Caffeine:** a modest amount, roughly a quarter of a cup of coffee's worth per portion, plus theobromine

The higher the cocoa percentage, the less sugar and the more cocoa compounds — and the more bitter the taste. There's no prize for forcing down 100% bars you hate; 70% is already solidly "dark."

## Real benefits

**Flavanols and heart health.** Cocoa flavanols have reasonable evidence for supporting blood vessel function and modestly lowering blood pressure. The effects in studies are real but small — think "nice contribution," not "protection plan."

**Minerals people actually run low on.** Magnesium and iron are two of the most commonly under-consumed minerals, and dark chocolate contributes both in a form people happily eat.

**Satisfaction per bite.** This is underrated: dark chocolate's intensity means a couple of squares often genuinely finish a craving, where milder sweets invite the whole packet. A planned, enjoyed treat is one of the best defenses against the deprive-then-binge cycle — the same principle behind our guide on [how to stop late-night snacking](/mila/blog/how-to-stop-late-night-snacking/).

**It pairs well with good habits.** Squares with afternoon coffee, chopped over yogurt, melted onto fruit — dark chocolate upgrades snacks that are already reasonable.

One honest note: some dark chocolate bars have tested higher in cadmium and lead than ideal, which matters mainly if you eat large amounts daily. Varying brands and keeping portions moderate — which you'd want to do anyway — addresses it.

## Sensible portions

- **A good everyday amount is about 20–30 g** — two to four squares, most days if you like.
- The research on benefits generally involves modest intakes; more chocolate doesn't mean more benefit, just more calories (a whole 100 g bar is 550–600 of them).
- Portion it consciously: break off your squares, put the bar away, sit down, and actually taste it. Dark chocolate rewards slow eating more than almost any food.

There is no need to "earn" chocolate with exercise or "pay for it" with skipped meals. It fits inside an ordinary day of eating, full stop.

## Easy ways to eat it

- **The after-dinner square ritual:** two squares with tea or coffee closes a meal remarkably well.
- **Chocolate + fruit:** squares with strawberries, orange segments, or a banana stretch the pleasure with fiber and volume.
- **Chopped over Greek yogurt or oatmeal** — dessert energy, breakfast structure.
- **Dark chocolate + nuts:** the classic trail-mix pairing; satisfying and portable, just portion it in advance since both are calorie-dense.
- **Hot chocolate from real cocoa or dark chocolate,** made with milk — far more satisfying than powder-packet versions, and you control the sugar.

If you keep chocolate at your desk, a smaller stash works with your brain rather than against it — more ideas in our roundup of [healthy snacks for work](/mila/blog/healthy-snacks-for-work/).

## Who should be mindful

- **Caffeine-sensitive people** may want to skip dark chocolate in the evening — a large portion carries enough caffeine and theobromine to nudge sleep.
- **Migraine sufferers** sometimes identify chocolate as a personal trigger; that's individual, not universal.
- **People with reflux (GERD)** often find chocolate relaxes the wrong valve at the wrong time.
- **Dogs, famously, can never have it** — theobromine is toxic to them.
- **Anyone managing diabetes or heart conditions** can usually still enjoy dark chocolate; the portion and the rest of the day just matter more.

If you have a condition where caffeine, fat, or sugar intake is medically relevant, your doctor or a registered dietitian can help you place chocolate sensibly in your plan — most people are pleasantly surprised by how rarely the answer is "none."

## FAQ

**What percentage of dark chocolate is healthiest?**
Bars of 70% and up give you more cocoa flavanols and less sugar. But the best percentage is the darkest one you *enjoy* — a loved 70% beats an abandoned 90%.

**Is dark chocolate good for weight loss?**
No food causes weight loss, but a planned portion of chocolate you savor can make an overall calorie-aware way of eating far easier to stick with.

**Is it okay to eat dark chocolate every day?**
For most people, yes — a 20–30 g daily portion fits comfortably in a balanced diet.

**Dark vs. milk chocolate — big difference?**
Moderate: dark has less sugar, more fiber, minerals, and flavanols. If you only love milk chocolate, a smaller savored portion is still a perfectly valid treat.
