Yes — cashew butter is a nutritious, creamy spread, essentially just cashews blended smooth, which means it carries the same good things: heart-friendly fats, useful minerals, and some plant protein. The two things worth paying attention to are the ingredient label (you want mostly cashews, not added sugar and oil) and the portion, since it's easy to over-serve a spread. Get those right and it's an easy one to enjoy.

## What's in it

A serving is about two tablespoons, roughly 32 g. Per serving, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 180–190
- **Fat:** roughly 15–16 g, mostly the heart-friendly monounsaturated kind
- **Protein:** around 5 g
- **Carbohydrates:** about 8–9 g, a touch higher than most nut butters, with a little fiber
- **Micronutrients:** copper (very high), magnesium, manganese, phosphorus, and zinc

Because it's just blended cashews, cashew butter mirrors the [whole cashew](/mila/foods/cashews/) closely — notably rich in copper and magnesium, slightly softer and more carb-containing than other nut butters, which is part of why it's so silky. A quick honest note on protein: around 5 g per serving is a genuine contribution, but like whole cashews it's best thought of as a healthy-fat food that also brings some protein.

## Real benefits

**Healthy fats in a spreadable form.** The fat is mostly monounsaturated, the type highlighted in our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/), and it pairs with protein and a little fiber to make a serving genuinely satisfying. Regular nut and nut-butter eating is consistently linked with favorable heart outcomes.

**A real mineral contribution.** A serving delivers a large share of a day's copper along with meaningful magnesium — nutrients involved in energy metabolism, nerve function, and bone health that many people run a bit short on.

**Some plant protein in an easy format.** At around 5 g per serving, cashew butter adds protein to toast, oatmeal, and snacks; its creaminess also makes it a natural base for dairy-free sauces and dressings.

**Satisfying enough to steady snacking.** The fat-protein combination slows digestion, so a measured portion on whole-grain toast or fruit tends to hold you — provided it's a measured portion rather than free-pouring from the jar.

## Sensible portions

**Two tablespoons (about 32 g)** is the sensible serving, and the single best habit is to measure it rather than eyeball spoonfuls straight from the jar, since a creamy spread is easy to over-serve. Just as important is the label: the best cashew butters list only cashews (and maybe a pinch of salt). Versions with added sugar, palm oil, or hydrogenated fats turn a healthy spread into something closer to a treat, so a quick check of the ingredients is worth the ten seconds. Within a measured portion of a clean-ingredient jar, cashew butter is a smart, nutrient-dense everyday choice.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **On whole-grain toast** — the classic; a measured spread on good bread.
2. **With apple or banana slices** — creamy dip that turns fruit into a filling snack.
3. **Stirred into oatmeal** — a spoonful melts into [oatmeal](/mila/foods/oatmeal/) for richness and staying power.
4. **Into smoothies** — a tablespoon adds creaminess and healthy fats to the blend.
5. **Dairy-free sauce base** — thinned with water, soy, and lime into a satay-style sauce for noodles.
6. **Swirled into yogurt** — through [Greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) with fruit for a dessert-like bowl.
7. **On rice cakes or crackers** — a quick, portable snack with staying power.
8. **In energy bites** — with oats and dates for a no-bake snack.

## Who should be mindful

- **Tree nut allergy:** cashew butter is made from tree nuts and among the more common triggers of serious reactions; a tree-nut allergy means strict avoidance.
- **People watching calories closely:** cashew butter is nutritious but energy-dense — the issue is portion size, not the food; measuring the serving keeps it sensible.
- **Anyone avoiding added sugar:** sweetened cashew butters edge toward dessert; check the label and choose versions that are basically just cashews.
- **People on sodium restriction:** some jars add a fair amount of salt; low- or no-salt versions are easy to find.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Is cashew butter healthier than peanut butter?**
They're comparable, each with strengths — cashew butter is notably higher in copper and a bit creamier, while peanut butter tends to be slightly higher in protein. Both are good choices when the ingredients are basically just the nut; variety is smarter than crowning one.

**Does cashew butter have protein?**
Yes — around 5 g per two-tablespoon serving. It's a useful addition to toast, oatmeal, and snacks, though it's best thought of as a healthy-fat food that also contributes some protein rather than a protein food first.

**How do I choose a healthy cashew butter?**
Read the ingredient list: the best options are just cashews, maybe with a little salt. Versions with added sugar, palm oil, or hydrogenated fats are closer to treats, so favor the clean-ingredient jars.

**Is cashew butter good for weight management?**
No food does that alone, but a measured serving is filling thanks to its fat and protein, which can steady snacking. The key is measuring the portion rather than free-pouring, since spreads are easy to over-serve.

**How much cashew butter should I eat a day?**
About two tablespoons (32 g) is a sensible serving. There's no strict limit for a healthy person, but its calorie density rewards measuring rather than eyeballing.
