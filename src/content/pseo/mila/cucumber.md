Yes — cucumber is a genuinely good food, as long as your expectations are honest. It won't load you with vitamins the way kale does, but it's hydrating, refreshing, nearly calorie-free, and it makes it easy to eat more vegetables and drink more water. Sometimes "low calorie and gets eaten" is exactly the point.

## What's in it

A serving is about one cup sliced, roughly 100–120 g. Per cup, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 15 — one of the lowest of any food
- **Water:** roughly 95%, which is essentially the whole story
- **Carbohydrates:** about 3–4 g, with a small amount of fiber (mostly in the skin)
- **Protein and fat:** negligible
- **Micronutrients:** modest amounts of vitamin K, vitamin C, potassium, and some antioxidants, more of them concentrated in the peel

Cucumber is not a nutrient powerhouse, and that's fine — its value is volume, water, and crunch for almost no calories, which is a real and useful role on a plate.

## Real benefits

**Hydration and volume for basically nothing.** At 95% water and 15 calories a cup, cucumber fills space on a plate and in your stomach without adding meaningful energy — the definition of a "volume" food that helps meals feel bigger and more satisfying.

**Makes healthy eating easier.** Cucumber's real strength is behavioral: it's crunchy, cooling, and effortless to prep, so it turns dips, salads, and water into things you'll actually reach for. A vegetable that gets eaten beats a "superfood" that sits in the drawer.

**A little fiber and antioxidants, mostly in the skin.** Leaving the peel on adds fiber and a bit more of the antioxidants and vitamin K, so it's worth eating unpeeled when you can — a small nudge toward your [daily fiber intake](/mila/blog/how-much-fiber-per-day/).

**A smart carrier and pairing.** Because it's so light, cucumber pairs beautifully with more nutrient-dense foods — [hummus](/mila/foods/hummus/), yogurt dips, cheese, or a protein — letting you enjoy those in sensible amounts while the cucumber does the bulk.

## Sensible portions

**As much as you like** — cucumber is one of the rare foods with essentially no downside to eating freely, and most people should feel encouraged to snack on it without a second thought. The only thing worth a glance is what accompanies it: cucumber itself is close to calorie-free, but drowning it in creamy dressing, or leaning on heavily salted pickled versions, changes the picture (pickles in particular can be very high in sodium). Fresh cucumber with a light dressing, a squeeze of lemon, or a yogurt dip is the everyday move.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Sliced with dip** — cucumber rounds with [hummus](/mila/foods/hummus/) or a yogurt dip is a near-effortless snack.
2. **Simple cucumber salad** — with a little vinegar, salt, and dill for a refreshing side.
3. **In water** — a few slices in a jug makes plain water more appealing, which quietly helps hydration.
4. **Greek-style salad** — with tomato, olives, feta, and olive oil.
5. **Tzatziki** — grated cucumber folded into [Greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) with garlic and lemon.
6. **Sandwich and wrap filler** — adds crunch and freshness for basically no calories.
7. **Smashed cucumber** — bashed and tossed with soy, sesame, and garlic for a punchy side.
8. **Grain-bowl topping** — diced over rice or quinoa bowls for cooling crunch.

Pair cucumber with a protein or a little healthy fat and it goes from a light nibble to part of a genuinely satisfying plate.

## Who should be mindful

- **People on sodium restriction:** fresh cucumber is fine, but pickled cucumbers can be extremely high in salt — a different food to watch.
- **Sensitive digestion:** some people find raw cucumber (especially the seeds or skin) causes gas or burping; peeling, seeding, or eating smaller amounts usually helps.
- **Anyone expecting it to be a nutrient powerhouse:** cucumber is a light, hydrating vegetable, not a major vitamin source — enjoy it for what it does well and get your dense nutrition from other vegetables too.
- **Pesticide concerns:** like many thin-skinned vegetables, washing well (or choosing organic if you prefer) addresses surface residue.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Is cucumber actually nutritious or just water?**
Both are true. It's about 95% water, so it's not a dense source of vitamins — but it still offers a little fiber, potassium, vitamin K, and antioxidants, plus hydration and volume, all for almost no calories. That's a useful role, just not a "superfood" one.

**Is cucumber good for weight loss?**
No single food drives weight loss, but cucumber is close to ideal as a high-volume, ultra-low-calorie food that fills a plate and satisfies crunch cravings, making balanced meals easier.

**Should I peel cucumber?**
Ideally not — the skin holds most of the fiber and extra antioxidants. Wash it well and eat it unpeeled unless the skin is waxed or bitter.

**Are pickles as healthy as fresh cucumber?**
Not quite — pickling can add a lot of sodium (and sometimes sugar). Pickles are fine in moderation, but fresh cucumber is the everyday choice.

**Does cucumber help with hydration?**
It contributes. It's mostly water and can be a pleasant way to boost fluid intake, but it complements drinking water rather than replacing it.
