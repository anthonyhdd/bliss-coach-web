Yes — sesame seeds are a nutritious little food that adds up to more than their size suggests. A spoonful sprinkled over a stir-fry or salad brings healthy fats, minerals, some plant protein, and unique plant compounds, for very few calories. They're also the base of tahini, the creamy paste behind hummus and countless sauces. Small as they are, sesame seeds are an easy, flavorful upgrade to food you already eat.

## What's in it

A serving is about one tablespoon (roughly 9 g). Per tablespoon, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 50–55
- **Fat:** roughly 4–5 g, mostly the unsaturated kind
- **Protein:** around 1.5–2 g
- **Carbohydrates:** about 2 g, with around 1 g of fiber
- **Micronutrients:** copper, manganese, magnesium, and some calcium and iron
- **Plant compounds:** lignans (sesamin and sesamolin), studied for antioxidant activity

Sesame seeds are mineral-rich for their size, particularly copper and manganese. A worthwhile honest note on calcium: whole sesame seeds are often praised as a calcium source, and they do contribute, but much of that calcium sits in the outer hull — so hulled (white) sesame seeds have less than unhulled. It's a nice contributor to overall calcium intake rather than a substitute for major sources.

## Real benefits

**Healthy fats and minerals in a small package.** The fat is mostly unsaturated, in line with our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/), and a spoonful delivers a real share of copper and manganese — minerals involved in antioxidant defense and energy metabolism.

**Some plant protein and fiber.** Modest per tablespoon, but sesame stacks up when eaten as tahini or by the handful, and it fits naturally alongside other [plant proteins](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/) across a day.

**Lignans with a favorable research file.** Sesame is a good source of sesamin and sesamolin, plant compounds studied for antioxidant and cholesterol-related effects. As with most such findings, think "regular habit, modest effect" rather than a targeted remedy.

**Flavor that earns its place.** Toasted sesame's nutty aroma makes vegetables, grains, and proteins more appealing — and making genuinely good food taste better is one of the most underrated nutrition wins there is.

## Sensible portions

**One to two tablespoons of seeds, or about two tablespoons of tahini, per day** is a sensible pattern. Sesame seeds are calorie-dense like all seeds, but they're usually eaten in small amounts as a garnish, which keeps portions naturally modest. Toasting them lightly deepens the flavor and makes a little go further. For tahini, measure it rather than eyeballing, since a creamy paste is easy to over-serve — and check that it's basically just sesame, without a lot of added oil. Within these amounts, sesame is a smart, flavorful everyday addition.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Sprinkled over stir-fries** — toasted seeds add nutty aroma and crunch at the end of cooking.
2. **On salads and grain bowls** — a spoonful of toasted sesame lifts almost any bowl.
3. **Tahini in hummus** — the sesame paste behind [hummus](/mila/foods/hummus/) and many dips.
4. **Tahini dressing** — thinned with lemon and water into a creamy sauce for salads and roasted veg.
5. **On yogurt or oatmeal** — a spoonful over [Greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) for crunch and minerals.
6. **Coating for fish or tofu** — pressed onto the surface before searing for a crust.
7. **Into homemade bread or crackers** — baked on top for flavor and texture.
8. **Gomasio and seasonings** — toasted sesame ground with a little salt as a savory sprinkle.

## Who should be mindful

- **Sesame allergy:** sesame is now recognized as a major allergen and can cause serious reactions; anyone diagnosed must avoid it strictly, including tahini and many baked goods.
- **People watching calories closely:** sesame seeds and especially tahini are energy-dense; as a garnish they're modest, but measure tahini rather than free-pouring.
- **People on sodium restriction:** seasoned sesame blends and some tahini-based sauces add salt; check labels or season at home.
- **Anyone relying on sesame for calcium:** it's a nice contributor, but hulled seeds have less than unhulled, so treat it as part of the picture rather than a main calcium source.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Are sesame seeds a good source of calcium?**
They contribute calcium, especially unhulled (whole) seeds where much of it sits in the hull. Hulled white sesame has less. They're a nice addition to overall calcium intake rather than a replacement for major sources like dairy or fortified foods.

**Is tahini healthy?**
Yes, in sensible portions. Tahini is essentially ground sesame, so it carries the same healthy fats and minerals. Look for versions that are basically just sesame, and measure it since a creamy paste is easy to over-serve.

**Do sesame seeds have protein?**
A little per tablespoon, but it adds up when eaten as tahini or by the handful. Sesame is best thought of as a mineral-rich, healthy-fat food that also contributes some plant protein.

**Should I toast sesame seeds?**
Toasting isn't necessary nutritionally, but it deepens the nutty flavor and aroma, which makes a small amount more satisfying and helps a little go further. It's a flavor upgrade more than a nutrition one.

**How many sesame seeds should I eat a day?**
There's no required amount. One to two tablespoons of seeds, or about two tablespoons of tahini, is a sensible daily pattern — and as a garnish, portions tend to stay modest naturally.
