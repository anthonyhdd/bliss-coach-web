Yes — Brussels sprouts are one of the more nutrient-dense vegetables you can put on a plate, and their bad reputation is almost entirely a cooking problem, not a food problem. Boiled to grey mush they taste like their worst stereotype; roasted until the edges crisp they turn into something people actually fight over. Underneath either version is the same quietly impressive package: fiber, vitamin C, vitamin K, and the plant compounds that make the cabbage family interesting to researchers.

## What's in it

A typical serving is about one cup of halved sprouts (roughly 90 g), or around 5–6 whole ones. Per 100 g of cooked Brussels sprouts, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 35–45 — a full cup lands near 40–55
- **Carbohydrates:** about 7–9 g, of which roughly 3–4 g is fiber
- **Protein:** around 2–3 g — genuinely high for a vegetable
- **Fat:** essentially none
- **Vitamin C:** a large contribution — a cup can cover most of a day's needs
- **Vitamin K:** very high — sprouts are among the richer everyday sources
- **Folate, potassium, and manganese:** solid amounts across the board
- **Plant compounds:** glucosinolates — the sulfur compounds behind both the aroma and the research interest in cruciferous vegetables

Two things deserve honest framing. First, the protein-to-calorie ratio here is unusually good for a vegetable, though you'd still never call sprouts a protein source in any practical sense. Second, that faint sulfur smell when they overcook is the glucosinolates breaking down — the very compounds worth eating them for. Gentle, fast cooking keeps both the flavor and the good stuff intact.

## Real benefits

**A cruciferous vegetable with a strong research file.** Brussels sprouts belong to the same family as [broccoli](/mila/foods/broccoli/) and kale, and higher intake of cruciferous vegetables is consistently associated with better long-term health markers in large observational studies. The honest version: this is a "eat these regularly as part of a good diet" story, not a single-serving cure. But as vegetables go, the cabbage family has one of the more credible track records.

**Fiber that does real work.** Three to four grams per cup is a meaningful contribution toward a target most people miss, and it comes with the fullness and digestive benefits fiber is known for. Sprouts stack easily alongside grains and beans to push a meal's fiber higher.

**Vitamin C and K, both in serious amounts.** A single cup makes a genuine dent in vitamin C for the day and delivers a large dose of vitamin K, which matters for normal blood clotting and bone health. Few foods this cheap deliver both at once.

**Filling for very few calories.** Sprouts are mostly water and fiber, which means a generous portion costs little energetically while taking up real room on the plate — useful whenever you want a satisfying side that leans vegetable-forward.

## Sensible portions

**One to two cups (about 90–180 g) as a side** is a very reasonable everyday pattern, and there's no practical upper limit for healthy people beyond appetite and, for some, digestion. Dietary guidance broadly encourages more vegetables across the board, and sprouts are an excellent way to fill part of that. If your gut isn't used to a big load of cruciferous fiber at once, work up gradually rather than starting with a giant bowl — the discomfort some people blame on the sprouts is usually just an abrupt jump in fiber.

The single highest-leverage move is how you cook them. Roasting or pan-searing with a little [olive oil](/mila/foods/olive-oil/) until the outsides brown transforms the flavor from bitter-sulfur to nutty-sweet, and it keeps far more texture and nutrients than boiling. If you only change one thing, change the method.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Roasted, halved, cut-side down** — toss with oil and salt, roast hot until the flat sides are deep golden and the loose leaves go crispy. The default that converts skeptics.
2. **Pan-seared in a hot skillet** — faster than the oven; press the halves cut-side down and leave them alone until they brown.
3. **Shredded raw in a slaw** — thinly sliced sprouts with lemon, oil, and something salty like parmesan make a crunchy, no-cook side.
4. **With a hit of acid or sweet** — a splash of balsamic, a squeeze of lemon, or a little honey at the end balances any lingering bitterness.
5. **Crisped with chili and garlic** — high heat plus chili flakes turns a plain side into something with real edge.
6. **Added to a grain bowl** — roasted sprouts over rice or [quinoa](/mila/foods/quinoa/) with a runny egg is a quick, filling lunch.
7. **Air-fried** — same crispy result as roasting in less time, if you have the gadget.

## Who should be mindful

- **People on warfarin:** Brussels sprouts are very high in vitamin K. That doesn't mean avoiding them — it means keeping your intake of vitamin K foods roughly consistent week to week, as the general guidance for warfarin goes; talk to your care team about a stable pattern.
- **People prone to gas or bloating:** the fiber and sulfur compounds that make sprouts good can also be hard on some guts in large amounts. Smaller portions, thorough cooking, and building up gradually usually help.
- **People with thyroid conditions:** cruciferous vegetables contain compounds that can theoretically affect thyroid function in very large raw amounts, but normal cooked portions are not a concern for the vast majority of people; cooking reduces the effect further.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition far better than any article.

## FAQ

**Why do Brussels sprouts smell bad when cooked?**
Overcooking breaks down their sulfur compounds, which produces that classic aroma. Cook them fast and hot — roast or sear rather than boil — and the smell mostly disappears while the flavor turns sweet and nutty.

**Are Brussels sprouts good for weight management?**
They fit almost any approach well: high in fiber, high in water, filling, and very low in calories. A generous portion satisfies without much energy cost, which is exactly the profile most eating plans want from a vegetable.

**Are raw Brussels sprouts safe to eat?**
Yes. Thinly shredded raw sprouts make an excellent slaw. Some people find them easier to digest cooked, so choose based on your own comfort.

**Do Brussels sprouts lose nutrients when cooked?**
Some water-soluble vitamin C is lost with heat, especially in boiling water, but roasting and steaming preserve a lot — and the trade for better flavor and easier digestion is usually well worth it.

**How many Brussels sprouts should I eat?**
There's no required amount. One to two cups as a side is a great pattern, and any portion that adds a vegetable to your plate is a win.
