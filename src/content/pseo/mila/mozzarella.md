Yes — mozzarella is one of the more diet-friendly cheeses, and it doesn't need much defending. It's a good source of protein and calcium, tends to be milder and lower in sodium than many aged cheeses, and its fresh version is relatively high in moisture (and so lower in calories per bite). The honest footnotes are small: it still contains saturated fat like any cheese, and the pre-shredded bagged type isn't quite the same as a fresh ball.

## What's in it

Mozzarella comes in two broad styles — fresh (the soft, watery ball) and low-moisture (the firmer block that shreds and melts on pizza). Per 100 g of mozzarella, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 250–300 depending on type; fresh mozzarella is on the lower end
- **Protein:** roughly 18–22 g — a decent amount for a cheese
- **Fat:** about 17–22 g, including saturated fat; fresh mozzarella tends to be lower
- **Carbohydrates:** minimal, around 2–3 g
- **Calcium:** a strong, well-absorbed source
- **Sodium:** moderate — notably lower than brined cheeses like feta
- **Other nutrients:** good B12, phosphorus, and riboflavin

An honest note on the two styles: fresh mozzarella (packed in water) is higher in moisture, which means fewer calories and less sodium per gram, but it's also mild and doesn't keep long. Low-moisture mozzarella is denser, melts beautifully, and stores well, but is a touch more calorie- and salt-concentrated. Both are fine; they just suit different jobs.

## Real benefits

**A relatively gentle cheese, nutritionally.** Compared with many aged and brined cheeses, mozzarella tends to be milder in sodium and, in its fresh form, lower in calories per bite — which makes it an easy cheese to enjoy without much fuss.

**Protein and calcium in a satisfying form.** Around 18–22 g of protein per 100 g plus well-absorbed calcium make mozzarella a genuinely useful contributor to bone health and satiety, in a food most people are happy to eat.

**Makes vegetables and whole foods more appealing.** A little fresh mozzarella turns a plate of tomatoes into a meal you look forward to — cheese that nudges you toward more vegetables is doing quiet good.

**Melts and pairs endlessly.** Its mild flavor and excellent melt make it flexible across salads, bakes, and quick meals, so it earns regular, sensible use rather than being a once-in-a-while indulgence.

**Lower in sodium than many cheeses.** For anyone watching salt, mozzarella's moderate sodium — well below brined cheeses like feta or aged hard cheeses — makes it one of the easier cheeses to work into a lower-sodium pattern without much fuss.

## Sensible portions

**A portion of around 30–50 g — a couple of thick slices of a fresh ball, or a modest handful shredded — is a sensible everyday amount.** Treated as a satisfying component rather than the bulk of a meal, mozzarella fits comfortably into a balanced pattern most days.

Fresh vs low-moisture is your main lever. For salads and cold plates, fresh mozzarella's higher water content means a lighter, lower-sodium option. For melting — pizza, bakes, toasted sandwiches — low-moisture shredded is the practical choice; just note it's a bit more concentrated, so a moderate scatter goes further than a heavy blanket. Pairing mozzarella with tomatoes, greens, and whole grains keeps the overall dish balanced, which matters more than agonizing over the cheese itself. Part-skim mozzarella exists too and trims a little fat, but the full-fat version melts and tastes better; since a sensible portion is already modest, the small saving rarely justifies the trade in satisfaction.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Caprese** — fresh mozzarella, tomato, basil, and [olive oil](/mila/foods/olive-oil/); a five-minute classic.
2. **On a veggie-loaded flatbread** — a modest scatter over tomato and vegetables.
3. **Melted over baked vegetables** — a little on top of roasted [broccoli](/mila/foods/broccoli/) or peppers.
4. **In a salad** — torn fresh mozzarella over greens with tomatoes and herbs.
5. **Grilled or toasted sandwich** — with tomato and basil for a lighter melt.
6. **Stuffed into chicken** — a small amount melted inside a baked chicken breast.
7. **Mozzarella and tomato skewers** — bite-size snacks or appetizers.
8. **On homemade pizza** — where a moderate layer beats a heavy one.

## Who should be mindful

- **People managing saturated fat or heart health:** like all cheese, mozzarella contains saturated fat, so a modest portion is the sensible approach — it's a gentler cheese here than many, but not fat-free.
- **People watching sodium:** mozzarella is moderate — lower than brined cheeses — but low-moisture and pre-packaged versions can add up, so keep portions reasonable if salt is a concern.
- **Pregnant people:** mozzarella made from pasteurized milk is generally considered safe; some traditional fresh mozzarella may use unpasteurized milk, so check the label.
- **People with lactose intolerance:** fresh mozzarella contains some lactose; tolerance varies, and low-moisture aged versions tend to be lower — test your own comfort.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

### Is mozzarella one of the healthier cheeses?
It's among the more diet-friendly, yes — often milder in sodium than aged or brined cheeses, and fresh mozzarella is relatively low in calories per bite thanks to its moisture. It still has saturated fat like any cheese, so portion sense applies, but it's an easy one to enjoy.

### Is fresh mozzarella better than shredded?
For cold dishes, fresh mozzarella is lighter and lower in sodium; for melting, low-moisture shredded is more practical and stores longer. Pre-shredded bags sometimes include anti-caking agents but are still fine. Choose by the job, not by a health ranking.

### Is mozzarella good for protein?
It's a decent source — roughly 18–22 g per 100 g. It's not a primary protein food the way meat or fish is, but it's a useful contributor, especially melted over or paired with vegetables and whole grains.

### Can I eat mozzarella during pregnancy?
Mozzarella made from pasteurized milk is generally considered safe in pregnancy, and most supermarket mozzarella is pasteurized. Check the label for traditional fresh versions, and confirm specifics with your care team.
