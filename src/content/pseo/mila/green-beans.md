Yes — green beans are one of the easiest vegetables to like and to cook. They're low in calories, decent in fiber, endlessly forgiving in the kitchen, and they work fresh, frozen, or canned. Few foods give you this much "eat more vegetables" for so little effort.

## What's in it

A typical serving is about a cup cooked, roughly 125 g. Per cup, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 35–45
- **Carbohydrates:** about 8–10 g, including roughly 3–4 g fiber
- **Protein:** around 2 g
- **Fat:** essentially none
- **Micronutrients:** vitamin C, vitamin K, folate, manganese, and some vitamin A (as carotenoids)
- **Water:** very high, which is why the volume is filling for so few calories

Botanically green beans are the whole young pod, so you get a bit more fiber and a touch more protein than many other "watery" vegetables. Canned versions are nutritionally similar but often much higher in sodium — rinsing helps.

## Real benefits

**Fiber and volume for very little.** A big serving of green beans fills a real portion of the plate for a tiny calorie cost, and the fiber contributes to fullness and digestion — a painless way to nudge toward a [better daily fiber intake](/mila/blog/how-much-fiber-per-day/). This "volume eating" quality is what makes vegetables so useful in a balanced plate.

**A steady vitamin K and folate source.** Green beans deliver a meaningful amount of vitamin K (relevant to bone and blood health) and folate, without any of the prep hassle that keeps people from eating leafier greens.

**Gentle on digestion.** Compared with beans you soak and boil (the mature dried kind), green beans are far lower in the compounds that cause gas, so most people tolerate a generous plate of them easily.

**Genuinely convenient.** Frozen green beans keep for months and cook from frozen in minutes, which quietly solves the "I had no vegetables in the house" problem. Convenience is an underrated nutrition feature.

## Sensible portions

**One to two cups cooked** is a normal, generous vegetable serving, and there's no real reason to cap it — green beans are the kind of food most people should eat *more* of, not less. Fill a third to half your plate with them and you've done something genuinely good with almost no downside. The only thing to watch is what they're cooked in: a light drizzle of olive oil is great, but the classic casserole drowned in cream and fried onions is a different food. With canned beans, rinse to cut the sodium, or buy no-salt-added.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Roasted** — toss with olive oil and salt, 200°C / 400°F for 15–18 minutes until blistered; the best-tasting version by a mile.
2. **Quick sauté** — a few minutes in a hot pan with garlic; the reliable weeknight side.
3. **Steamed with lemon** — steam 4–5 minutes, finish with lemon and a little olive oil.
4. **Blanched for salads** — 2 minutes in boiling water, then cold — crisp-tender for grain bowls and salads.
5. **Stir-fried** — they hold up beautifully with soy, ginger, and sesame alongside [tofu](/mila/foods/tofu/) or chicken.
6. **From frozen, straight to the pan** — no thawing needed; dinner side in under ten minutes.
7. **With toasted nuts** — sautéed beans finished with slivered almonds add crunch and a little healthy fat.
8. **Cold, as a snack** — leftover blanched beans dipped in hummus is an easy fridge grab.

A little fat (olive oil, a few nuts) actually helps you absorb the fat-soluble vitamins in green beans, so don't fear the drizzle.

## Who should be mindful

- **People on blood thinners (warfarin):** green beans contain vitamin K, which interacts with these medications — the goal is a *consistent* intake, not avoidance, and your clinician can advise.
- **People on sodium restriction:** canned green beans can be high in salt; choose no-salt-added or rinse well, and go easy on salty casserole versions.
- **Anyone reacting to raw beans:** raw or barely-cooked green beans can cause mild stomach upset in some people; a proper cook solves it.
- **Kidney conditions with potassium limits:** portions of most vegetables may need tailoring — that's individual, "ask your dietitian" territory.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can fit green beans into a plan that suits you.

## FAQ

**Are canned green beans as healthy as fresh?**
Nutritionally they're close. The main difference is sodium — choose no-salt-added or rinse before using, and canned green beans are a perfectly good, cheap staple.

**Are frozen green beans healthy?**
Yes — frozen at peak freshness, they keep most of their nutrients and are just as good as fresh. They're one of the most convenient vegetables you can stock.

**Do green beans have protein?**
A little — around 2 g per cup. They're not a protein source, but as a vegetable they carry more than most, and they pair well with real protein foods.

**Are green beans good for weight loss?**
No food does that alone, but green beans are exactly the kind of high-volume, low-calorie, fiber-containing food that makes filling, balanced plates easier to build.

**Do I need to cook green beans?**
It's best to. Cooking improves both digestibility and taste; raw green beans can be tough and cause mild stomach upset for some people.
