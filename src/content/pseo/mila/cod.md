Yes — cod is a genuinely healthy, unfussy fish that quietly does everything a lean protein should. It's high in protein, very low in fat and calories, and a good source of B12, iodine, and selenium. The one honest caveat is expectations: cod is a lean white fish, so it isn't the omega-3 powerhouse that oily fish like salmon are. It's excellent at what it is — just not a substitute for the fatty-fish role.

## What's in it

Cod is a lean white fish, which shapes its whole profile. Per 100 g of cooked cod, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 85–100 — one of the lower-calorie proteins you can put on a plate
- **Protein:** roughly 20–23 g
- **Fat:** about 1 g — genuinely minimal
- **Carbohydrates:** none
- **Vitamin B12:** a strong source
- **Iodine:** a notable source, important for thyroid function and often under-consumed
- **Selenium and phosphorus:** solid amounts of both

The honest framing on omega-3s: cod contains some, but far less than oily fish. If omega-3s are your goal, [salmon](/mila/foods/salmon/), sardines, and mackerel are the workhorses; cod is the lean-and-light option, and that's a perfectly good thing to be. Cod liver — and cod liver oil — is a different story, concentrated in vitamin D and omega-3s, but the fillet itself is prized for leanness rather than fat.

## Real benefits

**Maximum protein, minimum everything else.** Cod delivers around 20 g of protein per 100 g with almost no fat and very few calories — which makes it one of the most efficient lean proteins available when you want to stay full without much energy cost.

**Iodine that's easy to miss elsewhere.** Iodine supports thyroid and metabolic function, and outside of seafood, dairy, and iodized salt it's not always easy to get. Cod is a reliable contributor.

**B12 and selenium in a light package.** B12 supports nerve and blood-cell health and is animal-only; selenium supports antioxidant and thyroid systems. Cod supplies meaningful amounts of both without much fat or calories.

**Mild, flexible, and beginner-friendly.** Cod's neutral flavor and firm-but-flaky texture make it forgiving to cook and easy to season any direction — a practical virtue for eating fish more often. For anyone who finds stronger, oilier fish off-putting, cod is often the gateway that gets fish onto the plate at all.

**High protein density for satiety.** With protein doing almost all of the calorie work, cod is one of the more satiating foods per calorie — a genuine advantage when you want a meal that keeps you full without weighing the plate down with energy you didn't intend to eat.

## Sensible portions

**A palm-sized fillet, roughly 120–170 g cooked**, is a normal serving and lands you a generous 25–35 g of protein for very few calories. Health authorities generally suggest a couple of servings of fish per week, and lean white fish like cod is an easy, low-mercury way to fill part of that.

How you cook it matters more than how much you eat. Baked, steamed, grilled, or pan-seared cod stays light; battered and deep-fried cod (the classic fish-and-chips route) is a different, heavier food — delicious occasionally, but no longer the lean option the fish started as. The fish is innocent; the batter and fryer are the variables.

Fresh vs frozen: frozen cod is usually frozen at sea within hours, nutritionally comparable, cheaper, and available year-round. For most home cooking it's a smart default — buy whichever keeps you eating fish. One practical tip: because cod is so lean, it can overcook and turn rubbery quickly, so pulling it off the heat the moment it flakes keeps it tender and appealing — a small technique that does more for your enjoyment than any nutrient tweak.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Baked with lemon and herbs** — the ten-minute default; foolproof.
2. **Pan-seared** — a hot pan and a little [olive oil](/mila/foods/olive-oil/) gives a golden crust in minutes.
3. **Fish tacos** — flaked cod with cabbage, lime, and yogurt sauce.
4. **In a tomato stew** — poach cod in a garlicky tomato broth; the fish soaks up flavor.
5. **Sheet-pan dinner** — cod with vegetables roasted together on one tray.
6. **Steamed with ginger and scallion** — clean, fast, and light.
7. **Fish curry** — firm cod holds up well in coconut or tomato-based curries.
8. **Chowder** — flaked into a lighter, milk-based soup with potatoes and corn.

## Who should be mindful

- **Pregnant or breastfeeding people:** good news here — cod is a low-mercury fish and is generally recommended as one of the safer choices during pregnancy in the usual couple-of-servings-a-week range. Confirm specifics with your care team.
- **People with fish or shellfish allergies:** cod is a common finfish allergen; those with a diagnosed fish allergy should avoid it.
- **Anyone relying on cod for omega-3s:** it's the wrong fish for that job — pair or alternate with oily fish like salmon or sardines to cover omega-3s.
- **Watch the preparation:** breaded, battered, and deep-fried cod turns a lean food into a heavy one; that's a cooking choice, not a knock on the fish.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

### Is cod good for weight loss?
It's one of the friendlier proteins for that goal: very high protein, very low fat and calories, and filling. Just keep the cooking method light — baked or grilled cod is lean, battered-and-fried is not.

### Is cod high in mercury?
No — cod is generally considered a lower-mercury fish, which is part of why it's often recommended in the standard couple-of-servings-a-week guidance, including during pregnancy. As always, follow current local advice for your situation.

### Does cod have omega-3s?
Some, but much less than oily fish. Cod's strengths are lean protein, iodine, and B12; for omega-3s, lean on salmon, sardines, or mackerel instead. Both roles matter — they're just different fish for different jobs.

### Is frozen cod as healthy as fresh?
Essentially yes. It's typically frozen at sea shortly after catch, keeps its protein and nutrients well, and costs less. For everyday cooking, frozen is a perfectly good — often smarter — default.
