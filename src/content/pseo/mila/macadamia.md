Yes — macadamia nuts are a nutritious, buttery-rich nut, though they're the richest and most calorie-dense of the common nuts, which makes portioning matter more here than almost anywhere. Their fat is overwhelmingly the heart-friendly monounsaturated kind, and their creamy texture makes them a genuine treat. Lower in protein than most nuts, they're best understood as a healthy-fat food you enjoy in small amounts.

## What's in it

A serving is a small handful, roughly 28–30 g (around 10–12 macadamias). Per serving, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 200–204 — the highest of the common nuts
- **Fat:** roughly 21–22 g, the vast majority monounsaturated
- **Protein:** around 2 g — low for a nut
- **Carbohydrates:** about 4 g, with a couple of grams of fiber
- **Micronutrients:** manganese, thiamine, copper, and some magnesium

Two honest points frame macadamias well. First, they're notably higher in calories and fat than almonds, pistachios, or cashews — a serving is real energy. Second, their protein is modest (around 2 g), so unlike beans or peanuts they aren't a protein play; their value is the quality of the fat and the satisfaction of the texture.

## Real benefits

**One of the richest sources of monounsaturated fat.** Macadamias are unusually high in the same heart-friendly fat that makes olive oil and avocados stand out — the kind highlighted in our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/). Diets rich in monounsaturated fat, replacing less favorable fats, are associated with better cholesterol markers.

**Deeply satisfying in small amounts.** The richness means a few macadamias feel indulgent, which is genuinely useful — a small portion can scratch a "something rich" craving that might otherwise be met by a larger, more processed snack.

**Manganese and thiamine along for the ride.** A serving contributes a solid share of manganese and some thiamine — quiet nutritional extras rather than headline reasons to eat them.

**Whole-food fat with fiber.** Unlike refined snacks, the fat here arrives with fiber and micronutrients in a whole-food package, which is part of why nuts in general have such a favorable research record.

## Sensible portions

**A small handful (about 28–30 g), and genuinely small here** — because macadamias are the most calorie-dense common nut, a portion is more like 10–12 nuts than a big grab. Pre-portioning matters more than with any other nut; a bowl or small bag beats eating from a large container. Choose unsalted or lightly salted, and steer clear of chocolate-coated or heavily flavored versions that turn them into candy. Enjoyed as a small, rich treat rather than a bulk snack, macadamias fit comfortably into a healthy pattern — the substitution mindset (a few macadamias *instead of* a processed sweet) is where they shine.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **A small measured handful** — the simplest way; portion 10–12 and you're set.
2. **Chopped over yogurt** — a few over [Greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) add richness and crunch for very little effort.
3. **Salad crunch** — toasted and chopped over grain and green salads.
4. **On oatmeal** — a small scatter over [oatmeal](/mila/foods/oatmeal/) makes the bowl feel indulgent.
5. **Crust for fish** — crushed as a coating for baked white fish or salmon.
6. **Trail mix accent** — a few among other nuts and dried fruit, used as the "treat" element.
7. **Baking** — chopped into cookies or banana bread, where a little goes a long way.
8. **Macadamia butter** — blended smooth as a rich spread for toast or fruit, in a thin layer.

## Who should be mindful

- **Tree nut allergy:** macadamias are tree nuts and can trigger serious reactions; a tree-nut allergy means strict avoidance.
- **People watching calories closely:** macadamias are the most energy-dense common nut, so portion size matters more than usual — the food is nutritious, but a small serving is the sensible move.
- **People on sodium restriction:** salted and flavored macadamias add sodium; choose unsalted.
- **Anyone avoiding added sugar:** chocolate-coated and honey-glazed macadamias are essentially candy; plain versions are the everyday choice.
- **Dog owners, as a safety aside:** macadamias are toxic to dogs — keep them out of reach even though they're perfectly fine for people.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Are macadamia nuts too high in fat to be healthy?**
No — their fat is overwhelmingly the heart-friendly monounsaturated kind, the same type prized in olive oil. They are the most calorie-dense common nut, though, so the sensible approach is a genuinely small portion rather than avoidance.

**Do macadamia nuts have protein?**
Only a little — around 2 g per serving, low for a nut. They aren't a protein food; their value is the quality of their fat and how satisfying a small amount is. For protein, other nuts and legumes are better choices.

**Are macadamia nuts good for keto or low-carb diets?**
They're popular in those diets because they're high in fat and low in carbs. That said, "high fat" still means high calories, so portion size matters regardless of the eating pattern.

**Why are macadamia nuts so expensive and rich?**
They're harder to grow and harvest than most nuts, and they're unusually high in oil, which gives them that buttery texture. A little goes a long way, which helps offset the cost.

**How many macadamia nuts should I eat a day?**
A small handful — roughly 10–12 nuts (28–30 g) — is a sensible serving. There's no strict limit for a healthy person, but their calorie density rewards a smaller portion than other nuts.
