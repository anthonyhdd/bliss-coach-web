Yes — peanuts are a nutritious, satisfying food with an unusually good protein showing for something you eat by the handful. Technically they're legumes rather than true nuts, but nutritionally they sit right alongside them: healthy fats, plant protein, and a favorable research record. They're also among the cheapest protein-rich snacks going. The one big caveat is allergy, which is serious and covered below.

## What's in it

A serving is about a small handful, roughly 28–30 g. Per serving, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 160–170
- **Fat:** roughly 14 g, mostly the heart-friendly mono- and polyunsaturated kind
- **Protein:** around 7 g — high for a snack food
- **Carbohydrates:** about 4–6 g, with a couple of grams of fiber
- **Micronutrients:** niacin, folate, vitamin E, magnesium, and manganese

That ~7 g of protein per handful is the standout — more than most true nuts — which is worth stating plainly without overclaiming: it's a genuinely useful contribution to a day's protein, especially in plant-forward eating, though peanuts are still best thought of as a healthy-fat food that also brings protein.

## Real benefits

**Protein and healthy fats in one handful.** The fat is mostly unsaturated, the kind highlighted in our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/), and it pairs with real protein to make a small handful genuinely filling. Regular nut and peanut eating is consistently linked with favorable heart outcomes in large studies.

**Strong plant protein for the price.** At around 7 g per serving, peanuts stretch a snack or meal's protein cheaply; they sit comfortably among the [high-protein plant foods](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/) worth keeping on hand.

**Satisfying enough to steady snacking.** The fat-protein-fiber trio slows digestion, so a portion of peanuts tends to hold you between meals rather than leaving you grazing — provided it's a portion rather than a bottomless bowl.

**Micronutrients along for the ride.** Peanuts contribute niacin, folate, vitamin E, and magnesium — quiet but real additions across a week of snacking.

## Sensible portions

**One small handful (about 28–30 g)** is the sensible serving, and like all nuts they're calorie-dense, so it's easy to eat well past a portion without noticing. The single best habit is to portion peanuts into a bowl or bag rather than eating from a large container. Choose dry-roasted or raw unsalted where you can — heavily salted peanuts add up on sodium, and honey-roasted versions add sugar and edge toward candy. Within a sensible portion, though, peanuts are a smart, nutrient-dense everyday choice.

Peanut butter counts too: look for versions whose only ingredients are peanuts (and maybe salt), and see [peanut butter](/mila/foods/peanut-butter/) for the full rundown on picking a good jar.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **A measured handful** — the simplest snack; portion it out and you're set.
2. **Trail mix** — with a few dried fruits and other nuts for a balanced, portable snack.
3. **Stir-fry finish** — a scatter of peanuts adds crunch and richness to noodles and vegetable stir-fries.
4. **Peanut sauce** — blended with soy, lime, and a little chili for noodles, satay, or salads.
5. **On oatmeal or yogurt** — chopped over [oatmeal](/mila/foods/oatmeal/) for crunch and staying power.
6. **Homemade peanut butter** — just blend roasted peanuts until smooth; nothing else needed.
7. **In salads** — toasted peanuts add texture to slaws and grain bowls.
8. **Energy bites** — with oats and dates for a no-bake snack.

## Who should be mindful

- **Peanut allergy:** peanut allergy is common and can be severe or life-threatening; anyone diagnosed must avoid peanuts strictly, and households managing an allergy should be careful about cross-contact.
- **People on sodium restriction:** salted and flavored peanuts can carry a lot of salt; choose raw or dry-roasted unsalted.
- **People watching calories closely:** peanuts are nutritious but energy-dense — the issue is portion size, not the food; pre-portion to keep it sensible.
- **Anyone avoiding added sugar:** honey-roasted peanuts and sweetened peanut butters are closer to treats; plain versions are the everyday choice.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Do peanuts have more protein than other nuts?**
Generally yes — around 7 g per handful, which is higher than most true nuts, reflecting that peanuts are actually legumes. That makes them a strong, affordable protein snack, though they're still best thought of as a healthy-fat food that also brings protein.

**Are peanuts healthy or fattening?**
Peanuts are nutritious and their fat is mostly the heart-friendly kind. They're energy-dense, so the sensible move is portioning rather than avoidance — a real handful satisfies, while eating from the bag adds up quickly.

**Are salted peanuts bad for you?**
Not bad, but salt adds up if peanuts are a frequent snack. Choosing unsalted or dry-roasted keeps sodium in check while giving you the same nutrition.

**Is peanut butter as healthy as whole peanuts?**
It can be, as long as the ingredients are basically just peanuts. Versions with added sugar and hydrogenated oils are less ideal; look for natural jars, and mind the portion since it's easy to over-serve.

**How many peanuts should I eat a day?**
About one small handful (28–30 g) is a sensible daily serving. There's no strict limit for a healthy person, but their calorie density rewards portioning.
