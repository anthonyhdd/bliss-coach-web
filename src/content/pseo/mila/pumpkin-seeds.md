Yes — pumpkin seeds (pepitas) are a small but mighty snack: rich in magnesium, zinc, plant protein, and healthy fats, with plenty of crunch for salads and bowls. Like all seeds they're calorie-dense, so a handful is the right idea, but within that they're one of the more nutrient-packed things you can sprinkle on food.

## What's in it

A serving is about a small handful, roughly 28–30 g (the shelled green pepitas). Per serving, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 150–160
- **Fat:** roughly 13 g, mostly unsaturated
- **Protein:** around 8–9 g — high for a seed
- **Fiber:** a couple of grams (more if you eat the whole in-shell seeds)
- **Micronutrients:** magnesium (very high), zinc, iron, phosphorus, manganese, and some potassium

Pumpkin seeds are a standout for magnesium — a single handful covers a large share of the day — and they carry more protein than most seeds and nuts, which makes them punch above their size.

## Real benefits

**One of the best magnesium sources around.** Magnesium is involved in energy, muscle and nerve function, and sleep, and many people fall a little short; a handful of pumpkin seeds is among the most efficient food ways to top it up.

**Zinc and iron in a plant food.** Pumpkin seeds are a useful plant source of zinc (immune function) and iron, which makes them especially handy for people eating fewer animal products.

**Protein and healthy fats together.** At 8–9 g of protein and mostly unsaturated fat, pepitas contribute both to fullness — a compact combination featured in our [healthy fats guide](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/) and a natural companion to other [plant proteins](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/). That trio helps a snack actually satisfy.

**Crunch that upgrades whatever you add it to.** Sprinkled on yogurt, salads, or soups, pumpkin seeds turn ordinary meals into something with texture and a nutrient boost — a small habit that quietly improves a lot of plates.

## Sensible portions

**One small handful (about 28–30 g)** is the sensible serving. Because seeds are energy-dense, they're best used as a topping or a portioned snack rather than eaten by the fistful from a big bag. Choose raw or dry-roasted unsalted where you can — heavily salted pepitas add up on sodium, and sweetened or candied versions turn a nutritious food into a treat. Within a handful, though, pumpkin seeds are a smart, everyday way to add minerals and protein to almost anything.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **A measured handful** — portioned into a bowl or bag as a standalone snack.
2. **Yogurt and oatmeal topping** — over [Greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) or oats for crunch, protein, and magnesium.
3. **Salad sprinkle** — toasted pepitas add texture and healthy fat to any green or grain salad.
4. **Soup garnish** — scattered over a squash or vegetable soup for contrast.
5. **Trail mix** — with a few nuts and dried fruit for a balanced portable snack.
6. **Toasted with spices** — dry-roasted with a little smoked paprika or cumin as a savory snack.
7. **Homemade granola or bars** — folded into oat-based bakes for a nutrient boost.
8. **Grain-bowl finish** — over rice or [quinoa](/mila/foods/quinoa/) bowls for crunch and staying power.

A light toast in a dry pan for a couple of minutes deepens the flavor and makes them even more snackable.

## Who should be mindful

- **Seed or tree nut allergies:** while distinct from tree nuts, seed allergies exist and some people react to pumpkin seeds; anyone with known seed allergy should take care.
- **People on sodium restriction:** salted and flavored pepitas can be high in salt; choose raw or dry-roasted unsalted.
- **People watching calories closely:** pumpkin seeds are nutritious but energy-dense — the point is portion size, not avoidance; pre-portion and use them as a topping.
- **Sensitive digestion:** eating very large amounts of seeds can cause bloating for some; a normal handful rarely causes issues.
- For any diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can turn these general notes into advice that fits you.

## FAQ

**Are pumpkin seeds good for you?**
Yes — they're one of the most nutrient-dense seeds, especially rich in magnesium and zinc, with solid plant protein and healthy fats. A handful a day is a smart, easy addition to most diets.

**How many pumpkin seeds should I eat a day?**
About one small handful (28–30 g) is a sensible serving. There's no strict limit for a healthy person, but their calorie density rewards portioning rather than eating from the bag.

**Are pumpkin seeds a good source of protein?**
For a seed, yes — around 8–9 g per handful. They're best viewed as a healthy-fat and mineral food that also adds useful protein to snacks and plant-based meals.

**Are raw or roasted pumpkin seeds healthier?**
Both are fine. Dry-roasting improves flavor without added oil; raw is minimally processed. The main things to check are added salt and sugar, not raw versus roasted.

**Should I eat the shell?**
You can — whole in-shell pumpkin seeds add extra fiber and are perfectly edible, just chewier. The shelled green pepitas are softer and easier to sprinkle on food.
