Yes — broccoli is about as unambiguous as nutrition gets: huge micronutrient return for almost no calories, real fiber, and a place in essentially every eating pattern ever recommended. The only honest note is that no single vegetable is magic — broccoli's benefits come from eating vegetables regularly, not from broccoli specifically being a "superfood."

The more useful question isn't whether broccoli is healthy — everyone knows it is. It's why so many of us don't eat it: decades of sad, gray, boiled-to-death broccoli. Cooked properly, it's genuinely one of the best-tasting things on the plate, and the how-to below is arguably the most valuable part of this page.

## What's in it

A typical serving is **1 cup of chopped broccoli (about 90 g raw / 150 g cooked)**:

- **Calories:** roughly 30–55 (raw vs. cooked cup)
- **Carbs:** about 6–11 g, with 2–5 g of fiber
- **Protein:** about 2.5–4 g — trivial per cup, but notably high for a vegetable
- **Fat:** essentially zero
- **Micronutrients:** the headline act — a cup of cooked broccoli covers **a full day's vitamin C and vitamin K**, plus solid folate, potassium, and some vitamin A. It also contains sulforaphane-related compounds unique to the cruciferous family (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, kale) that researchers find genuinely interesting.

Fresh and frozen broccoli are nutritional equals — frozen is often picked and flash-frozen at peak, and it's cheaper and never turns to compost in your crisper drawer. Buy whichever you'll actually cook.

## Real benefits

**Maximum nutrition per calorie.** Few foods deliver this much vitamin C, K, folate, and fiber for 30–55 calories. If you're eating fewer total calories, foods like this keep the nutrient math working.

**Volume and fullness.** Fiber and water mean broccoli takes up real room on the plate and in your stomach. Half a plate of vegetables is the oldest portion trick in the book because it works.

**Fiber your gut wants.** A couple of cups contributes meaningfully toward the daily fiber most people fall short of — with the usual benefits for digestion, cholesterol, and steady energy.

**Cruciferous-family compounds.** Population research consistently associates regular cruciferous-vegetable intake with good long-term health outcomes. That's an association across dietary patterns, not a promise from any single floret — but it's a good reason the family earns a recurring spot in your week.

**It carries meals cheaply.** Especially frozen, broccoli is one of the best value vegetables per serving — a regular in [eating healthy on a budget](/mila/blog/eating-healthy-on-a-budget).

## Sensible portions

Here's the fun part: **there's no meaningful upper portion to worry about.** One to two cups with a meal is typical; if you eat three because it was roasted well, that's a win, not a slip. Broccoli is the food category where "more" is almost always the right direction for most people.

The more useful framing is frequency: aiming for some vegetable — broccoli or otherwise — at lunch and dinner does more for your health than any perfect single serving. Rotating the vegetable keeps it from becoming a chore.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Roast it (start here):** florets, olive oil, salt, 220°C/425°F for 20–25 minutes until the edges char and crisp. This is the version that converts broccoli skeptics.
2. **Garlic pan-fry:** sauté in olive oil with sliced garlic and a squeeze of lemon, finish with parmesan.
3. **Steam-then-dress:** 4–5 minutes until bright green (not gray!), then soy sauce + sesame oil, or butter + salt.
4. **Into pasta:** drop florets into the pasta pot for the last 3 minutes, then toss everything with olive oil, garlic, and parmesan.
5. **Stir-fry standard:** broccoli + any protein + soy-garlic-ginger sauce over rice.
6. **Broccoli-cheddar soup, homemade:** blended with potato for creaminess — vegetables in comfort-food form.
7. **Raw with a dip you love:** hummus or ranch. A dip you enjoy is not cheating; it's the reason the vegetables get eaten.
8. **Frozen, microwaved, upgraded:** steam-in-bag broccoli + butter + everything bagel seasoning = a two-minute side for real life. Pairs perfectly with the no-cook lunches in [healthy snacks for work](/mila/blog/healthy-snacks-for-work).

## Who should be mindful

- **People on blood thinners (like warfarin):** broccoli is rich in vitamin K, which interacts with these medications. You don't have to avoid it — clinicians usually advise keeping vitamin K intake *consistent* — but coordinate with your doctor.
- **People with IBS or sensitive digestion:** cruciferous vegetables can cause gas and bloating for some. Cooking thoroughly helps; increase gradually rather than going from zero to two cups a day.
- **People with thyroid conditions** sometimes worry about "goitrogens" in cruciferous vegetables — at normal food amounts, cooked, this is generally a non-issue for most people, but your endocrinologist can speak to your case.

For anything medication- or condition-related, your doctor or a registered dietitian is the right authority — this page is general information, not personal advice.

## FAQ

**Is raw broccoli healthier than cooked?**
They trade wins: raw preserves more vitamin C; light cooking may make some compounds more available and makes it easier to eat more. Steamed or roasted vs. raw is a rounding error compared to eating it at all.

**Does broccoli really have protein?**
A little — and unusually much for a vegetable — but nobody should count on it as a protein source. Think of it as a bonus, not a plan.

**Is frozen broccoli as good as fresh?**
Nutritionally, yes — sometimes better, since it's frozen at peak. It's also cheaper and always ready. Zero guilt in the freezer aisle.

**How often should I eat broccoli?**
There's no prescription. A few servings of cruciferous vegetables a week is a common, reasonable pattern — mixed with whatever other vegetables you enjoy.
