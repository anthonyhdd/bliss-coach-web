Yes — bell peppers are a genuinely healthy vegetable that's easy to underrate. They're crunchy, sweet, colorful, and mostly water, which sounds like they'd be short on substance. In fact they're one of the best everyday sources of vitamin C — often out-punching oranges gram for gram — while staying very low in calories. For a vegetable this cheap and versatile, that's a strong combination.

## What's in it

A typical serving is about one cup chopped (roughly 150 g), or one medium pepper. Per 100 g of raw bell pepper, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 26–31 depending on color — very low
- **Carbohydrates:** about 6 g, of which roughly 4 g is natural sugar and about 2 g is fiber
- **Protein:** around 1 g
- **Fat:** essentially none
- **Vitamin C:** the headline — a single red pepper can supply well over a full day's needs
- **Vitamin A (as beta-carotene):** notable in red and orange peppers especially
- **Vitamin B6 and folate:** modest, steady amounts
- **Water:** roughly 92%, part of why they're so crisp and low in calories
- **Plant compounds:** carotenoids and flavonoids, with the mix shifting by color

A quick color note, since it comes up: green peppers are simply unripe versions of the red, yellow, and orange ones. As peppers ripen they get sweeter and gain more vitamin C and carotenoids — so red peppers are the most nutrient-dense, green the least, with yellow and orange in between. All of them are healthy; the differences are real but modest.

## Real benefits

**An exceptional vitamin C source.** This is bell peppers' standout feature — a red pepper can more than cover a day's vitamin C, supporting immune function, collagen formation, and iron absorption from plant foods eaten alongside. Because they're often eaten raw, that vitamin C isn't lost to cooking.

**A lot of crunch and volume for very few calories.** Peppers deliver satisfying, sweet crunch at a low calorie cost, which makes them a genuinely useful thing to snack on or bulk up a meal with.

**Carotenoids for eyes and skin.** Red and orange peppers carry carotenoids associated with eye and skin health as part of an overall colorful-vegetable pattern.

**Fiber toward a target most people miss.** A couple of grams per cup won't close the gap alone, but peppers stack easily into salads, stir-fries, and snack plates across the day.

**Versatile enough to actually get eaten.** Nutrition you'll use beats nutrition on paper, and peppers work raw, roasted, sautéed, or stuffed — which makes them easy to keep in regular rotation.

## Sensible portions

**One to two cups (or a couple of peppers) a day is a great, generous pattern** — as a vegetable, there's no meaningful upper limit for healthy people beyond appetite. Filling part of your plate with vegetables like peppers is about as uncontroversial as nutrition advice gets, and their low calorie density means you can be generous without a second thought.

Raw versus cooked: both are excellent. Raw peppers keep the most vitamin C and the crispest texture; roasting or sautéing softens and sweetens them and pairs well with a little olive oil, which helps absorb their carotenoids. There's no wrong choice — variety keeps them interesting. Store whole peppers in the fridge, where they keep crisp for a week or more.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Raw strips with a dip** — pepper batons with [hummus](/mila/foods/hummus/); the classic crunchy, satisfying snack.
2. **Roasted** — tossed with olive oil and roasted until soft and sweet; great warm or in salads.
3. **In stir-fries** — sliced peppers add color, crunch, and sweetness to almost any pan.
4. **Stuffed** — halved and filled with grains, beans, or lean protein for a full meal.
5. **In fajitas** — sautéed with onions, the classic sizzling combo.
6. **Alongside broccoli** — roast with [broccoli](/mila/foods/broccoli/) on one tray for an easy two-vegetable side.
7. **Diced into eggs** — folded into an omelet or scramble for color and crunch.
8. **In salads** — raw diced peppers add sweetness and a satisfying bite to almost any bowl.

## Who should be mindful

- **People with a nightshade sensitivity:** bell peppers are part of the nightshade family (with tomatoes, eggplant, and potatoes). Most people tolerate them well, but a minority with specific sensitivities notice they feel better limiting nightshades — worth testing your own response if you suspect it.
- **People with reflux or a sensitive gut:** raw peppers or the skins can bother some people; roasting and peeling, or eating smaller portions, often helps.
- **People who find green peppers hard to digest:** the unripe green ones are more bitter and, for some, a little harder on the stomach than the sweeter ripe colors — an easy swap if they bother you.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

**Do bell peppers really have more vitamin C than oranges?**
Often, yes. A red bell pepper can supply more vitamin C per serving than an orange, and because peppers are frequently eaten raw, little of it is lost to cooking.

**Are red, yellow, and green peppers nutritionally different?**
Yes, modestly. Green peppers are unripe and lowest in vitamin C and carotenoids; red are the ripest and most nutrient-dense, with yellow and orange in between. All are healthy — the gap is real but not dramatic.

**Are cooked bell peppers as healthy as raw?**
Both are healthy. Raw peppers keep the most vitamin C and crunch; roasting or sautéing softens and sweetens them and, with a little oil, helps you absorb their carotenoids. Variety is the best approach.

**How many bell peppers can I eat a day?**
As a low-calorie vegetable, there's no strict limit for healthy people. One to two cups a day is a great, generous pattern.

**Can I eat bell peppers if I'm watching my blood sugar?**
Yes. They're very low in calories and carbohydrate and high in water and fiber, which makes them one of the easier vegetables to eat freely.
