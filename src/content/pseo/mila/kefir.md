Yes — kefir is a genuinely nutritious fermented milk drink that delivers protein, calcium, and a diverse mix of live cultures, and it's earned its reputation more honestly than most "gut health" products. The sensible caveats are two: the probiotic-and-gut-health research, while promising, is still developing rather than settled, and flavored versions can carry a surprising amount of added sugar. Plain kefir, though, is an easy nutritional win.

## What's in it

Kefir is milk fermented with a mix of bacteria and yeasts, which gives it a tangy taste, a slightly thick pourable texture, and a broader range of live cultures than most yogurts. Per 100 ml of plain kefir, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 55–65, similar to the milk it's made from
- **Protein:** roughly 3–4 g
- **Fat:** varies with the milk used — a few grams for full-fat, near zero for low-fat versions
- **Carbohydrates:** about 4–5 g; fermentation reduces some of the lactose
- **Calcium:** a strong, well-absorbed source
- **Live cultures:** a diverse mix of bacteria and yeasts — typically more varied than yogurt
- **Other nutrients:** good B12, riboflavin, phosphorus, and often some vitamin D if fortified

The honest framing on probiotics: kefir genuinely contains a diverse live-culture population, and fermented foods are a reasonable, low-risk way to support a varied gut microbiome. But "supports gut health" is a plausible, still-developing story rather than a proven cure for anything — enjoy it as a good habit, not medicine.

## Real benefits

**A diverse dose of live cultures.** Kefir stands out from yogurt for the sheer variety of its bacteria and yeasts. Fermented foods are consistently part of dietary patterns associated with good health, and kefir is one of the easiest to consume.

**Often easier to tolerate than milk.** Fermentation breaks down some of the lactose, so many people who find plain milk uncomfortable handle kefir better — a practical way to keep dairy's calcium and protein in the diet.

**Protein and calcium in a drinkable form.** Like the milk it comes from, kefir carries quality protein and well-absorbed calcium, useful for satiety and bone health, in a format that's quick to consume — comparable in role to [greek yogurt](/mila/foods/greek-yogurt/) but pourable.

**Flexible and low-effort.** Drunk straight, blended into smoothies, or poured over oats, kefir slots into an existing routine without much thought.

**A fermented food that's easy to keep up.** Much of the value of any fermented food comes from consistency, and kefir's biggest practical edge is that a daily glass is genuinely easy to sustain — no chewing through something you dislike, no elaborate prep. The habit that survives contact with a busy week is the one that counts.

## Sensible amounts

**A glass (roughly 150–250 ml) a day is a very reasonable habit**, and it counts toward your dairy servings much like milk or yogurt. There's no need to drink large amounts to "get the benefits" — a modest daily serving of a fermented food is exactly the kind of consistent, unremarkable habit that fits the evidence.

The main thing to watch is the flavored versions. Plain kefir is tangy and low in added sugar; strawberry, vanilla, and other flavored kefirs can carry as much added sugar as a dessert drink, which quietly undoes some of the appeal. Buying plain and sweetening it yourself with fruit or a drizzle of [honey](/mila/foods/honey/) keeps you in control. If you're new to the tang, start small and mix it into a smoothie.

## Easy ways to use it

1. **Straight from the glass** — the simplest daily habit; plain and cold.
2. **In smoothies** — blended with fruit; the tang mellows and the texture turns creamy.
3. **Over oats or granola** — a pourable, probiotic alternative to milk.
4. **As a base for overnight oats** — soaks in overnight for a tangy breakfast.
5. **Sweetened your way** — plain kefir with berries and a little honey beats pre-sweetened tubs.
6. **In salad dressings** — thinned with lemon and herbs for a tangy, creamy dressing.
7. **Blended with frozen fruit** — a quick, spoonable kefir "bowl."
8. **In marinades** — its acidity tenderizes chicken much like buttermilk.

## Who should be mindful

- **People with a milk (dairy) allergy:** kefir is made from milk, so a true dairy allergy means avoiding it — water kefir and some non-dairy versions exist, but read labels.
- **People who are lactose intolerant:** kefir is usually better tolerated than milk because fermentation lowers the lactose, but it's not lactose-free — test your own comfortable amount.
- **Anyone watching added sugar:** flavored kefirs can be surprisingly sugary; plain is the everyday default, sweetened at home if you like.
- **People who are immunocompromised:** live-culture foods are safe for most, but anyone with a significantly weakened immune system should check with their doctor before adding live-microbe foods.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

### Is kefir better than yogurt?
They're close, and both are excellent. Kefir tends to have a more diverse range of live cultures and a drinkable texture; yogurt is thicker and often higher in protein per serving (especially Greek). Neither is clearly superior — variety across fermented foods is the real win.

### Does kefir actually improve gut health?
It's a reasonable, low-risk way to add diverse live cultures and fermented food to your diet, which fits patterns associated with good health. But the science is still developing, so think "sensible habit with plausible benefit," not a guaranteed fix for digestive problems.

### Can lactose-intolerant people drink kefir?
Often, yes — fermentation breaks down much of the lactose, so many people tolerate kefir better than plain milk. It isn't fully lactose-free, though, so start with a small amount and see how you feel.

### Is store-bought kefir healthy, or do I need to make my own?
Store-bought plain kefir is perfectly healthy and convenient. Homemade lets you control ingredients and can be cheaper, but it's optional. The bigger factor is choosing plain over heavily sweetened flavored versions.
