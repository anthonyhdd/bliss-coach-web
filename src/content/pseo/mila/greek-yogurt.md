Yes — plain Greek yogurt is one of the easiest wins in the dairy aisle: unusually high in protein, generally well tolerated, and endlessly flexible. The main catch is that "Greek yogurt" on a label spans everything from plain nonfat to dessert-sweet flavored cups, and those are quite different foods.

## What's in it

A typical serving is about 170–200 g (a single-serve cup or roughly ¾ cup). For **plain** Greek yogurt, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 100 (nonfat) to 170–200 (full-fat/5%)
- **Protein:** roughly 15–20 g — about double regular yogurt, because straining removes whey and concentrates the rest
- **Carbohydrates:** about 5–8 g, naturally occurring lactose (this is not added sugar, even though it appears on the sugar line)
- **Fat:** from near zero (nonfat) up to about 9–11 g (full-fat), mostly saturated
- **Micronutrients:** calcium (a bit less than regular yogurt due to straining, but still meaningful), vitamin B12, phosphorus, selenium, and potassium
- **Live cultures:** most brands contain live active bacterial cultures; look for the "live and active cultures" note on the tub

Flavored versions can add 10–18 g of added sugar per cup — sometimes more than a scoop of ice cream — so the plain-vs-flavored choice changes the picture more than the brand does.

## Real benefits

**Serious protein for minimal effort.** Fifteen to twenty grams per cup, no cooking required, makes Greek yogurt one of the most convenient protein sources that exists — it anchors several entries in our [high-protein foods guide](/mila/blog/high-protein-foods/). Protein at breakfast and snacks is strongly tied to feeling full longer, which quietly makes the rest of the day easier.

**Gut-friendly cultures.** Fermented dairy provides live bacteria, and regular fermented-food intake is associated with a healthier gut microbiome. The honest caveat: strains and amounts vary by brand, and yogurt isn't a treatment for any condition — it's simply a reasonable, food-first way to include fermented foods.

**Easier on lactose.** The straining process and bacterial fermentation reduce lactose, so many people with mild lactose intolerance handle Greek yogurt better than milk. Not everyone — but it's worth a careful test if regular dairy bothers you.

**Bone-supporting nutrients.** Calcium, phosphorus, and protein together support bone maintenance, which matters at every age, not just childhood.

**Full-fat isn't the villain it was.** Evidence on fermented full-fat dairy has grown more neutral-to-favorable over the years. Nonfat, 2%, and full-fat are all legitimate choices — pick based on satisfaction and your overall diet rather than fear.

## Sensible portions

**One cup (about 170–200 g) once a day** is a very normal serving, and there's nothing wrong with a second one on days it fits. If you're using it as a full meal (breakfast bowl), 250–300 g with toppings is reasonable. The thing to keep loosely in mind isn't the yogurt itself but the toppings arithmetic — granola, honey, and nut butter are all fine, and they also add up fast. A useful pattern: let fruit provide most of the sweetness, and treat the crunchy or drizzly things as accents.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Berry bowl** — plain yogurt, frozen berries (they thaw and make their own sauce), a spoon of toasted oats or granola.
2. **Savory dip** — mix with lemon, garlic, and salt for an instant tzatziki-style dip for vegetables or pita.
3. **Sour cream stand-in** — dollop on chili, tacos, or baked potatoes; most people can't tell the difference.
4. **Smoothie base** — makes any smoothie creamier and genuinely filling instead of just sweet.
5. **Overnight oats upgrade** — half yogurt, half milk with your oats doubles the protein.
6. **Marinade** — yogurt-marinated chicken (with spices and lemon) comes out remarkably tender.
7. **Desk snack** — a single-serve cup plus a handful of nuts is one of the sturdiest afternoon snacks there is; more combos in our [healthy snacks for work](/mila/blog/healthy-snacks-for-work/) roundup.
8. **Dessert-ish** — plain yogurt with a square of dark chocolate chopped over it and a little honey scratches the evening-sweet itch.

## Who should be mindful

- **Milk allergy:** different from lactose intolerance — a true dairy allergy means avoiding Greek yogurt entirely.
- **Significant lactose intolerance:** many tolerate Greek yogurt well, but not all. Lactose-free and high-protein plant-based yogurts exist if it doesn't sit right.
- **People watching added sugar:** flavored cups are the main trap; the plain version plus your own fruit solves it.
- **People on potassium-restricted diets or with specific kidney conditions:** dairy portions may need tailoring — this is firmly "ask your dietitian" territory.
- As always, if you're managing a diagnosed condition, a registered dietitian or your doctor can fit yogurt into *your* plan better than any article can.

## FAQ

**Greek yogurt vs regular yogurt — which is healthier?**
Neither is "bad." Greek has roughly double the protein and a thicker texture; regular retains a bit more calcium and costs less. Pick the one you'll actually eat plain.

**Is nonfat or full-fat better?**
Both are fine choices. Nonfat maximizes protein per calorie; full-fat is more satisfying to many people. Consistency beats optimization here.

**Does the sugar on the label mean added sugar?**
Not necessarily — plain yogurt's 5–8 g is natural lactose. Check the "added sugars" line to see what was actually put in.

**Can I cook with Greek yogurt?**
Yes, though high direct heat can make it split. Stir it in off the heat, or temper it first in sauces.
