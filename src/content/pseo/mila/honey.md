Honey is a lovely, natural sweetener — and nutritionally, it's still mostly sugar, so the honest answer is "healthy in the way any sugar is: fine in small amounts, not a health food in large ones." The good news is that you don't need honey to be a superfood to enjoy it; you just need realistic expectations and a spoon, not a ladle.

## What's in it

One tablespoon of honey (about 21 g) contains roughly:

- **Calories:** about 60–65
- **Sugars:** around 16–17 g, mostly fructose and glucose
- **Everything else:** trace amounts of antioxidants (polyphenols), tiny quantities of minerals and enzymes, and small amounts of pollen compounds

Those trace compounds are real — darker honeys like buckwheat generally carry more of them — but they exist in milligram quantities. To get a meaningful dose of antioxidants from honey, you'd have to eat so much of it that the sugar would outweigh any benefit. Berries, tea, coffee, and vegetables deliver far more antioxidants per calorie.

Compared with white sugar, honey is slightly sweeter per gram (so you may use a little less), has a marginally gentler effect on blood sugar for some people, and tastes vastly better. Metabolically, your body treats both as sugar.

## Real benefits

**It's a more satisfying sweetener.** Because honey has actual flavor — floral, caramel, sometimes almost savory — a small amount does more sensory work than plain sugar. That often means less total sweetener for the same pleasure, which is quietly the most useful "health benefit" it has.

**Evidence-backed cough relief.** This one is legitimate: studies support a spoonful of honey for soothing coughs in adults and children over one year old, and it's a recommended home remedy in several pediatric guidelines.

**It carries trace antioxidants.** Modest, as noted above — a nice bonus, not a reason to eat more.

**It makes healthy food taste better.** Drizzled on plain yogurt, oatmeal, or roasted vegetables, a teaspoon of honey can be the difference between a food you tolerate and a food you look forward to. Sweeteners that help you enjoy nourishing meals are doing useful work.

## Sensible portions

Honey counts toward added sugar, and general guidance (like the American Heart Association's) suggests keeping *all* added sugars to roughly 25 g a day for women and 36 g for men — about 1.5 to 2 tablespoons of honey if it were your only source.

In practice:

- **1–2 teaspoons at a time** is the sweet spot for tea, yogurt, or toast.
- **A tablespoon in a recipe that serves several people** barely registers per portion.
- If you're also drinking sweetened coffee, soda, or eating desserts, honey shares that same daily budget — it doesn't get a separate "natural" allowance.

## Easy ways to eat it

- **In tea or warm (not boiling) water** with lemon — the classic, especially with a scratchy throat.
- **Drizzled over Greek yogurt with nuts** — a genuinely balanced snack where a teaspoon goes far.
- **On oatmeal or porridge** instead of packets of flavored instant oats (you'll use less sugar than the packet contains).
- **In salad dressings:** honey + mustard + olive oil + vinegar is a fridge staple.
- **Glazing roasted carrots, salmon, or halloumi** — a teaspoon caramelizes beautifully.
- **On good bread with butter** when you simply want something delicious. That's allowed.

Runny, set, raw, or supermarket-standard: choose by taste and price. "Raw" honey keeps a few more of those trace compounds but isn't meaningfully healthier at normal portions.

## Who should be mindful

- **Babies under 12 months must never have honey** — it can contain botulism spores that infant guts can't handle. This is the one hard rule on this page.
- **People with diabetes** should treat honey like any other sugar when counting carbs — "natural" doesn't change its effect on blood glucose.
- **Anyone actively reducing added sugar** should count honey in the same budget as syrup and sugar, not in a separate virtuous column.
- **Pollen-allergic individuals** occasionally react to raw honey.

If you're managing diabetes, prediabetes, or any condition where sugar intake matters, your doctor or a registered dietitian can help you find the amount that fits your treatment plan — that beats guessing.

## FAQ

**Is honey healthier than sugar?**
Marginally at best. It tastes better and is slightly sweeter, so you might use less — that's the real advantage. Body-wise, both are added sugar.

**Does honey help with weight loss?**
No sweetener causes weight loss. Honey can *support* pleasant, sustainable eating in small amounts — and if sweet cravings hit hardest at night, our guide on [how to stop late-night snacking](/mila/blog/how-to-stop-late-night-snacking/) tackles the actual cause.

**Is raw honey worth the extra money?**
For flavor, often yes. For health, the difference at teaspoon-sized portions is negligible.

**How much honey per day is okay?**
For most healthy adults, a tablespoon or so within an otherwise low-added-sugar day is entirely reasonable.
