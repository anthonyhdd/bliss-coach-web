Yes — potatoes are a healthy food, and few foods have been more unfairly maligned. They became a symbol of "carbs are bad" and got tangled up with fries and chips, but a plain potato is a whole, minimally processed food with potassium, vitamin C, fiber, and surprising staying power. The truth diet culture skips is simple: what usually makes potatoes unhealthy isn't the potato — it's the deep-frying oil, the salt, and the loaded toppings. The vegetable underneath is genuinely good.

## What's in it

A typical serving is one medium potato (roughly 150–170 g) with the skin on. Per 100 g of cooked potato with skin, expect approximately:

- **Calories:** around 85–90 — a medium potato lands near 130–160
- **Carbohydrates:** about 20 g, of which roughly 2 g is fiber and most of the rest is starch
- **Protein:** around 2 g — modest but with a good amino acid profile for a plant
- **Fat:** essentially none in the potato itself
- **Potassium:** a standout — potatoes are among the better everyday sources, often more per serving than a banana
- **Vitamin C:** a genuinely meaningful amount
- **Vitamin B6:** a solid contribution
- **Resistant starch (when cooled):** forms when cooked potatoes are chilled, feeding gut bacteria

The honest framing potatoes most need is a separation of the food from its usual delivery. A baked or boiled potato is nutrient-dense and filling; in fact, plain boiled potatoes rank among the most satiating foods ever tested. The problems people associate with potatoes — fries, crisps, greasy hash browns — come from what's added, not from the potato. Keep the skin on and you also keep most of the fiber and a good share of the nutrients.

## Real benefits

**Potassium in an amount that actually matters.** Potatoes are one of the better whole-food potassium sources, and potassium supports normal blood pressure and fluid balance — a mineral most people fall short on. A single potato makes a real contribution.

**Vitamin C from an unexpected place.** People rarely think of potatoes as a vitamin C food, but they carry a genuinely useful amount, and because portions are large, it adds up. Not eating them fried helps preserve it.

**Filling for the calories — genuinely.** Potatoes are famously satiating, which makes them a smart base for a meal if fullness is your goal. Prepared simply and paired with protein and vegetables, a potato anchors a satisfying plate without needing much energy density.

**Resistant starch when cooled.** Cook potatoes, chill them, and some of their starch converts to resistant starch — a fiber-like compound that feeds gut bacteria. Potato salad made from cooled boiled potatoes is a real, if underappreciated, gut-friendly food. A little [olive oil](/mila/foods/olive-oil/) in the dressing keeps it balanced.

## Sensible portions

**One medium potato, or a fist-sized portion, as your starch** is a very reasonable everyday amount. Potatoes are a starchy food, so they belong in the same mental slot as rice, pasta, or bread — a carbohydrate base, not a "free" vegetable — and portioning them that way handles most concerns. If you're managing blood sugar, note that potatoes do raise it fairly readily, but keeping the skin on, cooling and reheating them, and pairing them with protein and fat all soften that.

The single biggest lever isn't quantity — it's preparation. Baked, boiled, roasted, or mashed with restraint, potatoes are a wholesome food. Deep-fried into chips or fries, drowned in butter, or loaded with cheese and bacon, they become an occasional treat. Same vegetable, very different meal. If you want potatoes to be everyday food, cook them gently and keep the additions modest. For a different nutrient profile, [sweet potato](/mila/foods/sweet-potato/) is a lovely rotation, though ordinary potatoes are in no way inferior.

## Easy ways to eat it

1. **Baked whole, skin on** — the effortless classic; split it open and top with something protein-forward like beans, yogurt, or tuna.
2. **Boiled and buttered** — new potatoes boiled and tossed with herbs and a little butter, a two-ingredient side.
3. **Roasted in wedges** — tossed with a modest amount of oil and roasted until crisp-edged; far better than fries, barely any effort.
4. **Simple mash** — with milk and a little butter; use a light hand and it's a wholesome comfort food.
5. **Potato salad from cooled potatoes** — boiled, chilled, and dressed, which also builds resistant starch.
6. **Smashed and crisped** — boil, flatten, then roast for crispy edges and a fluffy middle.
7. **In soups and stews** — where they thicken and satisfy without any added starch.
8. **As breakfast potatoes** — cubed and pan-roasted with onion, a hearty base for eggs.

## Who should be mindful

- **People managing blood sugar or carbohydrates:** potatoes raise blood sugar fairly readily, so count them as a starch and portion accordingly. Skin-on, cooled-and-reheated, and protein-paired versions are gentler; your care team's plan wins.
- **Anyone watching what's added, not the potato itself:** the calories and downsides usually live in frying oil, butter, and loaded toppings. Watch those before you worry about the potato.
- **People who see green patches or sprouts:** green skin and sprouts contain solanine, which can be mildly toxic in quantity. Cut away green parts and sprouts, and store potatoes cool and dark; discard any that are extensively green or shriveled.
- As always, a registered dietitian or your doctor can adapt these general notes to a diagnosed condition better than any article.

## FAQ

**Are potatoes fattening?**
No food is inherently fattening. Plain potatoes are filling and nutrient-dense — among the most satiating foods tested. Weight concerns come from frying, butter, and loaded toppings, not from the potato itself.

**Are potatoes bad for blood sugar?**
They do raise blood sugar fairly readily, so if you're managing it, treat potatoes as a starch and portion them. Keeping the skin on, cooling and reheating them, and eating them with protein and fat all help slow the rise.

**Is the skin worth eating?**
Yes — the skin holds much of the potato's fiber and a good share of its nutrients. Scrub it and leave it on for baked, boiled, and roasted potatoes to get the most from them.

**Are sweet potatoes healthier than regular potatoes?**
They have different strengths — sweet potatoes are richer in beta-carotene, regular potatoes higher in potassium and vitamin C. Neither is superior; rotating between them gives you the best of both.

**How often can I eat potatoes?**
As a regular part of a varied diet, cooked simply, potatoes are everyday food. Portion them like any starch, keep preparations gentle, and there's no reason to limit them beyond that.
