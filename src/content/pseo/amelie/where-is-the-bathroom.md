Where is the bathroom in French is **où sont les toilettes ?** — maybe the single most practically urgent phrase a traveler can own. There's a grammar quirk (*toilettes* is always plural), a big cultural trap (never ask for the *salle de bain* in a restaurant), and a polite framing that turns a blunt demand into something a Parisian waiter will happily answer. Here's how to ask — and understand the directions you get back.

## The main way to ask it

**Où sont les toilettes ?** — pronounced **oo sohn lay twah-LET**.

Pronunciation notes:

- *Où* is a simple "oo."
- *Sont* is nasal — "sohn" — the *t* stays silent.
- *Toilettes* is **twah-LET**, stress on the last syllable, both *t*'s crisp. Note it's *always plural* — les toilette**s** — so the verb is *sont* ("are"), not *est*.

### The polite version (use this)

Walking up and barking *où sont les toilettes ?* is understood but abrupt. Frame it politely:

- **Excusez-moi, où sont les toilettes, s'il vous plaît ?** — "Excuse me, where's the bathroom, please?" The complete, courteous ask.
- **Pardon, les toilettes, s'il vous plaît ?** — shorter but still polite; the *s'il vous plaît* carries the manners.
- **Vous auriez des toilettes ?** — "would you have a restroom?" (asking a café whether they *have* one).

### The vocabulary trap

- **les toilettes** — the correct word for a public/restaurant restroom. Always your safest choice.
- **les WC** (say "vay-say" or "double-vay-say") — also common, from English "water closet."
- **les cabinets** — older, still heard.
- **la salle de bain** — this means a **bathroom with a bath/shower**, i.e. a *room in a home*. Asking for the *salle de bain* in a restaurant sounds like you want to take a shower. Don't.
- **le petit coin** — cute euphemism, "the little corner," casual/family use.

## Understanding the directions

The answer usually points you somewhere. Key words:

- **au fond** — at the back (*au fond à droite* = "at the back on the right").
- **à droite / à gauche** — on the right / on the left.
- **tout droit** — straight ahead (careful: *tout droit* = straight ahead, *à droite* = to the right).
- **en bas / en haut** — downstairs / upstairs (French café toilets are famously often *en bas*, down a tight spiral staircase).
- **au sous-sol** — in the basement.
- **derrière** — behind (*derrière le bar* = behind the bar).

## Hear it in context

**In a café (polite):**

> — Excusez-moi, où sont les toilettes, s'il vous plaît ?
> — Au fond à gauche, puis c'est en bas.
> — Merci beaucoup !

*Excuse me, where's the bathroom, please? — At the back on the left, then it's downstairs. — Thank you very much!*

**Asking a passerby (urgent but polite):**

> — Pardon, vous savez où sont les toilettes publiques ?
> — Il y en a près du parc, à droite après le kiosque.
> — Merci, vous me sauvez !

*Excuse me, do you know where the public toilets are? — There are some near the park, on the right past the newsstand. — Thanks, you're a lifesaver!*

## Common mistakes

- **Asking for the *salle de bain* in public.** That's a home bathroom with a tub. In a café or restaurant, always *les toilettes* or *les WC*.
- **Making *toilettes* singular.** It's plural: *où **sont** les toilettes* (not *où est la toilette*). *La toilette* singular means grooming/washing up, a different thing.
- **Skipping the politeness.** A bare *où sont les toilettes ?* can sound demanding. Lead with *excusez-moi* or close with *s'il vous plaît*.
- **Confusing *tout droit* and *à droite*.** *Tout droit* = straight ahead; *à droite* = to the right. They sound similar and send you two different places.
- **Expecting free public toilets everywhere.** Many café toilets are for customers; Paris does have free self-cleaning street toilets (*sanisettes*), but plan ahead.

## A Parisian note

In Paris, café and restaurant toilets are generally reserved for **paying customers** — the unwritten rule is you buy something (even just *un café* at the bar, the cheapest option) before using them. Many are tucked *en bas*, down a narrow, winding staircase, and some older ones still surprise tourists with their compact, no-frills design. Public *sanisettes* (the gray self-cleaning street toilets) are free and dotted around the city. And a small courtesy that goes a long way: greet the waiter with *bonjour* before asking — in France the greeting opens every request, and a *"Bonjour, excusez-moi, les toilettes s'il vous plaît ?"* gets a far warmer response than diving straight in.

## FAQ

**Why is "toilettes" always plural in French?**
It's simply fixed as a plural noun (*les toilettes*), so the verb agrees: *où **sont** les toilettes ?*. The singular *la toilette* exists but means "washing/grooming," not the room — so always use the plural for the restroom.

**Can I say "salle de bain" for a restaurant restroom?**
No — *salle de bain* means a bathroom with a bath or shower, i.e. a room in a home. In a café or restaurant, ask for *les toilettes* or *les WC*. Asking for the *salle de bain* sounds like you want to bathe.

**What's the polite way to ask where the bathroom is?**
*Excusez-moi, où sont les toilettes, s'il vous plaît ?* Leading with *excusez-moi* and closing with *s'il vous plaît* turns a blunt question into a courteous one.

**Do I have to buy something to use a café toilet in Paris?**
Usually yes — café and restaurant toilets are for paying customers. Ordering something small at the bar (like *un café*) is the norm. Free public *sanisettes* are also available on the street.

**How do I understand the directions I get?**
Listen for *au fond* (at the back), *à droite / à gauche* (right/left), *tout droit* (straight ahead), and *en bas / au sous-sol* (downstairs/basement) — many Paris café toilets are down a spiral staircase.
