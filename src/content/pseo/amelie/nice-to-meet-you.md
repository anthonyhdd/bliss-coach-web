Nice to meet you in French is famously **enchanté** — literally "enchanted," which sounds far more romantic than the plain English version. It's the one-word reflex the French reach for the instant they shake a new hand. But there's a spelling twist that trips up learners (it changes depending on *who's speaking*), and a couple of fuller, more formal alternatives worth having ready for a first day at work or a business dinner in Paris.

## The main way to say it

**Enchanté** — pronounced **ahn-shahn-TAY**.

Here's the twist: the ending changes to agree with **the speaker's** gender.

- A man says **enchanté** (ends in a clean "-ay" sound).
- A woman says **enchantée** — spelled with an extra *e*, but pronounced *exactly the same*: ahn-shahn-TAY.

So there's no difference to your ear, only to the pen. If you're writing it, match your own gender.

Pronunciation notes:

- Both *-an-* syllables are **nasal** — *ahn-shahn* — with the air resonating through the nose, not a hard "an."
- The **ch** is a soft "sh," as in "shoe," never the hard English "ch."
- Stress the final syllable: ahn-shahn-**TAY**.

### Fuller, more formal options

*Enchanté* on its own is warm and universal, but in professional or elegant settings you can expand it:

- **Enchanté de faire votre connaissance** — "delighted to make your acquaintance." Polished, formal.
- **Ravi de vous rencontrer** (ra-VEE duh voo rahn-KOHN-tray) — "delighted to meet you." A common, slightly formal alternative; a woman writes *ravie*.
- **Content de vous rencontrer** — "happy to meet you." Simpler, friendly.
- **Je suis heureux / heureuse de vous rencontrer** — "I'm happy to meet you." Warm and complete.

Note the *vous* in these: meeting someone new almost always calls for formal *vous*. Only switch to *te rencontrer* if you're clearly among peers your own age in a casual setting.

## Hear it in context

**A professional first meeting (formal):**

> — Bonjour, je suis Camille Durand, la nouvelle responsable marketing.
> — Enchanté, Camille. Ravi de vous rencontrer. Bienvenue dans l'équipe.
> — Merci beaucoup, enchantée également.

*Hello, I'm Camille Durand, the new marketing manager. — Nice to meet you, Camille. Delighted to meet you. Welcome to the team. — Thank you so much, nice to meet you too.*

**A casual introduction through a friend:**

> — Léa, je te présente Marco, un ami d'enfance.
> — Salut Marco, enchantée !
> — Enchanté ! Léa m'a beaucoup parlé de toi.

*Léa, this is Marco, a childhood friend. — Hi Marco, nice to meet you! — Nice to meet you! Léa's told me a lot about you.*

## Common mistakes

- **Worrying about the *-e* out loud.** *Enchanté* and *enchantée* sound identical. The extra *e* only matters when you write it — and it agrees with the speaker, not the listener.
- **Adding "vous" incorrectly.** It's not *enchanté vous* or *enchanté de vous*. Say *enchanté* alone, or the full *enchanté de faire votre connaissance*.
- **Using *tu* too fast.** Meeting someone new defaults to *vous*. Jumping straight to *tu* with an older person or in a work context can feel over-familiar.
- **Hardening the nasal vowels.** "En-chan-tay" with flat English vowels loses the whole sound. Let *ahn-shahn* hum through your nose.
- **Reserving it only for goodbyes.** Unlike English "nice to meet you" (often said when parting), *enchanté* is said at the *start* of the introduction, on the handshake.

## A Parisian note

The French introduction is a compact ritual: eye contact, a firm-but-not-crushing handshake, and *"Enchanté"* — all in one beat. Among friends and in social (non-work) settings, the handshake is often replaced by **la bise**, the cheek kisses, even on a first meeting. Reading which one is expected is a real Parisian skill: colleagues and strangers get a handshake; a friend-of-a-friend at a dinner party often gets *la bise*. When unsure, extend your hand and let the other person upgrade to *la bise* if they lean in. And always pair the intro with *bonjour* first — walking up and launching straight into *enchanté* skips the opening the French consider non-negotiable.

## FAQ

**Is it "enchanté" or "enchantée"?**
Both — the spelling agrees with the *speaker's* gender. A man writes *enchanté*, a woman writes *enchantée*. They're pronounced identically (ahn-shahn-TAY), so the difference only shows up in writing.

**What does "enchanté" literally mean?**
"Enchanted" or "charmed." It's a short form of an older phrase meaning "delighted to make your acquaintance," and it's the standard French way to say "nice to meet you."

**Is "enchanté" formal or casual?**
It works in both. It's polite enough for a business meeting and natural enough for a casual introduction. For extra formality, expand it to *enchanté de faire votre connaissance*.

**What can I say instead of "enchanté"?**
*Ravi(e) de vous rencontrer* ("delighted to meet you"), *content(e) de vous rencontrer* ("happy to meet you"), or *enchanté de faire votre connaissance* are all natural alternatives.

**Do I shake hands or do "la bise" when meeting someone?**
In professional settings, a handshake with *enchanté*. In casual social settings among friends-of-friends, *la bise* (cheek kisses) is common, even on a first meeting. When unsure, offer a handshake and follow the other person's lead.
