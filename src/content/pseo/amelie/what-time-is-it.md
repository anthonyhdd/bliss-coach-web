What time is it in French is **quelle heure est-il ?** — a phrase you'll use constantly and hear even more. But there's a formal version (the textbook one) and a much more common casual version French people actually say, plus a whole system for reading the answer that leans on the 24-hour clock. Master the question *and* how to understand the reply, and you'll navigate every train, meeting, and dinner reservation in Paris.

## The main way to ask it

**Quelle heure est-il ?** — pronounced **kel UHR eh-TEEL**.

Pronunciation notes:

- *Quelle* is "kel," clean and short.
- *Heure* ("hour") is **UHR** — the *h* is silent and the *eu* is that rounded French vowel, lips forward.
- *Est-il* links together: eh-**TEEL**, with the *t* pronounced to bridge the words (this liaison is what makes it flow).

### The casual version (what people really say)

In everyday speech, the inversion *est-il* feels a little formal. Far more common:

- **Il est quelle heure ?** (eel eh kel UHR) — same meaning, relaxed word order. This is the everyday default.
- **Tu as l'heure ?** (tew ah LUHR) — "do you have the time?" (informal *tu*).
- **Vous avez l'heure ?** (voo-zah-vay LUHR) — "do you have the time?" (formal *vous*). The polite way to ask a stranger.
- **Excusez-moi, vous auriez l'heure ?** — extra-polite, "would you happen to have the time?"

## How to read the answer

The reply always starts with **il est…** ("it is…"):

- **Il est une heure** — it's one o'clock.
- **Il est deux heures** — it's two o'clock (note: *heures* plural after 2+).
- **Il est trois heures et quart** — quarter past three (*et quart*).
- **Il est trois heures et demie** — half past three (*et demie*).
- **Il est quatre heures moins le quart** — quarter to four (*moins le quart* = "minus the quarter").
- **Il est cinq heures dix** — ten past five.
- **Il est six heures moins dix** — ten to six.

### The 24-hour clock

Officially — for schedules, trains, TV, appointments, invitations — France uses the **24-hour clock**:

- **quatorze heures** = 14:00 = 2 p.m.
- **vingt heures trente** = 20:30 = 8:30 p.m.

For noon and midnight there are special words: **midi** (noon) and **minuit** (midnight). *Il est midi et demi* = 12:30 p.m. (note: *demi* without the *-e* after *midi/minuit*, because they're masculine).

## Hear it in context

**Asking a stranger on the street (polite):**

> — Excusez-moi, vous avez l'heure, s'il vous plaît ?
> — Oui, il est quinze heures dix.
> — Merci beaucoup, bonne journée !

*Excuse me, do you have the time, please? — Yes, it's 3:10 p.m. — Thank you very much, have a good day!*

**Checking with a friend (casual):**

> — Il est quelle heure ? On va être en retard.
> — Attends… il est huit heures moins le quart.
> — Ah non, faut y aller !

*What time is it? We're going to be late. — Hang on… it's quarter to eight. — Oh no, we've got to go!*

## Common mistakes

- **Forgetting the plural *heures*.** It's *une heure* (singular) but *deux heures*, *trois heures*, etc. (plural). The *s* is silent but the spelling matters.
- **Saying "kwel" for *quelle*.** It's "kel" — no *w* sound.
- **Missing the liaison in *est-il*.** The linking *t* (eh-**T**EEL) is what makes the formal version sound right.
- **Confusing *et demie* and *demi*.** After a feminine *heure* it's *demie* (*trois heures et demie*); after masculine *midi/minuit* it's *demi* (*midi et demi*).
- **Reading *moins le quart* backward.** *Cinq heures moins le quart* is "quarter *to* five" (4:45), not quarter past. *Moins* means "minus."

## A Parisian note

Parisians run on the 24-hour clock for anything scheduled — a dinner invite for *"vingt heures trente"*, a train at *"dix-huit heures douze."* Getting comfortable reading 14:00 as "deux heures de l'après-midi" is essential for catching the right TGV. Socially, note the French elasticity around time: an invitation for 20h often means guests drift in around 20h15–20h30 (the polite *quart d'heure de politesse*), and showing up *exactly* on time to a dinner can even catch the host mid-preparation. Business, trains, and the theater, though, start on the dot — *à l'heure* means *à l'heure*.

## FAQ

**How do you ask "what time is it?" casually in French?**
The everyday casual version is *il est quelle heure ?* Even more relaxed: *tu as l'heure ?* (informal) or *vous avez l'heure ?* (polite, for strangers). The textbook *quelle heure est-il ?* is correct but sounds a touch formal.

**Do the French use a 12- or 24-hour clock?**
Both. In casual speech they often use 12-hour time (with *du matin*, *de l'après-midi*, *du soir* to clarify). But all schedules, invitations, trains, and appointments use the 24-hour clock — *quatorze heures* for 2 p.m., *vingt heures* for 8 p.m.

**How do I say "half past" and "quarter to"?**
"Half past" is *et demie* (*trois heures et demie* = 3:30). "Quarter past" is *et quart*. "Quarter to" is *moins le quart* (*quatre heures moins le quart* = 3:45).

**How do you say noon and midnight?**
*Midi* is noon (12:00) and *minuit* is midnight (00:00). For half past, use *demi* without the *-e*: *midi et demi* = 12:30 p.m.

**Why is it "une heure" but "deux heures"?**
*Heure* is singular after one (*une heure*) and plural after two or more (*deux heures*, *trois heures*). The final *s* is silent, but the spelling changes.
