I don't understand in French is **je ne comprends pas** — arguably the most valuable survival phrase there is, because it unlocks help. But the textbook version and the version French people actually say differ by one dropped word, and the real skill isn't just saying "I don't understand" — it's the follow-up phrases that get someone to *slow down*, *repeat*, or *speak English*. This guide covers all of it.

## The main way to say it

**Je ne comprends pas** — pronounced **zhuh nuh kohn-prahn PAH**.

Pronunciation notes:

- *Je* is "zhuh" — a soft "zh" like the *s* in "measure."
- *Comprends* is nasal: **kohn-PRAHN**, with the *-ds* silent.
- *Pas* is "pah" — the *s* stays silent.
- The whole thing rides on the **ne… pas** sandwich around the verb, which is how French makes things negative.

### The casual version (what people say)

In everyday spoken French, the *ne* is very often dropped:

- **Je comprends pas** (zhuh kohn-prahn PAH) — the natural, casual spoken form. Grammatically "incomplete," universally used.
- **J'comprends pas** — even more clipped, very colloquial.

Both are fine in conversation. Keep the full *je ne comprends pas* for writing and formal situations.

### The essential follow-ups

"I don't understand" is only step one. These get you unstuck:

- **Pouvez-vous répéter, s'il vous plaît ?** (poo-vay voo ray-pay-TAY) — "Can you repeat, please?"
- **Pouvez-vous parler plus lentement ?** (par-lay plew LAHNT-mahn) — "Can you speak more slowly?"
- **Je ne comprends pas bien** — "I don't understand well" (softer, "I'm partly lost").
- **Qu'est-ce que ça veut dire ?** (kess kuh sah vuh DEER) — "What does that mean?"
- **Comment dit-on… en français ?** — "How do you say… in French?"
- **Vous parlez anglais ?** (voo par-lay ahn-GLAY) — "Do you speak English?"
- **Je débute en français** — "I'm a beginner in French" (buys patience).
- **Pardon ?** — a quick "sorry, what?" when you missed one thing.

## Hear it in context

**Lost in a fast explanation (polite):**

> — …et donc il faut composter le billet avant de monter.
> — Pardon, je ne comprends pas. Pouvez-vous parler plus lentement ?
> — Bien sûr. Vous devez valider votre billet avant le train.
> — Ah, d'accord, merci !

*…and so you have to validate the ticket before boarding. — Sorry, I don't understand. Can you speak more slowly? — Of course. You have to validate your ticket before the train. — Ah, okay, thanks!*

**Asking what a word means (casual):**

> — C'est un truc un peu chelou, ça.
> — Attends, je comprends pas. Ça veut dire quoi, "chelou" ?
> — Ah, ça veut dire "bizarre." C'est de l'argot.

*That's a bit sketchy, that. — Wait, I don't get it. What does "chelou" mean? — Oh, it means "weird." It's slang.*

## Common mistakes

- **Over-pronouncing the *ne* and *pas*.** In speech the *ne* is usually dropped entirely (*je comprends pas*) and the *s* in *pas* is silent.
- **Saying *je ne comprends pas* and stopping.** Without a follow-up, the other person just repeats *exactly* what they said. Chain it with *plus lentement* or *pouvez-vous répéter*.
- **Confusing *comprends* with *entends*.** *Je ne comprends pas* = "I don't understand." *Je n'entends pas* = "I can't hear you." Different problems, different fixes.
- **Defaulting to English too fast.** A polite *je ne comprends pas, pouvez-vous répéter ?* keeps you in French and earns goodwill; leaping straight to *vous parlez anglais ?* can feel like giving up.
- **Forgetting *pardon ?*** For missing just one word, a simple rising-tone *pardon ?* is quicker and more natural than the full sentence.

## A Parisian note

Parisians have a reputation for impatience with fumbling French — but the reliable unlock is showing you're *trying*. Open with *bonjour*, admit *"je débute en français"* or *"je ne comprends pas bien, désolé,"* and most people soften immediately and slow down. The phrase that genuinely turns the tide is *"pouvez-vous parler plus lentement, s'il vous plaît ?"* — it signals effort and humility at once. What grates is switching to English *before even attempting* French. Even a clumsy attempt followed by an honest *je ne comprends pas* reads as respect, and respect is the currency that gets Parisians on your side.

## FAQ

**How do French people casually say "I don't understand"?**
In everyday speech they drop the *ne*: *je comprends pas* (or even *j'comprends pas*). The full *je ne comprends pas* is correct and used in writing and formal settings, but the *ne*-less version is what you'll hear in conversation.

**How do I ask someone to speak more slowly?**
*Pouvez-vous parler plus lentement, s'il vous plaît ?* ("Can you speak more slowly, please?"). It's the most useful follow-up to *je ne comprends pas* — otherwise people just repeat at the same speed.

**What's the difference between "je ne comprends pas" and "je n'entends pas"?**
*Je ne comprends pas* means "I don't understand" (the meaning isn't landing). *Je n'entends pas* means "I can't hear you" (a volume/sound problem). Use the right one so the other person fixes the right thing.

**How do I ask what a word means?**
*Qu'est-ce que ça veut dire ?* ("What does that mean?") or, for a specific word, *ça veut dire quoi, "[word]" ?* To go the other direction: *comment dit-on… en français ?* ("how do you say… in French?").

**Should I just ask if they speak English?**
You can (*vous parlez anglais ?*), but try French first. Parisians respond far better to an honest attempt plus *je ne comprends pas* than to an immediate switch to English before trying.
