Merry Christmas in French is **joyeux Noël** — warm, festive, and easy to say once you get the vowels right. But the French also lean heavily on a broader, more inclusive greeting, **bonnes fêtes** ("happy holidays"), which covers the whole December stretch from Christmas through New Year. Knowing when to use each — plus a few words about the very French *réveillon* feast — will let you trade season's greetings like a local.

## The main way to say it

**Joyeux Noël** — pronounced **zhwah-yuh no-EL**.

Pronunciation notes:

- *Joyeux* is "zhwah-yuh" — soft "zh" (like the *s* in "measure"), then a rounded French *-eux* ending, lips forward.
- *Noël* is **no-EL**, two clean syllables. The two dots (*ë*, a tréma) tell you to pronounce the *o* and *e* *separately* — not "nole." Stress the last syllable: no-**EL**.

### Bonnes fêtes and the wider greeting

- **Bonnes fêtes !** (bun FET) — "happy holidays," literally "good festivities." The catch-all for the season, and what you'll say to people you're not sure celebrate Christmas, or across the whole late-December period.
- **Joyeuses fêtes** (zhwah-yuhz FET) — "happy holidays," a slightly warmer variant.
- **Joyeux Noël et bonne année** — "Merry Christmas and happy New Year," the classic combined wish.
- **Bonne année !** (bun ah-NAY) — "Happy New Year," said on and around January 1st (the French say it a lot, often through most of January).
- **Bonnes fêtes de fin d'année** — "happy end-of-year holidays," the full formal version on cards.
- **Meilleurs vœux** (may-yuhr VUH) — "best wishes," the elegant written formula on holiday cards.

### On a card or in a message

- **Je te souhaite un joyeux Noël** — "I wish you a merry Christmas" (informal).
- **Je vous souhaite de joyeuses fêtes** — "I wish you happy holidays" (formal).

## Hear it in context

**Leaving work before the holidays (warm):**

> — Allez, je pars en vacances. Joyeux Noël à toute la famille !
> — Merci, à toi aussi ! Bonnes fêtes et repose-toi bien.
> — Merci, on se voit en janvier. Bonne année d'avance !

*Right, I'm off on holiday. Merry Christmas to the whole family! — Thanks, you too! Happy holidays and get some rest. — Thanks, see you in January. Happy New Year in advance!*

**Toasting at the Christmas Eve dinner (festive):**

> — Joyeux Noël, tout le monde ! Santé !
> — Joyeux Noël ! À la famille et aux absents.
> — Santé ! Et bon réveillon !

*Merry Christmas, everyone! Cheers! — Merry Christmas! To family and to those who couldn't be here. — Cheers! And happy Christmas Eve feast!*

## Common mistakes

- **Saying "nole" for *Noël*.** The tréma (*ë*) means you split the vowels: no-**EL**, two syllables. Running them together is the giveaway.
- **Using *joyeux Noël* for everyone in December.** If you're unsure someone celebrates Christmas, *bonnes fêtes* is the safe, inclusive choice.
- **Mixing up *bonne année* timing.** *Joyeux Noël* is for Christmas; *bonne année* is for New Year. Don't wish someone *bonne année* on December 24th (though *bonne année d'avance* — "in advance" — works).
- **Forgetting the accents.** *Noël* needs the tréma and *fêtes* needs the circumflex — they're part of the words, and they change the pronunciation cue.
- **Over-using *Merry Christmas* commercially.** The French holiday season centers on *bonnes fêtes* and the family *réveillon* more than a constant "Merry Christmas" the way English-speaking retail does.

## A Parisian note

In France, the heart of Christmas is **le réveillon de Noël** — the long, lavish dinner on **Christmas Eve** (December 24th), often stretching past midnight, with foie gras, oysters, *bûche de Noël* (the traditional yule-log cake), and plenty of wine and Champagne. Gifts are frequently opened that night or on Christmas morning, and many families attend *la messe de minuit* (midnight Mass). A week later comes **le réveillon de la Saint-Sylvestre** — New Year's Eve — another feast. Across the whole span, *bonnes fêtes* is the phrase that carries you through, and it's completely normal to keep wishing people *bonne année* well into January the first time you see them in the new year.

## FAQ

**How do you pronounce "Noël"?**
It's *no-EL*, two separate syllables, with the stress on the second. The two dots over the *ë* (a tréma) tell you to pronounce the *o* and the *e* separately — don't blend them into "nole."

**What's the difference between "joyeux Noël" and "bonnes fêtes"?**
*Joyeux Noël* means specifically "Merry Christmas." *Bonnes fêtes* means "happy holidays" and covers the whole season — use it when you're not sure someone celebrates Christmas, or to wish someone well across the late-December stretch.

**How do I say "Merry Christmas and Happy New Year"?**
*Joyeux Noël et bonne année.* On cards, the French also use *meilleurs vœux* ("best wishes") or *bonnes fêtes de fin d'année* ("happy end-of-year holidays").

**What is "le réveillon"?**
It's the festive feast on Christmas Eve (*le réveillon de Noël*) — a long, elaborate family dinner, often past midnight. New Year's Eve has its own *réveillon de la Saint-Sylvestre*.

**When do the French say "bonne année"?**
Around New Year's — on December 31st and January 1st, and typically the first time you see someone in the new year, often through much of January. Saying it "in advance" (*bonne année d'avance*) before the 31st is common too.
