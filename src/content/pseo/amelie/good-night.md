Good night in French is **bonne nuit** — pronounced **bun NWEE** — but here's the thing English speakers get wrong on day one: *bonne nuit* is **only** for when someone is actually going to bed. Leaving a dinner party at 11pm? That's **bonne soirée** or **bonsoir**, never *bonne nuit* — unless you want to imply your hosts should go to sleep. French splits the evening into zones, each with its own phrase, and using the wrong one is the single most common evening mistake learners make. Let's map the zones.

## The main way to say it

**Bonne nuit** — **bun NWEE**.

Breaking it down:

- *Bonne* = good (feminine, because *nuit* is feminine); *nuit* = night. Literally "good night," used exactly like the English **bedtime** good night — to someone heading to sleep, or as the last thing you say before lights out.
- The **ui** in *nuit* is a quick glide: "nwee," one syllable, not "noo-ee." Say "we" with an n in front and you're basically there.
- The **-onne** in *bonne* is a full "un/bun" sound, not nasal — unlike *bon*, where the n disappears into the vowel. *Bonne* = "bun"; *bon* = "boh(n)."

The natural companion is **fais de beaux rêves** (**feh duh boh REV**) — "sweet dreams," literally "make beautiful dreams." That's the tu form; with someone you address formally it becomes **faites de beaux rêves** (**fet duh boh REV**). Add **dors bien** (**dor BYAN**, "sleep well") and you have the full French bedtime kit.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | When to use it |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| Bonne nuit ! | Good night! | Bedtime only — someone is going to sleep |
| Fais de beaux rêves ! | Sweet dreams! | Affectionate, tu form — kids, partners, close friends |
| Faites de beaux rêves ! | Sweet dreams! | Same, vous form |
| Dors bien ! / Dormez bien ! | Sleep well! | Warm add-on after bonne nuit (tu / vous) |
| Bonne soirée ! | Have a good evening! | Leaving someone who still has an evening ahead |
| Bonsoir ! | Good evening! | Evening greeting — and a formal evening goodbye |
| À demain ! | See you tomorrow! | Perfect companion when parting at night |

**The evening map — this is the part that matters:**

- **Bonsoir** = the evening **hello** (from dusk onward, replacing *bonjour*). In more formal contexts it also works as an evening **goodbye**.
- **Bonne soirée** = the evening **goodbye** when the other person's evening is still in progress — leaving the office at 7pm, leaving a restaurant, ending a call at 9pm. It wishes them a good *rest of* the evening.
- **Bonne nuit** = strictly the **bedtime** goodbye. You say it to your kids, your partner, your roommate heading to their room — or to overnight guests as everyone turns in.

The rule of thumb: if the person is about to be **asleep** within the hour, *bonne nuit*. If they're about to be **out having fun** (or just awake), *bonne soirée*. If you're **arriving**, *bonsoir*.

## Hear it in context

**A family evening at home (bedtime):**

> — Allez, au lit. Bonne nuit, ma puce.
> — Bonne nuit, papa. Tu me lis une histoire ?
> — Une seule, alors. Et après, dodo. Fais de beaux rêves.

*— Come on, off to bed. Good night, sweetie. — Good night, Dad. Will you read me a story? — Just one, then. And after that, sleepy time. Sweet dreams.*

**Leaving a dinner party at 11pm (the trap, done right):**

> — Merci pour cette soirée, c'était délicieux. Bonne soirée à vous !
> — Merci d'être venus ! Rentrez bien.
> — À bientôt. Bonsoir !

*— Thanks for this evening, it was delicious. Enjoy the rest of your evening! — Thanks for coming! Get home safe. — See you soon. Good evening!*

Notice what nobody said: *bonne nuit*. The hosts aren't going to bed — they're doing the dishes.

## Common mistakes

- **Saying "bonne nuit" when leaving a party or restaurant.** The classic. *Bonne nuit* to someone who isn't going to bed sounds like you're tucking them in. Use *bonne soirée* (they still have an evening) or *bonsoir* (neutral, more formal).
- **Using "bonne nuit" as an evening greeting.** It's never a hello. Walking into a bar at 9pm, you say *bonsoir*. *Bonne nuit* as a greeting is as odd as walking into a party and announcing "sleep tight."
- **"Noo-ee" instead of "nwee."** *Nuit* is one syllable with a glide. Two syllables instantly reads as beginner.
- **Forgetting the tu/vous switch on the extras.** *Fais de beaux rêves* and *dors bien* are tu forms. To someone you *vouvoie* — or to several people — it's *faites de beaux rêves* and *dormez bien*.
- **Confusing "bonsoir" and "bonne soirée."** *Bonsoir* greets (and can close, formally); *bonne soirée* only closes, and wishes the person a good rest-of-evening. Saying *bonne soirée* when you arrive somewhere is backwards.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "bonne nuit" and "bonsoir"?**
*Bonsoir* is the evening greeting (hello from dusk onward) and a formal evening goodbye. *Bonne nuit* is only said to someone about to go to sleep. Mixing them up is the most common learner mistake of the French evening.

**How do you say "sweet dreams" in French?**
*Fais de beaux rêves* (tu) or *faites de beaux rêves* (vous) — literally "make beautiful dreams." Usually said right after *bonne nuit*.

**Can I say "bonne nuit" when leaving a party?**
Only if the hosts are visibly heading straight to bed. Otherwise say *bonne soirée* — it wishes them a good rest of the evening, which is what you actually mean.

**Is "bonne nuit" formal or informal?**
Neutral. What changes with register is the companions: *dors bien / fais de beaux rêves* with tu, *dormez bien / faites de beaux rêves* with vous.

**How do the French text "good night"?**
*Bonne nuit* works fine, often shortened to *bonne nuit :)* with *à demain* ("see you tomorrow"). Affectionate texts add *fais de beaux rêves* or simply *des bisous*.
