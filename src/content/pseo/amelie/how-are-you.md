How are you in French is **ça va ?** with friends and **comment allez-vous ?** in formal settings — and choosing between them is your first real encounter with the *tu/vous* system that structures every French conversation. The good news: **ça va** is the Swiss Army knife of French, working as the question, the answer, *and* the follow-up (*Ça va ? — Ça va. Et toi ? — Ça va !*). Master that little loop and the tu/vous line, and you can open a conversation with anyone from a Parisian barista to your partner's grandmother.

## The main way to say it

**Ça va ?** — pronounced **sah VAH**, with rising intonation.

The details that matter:

- **Ça** is "sah" — the ç (c-cedilla) is always a soft s, never a k. And **va** is a clean, open "vah."
- The question mark does all the work: the same two words with **rising** pitch ask the question, and with **falling** pitch answer it. *Ça va ?* ↗ = "you good?" *Ça va.* ↘ = "I'm good."
- Literally, *ça va* means "it goes" — the full logic being *comment ça va ?* ("how does it go?"), which is also completely standard and a touch fuller.

Then comes the register split, and it's non-negotiable:

- **Comment vas-tu ?** — informal *tu*, but slightly bookish; friends mostly just say *ça va ?* or *comment ça va ?*
- **Comment allez-vous ?** — pronounced **koh-mahn tah-lay VOO** — the formal *vous* version for strangers, elders, professional settings, and anyone you'd address as *monsieur* or *madame*. When in doubt, start here: nobody is ever offended by *vous*, while a misplaced *tu* can genuinely bristle.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Ça va ? | How's it going? | Casual, the everyday default |
| Comment ça va ? | How are you doing? | Casual-neutral, slightly fuller |
| Comment vas-tu ? | How are you? (*tu*) | Correct but a bit formal-sounding between friends |
| Comment allez-vous ? | How are you? (*vous*) | Formal, always safe with strangers |
| Vous allez bien ? | Are you well? | Warm-formal, very common |
| Tu vas bien ? | You doing okay? | Warm-informal |
| Quoi de neuf ? | What's new? | Casual |
| Ça roule ? | How's it rolling? | Slangy, young, Parisian |

And the standard answers, because French small talk is a closed loop that expects a return:

- **Ça va, merci. Et toi ? / Et vous ?** — fine, thanks, and you? (The *et toi ?* back is near-mandatory.)
- **Très bien, merci.** — very well, thanks. The polished formal answer.
- **Pas mal.** — not bad. Understated and very French.
- **Bof.** — the untranslatable shrug-in-a-word: "meh." With friends only.
- **On fait aller.** — "getting by." Honest without oversharing.

Cultural note: the beaming American **"Great!!"** doesn't exist in French. Enthusiasm is rationed — *ça va* or *pas mal* IS a positive answer, and a Parisian who says *très très bien !* is probably being ironic. Conversely, you don't greet a cashier with *ça va ?* the way English speakers toss out "how are you?" — in France the question implies you actually know the person. With staff and strangers, *bonjour* alone carries the politeness.

## Hear it in context

**Between friends (the classic loop):**

> — Salut Léa ! Ça va ?
> — Ça va, et toi ?
> — Ça va. On se prend un café ?
> — Avec plaisir !

*Hi Léa! How's it going? — Good, and you? — Good. Shall we grab a coffee? — Gladly!* (Yes, *ça va* appears three times. That's normal.)

**With your partner's parents (formal):**

> — Bonjour madame. Comment allez-vous ?
> — Très bien, merci. Et vous-même ?
> — Très bien. Merci encore pour l'invitation.
> — Je vous en prie, entrez, entrez !

*Good morning, ma'am. How are you? — Very well, thank you. And yourself? — Very well. Thanks again for the invitation. — You're welcome, come in, come in!*

## Common mistakes

- **Using "tu" with strangers.** *Comment vas-tu ?* to a shopkeeper or your new boss lands as overfamiliar. Default to *vous* with anyone you don't know; let *them* propose the switch (*on peut se tutoyer* — "we can use tu").
- **Pronouncing ça like "ka."** The cedilla exists precisely to make the c soft: **sah** va. "Ka va" is one of the most audible beginner tells.
- **Answering with a monologue.** *Ça va ?* expects *ça va*, not a health report. The genuine catch-up comes later in the conversation, if at all.
- **Forgetting the return volley.** Answering *ça va* without *et toi ?* (or *et vous ?*) cuts the ritual short and feels self-absorbed. Question, answer, return — always three beats.
- **Greeting cashiers with "ça va ?"** English "how are you?" is wallpaper; French *ça va ?* is personal. With strangers and staff, *bonjour* is the entire greeting.
- **Mixing registers.** *Comment allez-vous ?* followed by *et toi ?* clangs. Pick a lane — *vous* all the way through, or *tu* all the way through.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "ça va" and "comment allez-vous"?**
Register. *Ça va ?* is casual, for friends and peers; *comment allez-vous ?* is formal, for strangers, elders, and professional settings. Same question, different distance.

**Can "ça va" really be both the question and the answer?**
Yes — intonation does the work. Rising (*ça va ?*) asks; flat or falling (*ça va*) answers. The exchange *Ça va ? — Ça va, et toi ? — Ça va !* is perfectly normal French.

**When do I use "tu" vs "vous"?**
*Tu* for friends, family, children, and peers who've opened the door; *vous* for strangers, older people, and anyone in a professional or service context. When unsure, use *vous* — the French switch to *tu* by explicit invitation, not assumption.

**How do I answer "ça va ?" if I'm not doing great?**
*Bof* (meh), *on fait aller* (getting by), or *ça peut aller* (could be worse) — all signal "not amazing" without launching into details. Save the real story for close friends who ask twice.

**Do French people ask "ça va ?" to strangers like Americans say "how are you"?**
No. In France the question implies acquaintance. With cashiers, waiters, and strangers, the greeting is *bonjour* alone — asking *ça va ?* would feel oddly intimate.
