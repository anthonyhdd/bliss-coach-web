Congratulations in French is **félicitations** — a mouthful that sounds exactly as celebratory as it looks. But French splits the job of "congrats" across two words: **félicitations** for achievements (a promotion, a degree, a wedding) and **bravo** for performances and well-done efforts (a great meal, a nailed presentation, a kid's first bike ride). Knowing which to reach for is what makes your congratulations land like a native's.

## The main ways to say it

**Félicitations** — pronounced **fay-lee-see-tah-SYOHN**.

**Bravo** — pronounced **brah-VOH**.

Pronunciation notes:

- *Félicitations* stresses the final syllable: fay-lee-see-tah-**SYOHN**. The *-tion* ending is nasal, closer to "syohn" than the English "shun."
- The accents (**é**) are "ay" sounds: **fay**-lee-see-tah-syohn.
- *Bravo* is straightforward — brah-VOH — the same word English borrowed, but with the stress cleanly on the second syllable and a pure "oh."

### Félicitations vs. bravo

- **Félicitations** — for achievements and milestones: a new job, passing an exam, an engagement, a baby, a big win. It's the formal, "well done on this accomplishment" word.
- **Bravo** — for a job well done in the moment: a great performance, a delicious dinner, a clever solution, effort you witnessed. It's warmer and more spontaneous, and it doubles as applause.

Often they combine: *Bravo et félicitations pour ton diplôme !*

### Warmer and fuller versions

- **Toutes mes félicitations** — "my heartfelt congratulations." Warmer, more personal.
- **Félicitations à vous deux** — "congratulations to you both" (weddings, new parents).
- **Chapeau !** (sha-POH) — literally "hat!" — an idiom meaning "hats off to you," admiring and slightly playful.
- **Je suis fier / fière de toi** (fee-YAIR) — "I'm proud of you." Personal and affectionate.
- **Bien joué** (byan zhoo-AY) — "well played / nicely done." Casual, friendly.
- **Félicitations pour** + noun: *Félicitations pour ton mariage* ("Congrats on your wedding").

## Hear it in context

**Celebrating a promotion (warm, formal):**

> — J'ai eu la promotion ! Je deviens chef de projet.
> — Oh, toutes mes félicitations ! Tu le mérites vraiment.
> — Merci beaucoup, ça me touche.

*I got the promotion! I'm becoming project manager. — Oh, congratulations! You really deserve it. — Thank you so much, that means a lot.*

**After a friend's dinner (casual, spontaneous):**

> — Alors, ce risotto ?
> — Bravo, franchement ! C'était un des meilleurs que j'ai mangés.
> — Ah, chapeau à toi de le dire, merci !

*So, how was the risotto? — Bravo, honestly! One of the best I've had. — Well, hats off to you for saying so, thanks!*

## Common mistakes

- **Using *félicitations* for a great meal or performance.** That's *bravo* territory. *Félicitations* is for achievements and milestones, not for things done well in the moment.
- **Saying "fee-lih-si-TAY-shuns."** It's not the English "-tions." Keep it French: fay-lee-see-tah-**SYOHN**, with a nasal ending.
- **Forgetting *félicitations* is always plural.** It ends in *-s* and takes no singular form here. It's never *une félicitation* in this greeting.
- **Overusing *bravo* formally.** In a formal letter congratulating someone on a wedding, *félicitations* or *toutes mes félicitations* fits; a bare *bravo* can feel too light.
- **Translating "congrats on" literally.** Use *félicitations pour* + noun, e.g. *félicitations pour ton diplôme*, not *félicitations sur*.

## A Parisian note

The French don't gush the way American English does — a stacked "OMG congratulations, I'm SO happy for you!!!" can feel like a lot. A warm, sincere *"Félicitations, tu le mérites"* ("congratulations, you deserve it") lands better than volume. At celebrations, congratulations usually come with a toast — *santé !* — clinking glasses while making eye contact (skipping the eye contact is, per the superstition, bad luck). And for weddings and new babies, the classic printed or spoken formula is *"Tous mes vœux de bonheur"* ("all my wishes for your happiness"), a step more elegant than a plain *félicitations*.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "félicitations" and "bravo"?**
*Félicitations* is for achievements and milestones — a promotion, a degree, a wedding. *Bravo* is for a job well done in the moment — a performance, a meal, a clever solution. *Bravo* is warmer and more spontaneous; *félicitations* is the formal "congratulations."

**Is "félicitations" singular or plural?**
Always plural in this greeting — it ends in *-s*. You wouldn't say *une félicitation* to congratulate someone; it's *félicitations* or *toutes mes félicitations*.

**How do I say "congratulations on your wedding"?**
*Félicitations pour ton mariage* (informal) or *toutes mes félicitations pour votre mariage* (formal). You'll also hear the elegant *tous mes vœux de bonheur* ("all my wishes for your happiness").

**What does "chapeau" mean as congratulations?**
Literally "hat," it's short for "hats off to you" — an admiring, slightly playful way to say "well done," used when someone's impressed you.

**How do I say "I'm proud of you" in French?**
*Je suis fier de toi* (male speaker) or *je suis fière de toi* (female speaker). Both are pronounced the same (fee-YAIR) and are warm, personal ways to congratulate someone close.
