Excuse me in French isn't one phrase — it's a small toolkit, and picking the right tool is what separates a tourist from someone who *gets* French manners. The two you'll reach for constantly are **pardon** and **excusez-moi**. One is a quick reflex for slipping through a crowd; the other is a fuller, more polite way to flag a stranger before you ask for something. In Paris, where a packed métro and a stranger's attention are both things you navigate all day, knowing which to say is genuinely useful.

## The main ways to say it

**Pardon** — pronounced **pahr-DOHN**.

**Excusez-moi** — pronounced **ek-skew-zay-MWAH**.

A few pronunciation notes that matter:

- In *pardon*, the final **-on** is nasal — the air goes through your nose and you barely touch the "n." Think of the vowel in a French "on," not the English "don."
- The French **r** in *pardon* is made at the back of the throat. A soft, light r beats a hard American one every time.
- *Excusez-moi* stresses the last syllable: ek-skew-zay-**MWAH**. The *-ez* ending is a clean "ay" sound, and *moi* is "mwah," not "moy."

### When to use each

- **Pardon** — short, punchy, all-purpose. Use it to squeeze past someone (*pardon… pardon…* down a train aisle), when you didn't hear something ("*pardon?*" = "sorry, what?"), or as a quick "sorry" for a tiny bump.
- **Excusez-moi** — the polite opener. Use it to *get someone's attention* before asking a question: *Excusez-moi, où est la station de métro ?* It carries the formal *vous*, so it's your default with strangers.
- **Excuse-moi** — the same phrase with informal *tu*, for friends and family: *Excuse-moi, tu peux répéter ?*

There's also **je m'excuse** ("I excuse myself"), which you'll hear from natives all the time — but purists consider it slightly incorrect, since logically you can't excuse yourself; the other person does. It's fine in casual speech, but *excusez-moi* or *je vous prie de m'excuser* (formal) is the safer choice in writing or professional settings.

## Hear it in context

**Getting off a crowded métro (quick reflex):**

> — Pardon… pardon, je descends. Merci !
> — Je vous en prie, allez-y.

*Excuse me… excuse me, I'm getting off. Thanks! — Of course, go ahead.*

**Stopping a stranger to ask directions (polite opener):**

> — Excusez-moi, madame, je cherche la rue de Rivoli.
> — Ah, c'est tout droit, puis à gauche au feu.
> — Merci beaucoup, bonne journée !

*Excuse me, ma'am, I'm looking for rue de Rivoli. — Oh, it's straight ahead, then left at the light. — Thank you very much, have a good day!*

## Common mistakes

- **Using *pardon* to open a question with a stranger.** *Pardon* works, but *excusez-moi* is warmer and more complete when you're about to *ask* for something. Save the bare *pardon* for squeezing past.
- **Forgetting the nasal in *pardon*.** Saying "par-DON" with a hard English "don" is the giveaway. Let the "-on" resonate in your nose.
- **Defaulting to *je m'excuse* in formal settings.** It's common in speech but flagged as sloppy in writing. For an email or an apology to a boss, use *je vous prie de m'excuser* or *veuillez m'excuser*.
- **Mixing up *tu* and *vous*.** *Excuse-moi* to a stranger sounds too familiar; *excusez-vous* isn't a thing. With people you don't know: *excusez-moi*.
- **Over-apologizing.** French politeness is real, but a single well-placed *pardon* does the job — you don't need to stack "sorry, sorry, so sorry" the way English speakers reflexively do.

## A Parisian note

In Paris, *pardon* is the quiet grease of city life — murmured a hundred times a day on the métro, in narrow café aisles, at the boulangerie counter. It's less an apology than a signal: *I see you, I'm passing, no offense.* What actually reads as rude isn't bumping someone — it's bumping them in silence. A soft *pardon* costs nothing and instantly marks you as someone who knows the code. And when a Parisian says *pardon?* with a rising tone, they didn't hear you — repeat yourself, don't apologize.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "pardon" and "excusez-moi"?**
*Pardon* is a short, all-purpose "excuse me / sorry" for passing by, small bumps, or asking someone to repeat. *Excusez-moi* is fuller and more polite — the go-to for getting a stranger's attention before you ask a question.

**Is "je m'excuse" correct French?**
It's extremely common in speech, but grammar purists consider it slightly wrong (you can't literally excuse yourself). In formal writing, use *excusez-moi*, *veuillez m'excuser*, or *je vous prie de m'excuser* instead.

**How do you say "excuse me" to a friend?**
Use the informal *excuse-moi* (with *tu*) rather than *excusez-moi* (with *vous*). For example: *Excuse-moi, tu peux me passer le sel ?*

**How do I say "excuse me?" when I didn't hear something?**
A rising-tone *pardon ?* is the natural, polite way. *Comment ?* also works and is very common. Avoid a blunt *quoi ?* ("what?"), which can sound rude to strangers.

**Does "pardon" mean the same as in English?**
Close, but broader. In French *pardon* covers "excuse me," "sorry," and "come again?" all at once, and it's used far more casually and frequently than the somewhat formal English "pardon."
