How much is it in French is **combien ça coûte ?** — or, more often on the street, the snappier **c'est combien ?**. Asking a price is one of the first things you'll actually *need* to do in France, whether at a market stall, a boutique, or a café. This guide gives you the formal and casual versions, how to understand the number that comes back, and the market etiquette that keeps you from looking like a lost tourist.

## The main ways to ask it

**Combien ça coûte ?** — pronounced **kom-byan sah KOOT**.

**C'est combien ?** — pronounced **say kom-BYAN**.

Pronunciation notes:

- *Combien* is nasal: **kom-BYAN**, the *-ien* humming through the nose, stress on the last syllable.
- *Coûte* is "KOOT," a clean long "oo," final *e* silent.
- *Ça* is "sah"; *c'est* is "say." Both are tiny but do a lot of work.

### Casual vs. formal

- **C'est combien ?** — the everyday, casual "how much?" You'll use and hear this constantly.
- **Combien ça coûte ?** — slightly fuller, still casual-neutral.
- **Combien est-ce que ça coûte ?** — the complete textbook version.
- **Ça fait combien ?** (sah fay kom-BYAN) — "how much does that come to?" — very common at the register, totaling up.
- **Quel est le prix ?** / **Quel est le prix de… ?** — "what's the price of…?" More formal, good in a shop.
- **Je vous dois combien ?** (zhuh voo dwah kom-BYAN) — "how much do I owe you?" Polite, natural when paying.

### Asking about a specific item

- **Combien coûte ce…** + noun: *Combien coûte ce sac ?* ("How much is this bag?").
- **C'est combien, les fraises ?** — "How much are the strawberries?" (casual, market-style).
- **Ça coûte combien, le kilo ?** — "How much per kilo?"

## Understanding the answer

Prices come back fast. Key words:

- **euros** (uh-ROH) — the currency; often dropped in speech (*"trois cinquante"* = 3,50 €).
- French writes prices with a **comma**, not a point: **3,50 €** = "trois euros cinquante" or just "trois cinquante."
- **cher / chère** (shair) — expensive. *C'est trop cher* = "it's too expensive."
- **pas cher** — cheap / not expensive.
- **gratuit** (grah-TWEE) — free.
- **la monnaie** — change (coins/small money). *Vous avez la monnaie ?* = "do you have change?"

## Hear it in context

**At a market stall (casual):**

> — Bonjour ! C'est combien, les tomates ?
> — Trois euros le kilo, elles sont excellentes.
> — Parfait, j'en prends un kilo. Ça fait combien en tout ?
> — Trois euros tout rond.

*Hello! How much are the tomatoes? — Three euros a kilo, they're excellent. — Perfect, I'll take a kilo. How much altogether? — Three euros even.*

**In a boutique (a touch more formal):**

> — Excusez-moi, combien coûte cette écharpe ?
> — Elle est à quarante-cinq euros.
> — D'accord… c'est un peu cher pour moi. Merci quand même !

*Excuse me, how much is this scarf? — It's forty-five euros. — Okay… that's a bit expensive for me. Thanks anyway!*

## Common mistakes

- **Pronouncing *combien* flat.** The *-ien* is nasal (kom-BYAN), not "kom-bee-en." Let it resonate in the nose.
- **Reading French prices with a decimal point.** *3,50 €* uses a comma; it's "trois euros cinquante," not "three point fifty."
- **Forgetting to greet first.** *Bonjour* before *c'est combien ?* is expected, especially in small shops and markets. Price-first, greeting-never reads as rude.
- **Confusing *cher* meanings.** *Cher* means "expensive" *and* "dear" (as in a letter). Context sorts it out, but *c'est cher* = "it's pricey."
- **Trying to haggle in a regular shop.** Prices in boutiques and supermarkets are fixed. Bargaining is only (sometimes) okay at flea markets (*les puces*) or with a street vendor — not at a bakery.

## A Parisian note

At Parisian markets (*les marchés*), there's a rhythm worth learning: greet the vendor (*bonjour !*), let *them* pick and bag the produce (don't grab the fruit yourself — it's considered rude to handle it), ask *c'est combien ?* or *ça fait combien ?*, and pay with reasonably small notes if you can. Haggling isn't really a thing in Paris the way it is in some countries — prices at the market are what they are — though a loyal customer might get a *"je vous fais un prix"* ("I'll give you a deal") or a little extra tossed in. In fixed shops, boutiques, and supermarkets, the price on the tag is final. And if something's out of budget, a gracious *"c'est un peu cher pour moi, merci quand même"* is the polite exit.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "combien ça coûte" and "c'est combien"?**
They mean the same thing — "how much is it?" *C'est combien ?* is the shorter, more casual version you'll hear most on the street; *combien ça coûte ?* is slightly fuller. Both are perfectly natural in everyday shopping.

**How do I ask "how much do I owe you?"**
*Je vous dois combien ?* ("how much do I owe you?") or *ça fait combien ?* ("how much does that come to?"). Both are polite and natural when it's time to pay.

**How are French prices written and said?**
With a comma, not a decimal point: *3,50 €* is "trois euros cinquante" (often shortened to "trois cinquante"). The euro symbol usually comes after the number.

**Can I haggle on prices in France?**
Generally no. Shops, boutiques, and supermarkets have fixed prices. Some bargaining is acceptable at flea markets (*les puces*) or with street vendors, but never at a bakery, café, or regular store.

**How do I say something is too expensive?**
*C'est trop cher* ("it's too expensive") or, more softly, *c'est un peu cher pour moi* ("it's a bit expensive for me"). Add *merci quand même* ("thanks anyway") for a polite exit.
