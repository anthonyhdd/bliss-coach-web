You're welcome in French is **de rien** — pronounced **duh RYAN** (with a nasal ending) — and it will serve you in almost any casual situation. But French, being French, doesn't stop at one option: there's a whole ladder of "you're welcome" phrases, from the shrugging *de rien* to the elegant *je vous en prie*, and choosing the right rung is one of those small things that makes you sound genuinely fluent rather than merely understood. Here's the ladder, top to bottom.

## The main way to say it

**De rien** — **duh RYAN**.

Breaking it down:

- Literally "of nothing" — the same logic as Spanish *de nada* or English "it's nothing." You're waving the thanks away.
- The **r** is the soft French r from the back of the throat, and **-ien** is a nasal glide: "ryan" said through the nose, one syllable. Not "ree-en."
- Register: **casual to neutral**. Perfect with friends, family, shopkeepers, strangers who thank you for holding the door. In a formal or professional setting it can feel a touch offhand — that's where the next phrases come in.

The polite upgrade is **je t'en prie** (tu form, **zhuh tahn PREE**) / **je vous en prie** (vous form, **zhuh voo zahn PREE**) — literally "I beg (it of) you," functionally a graceful "you're welcome / please, don't mention it." This is what the waiter in a nice Paris restaurant says, what you say to your boss, and what French speakers reach for whenever *de rien* feels too light.

And the warm one: **avec plaisir** (**ah-vek pleh-ZEER**) — "with pleasure." It says the favor wasn't just no trouble, it was a joy. Very common, very likable, and (bonus) it's the signature "you're welcome" of southern France, though Parisians use it happily too.

## Variations

| Phrase | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| De rien | duh RYAN | The casual default — friends, small favors, everyday life |
| Je t'en prie | zhuh tahn PREE | Polished but warm — tu register |
| Je vous en prie | zhuh voo zahn PREE | The formal standard — service, work, strangers (vous) |
| Avec plaisir | ah-vek pleh-ZEER | "With pleasure" — warm, enthusiastic, any register |
| Il n'y a pas de quoi | eel nyah pah duh KWAH | "There's nothing to thank me for" — friendly, modest |
| Pas de quoi | pah duh KWAH | Clipped casual version of the above |
| Pas de problème | pah duh proh-BLEM | "No problem" — casual, among younger speakers |
| C'est moi qui vous remercie | say MWAH kee voo ruh-mehr-SEE | "It's I who thank *you*" — gracious, often just "c'est moi !" |

**How to choose, in one breath:** *de rien* for everyday life, *je vous en prie* when there's a vous in the room, *avec plaisir* when you want warmth, *c'est moi* when the thanks should really flow the other way (a customer thanking a shopkeeper, for instance, often gets *c'est moi !* back).

**A cultural note on that last one:** *c'est moi (qui vous remercie)* — "it's me (who thanks you)" — is a beloved bit of French politeness ping-pong. Someone thanks you for something that benefited you too, and you return the ball. In Parisian shops and cafés you'll hear the full exchange a dozen times a day: *Merci ! — C'est moi. Bonne journée !*

## Hear it in context

**At a café counter in Paris (formal, vous):**

> — Votre café, madame.
> — Merci beaucoup !
> — Je vous en prie. Bonne journée !
> — C'est moi, merci. À bientôt !

*— Your coffee, madam. — Thank you very much! — You're welcome. Have a good day! — Thank *you*. See you soon!*

**Between friends (casual, tu):**

> — Merci de m'avoir aidé à déménager, t'es un ange.
> — De rien ! Avec plaisir, même.
> — Je te revaudrai ça.
> — Pas de quoi. C'est ça, les amis.

*— Thanks for helping me move, you're an angel. — You're welcome! With pleasure, even. — I'll return the favor. — Don't mention it. That's what friends are for.*

## Common mistakes

- **Translating "you're welcome" literally as "vous êtes bienvenu."** In France, *bienvenue* means "welcome" as in welcoming someone to a place (*bienvenue à Paris !*), not as a reply to thanks. (Québec uses *bienvenue* for "you're welcome" — France doesn't.)
- **Using "de rien" with your boss or a client.** Not wrong, but light. When you're in vous territory, *je vous en prie* is the safe, polished default.
- **Mixing registers: "je t'en prie" to a stranger.** The t' is tu. To anyone you'd vouvoie — and to any group — it's *je vous en prie*. The two phrases are not interchangeable.
- **Pronouncing "rien" as two syllables.** It's one nasal glide: RYAN. "Ree-en" is the giveaway.
- **Answering "merci" with silence.** In France, thanks get a reply — even just *de rien* or *je vous en prie*. Leaving a *merci* hanging reads as cold, especially in shops where the politeness ritual (bonjour / merci / bonne journée) is near-sacred.

## FAQ

**What's the most common way to say you're welcome in French?**
*De rien* — the casual, everyday default. In formal situations (anyone you address as vous), *je vous en prie* is the standard.

**What's the difference between "je t'en prie" and "je vous en prie"?**
Register only. *Je t'en prie* is the tu form (friends, family); *je vous en prie* is the vous form (strangers, professional settings, groups). Same meaning: a polished "you're welcome."

**Does "bienvenue" mean you're welcome?**
Not in France — there it only means "welcome (to a place)." Using it as a reply to *merci* is a Québécois usage (and an anglicism); in Paris, say *de rien* or *je vous en prie* instead.

**Is "avec plaisir" too much?**
Not at all — it's warm, natural, and used everywhere. It signals the favor was a pleasure rather than a burden, which makes it one of the most likable replies you can give.

**What does "c'est moi" mean as a reply to thanks?**
Short for *c'est moi qui vous remercie* — "it's I who thank you." You'll hear it constantly in French shops and cafés when both sides have something to be thankful for.
