I'm sorry in French is **désolé** — but French, like English, has a whole spectrum of sorry, from a light "oops" to a heartfelt apology. Reach for the wrong register and you'll either sound cold (too casual for a real mistake) or theatrical (too heavy for a small bump). The core word is *désolé*; around it sit *pardon* for quick reflexes and *je suis vraiment désolé* for when you've genuinely messed up. Here's how to pick.

## The main way to say it

**Désolé** — pronounced **day-zoh-LAY**.

Like *enchanté*, the ending agrees with **the speaker's** gender:

- A man says **désolé**.
- A woman says **désolée** — extra *e*, but pronounced identically: day-zoh-LAY.

Pronunciation notes:

- The **é** at both ends is a crisp "ay" — day-zoh-**LAY** — not "ee" or "uh."
- The **s** between vowels sounds like a **z**: day-**z**oh-lay, not "day-soh-lay."
- Stress the final syllable, as always in French.

### Building the apology up

*Désolé* alone is the everyday "sorry." To make it fuller or more sincere:

- **Je suis désolé** — "I am sorry." The complete sentence; a touch more formal and heartfelt than the bare word.
- **Je suis vraiment désolé** — "I'm really sorry." For genuine mistakes.
- **Je suis navré / navrée** (na-VRAY) — "I'm deeply sorry." More formal and emotional; common for bad news or condolences.
- **Je m'excuse** — "I apologize" (casual speech; some purists dislike it).
- **Veuillez m'excuser** / **je vous prie de m'excuser** — "please excuse me." Formal, for writing and professional settings.
- **Pardon** (pahr-DOHN) — quick, reflexive "sorry" for small bumps or interruptions.

### Sorry *for* something

To say what you're sorry about, French uses two patterns:

- **désolé de** + verb: *Désolé d'être en retard* ("Sorry for being late").
- **désolé pour** + noun: *Désolé pour le retard* ("Sorry about the delay").

## Hear it in context

**Apologizing for being late (sincere):**

> — Je suis vraiment désolé, il y avait grève sur ma ligne.
> — Ce n'est pas grave, on vient de commencer.
> — Merci de ta compréhension.

*I'm really sorry, there was a strike on my line. — It's fine, we just started. — Thanks for understanding.*

**A quick bump on the street (light):**

> — Oh, pardon ! Désolée, je ne vous ai pas vu.
> — Je vous en prie, pas de souci.

*Oh, sorry! Sorry, I didn't see you. — Don't worry about it, no problem.*

## Common mistakes

- **Fussing over the spelling out loud.** *Désolé* and *désolée* sound the same. The extra *e* is only visible in writing and matches the speaker.
- **Using *désolé* for a tiny bump.** For brushing past someone, *pardon* is quicker and more natural. Save *désolé* for actual mistakes.
- **Saying "day-soh-lay."** The middle *s* is a *z* sound: day-**z**oh-lay. English speakers often harden it.
- **Reaching for *je m'excuse* in a formal email.** It's fine in speech but flagged as sloppy in writing. Use *veuillez m'excuser* or *je vous prie de m'excuser* instead.
- **Mixing up *désolé* and *pardon*.** *Désolé* = "I'm sorry" (regret). *Pardon* = "excuse me" (getting past, not hearing). They overlap but aren't interchangeable.

## A Parisian note

Parisians have a reputation for being sparing with apologies — but the reality is more about *register*. A crowded café, a jostled elbow, a squeeze past on the métro: those get a murmured *pardon*, not a *désolé*. Save *désolé* for when you've actually inconvenienced someone — arriving late, forgetting a favor, a genuine mistake. Over-apologizing the English way (a *désolé* for every micro-interaction) can even read as odd or insincere. And there's a very French deflection you'll hear in return: *"Ce n'est pas grave"* ("it's no big deal") or *"il n'y a pas de mal"* ("no harm done") — the graceful way to wave an apology away.

## FAQ

**Is it "désolé" or "désolée"?**
Both — the ending agrees with the *speaker's* gender. A man writes *désolé*, a woman *désolée*. They're pronounced identically (day-zoh-LAY); the difference is only in writing.

**What's the difference between "désolé" and "pardon"?**
*Désolé* means "I'm sorry" and expresses regret for a mistake. *Pardon* means "excuse me" — for squeezing past, interrupting, or not hearing something. They overlap in casual use but aren't fully interchangeable.

**How do I say "I'm really sorry" in French?**
*Je suis vraiment désolé(e)* is the natural way. For something more serious or formal, *je suis navré(e)* ("I'm deeply sorry") carries more weight.

**Is "je m'excuse" correct?**
It's very common in speech but considered slightly incorrect by purists, since you can't literally excuse yourself. In formal writing, use *veuillez m'excuser* or *je vous prie de m'excuser*.

**How do French people respond to an apology?**
Usually *ce n'est pas grave* ("it's no big deal"), *il n'y a pas de mal* ("no harm done"), *pas de souci* ("no worries"), or *je vous en prie* ("it's fine").
