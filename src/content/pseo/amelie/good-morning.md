Good morning in French is simply **bonjour** — and here's the twist: French has **no dedicated "good morning."** *Bonjour* literally means "good day," and it covers the entire stretch from sunrise until evening. The direct translation you might be tempted to build — *bonne matinée* — exists, but it's a *goodbye*, not a greeting. Getting *bonjour* right matters more than almost any other word in French, because in France the greeting isn't decoration: it's the password that opens every interaction, and skipping it is genuinely rude.

## The main way to say it

**Bonjour** — pronounced **bohn-ZHOOR**.

Two pronunciation notes that instantly make you sound better:

- The **on** is a nasal vowel: air flows through your nose, and the **n is never fully pronounced**. Say "bohn" while stopping your tongue before it touches the roof of your mouth — that's the French nasal.
- The **j** is the soft "zh" sound in "mea**s**ure" or "vi**si**on" — never the hard j of "jump." And the final **r** is a light, back-of-the-throat breath: bohn-**ZHOOR**.

Why no "good morning"? French simply carves the day differently: *bonjour* until roughly sunset, then **bonsoir** ("good evening") takes over. Morning, noon, and afternoon are all *bonjour* territory. If you want to acknowledge the early hour, you say *bonjour* and then comment on it — you don't change the greeting.

And the golden rule of French daily life: **bonjour comes first, always.** Walking into a bakery, a pharmacy, a tiny shop — you greet before you ask for anything. Launching straight into *"Une baguette, s'il vous plaît"* without a *bonjour* marks you instantly, and in Paris you may get a pointed *"Bonjour."* back as a correction.

## Variations

| Phrase | When to use it | Register |
|--------|----------------|----------|
| Bonjour | Universal — anyone, anywhere, until evening | Neutral, always safe |
| Bonjour à tous | "Good morning, everyone" — groups, meetings | Neutral |
| Salut | "Hi" — friends, family, peers only | Casual |
| Coucou | "Hey you!" — affectionate, texts and close friends | Very casual, playful |
| Bonsoir | The evening switch, from dusk onward | Neutral |
| Bonne journée | "Have a good day" — said when **leaving** | Neutral |
| Bonne matinée | "Have a good (rest of the) morning" — also a goodbye | Neutral |
| Re-bonjour / Re ! | Jokey second greeting when you cross paths again | Casual |

The pair that trips everyone up: **bonjour vs bonne journée**. *Bonjour* opens the interaction; *bonne journée* closes it. Greet the barista with *bonjour*, leave with *merci, bonne journée !* Reverse them and the sentence still parses — it just sounds charmingly backwards.

One more distinctly French rule: **you only say bonjour to the same person once per day.** Greeting a coworker with a second *bonjour* at 3 p.m. quietly implies you forgot the first one. That's why the French invented the winking *re-bonjour* (or just *"re !"*) for second encounters.

## Hear it in context

**At the boulangerie (the morning classic):**

> — Bonjour madame !
> — Bonjour monsieur. Qu'est-ce que je vous sers ?
> — Deux croissants et une baguette, s'il vous plaît.
> — Et voilà. Bonne journée !
> — Merci, à vous aussi !

*Good morning, ma'am! — Good morning, sir. What can I get you? — Two croissants and a baguette, please. — There you go. Have a good day! — Thanks, you too!*

**Between coworkers (casual):**

> — Salut Julie ! Ça va ce matin ?
> — Coucou ! Ça va, mais il me faut un café. T'en veux un ?
> — Volontiers. La machine est réparée ?
> — Même pas. On descend au coin de la rue ?

*Hi Julie! How's it going this morning? — Hey! I'm fine, but I need a coffee. Want one? — Gladly. Is the machine fixed? — Not even. Shall we go down to the corner café?*

## Common mistakes

- **Inventing "bonne matin."** It doesn't exist. *Matin* is masculine anyway, and no version of it works as a greeting. Morning hello = *bonjour*, full stop.
- **Using "bonne journée" as hello.** It means "have a good day" and only works on your way out. Hello = *bonjour*; goodbye = *bonne journée*.
- **Skipping bonjour before a request.** The single fastest way to earn Parisian frost. Greeting first, request second — in shops, at counters, even when asking a stranger for directions (*Bonjour, excusez-moi...*).
- **Saying bonjour twice to the same person.** Once per day per person. Second sighting? *Re-bonjour*, a smile, or a nod.
- **Pronouncing the n and the j hard.** "Bon-JOOR" with a full n and an English j is the classic beginner tell. Nasalize the *on*, soften the *j* to "zh": bohn-ZHOOR.
- **Saying "salut" to a stranger.** *Salut* is for people you know. To a shopkeeper, a waiter, or anyone behind a counter, it's *bonjour* — anything less reads as overfamiliar.

## FAQ

**Is there a literal "good morning" in French?**
No. *Bonjour* ("good day") covers the whole morning and afternoon. *Bonne matinée* exists but means "have a good morning" — a parting wish, never a greeting.

**When do I switch from bonjour to bonsoir?**
Around dusk — in practice, anywhere from 5 to 7 p.m. depending on season and speaker. If someone greets you with *bonsoir*, just mirror it back.

**What's the difference between bonjour and bonne journée?**
*Bonjour* says hello when you arrive; *bonne journée* wishes someone a good day as you leave. They bookend the interaction and are not interchangeable.

**Can I say bonjour to the same person twice in one day?**
Convention says no — a second *bonjour* implies you forgot the first meeting. Use *re-bonjour* (playful), or just smile and nod.

**Is "salut" okay in the morning?**
Yes, with friends, family, and peers. With strangers, staff, elders, or anyone in a professional setting, stick to *bonjour* — *salut* would sound too familiar.
