Good luck in French is **bonne chance** — clean, warm, and useful before an exam, an interview, or a first date. But French has a second, subtler phrase for when the challenge is more about grit than luck (*bon courage*), and a delightfully weird theater tradition where wishing someone *bonne chance* is actually *bad* luck — so performers say **merde** instead. Here's the full picture.

## The main way to say it

**Bonne chance** — pronounced **bun SHAHNSS**.

Pronunciation notes:

- *Bonne* is "bun" — the *-onne* is *not* nasal here (the double *n* opens it up), unlike the nasal *bon* in *bon courage*.
- *Chance* has a soft **ch** ("sh") and a nasal *-an-*: SHAHNSS. Don't say "chance" the English way.
- To specify what for, add **pour**: *bonne chance pour ton examen* ("good luck on your exam") or **avec**: *bonne chance avec ton entretien*.

### Bonne chance vs. bon courage

This distinction is very French and worth internalizing:

- **Bonne chance** (bun SHAHNSS) — wishes *luck*, for things partly out of the person's control: a lottery, an exam result, a job they've applied for.
- **Bon courage** (bohn koo-RAHZH) — wishes *strength/endurance*, for something hard the person has to push through: a long shift, a tough day, a difficult task, moving apartments. It's "hang in there / you've got this," and the French use it constantly.

If a friend faces a grueling day, *bon courage* is warmer and more apt than *bonne chance* — you're not wishing them luck, you're wishing them stamina.

### Other ways to encourage

- **Je croise les doigts** (zhuh krwahz lay DWAH) — "I'm crossing my fingers."
- **Je pense à toi** — "I'm thinking of you," for support before a big moment.
- **Tu vas y arriver** — "you're going to make it / you've got this."
- **Bon courage à toi** — a warm, personal send-off.

## The theater tradition: say "merde"

In French theater, dance, and even before exams, wishing someone *bonne chance* is thought to **jinx** them. Instead, you say **merde** — yes, literally the word for a certain expletive. The tradition dates to the era of horse-drawn carriages: a full, successful show meant many horses parked outside, and thus a lot of… *merde* on the street. More horse dung = bigger audience = good omen. Performers still whisper *"merde !"* to each other before curtain, and the superstitious reply is *not* "merci" (that breaks the charm) — you just accept it or say *merde* back. Students say it before exams too. It's crude on paper, affectionate in practice.

## Hear it in context

**Before an exam (standard):**

> — Bon, j'y vais, j'ai mon oral dans une heure.
> — Bonne chance ! Tu vas gérer, tu as bien révisé.
> — Merci, je croise les doigts.

*Right, I'm off, I've got my oral exam in an hour. — Good luck! You'll handle it, you studied well. — Thanks, fingers crossed.*

**Before a performance (theater tradition):**

> — C'est bientôt à nous. Stressée ?
> — Un peu… Allez, merde pour ce soir !
> — Merde à toi aussi !

*We're on soon. Nervous? — A little… Come on, "merde" for tonight! — "Merde" to you too!*

## Common mistakes

- **Using *bonne chance* when you mean *bon courage*.** For something hard and grinding (a long day, a tough task), *bon courage* is the natural, warmer choice — the French use it far more than learners expect.
- **Nasalizing *bonne*.** In *bonne chance* it's "bun," open, not the nasal "bohn" of *bon courage*. The double *n* is the clue.
- **Replying "merci" to a theatrical *merde*.** Per the superstition, thanking breaks the luck. Just accept it or say *merde* back.
- **Saying "chance" the English way.** It's SHAHNSS, with a soft "sh" and a nasal vowel.
- **Forgetting the connector.** "Good luck with X" is *bonne chance pour X* or *avec X*, not *bonne chance sur X*.

## A Parisian note

*Bon courage* is one of the most quietly Parisian things you can say — you'll hear it exchanged between strangers all day: a shopkeeper to a delivery driver in the rain, a colleague to someone facing a mountain of work, a neighbor to a parent wrangling three kids up the stairs. It signals solidarity in the daily grind, and offering it to a stressed stranger is a small, real kindness the French appreciate. *Bonne chance*, by contrast, is saved for genuine luck-of-the-draw moments. And yes — a Parisian actor friend will absolutely hiss *"merde !"* at you before your big moment, and mean it with total affection.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "bonne chance" and "bon courage"?**
*Bonne chance* wishes luck for things partly out of one's control (an exam result, a job application). *Bon courage* wishes strength and stamina for something hard to push through (a long day, a tough task). The French use *bon courage* constantly.

**Why do French people say "merde" for good luck?**
It's a theater superstition: wishing *bonne chance* is thought to jinx a performance. The word traces back to horse-drawn carriage days, when more dung outside meant a bigger audience — so *merde* became a good omen. Performers and students still say it before big moments.

**How do I respond when someone says "merde" to me?**
Don't say "merci" — the superstition holds that thanking breaks the luck. Just accept it, nod, or say *merde* back.

**How do you say "good luck with your exam"?**
*Bonne chance pour ton examen* (informal) or *bonne chance pour votre examen* (formal). *Bon courage pour ton examen* also works if you're emphasizing endurance over luck.

**Is "bonne chance" formal or casual?**
It's neutral — appropriate in both casual and formal settings. To make it warmer, add *à toi* (*bonne chance à toi*) or specify the occasion with *pour*.
