French doesn't have one "see you later" — it has a little family of them, and each one quietly encodes *when* you expect to see the person again. Say the wrong one and you've promised to meet in five minutes when you meant next month. The three you'll use most are **à plus** (casual, open-ended), **à tout à l'heure** (later today), and **à bientôt** (soon-ish, no fixed time). Getting the timing right is the whole game.

## The main ways to say it

**À plus** — pronounced **ah PLEUSS** (the *s* is pronounced here, unusually).

**À tout à l'heure** — pronounced **ah-too-tah-LEUR**.

**À bientôt** — pronounced **ah byan-TOH**.

Pronunciation notes worth knowing:

- In **à plus**, the final *s* is voiced — *ah PLUSS* — when it's a casual goodbye. Confusingly, in *à plus tard* the *s* of *plus* goes silent (ah plew TAHR). French *plus* is a chameleon like that.
- **À bientôt** has a nasal *-en* (byan) and a nasal *-on* pushed toward "oh." Keep both airy.
- The written **à** with the grave accent is just the preposition "to/at" — it starts nearly every French goodbye of this type.

### Which one, when

- **À tout à l'heure** — you'll see them again *the same day*. Often shortened in speech to *à tout' *.
- **À plus tard** — "see you later," genuinely later today or soon; neutral and safe.
- **À plus** — the texting/casual clip of *à plus tard*. Friends, chats, offhand goodbyes.
- **À bientôt** — "see you soon," warm but with no set time. Great for ending a friendly message.
- **À demain** — "see you tomorrow" (literally). Same pattern: *à + [when]*.
- **À la prochaine** — "till next time," casual and friendly.

Notice the logic: **à + a time word = a goodbye promising that timeframe.** Once you see the pattern, you can build your own: *à ce soir* (see you tonight), *à lundi* (see you Monday), *à la semaine prochaine* (see you next week).

## Hear it in context

**Leaving the office, back soon (casual):**

> — Je file déjeuner, à tout à l'heure !
> — Ok, à plus. Ramène-moi un café si tu peux.
> — Ça marche !

*I'm off to lunch, see you in a bit! — Ok, see you. Grab me a coffee if you can. — Will do!*

**Ending a message with a friend (warm, no fixed time):**

> — C'était super de te voir. On se refait ça vite.
> — Carrément ! À bientôt, bises.
> — À bientôt !

*It was great seeing you. Let's do it again soon. — Absolutely! See you soon, kisses. — See you soon!*

## Common mistakes

- **Saying *à tout à l'heure* when you won't see them today.** It specifically means *later today*. If there's no plan to meet again soon, *à bientôt* or *à la prochaine* is the honest choice.
- **Pronouncing the *s* in *à plus tard*.** Here it's silent: ah plew TAHR. But in the standalone *à plus*, you *do* say it. Yes, it's inconsistent.
- **Treating *à bientôt* as a firm plan.** It's the friendly, vague "see you soon" — like "let's catch up sometime," not an appointment.
- **Overusing *au revoir* with friends.** *Au revoir* is the neutral "goodbye," never wrong, but among friends the *à + time* goodbyes feel warmer and more natural.
- **Forgetting *salut* can also mean bye.** Among friends, *salut* works as both hello and a casual goodbye.

## A Parisian note

In Paris, the goodbye is rarely just words — it's paired with **la bise**, the cheek kisses (two in Paris, one on each side). Friends part with *"Allez, à bientôt !"* and a quick brush of cheeks; colleagues might just wave a *"Bonne soirée, à demain."* Text-message French leans hard on **à plus**, often abbreviated to **A+** — you'll see it constantly in messages and comments. And Parisians love stacking the closing ritual: *"Bon, allez, je file — à plus, bonne journée, bises !"* The pile-up isn't padding; it's the warmth.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "à plus" and "à bientôt"?**
*À plus* (short for *à plus tard*) means "see you later," fairly soon and casual. *À bientôt* means "see you soon" — warmer but vaguer, with no implied timeframe. Both are friendly; *à plus* is the more offhand of the two.

**Do you pronounce the "s" in "à plus"?**
In the standalone casual goodbye *à plus*, yes — say "ah PLUSS." But in the fuller *à plus tard*, the *s* goes silent: "ah plew TAHR." It's one of French's quirky exceptions.

**What does "à tout à l'heure" mean exactly?**
"See you in a little while" — specifically later the *same day*. Don't use it if you won't see the person again today; reach for *à bientôt* instead.

**Is "à plus" too informal for work?**
With close colleagues it's fine. In more formal or hierarchical settings, *à tout à l'heure*, *à demain*, or a simple *au revoir* is safer.

**How do the French say "see you later" in a text?**
Usually *à plus*, often shortened to **A+**. You'll also see *à toute* (clipped *à tout à l'heure*) and *à +* with the plus sign.
