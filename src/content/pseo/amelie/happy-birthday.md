Happy birthday in French is **joyeux anniversaire !** — and the good news is that, unlike in Mexico, the French sing it to the exact same melody as "Happy Birthday to You," so you already know the tune. What you need is the phrase itself, the very common alternative *bon anniversaire*, and one trap to avoid: *bonne fête*, which means happy birthday in Québec but something completely different in Paris. Get all of it here and you'll wish someone well like a local, not like a phrasebook.

## The main way to say it

**Joyeux anniversaire !** — pronounced **zhwah-yeuh zah-nee-vehr-SEHR**.

Breaking it down:

- *Joyeux* = joyful/happy; *anniversaire* = birthday, from the Latin for "the returning year." Note that *anniversaire* covers both "birthday" and "anniversary" in French — context (or *anniversaire de mariage* for weddings) does the sorting.
- The **j** in *joyeux* is the soft French j, like the *s* in "measure": **zhwah-YEUH**. No hard English "j."
- There's a **liaison**: because *anniversaire* starts with a vowel, the silent x of *joyeux* wakes up as a z sound. Said naturally, it's one flowing phrase: *joyeuz‿anniversaire* — **zhwah-yeuh-**zah**-nee-vehr-SEHR**. Pausing between the two words is the fastest way to sound like a textbook.

The equally correct alternative is **bon anniversaire !** (**bohn ah-nee-vehr-SEHR**) — "good birthday!" Both are used everywhere in France; *joyeux anniversaire* is what everyone sings, *bon anniversaire* is arguably what you'll hear slightly more often in speech. Pick either; nobody will blink.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | When to use it |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| Joyeux anniversaire ! | Happy birthday! | Universal, any register — and it's the song |
| Bon anniversaire ! | Happy birthday! | Just as common in conversation |
| Joyeux anniv' ! | Happy bday! | Casual clipped form — friends, texts |
| Bon anniv' ! | Happy bday! | Same, extremely common in texts |
| Passe une excellente journée ! | Have an excellent day! | Warm follow-up (tu form) |
| Je te souhaite un très joyeux anniversaire | I wish you a very happy birthday | Fuller, card-worthy (tu form) |
| Je vous souhaite un joyeux anniversaire | I wish you a happy birthday | Polite/formal (vous form) |
| Que tous tes vœux se réalisent ! | May all your wishes come true! | Sweet, for cards and toasts |

**Tu or vous?** The phrase *joyeux anniversaire* itself is register-neutral — you can say it to your best friend or your boss. The register shows up in what you wrap around it: *je **te** souhaite...* with friends and family, *je **vous** souhaite...* with your manager, your in-laws (early on), or anyone you'd address formally. When in doubt in a professional setting, go with *vous*.

**The cultural package.** French birthdays come with their own rituals:

- **The song is the same tune, French words:** *Joyeux anniversaire, joyeux anniversaire, joyeux anniversaire [prénom], joyeux anniversaire !* Then the candles: the birthday person makes a silent wish (*faire un vœu*) and must blow them all out in one breath for it to come true.
- **You bring your own cake.** In a Parisian office, the birthday person treats the team — croissants in the morning or a cake at lunch. If it's your birthday in France, the pastries are on you. Delightfully backwards, very French.
- **La bise:** among friends and family, birthday wishes often come with cheek kisses. In the office, a handshake or just the words are fine.

## Hear it in context

**Texting a friend at midnight (casual):**

> — Joyeux anniv' !! 🎉 Passe une super journée.
> — Merci ! Tu viens samedi pour fêter ça ?
> — Évidemment ! J'apporte quoi ?
> — Rien, juste ta bonne humeur.

*Happy bday!! Have a great day. — Thanks! Are you coming Saturday to celebrate? — Obviously! What should I bring? — Nothing, just your good mood.*

**At the office, cake time (polite):**

> — Bon anniversaire, Claire ! Je vous souhaite une très belle journée.
> — Merci beaucoup ! J'ai apporté des croissants, servez-vous.
> — Alors là, c'est le meilleur anniversaire de l'année.

*Happy birthday, Claire! I wish you a lovely day. — Thank you so much! I brought croissants, help yourselves. — Well then, this is the best birthday of the year.*

## Common mistakes

- **Saying "bonne fête" in France.** In Québec, *bonne fête !* means happy birthday. In France, it refers to someone's **saint's day** (*la fête du prénom* — every first name has a date on the calendar). Wish a Parisian *bonne fête* on their birthday and you'll get a puzzled smile.
- **Skipping the liaison.** *Joyeux + anniversaire* must link with a z: zhwah-yeuh-**zah**-nee-vehr-SEHR. A hard stop between the words instantly flags you as a beginner.
- **"Heureux anniversaire."** *Heureux* does mean happy, but nobody uses it here. The fixed phrases are *joyeux* or *bon anniversaire* — full stop.
- **Translating "anniversary" carelessly.** *Anniversaire* alone usually means birthday. For a wedding anniversary, say *anniversaire de mariage*, or you may accidentally wish your colleague a happy birthday on their wedding date.
- **Hard English "j" in joyeux.** It's the soft *zh* of "vision," not the "j" of "joy." One small consonant, big authenticity gain.

## FAQ

**Is it "joyeux anniversaire" or "bon anniversaire"?**
Both are correct and both are used all over France. *Joyeux anniversaire* is the one in the song; *bon anniversaire* is at least as common in everyday speech. Pick whichever you pronounce more confidently.

**How do you say "happy belated birthday" in French?**
*Joyeux anniversaire en retard !* or *bon anniversaire avec un peu de retard* — completely normal to say or text when you missed the day.

**Does "bonne fête" mean happy birthday?**
Only in Québec. In France, *bonne fête* is for someone's saint's day (name day), not their birthday. Stick to *joyeux anniversaire* in Paris.

**Do the French sing "Happy Birthday"?**
Yes — same melody as the English song, with French lyrics: *Joyeux anniversaire* repeated four times, with the person's name in the third line. Candles, silent wish, one breath.

**How do I make it more formal?**
Wrap it in a vous-form sentence: *Je vous souhaite un très joyeux anniversaire.* The core phrase stays the same; the pronoun does the politeness.
