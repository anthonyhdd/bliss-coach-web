Sweet dreams in Spanish is **dulces sueños** — pronounced *DOOL-sehs SWEH-nyohs*. It's a direct match for the English phrase: *dulce* means sweet, *sueños* means dreams, and together they're the tender little bow you tie on top of *buenas noches*.

But here's what textbooks skip: in Mexico, *dulces sueños* is only one member of a whole family of bedtime wishes, and it's not even the most common one. The everyday workhorse is **que descanses** — "rest well" — which is what most Mexicans actually say to anyone heading to bed. Learn both, and you'll know exactly which one to reach for.

## The main way to say it

**Dulces sueños** — *DOOL-sehs SWEH-nyohs*

Pronunciation pointers:

- The **ñ** in *sueños* is the "ny" sound in "canyon": SWEH-**nyohs**. If you say *swen-os* with a plain n, you're accidentally saying a different (nonexistent) word.
- The **ue** in *sueños* glides: "sweh," one syllable, not "su-eh."
- Both words stress the first syllable: **DOOL**-sehs **SWEH**-nyohs.

Grammar in ten seconds: *sueño* means both "dream" and "sleepiness" — *tengo sueño* is "I'm sleepy," not "I have a dream." As a bedtime wish it's always plural, *dulces sueños*, wishing someone several sweet dreams, and the adjective agrees: *dulce**s** sueño**s***.

**Who do you say it to?** Kids, partners, close friends, family. It's affectionate by nature. You wouldn't text your landlord *dulces sueños* — for neutral situations, *que descanses* or plain *buenas noches* does the job.

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| Dulces sueños | DOOL-sehs SWEH-nyohs | The classic "sweet dreams" — affectionate |
| Que descanses | keh dess-KAHN-sess | "Rest well" — the everyday Mexican default (informal *tú*) |
| Que descanse | keh dess-KAHN-seh | Same wish, formal *usted* — elders, guests |
| Que tengas dulces sueños | keh TEN-gahs... | "May you have sweet dreams" — the full, extra-warm version |
| Que sueñes con los angelitos | keh SWEH-nyess kohn lohs ahn-heh-LEE-tohs | "Dream of the little angels" — the classic Mexican phrase for children |
| Que sueñes bonito | keh SWEH-nyess boh-NEE-toh | "Dream something lovely" — very Mexican, very sweet |
| Descansa | dess-KAHN-sah | "Rest" — short, warm command form, common in texts |
| Hasta mañana, que descanses | AHS-tah mah-NYAH-nah... | "See you tomorrow, rest well" — the full sign-off combo |

Two of these deserve a spotlight. **Que sueñes con los angelitos** — "dream of the little angels" — is what Mexican parents and grandparents have whispered to children for generations; use it with a kid and you'll sound like you grew up there. And **que sueñes bonito** is the grown-up cousin: soft, affectionate, and very common in late-night texts between couples. If someone sends you *que sueñes bonito*, that's a good sign.

Note that *que descanses* and *que sueñes* use the **subjunctive** (-es ending) because they're wishes: "may you rest," "may you dream." That final -es is what makes it a blessing instead of a statement.

## Hear it in context

**Couple saying good night by text:**

> — Ya me voy a dormir, mañana entro temprano.
> — Bueno, descansa. Dulces sueños, amor.
> — Igualmente. Que sueñes bonito.

*— I'm going to sleep now, I start early tomorrow.*
*— Okay, rest up. Sweet dreams, love.*
*— You too. Dream something lovely.*

**Grandma putting a child to bed:**

> — ¿Ya te lavaste los dientes?
> — Sí, abuelita.
> — Muy bien. A dormir. Que sueñes con los angelitos, mi cielo.
> — Buenas noches, abue.

*— Did you brush your teeth?*
*— Yes, grandma.*
*— Very good. Off to sleep. Dream of the little angels, sweetheart.*
*— Good night, grandma.*

Notice the natural replies: **igualmente** ("likewise") and a simple *buenas noches* back. You don't need to invent a new wish — mirroring is exactly what natives do.

## Common mistakes

- **"Dulce sueños."** The adjective must be plural too: *dulce**s** sueños*. Mixed singular-plural is a dead giveaway.
- **Confusing "sueño" (dream) with "sueño" (sleepiness).** *Tengo sueño* = "I'm sleepy." *Tuve un sueño* = "I had a dream." Same word, context decides — but the bedtime wish is always *dulces sueños*.
- **Saying "que descansas" instead of "que descanses."** Wishes take the subjunctive: *que descans**es***. With -as it's just an odd statement ("that you rest"), not a wish.
- **Using "dulces sueños" in neutral or formal settings.** It's affectionate. To a hotel receptionist or your boss, *buenas noches* or *que descanse* is the right register.
- **Translating "sleep tight" literally.** There's no *duerme apretado*. The Spanish equivalents are *que descanses* or *duerme bien* ("sleep well").

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "dulces sueños" and "que descanses"?**
*Dulces sueños* ("sweet dreams") is affectionate — for kids, partners, close friends. *Que descanses* ("rest well") is the everyday default you can say to almost anyone going to bed, including in polite settings (as *que descanse*).

**Is "dulces sueños" romantic?**
It can be, especially in a late-night text, but it's not exclusively romantic — parents say it to children constantly. *Que sueñes bonito* and *dulces sueños, amor* lean more clearly romantic.

**How do I reply to "dulces sueños"?**
*Igualmente* ("likewise"), *gracias, tú también* ("thanks, you too"), or return the wish: *dulces sueños para ti también*.

**What does "que sueñes con los angelitos" mean?**
Literally "may you dream of the little angels." It's the classic Mexican bedtime phrase for children — tender, traditional, and instantly recognizable to anyone raised in Mexico.

**Do people in Spain say "dulces sueños" too?**
Yes — *dulces sueños* is understood and used everywhere Spanish is spoken. The children's phrase *que sueñes con los angelitos*, though, is distinctly Latin American and especially Mexican.
