Be careful in Spanish is **ten cuidado** — literally "have care." But this is a phrase family with real range: there's the gentle **cuídate** ("take care") that doubles as an affectionate goodbye, the urgent shout **¡cuidado!** when a cyclist is about to flatten someone, and the gloriously Mexican **¡aguas!** — literally "waters!" — which is how Mexico City warns you about everything from wet floors to oncoming traffic. Knowing which one to deploy, and how fast, is the point of this guide.

## The main way to say it

**Ten cuidado** — pronounced **ten kwee-DAH-doh**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- Stress lands on **DAH**: kwee-**DAH**-doh. Four smooth syllables total, with *cui-* gliding as one beat (kwee), not "koo-ee."
- The final **d** in *cuidado* softens between vowels in native speech — closer to the *th* in "this." A regular d is understood everywhere; the soft one just sounds more native.
- The word *cuidado* is the noun "care/caution"; *ten* is the informal command "have." So *ten cuidado* = "have caution."

The formal version swaps the command: **tenga cuidado** (for *usted*). And for a group: **tengan cuidado**.

For sudden danger, drop the verb entirely and shout the noun: **¡Cuidado!** — the Spanish equivalent of "Watch out!" It's what you yell, not what you advise.

There's also the verb *cuidarse* ("to take care of oneself"), which gives us the single most affectionate member of the family: **cuídate** (KWEE-dah-teh) — "take care (of yourself)." In Mexico it's a standard warm way to end a conversation, right up there with *nos vemos*.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Ten cuidado | Be careful (informal) | Universal, everyday advice |
| Tenga cuidado | Be careful (formal *usted*) | Formal, polite |
| ¡Cuidado! | Watch out! | Urgent — immediate danger |
| Cuídate | Take care (of yourself) | Warm, standard goodbye |
| Cuídate mucho | Take good care of yourself | Extra warm, affectionate |
| ¡Aguas! | Watch out! / heads up! | Mexican slang — very common |
| ¡Ojo! | Careful! / pay attention! ("eye!") | Casual warning, universal |
| ¡Ábrete! / ¡Quítate! | Move! / get out of the way! | Urgent, blunt |
| Con cuidado | Carefully / easy does it | Gentle — handing something fragile |
| Ve con cuidado | Travel safely / go carefully | Warm send-off for someone leaving |

The star here is **¡aguas!** — the most Mexican warning there is. The origin story: in colonial times, people emptied wash water out of upper windows into the street and shouted *¡aguas!* ("waters!") so pedestrians below could dodge. The water is gone; the shout survived. Today it covers any incoming hazard: *¡Aguas con el escalón!* ("watch the step!"), *¡aguas con ese perro!* ("careful with that dog!"). It's informal but universally understood in Mexico — and using it well is instant street cred.

**¡Ojo!** — literally "eye!" — is the softer cousin, often accompanied by a finger pointing below the eye. It means "pay attention to this," great for warnings about scams, tricky contracts, or gossip: *Ojo con ese vendedor* ("watch out for that salesman").

## Hear it in context

**Saying goodbye after a visit (warm):**

> — Bueno, ya me voy. Está lloviendo horrible.
> — ¿Manejas tú? Ve con cuidado, ¿eh?
> — Sí, tranquila. Nos vemos el jueves.
> — Va. ¡Cuídate mucho!

*Well, I'm off. It's raining horribly. — You're driving? Go carefully, okay? — Yeah, don't worry. See you Thursday. — Cool. Take good care of yourself!*

**On the street in Mexico City (urgent, very Mexican):**

> — ¡Aguas, aguas! ¡Viene una bici!
> — ¡Uf! Casi no la veo. Gracias.
> — Ten cuidado, aquí pasan volando.

*Watch out, watch out! Bike coming! — Whew! I almost didn't see it. Thanks. — Be careful, they fly through here.*

## Common mistakes

- **"Sé cuidado."** Translating "be" literally with *ser*. Spanish "has" care rather than "being" it: *ten cuidado*, with *tener*. (*Sé cuidadoso* — with the adjective — exists, but it's stiff; nobody warns you that way.)
- **Using "cuídate" for immediate danger.** *Cuídate* is a warm "take care of yourself" for goodbyes and life advice. If a car is coming, it's *¡cuidado!* or *¡aguas!* — short, loud, now.
- **"Ten cuidado de..." vs "ten cuidado con..."** For warning about a specific thing, Spanish overwhelmingly uses **con**: *ten cuidado **con** el tráfico* ("be careful with the traffic"). Reaching for *de* by analogy with English "careful of" is the classic slip.
- **Hearing "¡aguas!" and looking for water.** It has nothing to do with rain or drinks anymore — someone is telling you to watch out, right now. React first, ask questions later.
- **Stressing the wrong syllable in "cuídate."** It's **KWEE**-dah-teh, stress up front — that's the accent mark's job. Saying "kwee-DAH-teh" turns the command into something that sounds off to native ears.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "ten cuidado" and "cuídate"?**
*Ten cuidado* warns about something specific and external — traffic, a scam, an icy step. *Cuídate* is broader and warmer: "take care of yourself," used as a goodbye or general well-wishing. One points at a hazard; the other at the person.

**What does "¡aguas!" really mean in Mexico?**
"Watch out!" It dates back to colonial times, when people shouted it before tossing wash water out of windows. Today it's the all-purpose Mexican heads-up for any incoming hazard — informal, but understood by absolutely everyone.

**Is "¡cuidado!" rude to shout at a stranger?**
Not at all — in urgent situations it's expected and appreciated. Shouting *¡cuidado!* or *¡aguas!* at a stranger about to step into traffic is a public service, not an intrusion.

**How do I say "be careful with..." something specific?**
*Ten cuidado con* + the thing: *ten cuidado con el perro* (careful with the dog), *ten cuidado con lo que firmas* (careful what you sign). The preposition is *con*, not *de*.

**Do people in Spain say "¡aguas!"?**
No — it's distinctly Mexican (and Central American). In Spain you'll hear *¡cuidado!* or *¡ojo!* instead. Both of those work everywhere, so they're your safe defaults outside Mexico.
