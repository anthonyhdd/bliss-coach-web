Goodbye in Spanish is **adiós** — but here's the twist textbooks skip: native speakers use it far less than you'd expect. In Mexico, *adiós* can feel weirdly final, like you're leaving forever. Day to day, people reach for **hasta luego** ("see you later") or **nos vemos** ("we'll see each other") instead. Learning which farewell fits which moment is the difference between exiting a conversation smoothly and accidentally sounding like you're boarding a one-way flight.

## The main way to say it

**Adiós** — pronounced **ah-DYOHS**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- It's **two syllables**, not three: ah-DYOHS. English speakers love to stretch it into "ah-dee-OHS"; natives compress the *-diós* into one smooth beat.
- The stress lands on the final syllable — that's what the written accent on the **ó** is telling you.
- The final **s** is crisp in Mexican Spanish. (In Caribbean Spanish it often softens or disappears, but in Mexico, say it.)

Etymology bonus: *adiós* comes from the old phrase *a Dios* — "(I commend you) to God." That religious weight is part of why it can feel heavy: historically it was what you said before a long, uncertain separation. The everyday goodbyes below are lighter.

One genuinely Mexican quirk worth knowing: in Mexico, **adiós doubles as a passing greeting**. If you walk past a neighbor on the street and neither of you is stopping, a wave plus *¡adiós!* means "hi and bye at once." It confuses learners every time.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Adiós | Goodbye | Universal, slightly final |
| Hasta luego | See you later | Universal — the everyday default |
| Nos vemos | See you / we'll see each other | Casual, warm, extremely common in Mexico |
| Hasta mañana | See you tomorrow | Universal |
| Hasta pronto | See you soon | Warm, a bit more heartfelt |
| Que te vaya bien | "May it go well for you" | The Mexican send-off — said to the person *leaving* |
| Que estés bien | Take care / be well | Warm, casual |
| Cuídate | Take care | Casual, affectionate |
| Bye / bai | Bye | Very casual — yes, Mexicans say it constantly |
| Ahí nos vemos | See you around | Casual, Mexican |
| Ahí nos vidrios | Slangy "see ya" (pun on *nos vemos*) | Playful Mexican slang, friends only |

The one to steal from Mexican daily life is **que te vaya bien**. When you leave a shop, the clerk will almost always send you off with it — literally "may it go well for you." The polite move is to answer *gracias, igualmente* ("thanks, same to you"). Note the direction: you say it to the person who's leaving, so if *you're* the one staying put, this is your line.

And don't sleep on plain **bye** — English loanword, pronounced "bai," and genuinely one of the most common casual goodbyes among Mexican friends, especially on the phone and in texts.

## Hear it in context

**Leaving a small shop (everyday politeness):**

> — Aquí está su cambio. ¡Que le vaya bien!
> — Gracias, igualmente. Hasta luego.
> — Adiós, ¡buen día!

*Here's your change. Have a good one! — Thanks, same to you. See you later. — Bye, have a good day!*

**Two friends wrapping up (casual, very Mexican):**

> — Bueno, ya me voy, que mañana madrugo.
> — Va. Nos vemos el sábado, ¿no?
> — Claro. ¡Cuídate! Ahí nos vidrios.
> — ¡Bye!

*Well, I'm off — I'm up early tomorrow. — Cool. See you Saturday, right? — Of course. Take care! See ya around. — Bye!*

## Common mistakes

- **Using "adiós" for a quick "see you tomorrow."** Grammatically fine, tonally heavy. If you'll see the person again soon, *hasta luego* or *nos vemos* is the natural pick; *adiós* leans toward longer or more definitive partings.
- **Three-syllable "ah-dee-OHS."** Compress it: ah-DYOHS, two beats, stress at the end.
- **"Hasta la vista."** Thanks to Terminator, learners think this is standard. Real speakers almost never say it — it sounds like a movie quote. Use *hasta luego*.
- **Answering "que te vaya bien" with another "que te vaya bien."** It's directional — it goes to the person leaving. If you're the one walking out the door, the reply is *gracias, igualmente*.
- **Leaving without saying anything.** In Mexico, exits are ritual. Slipping out of a gathering without a round of goodbyes (the *despedida* can take twenty minutes — it's a cultural institution) reads as cold or rude.

## FAQ

**Is "adiós" formal or informal?**
Neither — it works at every register. Its issue isn't formality but finality: it can feel like a longer or more definitive parting. For a casual "later!", *hasta luego* or *nos vemos* fits better.

**What's the difference between "hasta luego" and "nos vemos"?**
Both mean roughly "see you later." *Hasta luego* is the neutral all-purpose choice, safe with strangers and clerks; *nos vemos* is warmer and more personal, best with people you actually expect to see again.

**Do Mexicans really say "bye" in English?**
Constantly. "Bye" (often spelled *bai* in texts) is a completely normal casual goodbye in Mexico, especially ending phone calls. It's informal, so skip it in professional settings.

**What does "ahí nos vidrios" mean?**
It's playful slang for "see you around" — a pun swapping *vemos* ("we see") for *vidrios* ("glass panes") just because it sounds funny. Friends only; don't try it on your bank teller.

**How do I respond to "que te vaya bien"?**
*Gracias, igualmente* ("thanks, likewise") or just *gracias, hasta luego*. Don't repeat the phrase back as-is — it's meant for the person leaving, and if they said it to you, you're that person.
