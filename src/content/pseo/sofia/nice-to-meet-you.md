The way to say "nice to meet you" in Spanish is **mucho gusto** — two words, works everywhere, from a job interview in Monterrey to meeting your friend's cousins at a carne asada. It literally means "much pleasure," and in Mexico it's by far the standard choice. Learn it, learn the reply (**igualmente**), and you've mastered 90% of Spanish introductions. The other 10% — the variations, the handshake-and-cheek-kiss choreography, the follow-ups — is below.

## The main way to say it

**Mucho gusto** — pronounced *MOO-choh GOOS-toh*.

It's short for *mucho gusto en conocerte* ("much pleasure in meeting you"), but nobody needs the long version — the two-word form is complete and polite on its own. Use it the moment you're introduced:

- — Ella es Mariana. — **¡Mucho gusto, Mariana!** — This is Mariana. — Nice to meet you, Mariana!
- **Mucho gusto, soy David.** — Nice to meet you, I'm David.

When someone says *mucho gusto* to you, the standard reply is **igualmente** (*ee-gwahl-MEN-teh*) — "likewise" — or the slightly warmer **el gusto es mío** ("the pleasure is mine").

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | Register | When to use it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mucho gusto | MOO-choh GOOS-toh | Universal | The default — safe everywhere |
| Encantado / Encantada | en-kahn-TAH-doh / -dah | Neutral–formal | "Delighted" — match it to YOUR gender |
| Es un placer | ehs oon plah-SEHR | Formal | "It's a pleasure" — business settings |
| Mucho gusto en conocerte | ...en koh-noh-SEHR-teh | Warm | Full version, informal "you" |
| Mucho gusto en conocerlo/la | ...en koh-noh-SEHR-loh/lah | Formal | Full version with *usted* |
| Igualmente | ee-gwahl-MEN-teh | Universal | The reply: "likewise" |
| El gusto es mío | el GOOS-toh ehs MEE-oh | Warm | The reply: "the pleasure is mine" |
| Qué milanesas | keh mee-lah-NEH-sahs | Slang | Joke greeting for someone you haven't seen in ages |

About **encantado/encantada**: this one inflects for the *speaker's* gender — a man says *encantado*, a woman says *encantada*, regardless of who they're meeting. It's more common in Spain; in Mexico it's understood and perfectly fine, but *mucho gusto* dominates.

And **qué milanesas**? It's a Mexican pun — *milanesas* (breaded cutlets) stands in for *¡Qué milagro!* ("what a miracle!"), the phrase for bumping into someone you haven't seen in forever. The full joke version: *¡Qué milanesas que te dejas bisteces!* Pure play on food words. You won't use it when first meeting someone, but you'll hear it among friends and it's too good not to know.

## Hear it in context

**Dialog 1 — Being introduced at a party**

> — Oye, te presento a mi amiga Fernanda.
> — **¡Mucho gusto, Fernanda! Soy Alex.**
> — **Igualmente, Alex. ¿Y de dónde eres?**
>
> — Hey, let me introduce you to my friend Fernanda.
> — Nice to meet you, Fernanda! I'm Alex.
> — Likewise, Alex. And where are you from?

**Dialog 2 — A formal business introduction**

> — Buenos días, soy la licenciada Torres, encargada del proyecto.
> — **Mucho gusto en conocerla, licenciada. Es un placer trabajar con ustedes.**
> — **El gusto es mío.** Bienvenido al equipo.
>
> — Good morning, I'm Ms. Torres, in charge of the project.
> — Very nice to meet you, ma'am. It's a pleasure to work with you.
> — The pleasure is mine. Welcome to the team.

Cultural note: in Mexico, introductions come with physical choreography — a handshake in formal settings; between a man and a woman or two women in social settings, usually one light kiss on the cheek. Follow the local's lead and you can't go wrong.

## Common mistakes

1. **Saying "encantado" when you're a woman (or vice versa).** It agrees with the speaker: *encantado* if you're male, *encantada* if you're female. Mismatching it is the most common beginner slip with this phrase.

2. **Translating literally: "agradable conocerte."** Grammatical, but nobody says it. Fixed phrases win: *mucho gusto*.

3. **Leaving "mucho gusto" unanswered.** Silence after an introduction feels cold. Always volley back *igualmente* or *el gusto es mío*.

4. **Using "¿Cómo estás?" as the introduction itself.** In Mexico you greet, introduce, THEN ask how they are. *Hola, mucho gusto, soy Ana. ¿Cómo estás?* — that order sounds natural.

5. **Overdoing formality with young people.** *Es un placer conocerlo* to a 22-year-old at a house party creates distance. Match the room: casual setting, casual phrase.

## FAQ

**Do I say "mucho gusto" before or after hearing the person's name?**
Either works. Most naturally, you exchange names first — *Soy Ana. — Mucho gusto, Ana, soy Luis* — but leading with *mucho gusto* is fine too.

**Is "encantado" too formal for Mexico?**
Not too formal, just less common than in Spain. It reads slightly elegant. *Mucho gusto* is the everyday Mexican default.

**How do I say "nice to meet you too"?**
*Igualmente* covers it perfectly. For extra warmth: *el gusto es mío*.

**What about meeting someone again — "nice to see you"?**
That's *qué gusto verte* ("how nice to see you") or, after a long absence, *¡qué milagro!* — the phrase behind the *milanesas* joke above.
