The most common way to say "excuse me" in Spanish is **disculpe** (formal) or **disculpa** (casual) — but Spanish actually splits "excuse me" into three different phrases depending on what you're doing. If you're getting someone's attention, say *disculpe*. If you bumped into someone, say *perdón*. If you need to squeeze past someone, say *con permiso*. Pick the right one and you'll instantly sound like you know what you're doing.

## The main way to say it

**Disculpe** — pronounced *dees-KOOL-peh*.

This is your go-to when you want to get someone's attention politely: a waiter, a stranger on the street, a shop employee. It literally comes from the verb *disculpar* (to excuse/forgive), and in the *usted* (formal) form it's the safest choice with people you don't know.

- **Disculpe, ¿me puede ayudar?** — Excuse me, can you help me?
- **Disculpe, ¿cuánto cuesta esto?** — Excuse me, how much is this?

With friends, people your age, or anyone you'd address informally, switch to **disculpa** (*dees-KOOL-pah*).

## Variations

Here's the full toolkit, from formal to street-level Mexican:

| Spanish | Pronunciation | Register | When to use it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Disculpe | dees-KOOL-peh | Formal | Getting a stranger's or older person's attention |
| Disculpa | dees-KOOL-pah | Casual | Same, but with friends or peers |
| Perdón | pehr-DOHN | Neutral | You bumped into someone or made a small mistake |
| Con permiso | kohn pehr-MEE-soh | Polite | Passing through a crowd, leaving a table, reaching past someone |
| Permiso | pehr-MEE-soh | Casual | Short version of the above — very common in Mexico |
| ¿Mande? | MAHN-deh | Very Mexican | "Excuse me?" when you didn't hear what someone said |

A note on that last one: **¿Mande?** is one of the most Mexican words there is. Where a Spaniard might say *¿Qué?* or *¿Cómo?*, Mexicans overwhelmingly say *¿Mande?* when they didn't catch something. Using it in Mexico immediately signals you've learned real, living Spanish — not textbook Spanish.

And *con permiso* deserves special attention: in Mexico it's practically a social ritual. You say it when walking between two people talking, when getting off a crowded metro car, when leaving the dinner table. The standard reply is **propio** or simply **pase** ("go ahead").

## Hear it in context

**Dialog 1 — Getting a waiter's attention**

> — **Disculpe, ¿me trae la cuenta, por favor?**
> — Claro que sí, ahorita se la traigo.
>
> — Excuse me, could you bring me the check, please?
> — Of course, I'll bring it right away.

(Bonus Mexican flavor: *ahorita* means "right away"... in theory. In practice it can mean anywhere from ten seconds to never.)

**Dialog 2 — Squeezing through a crowded market**

> — **Con permiso... con permiso... gracias.**
> — Pásele, joven.
>
> — Excuse me... coming through... thanks.
> — Go ahead, young man.

*Pásele* is another very Mexican touch — vendors and hosts use it constantly to mean "come on in" or "go ahead."

## Common mistakes

1. **Using "perdón" for everything.** It works, but it's imprecise. *Perdón* implies a small apology. If you're just trying to get a waiter's attention, *disculpe* is the natural choice — *perdón* can make it sound like you did something wrong.

2. **Saying "excúseme."** It exists in dictionaries, but nobody says it in Mexico. It sounds like a word-for-word translation from English. Stick with *disculpe*.

3. **Forgetting "con permiso" entirely.** English speakers use "excuse me" for passing through, so they say *disculpe* while pushing past people. Mexicans will understand you, but the natural phrase in that situation is always *con permiso*.

4. **Mixing up disculpe and disculpa.** *Disculpe* = formal (usted), *disculpa* = informal (tú). Using the informal with an elderly stranger isn't offensive, but the formal version shows the respect Mexican culture expects.

5. **Answering "¿Mande?" with confusion.** If someone says *¿Mande?* to you, they're not giving you an order — they're asking you to repeat yourself. Just say it again, slower.

## FAQ

**Is "disculpe" or "perdón" more polite?**
They're equally polite — they just do different jobs. *Disculpe* opens a conversation; *perdón* apologizes for something small. For maximum courtesy with a stranger, *disculpe* is the standard opener.

**What do Mexicans say when someone sneezes?**
*¡Salud!* ("health"). It's automatic — even between strangers.

**Can I use "oye" to get attention?**
*Oye* ("hey, listen") works with friends, but it's too direct for strangers. With someone you don't know, *disculpe* first, always.

**How do I respond when someone says "con permiso" to me?**
Say *propio*, *pase*, or *adelante* — all mean "go ahead." Or just step aside with a smile; that works in every language.
