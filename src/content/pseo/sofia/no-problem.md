No problem in Spanish is **no hay problema** — clear, friendly, understood everywhere. But the phrase Mexicans actually reach for a hundred times a day is **no pasa nada** ("nothing happens / it's all good"), a beautifully relaxed way to wave off apologies, thanks, and minor disasters alike. Learn both, plus the fatalistic little **ni modo** ("oh well"), and you'll sound genuinely easygoing instead of like a textbook.

## The main way to say it

**No hay problema** — pronounced **noh eye pro-BLEH-mah**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- **Hay** sounds exactly like English "eye" — the *h* is silent, always.
- **Problema** stresses the middle: pro-**BLEH**-mah. Note it ends in *-a* but is a **masculine** noun (*el problema*) — a classic Greek-origin exception.
- Run it smoothly: *noh-eye-pro-BLEH-mah*.

The everyday Mexican alternative:

- **No pasa nada** — pronounced *noh PAH-sah NAH-dah*. Literally "nothing happens," used for "it's fine / don't worry about it / no big deal." It's warmer and more casual than *no hay problema*.

Both answer the same situations: someone apologizes, thanks you, or worries they've inconvenienced you. You reassure them: *no pasa nada* / *no hay problema*.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| No hay problema | No problem | Universal |
| No pasa nada | It's all good / no big deal | Casual, very Mexican |
| No te preocupes | Don't worry | Warm, reassuring |
| No hay bronca | No worries (bronca = trouble) | Casual, Mexican slang |
| Sin problema | No problem (agreeing to do something) | Casual |
| Está bien | It's fine / okay | Universal |
| Ni modo | Oh well / it can't be helped | Casual, resigned |
| Con gusto | With pleasure (reply to thanks) | Polite, Mexican |
| De nada | You're welcome | Universal |

Two Mexican gems: **no hay bronca** (*bronca* means "hassle/trouble") is street-friendly "no worries," and **ni modo** is the shrug of acceptance — "oh well, nothing to be done." *Ni modo* isn't quite "no problem"; it's used when something *did* go wrong and you're accepting it: *se acabó, ni modo* ("it's over, oh well").

When someone thanks you and you want to say "no problem" as a reply, Mexicans love **con gusto** ("with pleasure") and **de nada** ("you're welcome") — softer than a flat *no hay problema*.

## Hear it in context

**Reassuring someone who apologizes (casual):**

> — Perdón, llegué tardísimo, había mucho tráfico.
> — No pasa nada, apenas voy pidiendo.
> — ¿Seguro? Me siento mal.
> — En serio, no hay bronca. Siéntate.

*Sorry, I'm super late, there was a lot of traffic. — It's all good, I'm just now ordering. — You sure? I feel bad. — Seriously, no worries. Sit down.*

**Replying to a thank-you (polite):**

> — Muchas gracias por el aventón, en serio.
> — Con gusto, no hay problema. Me quedaba de camino.
> — Te debo una.
> — Para nada, cuando quieras.

*Thanks so much for the ride, really. — With pleasure, no problem. It was on my way. — I owe you one. — Not at all, anytime.*

## Common mistakes

- **Pronouncing the "h" in "hay."** It's silent — *hay* = "eye." *No hay problema* has no breathy "h" sound.
- **Making "problema" feminine.** Despite the *-a* ending, it's *el problema* (masculine): *no hay ningún problema*, not *ninguna problema*.
- **Using "ni modo" as a cheerful "no problem."** *Ni modo* is resigned ("oh well"), used when something went wrong. To brush off an apology warmly, use *no pasa nada* or *no te preocupes*.
- **Over-literal "no problemo."** That's a joke-English word, not Spanish. The real phrase is *no hay problema* or *no pasa nada*.

## Mexico vs Spain notes

*No hay problema* and *no pasa nada* are both used and understood across the whole Spanish-speaking world.

- **No pasa nada** is extremely common in both Mexico and Spain — one of those phrases that unites the language.
- **No hay bronca** is Mexican/Latin American slang; in Spain you'd more likely hear **no hay problema** or the casual **tranqui** (short for *tranquilo*, "chill").
- **Con gusto** as "you're welcome / no problem" is especially Mexican; Spain leans on **de nada** or **nada, nada**.
- Pronunciation is regionally neutral here — nothing lisps.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "no hay problema" and "no pasa nada"?**
Both mean "no problem." *No hay problema* is a touch more neutral/standard; *no pasa nada* ("it's all good") is warmer and more casual — the everyday Mexican reflex for shrugging off apologies or thanks.

**Is "problema" masculine or feminine?**
Masculine: *el problema*, despite the *-a* ending. It's a Greek-origin word, like *el tema* and *el sistema*. So it's *ningún problema*, not *ninguna*.

**What does "ni modo" mean?**
"Oh well / it can't be helped" — a resigned acceptance used when something went wrong, not a cheerful "no problem." Save it for *se canceló, ni modo* ("it got cancelled, oh well").

**Is "no problemo" real Spanish?**
No. It's a pop-culture joke. The genuine phrases are *no hay problema* and *no pasa nada*.

**How do I say "no problem" when someone thanks me?**
Use *de nada* ("you're welcome"), *con gusto* ("with pleasure"), or *no hay problema*. In Mexico, *con gusto* sounds especially warm and natural.
