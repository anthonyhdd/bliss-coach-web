I love it in Spanish is **me encanta** — the go-to way to say you love a thing, a place, a song, or an activity (as opposed to loving a *person*, which is a different verb entirely). The catch that trips up every learner: *encantar*, like *gustar*, works backwards from English. You don't "love it" — literally, "it enchants me." Once that click happens, a whole family of expressions opens up.

## The main way to say it

**Me encanta** — pronounced **meh en-KAHN-tah**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- **Me** is a short "meh."
- **Encanta** stresses the middle: en-**KAHN**-tah. The *en* is nasal and quick.
- Say it as a smooth phrase: *meh-en-KAHN-tah*, letting the words run together.

Now the grammar that matters. *Encantar* agrees with the **thing loved**, not with you:

- **Me encanta** + singular noun or a verb: *me encanta esta canción* ("I love this song"), *me encanta bailar* ("I love dancing").
- **Me encantan** + plural noun: *me encantan los tacos* ("I love tacos"), *me encantan tus zapatos* ("I love your shoes").

The *me* is the person doing the loving; the verb bends to match the item. So one song = *me encanta*, many tacos = *me encanta**n***. This is the same machinery as *me gusta / me gustan*.

Important: use **encantar** for *things and activities*. To say you love a *person* romantically, that's *te amo* / *te quiero* — a separate lane covered in its own guide.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Me encanta | I love it | Universal |
| Me encantan | I love them (plural) | Universal |
| Me gusta mucho | I really like it | Universal, a notch below "love" |
| Me fascina | I'm fascinated by it / I adore it | Emphatic |
| Me choca (opposite) | I can't stand it | Casual, Mexican |
| Me late | I'm into it / I like the idea | Casual, very Mexican |
| Está buenísimo / -a | It's amazing | Casual |
| ¡Qué padre! | How cool! / Love it! | Casual, Mexican |
| Lo amo (things, playful) | I love it (emphatic, informal) | Very casual, trendy |

The intensity ladder runs *me gusta* (I like it) → *me gusta mucho* (I really like it) → *me encanta* (I love it) → *me fascina* (I adore it). For everyday enthusiasm, Mexicans lean hard on **me encanta** and the exclamation **¡qué padre!** ("how cool!").

Two very Mexican extras: **me late** ("I'm into it," literally "it beats me," as in a heartbeat) for warming to an idea or plan, and its opposite **me choca** ("I can't stand it") when you *don't* love something.

## Hear it in context

**Reacting to food (everyday enthusiasm):**

> — Prueba el mole, es receta de mi abuela.
> — Mmm… ¡me encanta! ¿Qué lleva?
> — Chile, chocolate, muchas cosas. ¿Te gusta?
> — Me fascina. En serio, está buenísimo.

*Try the mole, it's my grandma's recipe. — Mmm… I love it! What's in it? — Chili, chocolate, lots of things. Do you like it? — I adore it. Seriously, it's amazing.*

**Agreeing to a plan (very Mexican):**

> — ¿Vamos a la playa el finde?
> — ¡Me late! ¿A quién más invitamos?
> — A los de siempre. Me encanta que se arme.
> — Órale, yo llevo la música.

*Want to go to the beach this weekend? — I'm into it! Who else should we invite? — The usual crew. I love that it's happening. — Cool, I'll bring the music.*

## Common mistakes

- **"Yo encanto esto."** *Encantar* doesn't work like English "I love." The thing does the enchanting: *me encanta esto* ("this enchants me"). The subject is the item, not you.
- **Not matching plural.** For plural things you need *encanta**n***: *me encanta**n** los perros* ("I love dogs"), not *me encanta los perros*.
- **Using "me encanta" for a person you're dating.** With people, *me encanta* means you find them lovely/attractive, but "I love you" romantically is *te amo* / *te quiero*. Don't swap them.
- **Adding "lo" needlessly.** You don't say *lo me encanta*. The *me* already carries it: just *me encanta*.

## Mexico vs Spain notes

*Me encanta* / *me encantan* is completely standard everywhere — no regional adjustment needed.

- **¡Qué padre!** ("how cool") is Mexican; a Spaniard would say **¡qué guay!** or **¡me flipa!** for the same "I love it" energy.
- **Me late** and **me choca** are Mexican colloquialisms; in Spain you'd hear **me mola** (I'm into it) and **no lo soporto** (I can't stand it) instead.
- Pronunciation is the same across regions — no lisped sounds in *encanta*.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "me encanta" and "me gusta"?**
*Me gusta* is "I like it"; *me encanta* is stronger — "I love it." There's also *me gusta mucho* ("I really like it") sitting between them. Use *me encanta* for genuine enthusiasm.

**When do I use "me encanta" vs "me encantan"?**
Match the thing loved: singular (or a verb) takes *me encanta* — *me encanta el café*; plural takes *me encantan* — *me encantan los libros*.

**Can I say "me encanta" about a person?**
Yes, but it means you find them wonderful or attractive, not "I love you." Romantic "I love you" is *te amo* or *te quiero*. Be careful not to confuse the two.

**What does "me late" mean?**
It's Mexican slang for "I'm into it / I like the idea," literally "it beats (like a heartbeat) to me." It's great for warming to a plan or suggestion.

**How do I say "I love it!" super casually in Mexico?**
*¡Me encanta!* or the exclamation *¡qué padre!* ("how cool!"). Both are everywhere in Mexican speech.
