The way to say "welcome" in Spanish is **¡Bienvenido!** — pronounced *byen-veh-NEE-doh* — literally "well come," just like the English. There's one twist English doesn't have: the word changes to match who you're welcoming. A man is *bienvenido*, a woman is *bienvenida*, a group is *bienvenidos* (or *bienvenidas* if it's all women).

And one giant clarification before anything else: this is "welcome" as a **greeting**. If you want "you're welcome" as the reply to "thank you," that's a completely different phrase — **de nada** — and mixing them up is the #1 mistake learners make with this word. We'll sort both out below.

## The main way to say it

**¡Bienvenido!** — *byen-veh-NEE-doh*

Pronunciation notes:

- Four syllables, stress on the third: byen-veh-**NEE**-doh.
- The **v** in Spanish sounds like a soft **b** — *bien-beh-NEE-doh* is actually closer to what natives say.
- The opening **bien** is one quick syllable: "byen," not "bee-en."

Now the agreement rule, because Spanish welcomes people precisely:

| Who arrives | You say |
|---|---|
| One man | ¡Bienvenido! |
| One woman | ¡Bienvenida! |
| A mixed group or all men | ¡Bienvenidos! |
| A group of women | ¡Bienvenidas! |

Add the destination with **a**: *Bienvenidos a México* (welcome to Mexico), *Bienvenida a la familia* (welcome to the family).

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| ¡Bienvenido/a/os/as! | byen-veh-NEE-doh | The standard welcome, matched to the guest |
| ¡Pásale! | PAH-sah-leh | Very Mexican: "come on in!" — at the door |
| Pásale, estás en tu casa | ... ehs-TAHS en too KAH-sah | "Come in, make yourself at home" — the full Mexican welcome |
| Mi casa es tu casa | mee KAH-sah ess too KAH-sah | "My house is your house" — famous for a reason; Mexicans really say it |
| Siéntete como en tu casa | SYEN-teh-teh KOH-moh en too KAH-sah | "Make yourself at home" |
| Adelante, por favor | ah-deh-LAHN-teh | Formal "please, come in" — offices, usted situations |
| ¡Qué gusto verte! | keh GOOS-toh VEHR-teh | "So good to see you!" — welcoming a familiar face |
| ¡Qué milagro! | keh mee-LAH-groh | "What a miracle!" — teasing welcome for someone you haven't seen in ages |

The cultural star here is **¡Pásale!** At a Mexican home, the door opens and before you can say a word you'll hear *"¡Pásale, pásale! ¿Ya comiste?"* — "Come in, come in! Have you eaten?" Hospitality in Mexico moves fast: you'll be welcomed, seated, and holding a plate within minutes. **Estás en tu casa** ("you're in your own house") isn't a figure of speech so much as a standing policy — and the correct response is warm thanks, not taking inventory of the fridge.

## Hear it in context

**Arriving at a friend's house:**

> — ¡Hola! Ya llegamos.
> — ¡Bienvenidos! Pásenle, pásenle, están en su casa.
> — Gracias. Trajimos un pastel.
> — ¡No se hubieran molestado! Siéntense, ¿qué les ofrezco de tomar?

*— Hi! We're here.*
*— Welcome! Come in, come in, make yourselves at home.*
*— Thanks. We brought a cake.*
*— You shouldn't have! Sit down — what can I get you to drink?*

**A new coworker's first day:**

> — Ella es Laura, empieza hoy en el equipo.
> — ¡Bienvenida, Laura! Qué gusto tenerte aquí.
> — Gracias, estoy muy emocionada.
> — Cualquier cosa que necesites, aquí estamos.

*— This is Laura, she starts on the team today.*
*— Welcome, Laura! Great to have you here.*
*— Thank you, I'm really excited.*
*— Whatever you need, we're here.*

Notice *pásenle* and *siéntense* in the first dialog — Mexican Spanish uses **ustedes** for any group, so door-side commands take the plural form.

## Common mistakes

- **Using "bienvenido" to mean "you're welcome."** Someone says *gracias*? The answer is **de nada** ("it's nothing"), **no hay de qué**, or the very Mexican **con gusto** / **para servirle**. Answering *"¡bienvenido!"* to a thank-you is a direct English calque and a guaranteed puzzled look.
- **Forgetting gender agreement.** Greeting a woman with *"¡Bienvenido!"* is a small but visible slip. Match the ending to the person: *bienvenida*.
- **Welcoming a group with the singular.** Two or more people get **bienvenidos** (or *bienvenidas*). English "welcome" never changes, so this one takes conscious practice.
- **Saying "bienvenido a" when you mean "you're welcome to..."** English "you're welcome to use the pool" is an offer, not a welcome: in Spanish, *puedes usar la alberca con confianza*. (*Eres bienvenido* does exist — *eres bienvenido cuando quieras*, "you're welcome anytime" — but it's about people being welcome, not permission to do things.)
- **Ignoring ¡Pásale! because it's not in the textbook.** If you only ever say *bienvenido* at the door, you'll sound like a hotel lobby sign. Mix in *pásale* and *estás en tu casa* and you'll sound like a host.

## FAQ

**How do I say "you're welcome" in Spanish?**
**De nada.** Also common in Mexico: *no hay de qué*, *con gusto*, and the courteous *para servirle*. Never *bienvenido* — that's only the greeting.

**What does "¡Pásale!" literally mean?**
"Pass on in." It's the everyday Mexican way to usher someone through the door. For a group: *¡Pásenle!*

**Is "mi casa es tu casa" really used, or is it a cliché?**
Really used — sincerely. You'll also hear the polite-form version *está usted en su casa*. It expresses hospitality, not a literal transfer of property.

**How do I welcome someone formally?**
*Bienvenido/a* plus **usted** forms: *Adelante, por favor. Está usted en su casa.* Perfect for meeting a partner's parents or receiving a client.
