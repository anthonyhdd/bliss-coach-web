What's your name in Spanish is **¿cómo te llamas?** — which, translated literally, asks "how do you call yourself?" That's not a typo: Spanish builds this question around the reflexive verb *llamarse* ("to call oneself") rather than the noun "name." It's usually the second sentence out of anyone's mouth in a new conversation, right after the hello — which makes it, along with the answer *me llamo...*, one of the highest-value lines a beginner can own.

## The main way to say it

**¿Cómo te llamas?** — pronounced **KOH-moh teh YAH-mahs**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- The double **ll** in *llamas* sounds like the English **y** in "yes" in Mexico: YAH-mahs. (In Argentina it becomes a "zh/sh" sound — SHAH-mahs — but that's a Río de la Plata thing.)
- Stress falls on **CÓ** and **LLA**: KOH-moh teh YAH-mahs. The accent on *cómo* is required in writing because it's a question word.
- Spanish questions get an upward melody at the end — and in writing, that opening **¿** is not optional decoration; it's part of correct spelling.

The grammar under the hood: *te llamas* = "you call yourself." So the question is literally "How do you call yourself?" and the answer mirrors it: **me llamo** ("I call myself") + your name. *Me llamo Ana. ¿Y tú?*

You have three ways to give your name back, in increasing casualness:

- **Me llamo Ana.** — "My name is Ana." The classic, always correct.
- **Soy Ana.** — "I'm Ana." Shorter, very natural in real life.
- **Ana.** — Just the name, with a handshake or a nod. Perfectly normal among peers.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| ¿Cómo te llamas? | What's your name? (informal *tú*) | Casual — peers, kids, most everyday settings |
| ¿Cómo se llama? | What's your name? (formal *usted*) | Formal — elders, clients, officials |
| ¿Cómo se llama usted? | Same, with *usted* spelled out | Extra clear/formal |
| ¿Cuál es tu nombre? | What is your name? (literal) | Neutral — common on forms and phone menus |
| ¿Cuál es su nombre? | Formal version of the above | Formal/administrative |
| ¿Cómo te llamas tú? | And *your* name is...? | Casual, adds emphasis when returning the question |
| ¿Y tú eres...? | And you are...? | Casual, friendly party-style opener |
| ¿Cómo se llama él/ella? | What's his/her name? | Asking about a third person |

The **tú vs. usted** choice is the whole game here. In Mexico, *¿cómo te llamas?* is fine with people your age or younger and in relaxed settings. With an elder, a client, or anyone you'd address as "sir/ma'am," switch to **¿cómo se llama?** — same verb, formal conjugation. When in doubt in Mexico, start formal; letting the other person invite you to *tutear* (use *tú*) is the polite path.

One heads-up on *¿cómo se llama?*: because *se* can also mean "he/she," this sentence can equally mean "what's his/her name?" Context sorts it out — eye contact means "you," a nod toward someone else means "them."

## Hear it in context

**Meeting at a party (casual):**

> — ¡Hola! ¿Cómo te llamas?
> — Me llamo Diego, ¿y tú?
> — Soy Mariana. Mucho gusto.
> — Igualmente.

*Hi! What's your name? — My name's Diego, and you? — I'm Mariana. Nice to meet you. — Likewise.*

**Checking in at an office (formal):**

> — Buenos días. ¿Cómo se llama usted?
> — Me llamo Carlos Herrera. Tengo cita a las diez.
> — Perfecto, señor Herrera. Tome asiento, por favor.

*Good morning. What is your name? — My name is Carlos Herrera. I have an appointment at ten. — Perfect, Mr. Herrera. Please take a seat.*

## Common mistakes

- **"¿Cómo te llamo?"** Mixing up the reflexive: *te llamo* means "I call you." The question needs *te llam**as*** — "you call yourself." One vowel, completely different sentence.
- **"Me llamo es Ana."** The Frankenstein blend of *me llamo Ana* and *mi nombre es Ana*. Pick one: *me llamo* already contains the verb, so no *es*.
- **"¿Qué es tu nombre?"** Translating "what" as *qué*. For names, Spanish uses *cuál*: *¿**cuál** es tu nombre?* (*¿Qué es tu nombre?* sounds like you're asking for a philosophical definition of their name.)
- **Pronouncing ll like an English L.** *Llamas* is YAH-mahs, not LAH-mahs — unless you mean the animal, and even that one is pronounced with a y-sound in Spanish.
- **Using *tú* with everyone.** Asking your friend's grandmother *¿cómo te llamas?* isn't offensive, but it misses the register. *¿Cómo se llama?* costs nothing and reads as good manners.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "¿cómo te llamas?" and "¿cómo se llama?"?**
Formality. *¿Cómo te llamas?* uses informal *tú*; *¿cómo se llama?* uses formal *usted* (and can also mean "what's his/her name?"). In Mexico, use the formal version with elders, clients, and strangers in professional settings.

**Is "¿cuál es tu nombre?" wrong?**
Not at all — it's grammatically perfect and common on forms, phone lines, and apps. In face-to-face conversation, though, *¿cómo te llamas?* is what people actually say.

**How do I answer "¿cómo te llamas?"**
*Me llamo* + name ("I call myself..."), *soy* + name ("I'm..."), or simply your name. Then return the question: *¿y tú?* — or *¿y usted?* in formal settings.

**Why does Spanish say "how do you call yourself" instead of "what is your name"?**
The reflexive verb *llamarse* is simply the idiomatic way names work in Spanish, as in many Romance languages (French *comment tu t'appelles?* is built the same way). The literal option exists (*¿cuál es tu nombre?*), but *llamarse* dominates in speech.

**Is it rude to just say my name without "me llamo"?**
No — answering *"Diego"* with a handshake is completely natural among peers. In formal contexts, the full *me llamo Diego Ramírez* (often with the surname) is the safer, more polished choice.
