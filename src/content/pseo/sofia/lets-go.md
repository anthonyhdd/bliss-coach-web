Let's go in Spanish is **vamos** — the word soccer stadiums run on. But Spanish actually splits "let's go" into two words English mashes together: **vamos** ("let's go [to somewhere]") and **vámonos** ("let's leave [from here]"). Add the shape-shifting Mexican classic **ándale** — which can mean "hurry up," "exactly!," or "okay, deal" depending on tone — and you've got a tiny phrase family that carries a huge share of everyday conversation.

## The main way to say it

**Vamos** — pronounced **BAH-mohs**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- The Spanish **v** sounds like a soft English **b**: BAH-mohs, not VAH-mohs. There is no v/b distinction in Spanish — *vamos* and a hypothetical "bamos" would sound identical.
- Stress falls on the first syllable: **BAH**-mohs. Two clean beats.
- **Vámonos** is three syllables with the stress up front — **BAH**-moh-nohs — which is exactly what the accent mark on *vá* is signaling.

Now the distinction that separates learners from speakers:

- **Vamos** = let's go — heading *toward* something, often named: *Vamos a la playa* ("let's go to the beach"), *vamos a comer* ("let's go eat").
- **Vámonos** = let's leave — away *from here*, destination irrelevant: the party's dead, the check is paid, *vámonos*.

The grammar behind it: *vámonos* is *vamos* + the reflexive *nos* (from *irse*, "to leave"), with one s dropped for pronunciation. You don't need the theory — you need the instinct: naming a destination? *Vamos.* Just want out? *Vámonos.*

Bonus structure: **vamos a + infinitive** is also how Spanish says "we're going to..." — *vamos a ver* ("let's see / we'll see") might be the single most-used phrase in the language.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Vamos | Let's go (toward something) | Universal |
| Vámonos | Let's leave / let's get out of here | Universal, casual |
| ¡Vamos! | Come on! / Let's go! (cheering) | Universal — the sports classic |
| Ándale | Come on / hurry up / that's right | Mexican, very casual |
| Ándale pues | Okay then / alright, deal | Mexican, sealing an agreement |
| Órale | Let's do it / whoa / okay | Mexican slang, all-purpose |
| Ya vámonos | Come on, let's go already | Casual, impatient |
| Vámonos ya | Let's leave right now | Casual, urgent |
| ¿Nos vamos? | Shall we go? | Polite, softer |
| A darle | Let's get to it / let's hit it | Mexican, energetic |

**Ándale** deserves its own paragraph because it does at least four jobs in Mexico: (1) hurry someone up — *¡ándale, que llegamos tarde!* ("come on, we're late!"); (2) agree enthusiastically — *¡ándale, eso es!* ("exactly, that's it!"); (3) close a deal — *ándale pues, nos vemos a las ocho* ("alright then, see you at eight"); (4) pure filler encouragement, the way English uses "come on." Tone does all the work.

**Órale** is its cousin — equal parts "let's go," "whoa," and "okay cool." Together they're two of the most Mexican words in existence; use them naturally and you'll get a delighted double-take.

## Hear it in context

**Rallying friends to leave the house (casual):**

> — ¿Ya están listos? Vamos al cine, ¿no?
> — Sí, ya casi. Cinco minutos.
> — ¡Ándale, que empieza a las siete!
> — Ya, ya. ¡Vámonos!

*You guys ready? We're going to the movies, right? — Yeah, almost. Five minutes. — Come on, it starts at seven! — Okay, okay. Let's go!*

**Wrapping up a get-together (making plans, very Mexican):**

> — Bueno, ya es tarde. ¿Nos vamos?
> — Va. ¿Entonces el sábado vamos al partido?
> — Ándale pues. Yo compro los boletos.
> — ¡Órale! Ahí nos vemos.

*Well, it's late. Shall we go? — Cool. So Saturday we're going to the game? — Alright then. I'll buy the tickets. — Awesome! See you there.*

## Common mistakes

- **Using "vamos" when you mean "let's leave."** *¿Vamos?* mid-party sounds like "shall we go (somewhere)?" If the message is "I want out of here," it's *vámonos*. Speedy Gonzales had it right: *¡ándale, ándale! ¡Arriba!* is about moving *now*.
- **"VAH-mohs" with an English v.** Spanish v = soft b. BAH-mohs. This one adjustment upgrades your entire accent, since v appears everywhere.
- **"Vámosnos" or "vamonós."** The word is **vámonos** — one s in the middle, stress on the first syllable. It's *vamos + nos* with the first s dropped.
- **Thinking "ándale" only means "hurry up."** Reply *ándale* to someone's suggestion and you're agreeing ("right, exactly"), not rushing them. Context and tone decide — listen before you assume someone's being pushy.
- **"Vamos ir al cine."** The structure needs *a*: *vamos **a** ir*, *vamos **a** comer*. English "going to eat" hides the preposition; Spanish never drops it.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "vamos" and "vámonos"?**
Direction. *Vamos* points toward a destination ("let's go to the beach"); *vámonos* points away from where you are ("let's get out of here"). If you're naming where you're headed, use *vamos*; if the point is leaving, *vámonos*.

**What does "ándale" actually mean?**
It's a Mexican multi-tool: "hurry up," "come on," "exactly!," or "okay, deal" (*ándale pues*), all depending on tone and context. It comes from the verb *andar* ("to walk/go") plus *le*, a very Mexican particle that attaches to commands for emphasis.

**Is "vamos" or "vamonos" correct for cheering at a game?**
*¡Vamos!* — stadiums worldwide run on it (*¡vamos, México!*). *Vámonos* at a game would mean "let's leave," which is the opposite of cheering.

**What does "órale" mean?**
It's all-purpose Mexican slang: "let's go," "okay cool," "whoa," or "hurry up," depending on delivery. Like *ándale*, it's built from a verb root plus the emphatic *-le*. Informal, friendly, and extremely Mexican.

**Can I say "vamos a ver" for "let's see"?**
Yes — *vamos a ver* (or just *a ver*) is exactly "let's see" and gets used constantly: stalling for time, opening a discussion, or checking something. It's one of the highest-frequency phrases in spoken Spanish.
