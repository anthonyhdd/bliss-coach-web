I don't know in Spanish is **no sé** — two tiny words that carry a surprising amount of grammar. That accent on *sé* isn't decoration: it's the difference between "I know" (the verb) and *se* (a reflexive pronoun). Say it right and you sound natural; miss it and you're technically saying something else. Beyond the basics, Mexican Spanish has some of the most charming ways in the language to shrug — *quién sabe*, *ni idea*, *sepa*.

## The main way to say it

**No sé** — pronounced **noh SEH**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- **Sé** is one clean syllable, stressed, with a clear "eh" vowel: SEH, not "say." No diphthong glide at the end.
- The accent mark on **sé** marks it as the verb *saber* ("to know [facts]"), first person: *yo sé* = "I know." *No sé* = "I don't know."
- Say it as two even beats: *noh — SEH*. Don't swallow the *no*.

The key distinction English hides: Spanish has **two** verbs for "to know."

- **Saber** = to know *information, facts, how to do something*. *No sé la respuesta* ("I don't know the answer"), *no sé nadar* ("I don't know how to swim").
- **Conocer** = to know *a person or place* (be familiar with). *No conozco a María* ("I don't know María"), *no conozco esa ciudad* ("I don't know that city").

So "I don't know" as in "I lack that fact" is almost always **no sé** (*saber*). If someone asks whether you know a person, you'd say *no lo/la conozco*.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| No sé | I don't know | Universal |
| No lo sé | I don't know (it) | Universal, slightly fuller |
| No tengo idea | I have no idea | Universal, emphatic |
| Ni idea | No clue | Casual |
| No tengo ni idea | I have no clue at all | Casual, emphatic |
| Quién sabe | Who knows | Casual, very Mexican |
| Sepa | Beats me / who knows | Very casual, Mexican slang |
| Sepa la bola | Beats me (nobody knows) | Casual, playful, Mexican |
| No estoy seguro / segura | I'm not sure | Softer, polite |

The famous Mexican shrug is **quién sabe** — literally "who knows," delivered with a little lift of the shoulders. Even more colloquial is **sepa** (or the fuller **sepa la bola**), a fixed slang expression meaning "who could possibly know." It's affectionate and very local; use it with friends, not in a job interview.

When you want to be gentle rather than blunt, **no estoy seguro** ("I'm not sure") softens the "no sé" and buys you room.

## Hear it in context

**Answering a question you genuinely can't (casual):**

> — ¿A qué hora abre el museo?
> — Uf, no sé. Déjame checar en mi celular.
> — Va. Gracias.
> — Ni idea, la verdad, pero ahorita lo vemos.

*What time does the museum open? — Ugh, I don't know. Let me check on my phone. — Cool. Thanks. — No clue, honestly, but we'll see in a sec.*

**Very Mexican shrug between friends:**

> — ¿Por qué canceló la fiesta?
> — Quién sabe. No dijo nada.
> — Qué raro, ¿no?
> — Sepa. Ya nos enteraremos.

*Why did she cancel the party? — Who knows. She didn't say anything. — Weird, right? — Beats me. We'll find out eventually.*

## Common mistakes

- **Dropping the accent and saying "no se."** In writing that's a different word. In speech, make sure you're saying the verb *sé* (SEH), stressed, meaning "I know."
- **Using "no conozco" for facts.** *No conozco* is for people and places, not information. "I don't know the answer" is *no sé la respuesta*, never *no conozco la respuesta*.
- **Translating "no idea" as "no idea."** Spanish needs the verb: *no tengo idea* or *ni idea*, not a bare *no idea*.
- **Over-formalizing.** In casual talk, natives just say *no sé* or *quién sabe*. A full *no tengo conocimiento de eso* sounds stiff and bureaucratic.

## Mexico vs Spain notes

The plain answer, *no sé*, is universal — say it anywhere and you're understood.

- **Quién sabe** is used across Latin America but is especially a Mexican reflex, often with the shoulder shrug.
- **Sepa / sepa la bola** is distinctly Mexican slang; a Spaniard would more likely say *ni idea* or the very Spanish *no tengo ni pajolera idea* (mild, informal emphasis).
- The pronunciation is essentially the same on both sides of the Atlantic; the *s* sound in *sé* doesn't lisp in either place because it's an *s*, not a *c/z*.

## FAQ

**Why does "sé" have an accent?**
The accent distinguishes the verb *sé* ("I know") from the pronoun *se*. Without the accent, *se* is a completely different word, so *no sé* needs it to mean "I don't know."

**What's the difference between "no sé" and "no conozco"?**
*No sé* (from *saber*) is for facts, information, and skills. *No conozco* (from *conocer*) is for people and places. "I don't know the way" = *no sé el camino*; "I don't know her" = *no la conozco*.

**What does "quién sabe" mean?**
Literally "who knows" — a casual, very Mexican way to say "I don't know / beats me," usually with a shrug. It's friendly and extremely common in everyday speech.

**Is "sepa" rude?**
Not rude, just very informal slang. *Sepa* (or *sepa la bola*) means "who could know" and is best kept for friends and relaxed settings, not formal ones.

**How do I say "I'm not sure" more politely?**
*No estoy seguro* (if you're male) or *no estoy segura* (if you're female). It's softer than a flat *no sé* and useful when you want to hedge.
