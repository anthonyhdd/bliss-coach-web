To say "I miss you" in Spanish — at least the way it's said in Mexico and most of Latin America — say **Te extraño**, pronounced *teh eks-TRAH-nyoh*. It comes from the verb *extrañar* (to miss someone or something), and it's the phrase filling millions of late-night texts between Mexico City and everywhere else.

You may have also seen **Te echo de menos**. That version is correct too, but it's the Spain flavor — say it in Guadalajara and you'll be understood perfectly, you'll just sound like you learned your Spanish in Madrid. Since Sofia teaches Mexican Spanish, we'll build your toolkit around *te extraño* and its very expressive family.

## The main way to say it

**Te extraño** — *teh eks-TRAH-nyoh*

Pronunciation notes:

- The **ñ** is the key sound: "ny" as in "canyon." *Extra-nyo*, three smooth syllables after the *te*.
- Stress lands on **TRA**: eks-**TRAH**-nyoh.
- The **x** here sounds like "ks," lightly — don't overdo it. In fast speech it can soften toward "es-TRAH-nyoh."

Grammar in ten seconds: *extrañar* works like English "to miss" — the person you miss is the object. **Te** extraño (I miss *you*), **los** extraño (I miss *you all / them*), **la** extraño (I miss *her*, or formal *you*). Past tense for reunions: **Te extrañé** (*I missed you*).

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| Te extraño | teh eks-TRAH-nyoh | The everyday default in Mexico |
| Te extraño mucho | ... MOO-choh | "I miss you a lot" — the standard intensifier |
| Te extraño muchísimo | ... moo-CHEE-see-moh | Turn it up: "I miss you SO much" |
| Ya te extraño | yah teh eks-TRAH-nyoh | "I miss you already" — said before or right after a goodbye |
| Me haces falta | meh AH-sess FAHL-tah | Deeper: "I feel your absence / something's missing without you" |
| Te extraño un buen | ... oon bwen | Mexican slang: "I miss you a ton" |
| Te extraño un chorro | ... oon CHOH-rroh | Even more Mexican slang, same idea, very casual |
| Cuánto te extraño | KWAHN-toh... | "How much I miss you" — dramatic, poetic, song-lyric energy |
| Te echo de menos | teh EH-choh deh MEH-nohs | The Spain version; understood everywhere, rarely used in Mexico |

The one to savor here is **Me haces falta**. Literally "you make a lack in me" — you're missing *from* me, like a missing piece. It's warmer and heavier than *te extraño*, which makes it perfect for partners, close family, and grand romantic gestures, and a bit much for a coworker who changed offices. Mexican songs are absolutely full of it for a reason.

And *ya te extraño* is a small phrase with big charm: say it while hugging someone goodbye at the airport — "I miss you *already*" — and watch them melt.

## Hear it in context

**Long-distance couple texting:**

> — ¿Cómo estuvo tu día, amor?
> — Bien, pero largo. Ya quiero que sea diciembre para verte.
> — Yo también. Te extraño muchísimo.
> — Y tú a mí me haces mucha falta. Tres semanas más.

*— How was your day, love?*
*— Good, but long. I can't wait for December so I can see you.*
*— Me too. I miss you so much.*
*— And I feel your absence so much. Three more weeks.*

**Calling grandma on Sunday:**

> — ¡Abuelita! ¿Cómo estás?
> — ¡Mijo! Qué gusto escucharte. ¿Cuándo vienes?
> — En las vacaciones, lo prometo. Te extraño un buen.
> — Y yo a ti. Te voy a hacer tus tamales favoritos.

*— Grandma! How are you?*
*— Sweetheart! So good to hear from you. When are you coming?*
*— On vacation, I promise. I miss you a ton.*
*— And I miss you too. I'll make you your favorite tamales.*

Notice grandma's reply: **Y yo a ti** — "and I (miss) you" — the natural short response to *te extraño*.

## Common mistakes

- **Reaching for "perder."** In English you "miss" a bus and "miss" a person with the same verb; Spanish splits them. Missing transport is *perder*: *Perdí el camión* (Mexican Spanish for "I missed the bus"). Saying *te pierdo* to a person means you're *losing* them — a much more alarming text.
- **Word-for-word "I miss you" → "Yo extraño tú."** The person missed is an object pronoun placed before the verb: **Te** extraño. No *yo* needed, no *tú* at the end.
- **Confusing the other meaning of extrañar.** *Me extraña que no llame* means "it surprises me that he doesn't call." When *extrañar* points at a situation rather than a person, it means "to find strange." Context keeps these apart; just don't panic when you hear it.
- **Using "te echo de menos" to sound native in Mexico.** It's grammatically flawless — and instantly flags you as Spain-trained. Not wrong, just not local.
- **Overdeploying "me haces falta."** Save it for real intimacy. Telling your new gym buddy *me haces falta* is the emotional equivalent of a hand-written love letter about their spotting technique.

## FAQ

**Te extraño or te echo de menos — which should I learn?**
Learn *te extraño*. It's the standard across Mexico and most of Latin America. Recognize *te echo de menos* so Spanish films don't confuse you.

**Is "me haces falta" romantic?**
Usually, yes — it carries more emotional weight than *te extraño*. It's also natural within close family. Between casual acquaintances it sounds intense.

**How do I say "I miss you too"?**
**Yo también te extraño**, or the shorter, very natural **Y yo a ti**.

**How do I say I miss a place or thing?**
Same verb: *Extraño México* (I miss Mexico), *Extraño los tacos de mi barrio* (I miss my neighborhood's tacos). Everyone will nod solemnly at the second one.
