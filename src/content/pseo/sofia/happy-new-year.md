Happy New Year in Spanish is **feliz año nuevo** — a phrase where one tiny mark, the tilde on the **ñ**, carries enormous weight. *Año* (with the ñ) means "year"; *ano* (without it) means something you very much did not intend to wish someone. Get the ñ right and you're spreading holiday cheer; drop it and you've made the most infamous mistake in Spanish. Beyond the greeting itself, Mexico has its own warm New Year's rituals and phrases worth knowing.

## The main way to say it

**Feliz año nuevo** — pronounced **feh-LEES AH-nyoh NWEH-voh**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- **Feliz** = "feh-LEES," stress on the second syllable, ending in a soft "s" in Mexico (not the Spain "th").
- **Año** = "AH-nyoh." The **ñ** is a "ny" sound, like the "ni" in English "onion." This is the make-or-break sound.
- **Nuevo** = "NWEH-voh," with the Spanish *v* sounding like a soft *b*: NWEH-boh.

The all-important warning: **año** (AH-nyoh, "year") vs **ano** (AH-noh, "anus"). The ñ is not optional decoration — it's a different letter that changes the word entirely. Always pronounce the "ny," and in writing always include the tilde: *año*.

A common fuller greeting is **próspero año nuevo** ("prosperous new year"), and the two often combine with Christmas: **feliz Navidad y próspero año nuevo** ("merry Christmas and a prosperous new year").

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Feliz año nuevo | Happy New Year | Universal |
| Feliz año | Happy New Year (clipped) | Casual, very common |
| Próspero año nuevo | Prosperous New Year | Warm, traditional |
| Feliz Navidad y próspero año nuevo | Merry Christmas and a happy New Year | Universal, the classic combo |
| ¡Que tengas un excelente año! | Have a great year! | Warm |
| Que este año te traiga… | May this year bring you… | Heartfelt, for cards |
| ¡Feliz año a todos! | Happy New Year, everyone! | Warm, group |
| Salud, por el año nuevo | Cheers, to the new year | Toasting |

In casual speech, Mexicans often shorten it to just **feliz año** — dropping *nuevo* entirely, the way English sometimes says just "happy new year" fast. For a warm toast at midnight, **¡salud, por el año nuevo!** ("cheers, to the new year!") does the job.

Mexican New Year's is rich with traditions you might hear referenced: **las doce uvas** (eating twelve grapes at midnight, one per chime, for twelve wishes), and wearing colored underwear for luck — *rojo* for love, *amarillo* for money.

## Hear it in context

**Midnight on New Year's Eve (warm, celebratory):**

> — ¡Ya son las doce! ¡Feliz año nuevo!
> — ¡Feliz año! ¿Ya te comiste tus uvas?
> — ¡Todas! Doce deseos. Un abrazo, que este año te traiga lo mejor.
> — Igualmente, salud. ¡Por un gran año!

*It's midnight! Happy New Year! — Happy New Year! Did you eat your grapes? — All of them! Twelve wishes. A hug, may this year bring you the best. — Likewise, cheers. To a great year!*

**Sending good wishes to family (heartfelt):**

> — Feliz Navidad y próspero año nuevo, tía.
> — Gracias, mi niño. Que Dios te bendiga este año.
> — Que estén todos muy bien. Los quiero mucho.
> — Igualmente. Cuídate mucho.

*Merry Christmas and a happy New Year, auntie. — Thank you, sweetheart. May God bless you this year. — May you all be well. I love you lots. — Likewise. Take good care.*

## Common mistakes

- **Dropping the ñ and saying/writing "ano."** The famous one. *Feliz año* (AH-nyoh) is "happy year"; *feliz ano* is a body part. Always pronounce the "ny" and write the tilde.
- **"Feliz nuevo año."** The word order is *feliz **año nuevo*** — adjective *nuevo* after the noun, as Spanish usually does. "Feliz nuevo año" is an English-order calque.
- **Using the Spain "th" for "feliz."** In Mexico, *feliz* ends in an "s" sound: feh-LEES. The lisped feh-LEETH is Castilian.
- **Pronouncing "nuevo" with an English "v."** It's a soft *b*: NWEH-boh.

## Mexico vs Spain notes

*Feliz año nuevo* and *próspero año nuevo* are universal — identical across all Spanish-speaking countries.

- **Pronunciation of "feliz":** Mexico says feh-LEE**s**; Spain says feh-LEE**th** (the final *z* lisps). Both are correct in their regions.
- **The twelve grapes** tradition (*las doce uvas de la suerte*) actually originated in **Spain** and is huge there; Mexico adopted it and added its own touches like the colored-underwear luck charm.
- The greeting itself needs no regional swap — say *feliz año nuevo* anywhere and you're perfectly understood.

## FAQ

**Why is the ñ in "año" so important?**
Because it changes the word completely: *año* (with ñ, "AH-nyoh") means "year," but *ano* (without it) means "anus." Always pronounce the "ny" sound and always write the tilde — this is the classic Spanish mistake to avoid.

**Is it "feliz año nuevo" or "feliz nuevo año"?**
*Feliz año nuevo.* Spanish normally places the adjective after the noun, so *nuevo* follows *año*. "Feliz nuevo año" copies English word order and sounds off.

**What does "próspero año nuevo" mean?**
"Prosperous New Year" — a warmer, more traditional wish, often paired with Christmas: *feliz Navidad y próspero año nuevo*.

**How do Mexicans celebrate at midnight?**
Common traditions include eating *las doce uvas* (twelve grapes, one per chime, each a wish) and wearing colored underwear for luck — red for love, yellow for money. You'll hear these referenced around New Year's.

**Can I just say "feliz año"?**
Yes — dropping *nuevo* is very common in casual speech: *¡feliz año!* is a natural, everyday way to wish someone a happy New Year.
