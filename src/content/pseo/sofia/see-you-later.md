The most common way to say "see you later" in Spanish is **hasta luego** — but in Mexico, the goodbye you'll hear most between friends is **nos vemos** ("we'll see each other"). Spanish has a whole family of goodbyes graded by how soon you expect to meet again, and Mexicans have added a few of their own that no textbook teaches. Here's the full map.

## The main way to say it

**Hasta luego** — pronounced *AHS-tah LWEH-goh*.

Literally "until later," this is the universal, always-safe goodbye. It works with your taxi driver, your teacher, the lady at the tortillería, and your friends. Note the silent *h* — it's *AHS-tah*, never *HAS-tah*.

- **Gracias por todo, ¡hasta luego!** — Thanks for everything, see you later!
- **Hasta luego, que le vaya bien.** — See you later, take care. (A very Mexican send-off from shopkeepers.)

Between friends, **nos vemos** (*nohs VEH-mohs*) is even more common: short, warm, and it doesn't commit you to a specific time.

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | Register | When to use it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hasta luego | AHS-tah LWEH-goh | Universal | Safe everywhere, with everyone |
| Nos vemos | nohs VEH-mohs | Casual | Friends and peers — "see ya" |
| Ahí nos vemos | ah-EE nohs VEH-mohs | Very Mexican | "See you around" — relaxed, super common |
| Al rato / Nos vemos al rato | ahl RAH-toh | Very Mexican | "See you in a bit" — later the same day |
| Hasta mañana | AHS-tah mah-NYAH-nah | Universal | "See you tomorrow" |
| Hasta pronto | AHS-tah PROHN-toh | Neutral | "See you soon" — warm, slightly formal |
| Cuídate | KWEE-dah-teh | Casual | "Take care" — affectionate closer |
| Ahí nos vidrios | ah-EE nohs VEE-dryohs | Slang | Playful Mexican pun on *nos vemos* |

Two of these deserve a closer look:

**Al rato** is pure Mexican timekeeping. *Un rato* is "a while," so *nos vemos al rato* means "see you in a bit" — usually later the same day. Don't confuse it with *un ratito* ("a little while"), which shrinks the wait even further, at least in theory.

**Ahí nos vidrios** is a classic Mexican albur-style pun — *vidrios* (window panes/glass) stands in for *vemos* just because it sounds similar. It's jokey and generational; use it with friends who'll appreciate the wordplay, not in a business meeting.

## Hear it in context

**Dialog 1 — Leaving a taquería**

> — Ya me voy, gracias. ¿Cuánto le debo?
> — Son ochenta pesos. **¡Hasta luego, que le vaya bien!**
> — Igualmente, **¡hasta luego!**
>
> — I'm off, thanks. How much do I owe you?
> — Eighty pesos. See you later, have a good one!
> — Likewise, see you later!

**Dialog 2 — Two friends parting after lunch**

> — Bueno, tengo que regresar a la oficina.
> — Va. **Nos vemos al rato, ¿no? ¿Sigues yendo al gym?**
> — Sí, a las siete. **Ahí nos vemos, ¡cuídate!**
>
> — Well, I have to get back to the office.
> — Cool. See you in a bit, right? Still going to the gym?
> — Yeah, at seven. See you there, take care!

That little **va** is Mexican shorthand for "okay/deal" — one more marker of everyday CDMX speech.

## Common mistakes

1. **Using "adiós" for every goodbye.** *Adiós* is correct but carries finality — it can feel like you don't expect to see the person again soon. Among friends, *nos vemos* or *hasta luego* is warmer.

2. **Pronouncing the "h" in hasta.** Spanish *h* is always silent. *AHS-tah*, not *HAS-tah*. This one mistake instantly flags a beginner.

3. **Translating "see you later" literally.** *Te veo más tarde* is grammatical but stiff. Native speakers reach for the fixed phrases: *nos vemos*, *al rato*, *hasta luego*.

4. **Confusing "hasta luego" with "hasta ahora."** *Hasta luego* = see you later (unspecified). If you genuinely mean minutes from now, Mexicans say *al rato* or *ahorita vengo* ("I'll be right back").

5. **Treating "al rato" as a precise promise.** Like *ahorita*, *al rato* is famously elastic in Mexico. It expresses intention, not a timestamp. Don't set your watch by it.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "hasta luego" and "nos vemos"?**
Register, mostly. *Hasta luego* works everywhere; *nos vemos* is the friendlier, more casual choice between people who know each other. Meaning-wise they're interchangeable.

**Does "adiós" sound too dramatic?**
Not dramatic, just more final. It's perfectly normal when leaving a shop or ending a phone call, but with friends you'll see soon, *nos vemos* feels more natural.

**What does "que te vaya bien" mean?**
Literally "may it go well for you" — the Mexican way to say "take care / have a good one" as someone leaves. Formal version: *que le vaya bien*. Reply with *igualmente* ("likewise").

**How do I say "see you tomorrow" or "see you Monday"?**
*Hasta mañana* and *hasta el lunes*. The *hasta + time* pattern works for any day: *hasta el sábado*, *hasta la próxima* ("until next time").
