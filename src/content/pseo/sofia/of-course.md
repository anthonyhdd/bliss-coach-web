Of course in Spanish is **claro** — pronounced *KLAH-roh* — often warmed up to **claro que sí** ("of course, yes"). Its slightly more buttoned-up sibling is **por supuesto** (*pohr soo-PWESS-toh*), and Mexico adds a charmer of its own: **cómo no** — literally "how not?" — which is how waiters, hosts, and friendly strangers say "of course!" all day long.

These little agreement words are secretly some of the highest-value vocabulary you can learn. You'll use them dozens of times a day, they make you sound instantly more fluent than textbook *sí*, and they're how native speakers keep a conversation flowing while you're still composing your next sentence.

## The main way to say it

**Claro** — *KLAH-roh*

Pronunciation pointers:

- The Spanish **r** between vowels is a single quick tap of the tongue, like the *tt* in American "butter": KLAH-*roh*. No need to roll it.
- Stress the first syllable: **KLAH**-roh.
- **Claro que sí**: *KLAH-roh keh SEE*. The *que* is unstressed and glides by fast.

*Claro* literally means "clear" — so answering *claro* is saying "clearly!" or "obviously!" It signals that the answer is so evident it barely needed asking, which is exactly the energy of English "of course."

**The register ladder**, from casual to formal:

1. **Claro** — the everyday default, fits almost everywhere.
2. **Claro que sí** — warmer and more emphatic; great for enthusiastic yeses.
3. **Por supuesto** — a notch more formal; perfect with strangers, at work, or when you want to sound composed.
4. **Desde luego** — formal and a bit bookish; more common in Spain than in Mexico.

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| Claro | KLAH-roh | The all-purpose "of course" |
| Claro que sí | KLAH-roh keh SEE | Emphatic, warm: "of course, absolutely" |
| Por supuesto | pohr soo-PWESS-toh | More formal or emphatic — work, strangers |
| Por supuesto que sí | pohr soo-PWESS-toh keh SEE | Maximum emphasis: "absolutely, yes" |
| Cómo no | KOH-moh NOH | Very Mexican, gracious: "of course! / gladly" |
| Claro que no | KLAH-roh keh NOH | "Of course not" |
| Desde luego | DEHS-deh LWEH-goh | Formal "certainly" — Spain-flavored |
| Obvio | OH-byoh | "Obviously" — casual, youthful, can sound sassy |
| Sí, claro | see KLAH-roh | Softer agreement; tone decides if it's sincere or sarcastic |

The star here is **cómo no**. Literally it's a question — "how could I not?" — but in Mexico it functions as the most gracious yes available. Ask a waiter for extra salsa and you'll get *¡cómo no!*; ask a stranger if you can share their table and you'll hear the same. It says "of course" with a smile built in. One caution: with a sharp, drawn-out tone, *cóóómo no* can flip into sarcasm ("yeah, riiight") — but context and a friendly voice keep you safe.

Meanwhile **obvio** is the texting-generation option. It's fine between friends, but answering your boss's question with *obvio* has the same eyebrow-raising effect as "obviously" in English.

## Hear it in context

**At a taquería in Guadalajara:**

> — ¿Me puede traer más salsa verde, por favor?
> — ¡Cómo no! ¿Algo más?
> — ¿Tienen agua de horchata?
> — Claro que sí. Ahorita se la traigo.

*— Could you bring me more green salsa, please?*
*— Of course! Anything else?*
*— Do you have horchata?*
*— Of course we do. I'll bring it right over.*

**Between friends making plans:**

> — ¿Vienes a la fiesta el sábado?
> — ¡Claro! ¿Llevo algo?
> — ¿Puedes traer hielo?
> — Por supuesto. Nos vemos a las ocho.

*— Are you coming to the party on Saturday?*
*— Of course! Should I bring something?*
*— Can you bring ice?*
*— Absolutely. See you at eight.*

Notice *ahorita* in the first dialogue — Mexico's famously elastic "right now" — and how the waiter stacks *cómo no* and *claro que sí* in one exchange. Natives rotate through these constantly; using only one on repeat is what sounds foreign.

## Common mistakes

- **Answering everything with just "sí."** It's correct but flat. Sprinkling in *claro*, *claro que sí*, and *cómo no* is one of the fastest ways to sound natural — these are the lubricant of Mexican small talk.
- **Taking "cómo no" literally.** It looks like a question ("how not?") but it's an enthusiastic yes. Don't respond by explaining how not.
- **"Por supuesto" with the wrong stress.** It's soo-**PWESS**-toh, stress on the middle. And it's two words — *por supuesto*, never *porsupuesto*.
- **Using "obvio" upward.** Fine with friends, risky with your boss or your in-laws — like English "obviously," it can imply the question was dumb.
- **Missing sarcastic "sí, claro."** Delivered with a flat tone and an eye-roll, *sí, claro* means "yeah, sure it is." Tone carries the meaning; listen before you take it at face value.
- **Confusing "claro" (of course) with "clara" (egg white / light-colored, fem.).** The agreement word is always *claro*, invariable.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "claro" and "por supuesto"?**
They're interchangeable in meaning; *claro* is the casual everyday default, while *por supuesto* is a touch more formal or emphatic. In a job interview, lean *por supuesto*; with friends, *claro* all day.

**What does "cómo no" actually mean?**
Literally "how not?", but in practice it's a warm, very Mexican "of course! / gladly!" — especially common from waiters, hosts, and anyone granting a request.

**Is "claro que sí" stronger than "claro"?**
Slightly, yes. *Claro que sí* adds warmth and emphasis — "of course, absolutely!" — while bare *claro* is a quick, neutral "of course."

**How do I say "of course not"?**
**Claro que no** or **por supuesto que no**. Both are common: *¿Te molesta? — ¡Claro que no!* ("Does it bother you?" — "Of course not!").

**Can "claro" be sarcastic?**
It can — *sí, claro* with a flat or exaggerated tone means "yeah, right." The words are identical; the tone (and sometimes a raised eyebrow) does all the work.
