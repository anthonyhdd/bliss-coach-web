The way to say "cheers" in Spanish is **¡Salud!** — pronounced *sah-LOOD* — and it works everywhere Spanish is spoken, from a rooftop bar in Mexico City to a family dinner in Guadalajara. Literally, it means "health," which is exactly the same idea behind toasts in dozens of languages: you raise your glass and wish everyone good health.

That one word will get you through 95% of toasting situations. But Spanish — and Mexican Spanish in particular — has a whole toasting culture around it, including a famous four-part chant you'll absolutely hear at a Mexican party. Let's get you ready for all of it.

## The main way to say it

**¡Salud!** — *sah-LOOD*

A few pronunciation notes so you sound natural:

- The stress falls on the second syllable: sah-**LOOD**.
- The final **d** is very soft in Mexican Spanish — almost like a whisper of a "th" or barely there at all. Don't punch it like the "d" in "loud."
- The **u** is a pure "oo" sound, like in "food," never like the "u" in "cup."

You say it while raising your glass and making eye contact, then everyone clinks and drinks. If you want to toast *to* something or someone, use **brindar por**: "Brindemos por Ana" (*Let's toast to Ana*) or simply raise your glass and say **¡Por Ana!** (*To Ana!*).

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| ¡Salud! | sah-LOOD | The universal default, any occasion |
| ¡Salucita! | sah-loo-SEE-tah | Playful Mexican diminutive, casual gatherings with friends |
| ¡Por ustedes! | por oos-TEH-dess | "To you all!" — warm, works at dinners and celebrations |
| Brindemos por... | breen-DEH-mohs por | "Let's toast to..." — the formal way to open a toast or speech |
| ¡Hasta el fondo! | AHS-tah el FON-doh | "Bottoms up!" — casual, means you're finishing the glass |
| ¡Arriba, abajo, al centro, pa' dentro! | ah-RREE-bah, ah-BAH-hoh, al SEN-troh, pah DEN-troh | The classic Mexican party toast (see below) |

That last one deserves an explanation, because it's a genuine Mexican institution. **"¡Arriba, abajo, al centro, pa' dentro!"** means "Up, down, to the center, inside!" — and it comes with choreography. You raise your glass up (*arriba*), lower it down (*abajo*), bring it to the middle to clink with everyone (*al centro*), and then drink (*pa' dentro* — "inside," meaning into your mouth). You'll hear it with tequila shots at birthdays, weddings, and any Friday night that's going well. Join in and you'll instantly make friends.

One more you may know from movies: **¡Chin chin!** It exists in Spanish, but in Mexico it's used far less than in Spain or Italy — partly because "chin" sounds close to some very colorful Mexican slang. Stick with *¡Salud!* and you'll never go wrong.

## Hear it in context

**At a birthday dinner:**

> — Bueno, quiero brindar por Sofía, que cumple veinticinco años. ¡Salud!
> — ¡Salud!
> — ¡Que cumplas muchos más!

*— Okay, I want to toast to Sofía, who's turning twenty-five. Cheers!*
*— Cheers!*
*— May you have many more!*

**With friends and a round of tequila:**

> — ¿Listos? ¡Arriba, abajo, al centro, pa' dentro!
> — ¡Eso! ¡Hasta el fondo!
> — Uff, está fuerte este tequila.

*— Ready? Up, down, to the center, drink!*
*— That's it! Bottoms up!*
*— Whew, this tequila is strong.*

## Common mistakes

- **Using ¡Provecho! as a toast.** *¡Provecho!* (or *¡Buen provecho!*) means "enjoy your meal" — you say it to people who are eating, not when raising a glass. Mixing them up is a classic beginner slip.
- **Hitting the final "d" too hard.** Saying "sa-LOODD" with a hard English "d" marks you as a learner immediately. Let it fade out softly.
- **Translating the email sign-off "Cheers."** If you end an email with "cheers" in English, the Spanish equivalent is **Saludos** (greetings/regards), not *¡Salud!* Writing "¡Salud!" at the end of a work email reads like you're toasting your coworker.
- **Forgetting that "salud" is also what you say after a sneeze.** Someone sneezes? Say *¡Salud!* — same word, totally normal. Context does all the work; don't overthink it.
- **Toasting with water.** Not a language mistake, but many Mexicans consider it bad luck. If you don't drink alcohol, toast with whatever's in your glass — just know someone might tease you about the *agua*.

## FAQ

**Is "salud" formal or informal?**
Both. It's completely neutral — use it with your friends, your boss, or your in-laws. Formality comes from what you add around it (*Brindemos por...* sounds more polished).

**What does "arriba, abajo, al centro, pa' dentro" mean exactly?**
"Up, down, to the center, inside." It's a call-and-response toast with hand movements: glass up, glass down, clink in the middle, then drink. Extremely common in Mexico with shots.

**Do Mexicans say "chin chin"?**
You'll hear it occasionally, but *¡Salud!* is overwhelmingly the standard. In Mexico, "chin chin" can draw smirks because it echoes some rude slang, so it's not the safest choice for a learner.

**Do I need to make eye contact when toasting?**
It's good manners in Mexico, yes — clink glasses (or tap the table) and look at the person you're toasting with. It signals warmth and respect.
