To wish someone a good day in Spanish, say **que tengas un buen día** — pronounced *keh TEN-gahs oon bwen DEE-ah*. Literally "may you have a good day," it's the phrase you'll hear from friends, cashiers, and taxi drivers all over Mexico as you part ways.

And Mexico has its own signature twist: **bonito día** — "(have a) lovely day." Waiters, Uber drivers, and shop clerks fire off *¡que tenga bonito día!* all day long, and it carries a warmth that the flat English "have a good day" never quite manages. Since Sofia teaches Mexican Spanish, we'll make sure you can produce both.

## The main way to say it

**Que tengas un buen día** — *keh TEN-gahs oon bwen DEE-ah*

Pronunciation pointers:

- **Que** is just "keh" — short, no "kw" sound.
- **Tengas** stresses the first syllable: **TEN**-gahs.
- **Día** is two syllables with the stress on the first: **DEE**-ah. The written accent on the í is your reminder.
- The whole thing flows as one breath: *keh-TEN-gahs-oon-bwen-DEE-ah*.

Grammar in ten seconds: *que tengas* uses the **subjunctive** of *tener* (to have) because it's a wish — "may you have." That opening *que* is the wish-marker; you'll see it in *que descanses*, *que te vaya bien*, and half the kind things Spanish speakers say to each other. The *un* is optional in practice: *que tengas buen día* is just as common.

**Formality switch:** to a stranger, an elder, or a customer, use the *usted* form: **que tenga un buen día** (no -s). One letter, big difference in politeness.

The one to steal from the locals is **que te vaya bien**. It's not literally "have a good day" — it's "may it go well for you" — but it fills the same slot in conversation and is arguably *more* common in Mexico when someone walks out the door. Shopkeepers say it to departing customers dozens of times a day. If someone says it to you, reply *gracias, igualmente*.

And **bonito día** is pure Mexican sunshine. In Spain you'd hear *que tengas un buen día* or *buenos días* on parting, but *bonito día* would sound imported — it's a Mexican (and broadly Latin American) trademark. Use it and you'll sound like you learned your Spanish in the right place.

## Hear it in context

**Leaving a coffee shop in Mexico City:**

> — Aquí tiene su cambio. ¿Algo más?
> — No, eso es todo. Gracias.
> — Gracias a usted. ¡Que tenga bonito día!
> — Igualmente, hasta luego.

*— Here's your change. Anything else?*
*— No, that's everything. Thanks.*
*— Thank you. Have a lovely day!*
*— Likewise, see you later.*

**Friends parting after breakfast:**

> — Bueno, me voy, tengo junta a las diez.
> — Va. Que te vaya bien en la junta.
> — Gracias. ¡Que tengas buen día!
> — Tú también. Nos vemos el viernes.

*— Well, I'm off, I have a meeting at ten.*
*— Okay. Hope the meeting goes well.*
*— Thanks. Have a good day!*
*— You too. See you Friday.*

Notice the replies: **igualmente** ("likewise") and **tú también** ("you too"). Either one closes the loop naturally — no need to repeat the whole phrase back.

## Common mistakes

- **"Que tengas un bueno día."** Before a masculine singular noun, *bueno* drops the -o: *un **buen** día*. This is the single most common slip.
- **Mixing up tú and usted: "que tenga" vs "que tengas."** With the -s (*tengas*) it's informal; without (*tenga*) it's formal. Saying *que tengas* to an elderly stranger isn't offensive, but *que tenga* is the polished move.
- **Saying "ten un buen día."** It's grammatically possible but sounds like a robotic translation of English. Natives wrap the wish in *que* + subjunctive: *que tengas...*
- **Confusing "buen día" with "buenos días."** *Buenos días* is the standard morning greeting; *buen día* (singular) works as both a greeting and a parting wish, and is common in Mexico. As a learner, greet with *buenos días* and part with *que tengas buen día*.
- **Forgetting it's a daytime phrase.** After dark, switch to *que tengas buena noche* or [buenas noches](/sofia/how-to-say/good-night/).

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "que tengas un buen día" and "que te vaya bien"?**
Both are parting wishes. *Que tengas un buen día* wishes them a good day specifically; *que te vaya bien* ("may it go well for you") is broader and extremely common in Mexico when someone leaves. You can't go wrong with either.

**How do I say "have a good day" formally?**
Drop the -s: **que tenga un buen día** (usted form). To a group: *que tengan un buen día*.

**What does "bonito día" mean?**
Literally "lovely day" — it's the very Mexican short form of *que tengas un bonito día*. You'll hear it constantly from service staff and in friendly texts.

**How do I reply when someone wishes me a good day?**
*Igualmente* ("likewise"), *gracias, tú también* ("thanks, you too") — or to a formal *que tenga buen día*, reply *gracias, igualmente*.

**Can I just say "buen día"?**
Yes — in Mexico *buen día* works as a quick greeting and as a parting wish. It's a bit brisker than the full *que tengas un buen día*, which carries more warmth.
