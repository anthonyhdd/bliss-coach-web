Good morning in Spanish is **buenos días** — and yes, it literally means "good days," plural, which surprises almost everyone. It's the single most useful greeting you can learn, because in the Spanish-speaking world you say it to *everyone*: the taxi driver, the barista, the stranger in the elevator. Walking into a small shop in Mexico without a *buenos días* is genuinely a little rude — the greeting comes first, then whatever you actually wanted to say.

## The main way to say it

**Buenos días** — pronounced **BWEH-nohs DEE-ahs**.

Two pronunciation notes that instantly make you sound better:

- The **d** in *días* is softer than an English d — closer to the *th* in "this" when it comes between vowels. Don't stress about it; a regular d is understood everywhere.
- The stress falls on **DEE**: bweh-nohs **DEE**-ahs. The written accent on *días* is doing exactly that job.

Why plural? Nobody knows for certain — the leading theory is that it's a shortening of an old blessing like *buenos días os dé Dios* ("may God give you good days"). What matters is that the plural is fixed: it's always *buenos días*, never *bueno día*.

And a grammar detail that trips up beginners: *día* is **masculine** despite ending in -a. That's why it's *buen**os*** días but *buen**as*** tardes and *buen**as*** noches (*tarde* and *noche* are feminine).

## Variations

| Phrase | When to use it | Register |
|--------|----------------|----------|
| Buenos días | Universal — anyone, anywhere, until around midday | Neutral, always safe |
| Buen día | Same meaning, singular; common in Argentina, heard in Mexico too | Neutral |
| ¡Buenas! | Clipped all-purpose greeting, works most of the day | Casual, very common in Mexico |
| Muy buenos días | Extra-warm or slightly formal opener (radio hosts love it) | Warm/formal |
| Buenos días, ¿cómo amaneciste? | "Good morning, how did you wake up?" — a beloved Mexican morning question | Casual/affectionate |
| ¿Cómo amaneció? | Same question with *usted*, for elders or formal settings | Formal |
| Feliz día | "Have a happy day" — often added after the greeting | Warm |

That verb **amanecer** deserves a highlight: it literally means "to dawn," and Mexicans use it for people. *¿Cómo amaneciste?* is essentially "how are you this morning?" and hearing it from a host or a coworker is a small daily dose of warmth. A natural answer: *Amanecí muy bien, gracias — ¿y tú?*

**Timing rule:** *buenos días* works until roughly midday or until you've eaten lunch — whichever mental clock the speaker uses. After that, switch to *buenas tardes*. If you're not sure, the clipped *¡Buenas!* covers you at almost any hour.

## Hear it in context

**At the hotel front desk (formal):**

> — Buenos días, señora. ¿En qué le puedo ayudar?
> — Buenos días. Tengo una reservación a nombre de García.
> — Claro que sí, permítame un momento.

*Good morning, ma'am. How can I help you? — Good morning. I have a reservation under García. — Of course, give me one moment.*

**Between roommates (casual, very Mexican):**

> — ¡Buenos días! ¿Cómo amaneciste?
> — Amanecí medio dormido, la verdad. ¿Ya hay café?
> — Sí, acabo de hacer. Sírvete.

*Good morning! How'd you wake up? — Half asleep, honestly. Is there coffee yet? — Yeah, just made some. Help yourself.*

## Common mistakes

- **"Buenas días."** The most common slip. *Día* is masculine, so it takes *buenos*. If it helps: **-os** goes with dí**a**s, **-as** goes with tard**e**s and noch**e**s — the endings deliberately don't match.
- **Using it all day.** *Buenos días* after 2 p.m. marks you as a beginner. Afternoon = *buenas tardes*, after dark = *buenas noches*.
- **Translating "morning" separately.** "Good morning" is not *buena mañana*. *Mañana* means both "morning" and "tomorrow," but the greeting is always *buenos días*.
- **Skipping the greeting entirely.** In Mexico, launching straight into a question — *"¿Dónde está el baño?"* — without a *buenos días* first feels abrupt. Greeting first, request second, always.
- **Pronouncing the h... wait, there is no h.** But English speakers sometimes write *"buenos dias"* without the accent and then stress the wrong syllable: it's DEE-ahs, not dee-AHS.

## FAQ

**Is it "buenos días" or "buenas días"?**
Always *buenos días*. *Día* is a masculine noun despite the -a ending, so the adjective is masculine plural.

**Can I just say "buenas" in the morning?**
Yes — *¡Buenas!* is a friendly, slightly informal catch-all you'll hear constantly in Mexico. In a formal setting (a job interview, addressing an elder), use the full *buenos días*.

**What's the difference between "buenos días" and "buen día"?**
Same meaning. *Buen día* (singular) is the default in Argentina and Uruguay and perfectly understood in Mexico, where the plural *buenos días* dominates.

**How do I respond to "buenos días"?**
Mirror it back — *buenos días* — often with a follow-up: *Buenos días, ¿cómo está?* Echoing the greeting is expected, not optional.
