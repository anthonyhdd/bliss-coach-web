To say "good luck" in Spanish, say **¡Buena suerte!** — pronounced *BWEH-nah SWEHR-teh* — or simply **¡Suerte!** for short, which is what you'll hear most often in everyday Mexican conversation. Friend heading into a job interview? *¡Suerte!* Cousin about to take her driving test? *¡Buena suerte!* It's warm, it's universal, and it's impossible to get wrong.

But Mexicans have a richer toolbox for wishing someone well, and some of the best options don't mention luck at all. Let's walk through them so you can pick the one that fits.

## The main way to say it

**¡Buena suerte!** — *BWEH-nah SWEHR-teh*

Pronunciation pointers:

- **Buena** starts with a "bw" glide: *BWEH-nah*. Two syllables, stress on the first.
- **Suerte** has the Spanish "ue" diphthong: *SWEHR-teh*. Roll or tap the **r** lightly if you can; if not, a soft English "r" is understood.
- The final **e** in *suerte* is a crisp "eh," never a silent "e" or an "ee."

In quick, casual speech, Mexicans very often drop the *buena* and just say **¡Suerte!** as someone walks out the door. You can also make it bigger: **¡Mucha suerte!** (*lots of luck!*) or wrap it in a full sentence: **Te deseo mucha suerte** (*I wish you lots of luck* — a touch more formal, great for messages).

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| ¡Suerte! | SWEHR-teh | Quick and casual, the everyday default |
| ¡Mucha suerte! | MOO-chah SWEHR-teh | Extra warmth: "lots of luck!" |
| Te deseo mucha suerte | teh deh-SEH-oh MOO-chah SWEHR-teh | Slightly formal; perfect in texts and emails |
| ¡Éxito! | EK-see-toh | "Success!" — very common in Latin America for exams, interviews, presentations |
| ¡Que te vaya bien! | keh teh VAH-yah byen | "May it go well for you!" — said as someone leaves |
| ¡Que te vaya bonito! | keh teh VAH-yah boh-NEE-toh | A sweeter, very Mexican version of the above |
| ¡Échale ganas! | EH-chah-leh GAH-nahs | Mexican classic: "give it your all!" |
| ¡Ánimo! | AH-nee-moh | "You've got this!" — encouragement when someone's nervous or down |
| Cruzo los dedos | KROO-soh lohs DEH-dohs | "I'm crossing my fingers" |

Two of these deserve a spotlight because they're so deeply Mexican:

**¡Échale ganas!** literally means "throw effort at it." It shifts the focus from luck to grit — you're telling someone to give it everything they've got. Parents say it to kids before exams, friends say it before a big game, and it always lands as supportive rather than bossy.

**¡Que te vaya bien!** is technically a farewell ("may things go well for you"), but in practice it doubles as a good-luck wish whenever someone is leaving *to do* something — an interview, a trip, a first date. If they're leaving, this is often more natural than *buena suerte*.

## Hear it in context

**Before a job interview:**

> — Mañana tengo la entrevista para el trabajo nuevo.
> — ¡No me digas! ¿A qué hora?
> — A las nueve de la mañana. Estoy nerviosa.
> — Tranquila, te va a ir muy bien. ¡Échale ganas y mucha suerte!

*— Tomorrow I have the interview for the new job.*
*— No way! What time?*
*— At nine in the morning. I'm nervous.*
*— Relax, it's going to go great. Give it your all, and good luck!*

**A student leaving for an exam:**

> — Ya me voy, tengo examen de matemáticas.
> — ¡Suerte! ¿Estudiaste?
> — Toda la noche.
> — Entonces no es suerte, es talento. ¡Que te vaya bonito!

*— I'm off, I have a math exam.*
*— Good luck! Did you study?*
*— All night.*
*— Then it's not luck, it's talent. Hope it goes beautifully!*

## Common mistakes

- **Saying "buen suerte."** *Suerte* is feminine, so the adjective must agree: **buena** suerte. This is one of the most common learner slips, and native ears catch it instantly.
- **Translating "break a leg" literally.** *"Rómpete una pierna"* means someone genuinely wants your leg broken. In Mexican theater circles, the traditional phrase is the cheeky **¡Mucha mierda!** — but that's insider slang; outside the stage, just say *¡Mucha suerte!*
- **Using "¡Que te vaya bien!" when nobody is going anywhere.** It's tied to departures. If your friend is nervous but staying put, use *¡Ánimo!* or *¡Suerte!* instead.
- **Forgetting ¡Éxito! exists.** For anything achievement-flavored — thesis defense, pitch, recital — *¡Éxito!* often sounds more polished than *suerte*, because it wishes success rather than chance.
- **Flat delivery.** These phrases carry their warmth in the intonation. A monotone "buena suerte" can sound sarcastic, the way a flat "good luck with that" does in English.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between ¡Suerte! and ¡Éxito!?**
*¡Suerte!* wishes luck; *¡Éxito!* wishes success. Both work before exams or interviews, but *¡Éxito!* subtly implies the outcome depends on the person's ability, which many people find more encouraging.

**Is "¡Échale ganas!" rude or pushy?**
Not at all — in Mexico it's affectionate encouragement, like "go get 'em!" Tone matters, but it's a phrase families and friends use constantly.

**How do I respond when someone wishes me good luck?**
A simple **¡Gracias!** works everywhere. Warmer: **Gracias, ojalá me vaya bien** (*Thanks, I hope it goes well*).

**Can I say "buena suerte" sarcastically like in English?**
Yes — "pues... buena suerte" with a shrug reads exactly like "well... good luck with that." Delivery is everything.
