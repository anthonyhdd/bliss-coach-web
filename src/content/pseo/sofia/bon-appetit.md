Enjoy your meal in Spanish is **buen provecho** — and in Mexico it's not a fancy restaurant flourish but an everyday courtesy you'll hear (and be expected to say) constantly. Walk past someone eating, sit down at a shared table, or finish your own meal and get up to leave, and the polite move is a quick *provecho*. It's one of those small phrases that instantly marks you as someone with manners.

## The main way to say it

**Buen provecho** — pronounced **bwen pro-BEH-choh**.

The pronunciation details that matter:

- **Buen** is one syllable: "bwen," with the *b* soft (nearly a "v"-less blend). It's the shortened form of *bueno* used before a masculine noun.
- **Provecho** stresses the middle: pro-**BEH**-choh. The *ch* is exactly the English "ch" in "cheese."
- Together: *bwen pro-BEH-choh*, three beats.

In casual Mexican use it often gets clipped to just **provecho** — a single warm word tossed to anyone eating. The literal meaning of *provecho* is "benefit" or "profit," so you're wishing the food does them good.

Who says it, and when:

- **To others who are eating**, as you arrive at or leave a table — even strangers at the next table in a fonda.
- **From a waiter** setting down your plate: *buen provecho*.
- **As you leave a table** where others are still eating, out of courtesy.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Buen provecho | Enjoy your meal | Universal, polite |
| Provecho | (clipped) enjoy / bon appétit | Casual, everyday Mexican |
| Que aproveche | May it do you good | Common, slightly more Spain |
| ¡Buen provecho a todos! | Enjoy, everyone! | Warm, at a shared table |
| Igualmente (reply) | You too / likewise | The standard response |
| Gracias, igualmente | Thanks, you too | Polite reply |
| ¡A comer! | Let's eat! (calling to table) | Casual |

The key thing learners miss is the **reply**. When someone says *provecho* to you and they're also eating (or about to), you answer **igualmente** ("likewise / you too") or **gracias, igualmente**. If you're the only one eating, a simple *gracias* is enough.

**Que aproveche** is the same idea phrased as a wish ("may it do you good"); it's heard in Mexico but is a bit more characteristic of Spain.

## Hear it in context

**Arriving where friends are already eating (everyday courtesy):**

> — ¡Ey, buen provecho! No sabía que ya habían pedido.
> — Gracias, igualmente. Siéntate, apenas llegó la comida.
> — Va, muero de hambre.
> — Provecho, pues. Pide lo que quieras.

*Hey, enjoy your meal! I didn't know you'd already ordered. — Thanks, you too. Sit down, the food just came. — Cool, I'm starving. — Enjoy, then. Order whatever you want.*

**Leaving a table in a small restaurant (polite to strangers):**

> — Con permiso, ya nos vamos. Buen provecho.
> — Gracias, igualmente. Que les vaya bien.
> — Igual. Hasta luego.

*Excuse us, we're heading out. Enjoy your meal. — Thanks, likewise. Take care. — You too. See you.*

## Common mistakes

- **Saying "bon appétit" and assuming it works.** That's French. In Spanish it's *buen provecho* — different words entirely.
- **Not replying.** If someone wishes you *provecho* while they're also eating, the expected answer is *igualmente*, not silence or just a nod.
- **Overthinking who to say it to.** In Mexico it's normal to say *provecho* even to strangers at a nearby table when you get up to leave. It's not weird; it's polite.
- **Mispronouncing "provecho" with a hard "k."** The *ch* is a soft "ch" (as in "cheese"), so it's pro-BEH-choh, not pro-VEK-oh.

## Mexico vs Spain notes

*Buen provecho* is understood everywhere, but usage habits differ.

- In **Mexico**, saying *provecho* to strangers as you pass or leave a table is common and expected — a genuine daily courtesy.
- In **Spain**, the equivalent **que aproveche** is heard, but Spaniards are a little less likely to address strangers with it; it's more within your own group.
- Pronunciation is regionally neutral here — no lisped sounds in *provecho*.
- The reply *igualmente* works identically in both regions.

## FAQ

**What does "buen provecho" literally mean?**
Roughly "good benefit" — *provecho* means "benefit" or "profit," so you're wishing that the meal does the person good. In practice it's exactly "enjoy your meal" / "bon appétit."

**How do I reply when someone says "provecho"?**
If they're eating too, say *igualmente* ("you too") or *gracias, igualmente*. If you're the only one eating, a simple *gracias* is enough.

**Is it "buen provecho" or just "provecho"?**
Both. *Buen provecho* is the full, slightly more polite version; *provecho* on its own is the casual, everyday clip Mexicans use constantly.

**Do Mexicans really say it to strangers?**
Yes — saying *provecho* to people at a nearby table when you arrive or leave is a normal, well-mannered thing to do in Mexico.

**What's "que aproveche"?**
It's another way to say "enjoy your meal," phrased as a wish ("may it do you good"). It's understood in Mexico but somewhat more common in Spain.
