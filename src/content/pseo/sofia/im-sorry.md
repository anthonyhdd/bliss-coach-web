The most common way to say "I'm sorry" in Spanish is **lo siento** — but here's the thing most courses skip: Mexicans reserve *lo siento* for serious moments, and use **perdón** for everyday slip-ups. Say *lo siento* after stepping on someone's toe and it sounds oddly dramatic, like saying "my deepest condolences" for a spilled coffee. Getting the weight of your apology right is what this page is about.

## The main way to say it

**Lo siento** — pronounced *loh SYEN-toh*.

Literally "I feel it," this is the apology for things that genuinely matter: bad news, someone's loss, a real mistake that hurt someone.

- **Lo siento mucho, no era mi intención.** — I'm very sorry, it wasn't my intention.
- **Siento mucho lo de tu abuela.** — I'm so sorry about your grandmother.

For everyday stuff — bumping into someone, arriving late, interrupting — the natural word is **perdón** (*pehr-DOHN*). It's quick, light, and does 80% of the apologizing you'll ever need in Mexico.

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | Register | When to use it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lo siento | loh SYEN-toh | Neutral–serious | Genuine apologies, sympathy, bad news |
| Lo siento mucho | loh SYEN-toh MOO-choh | Serious | Stronger version: "I'm so sorry" |
| Perdón | pehr-DOHN | Casual | Everyday slip-ups: bumping, interrupting, being late |
| Perdóname | pehr-DOH-nah-meh | Personal | "Forgive me" — heartfelt, between people who are close |
| Discúlpame | dees-KOOL-pah-meh | Casual–neutral | "Excuse/forgive me" with friends and peers |
| Discúlpeme | dees-KOOL-peh-meh | Formal | Same, but with *usted* — bosses, elders, strangers |
| Una disculpa | OO-nah dees-KOOL-pah | Very Mexican | "My apologies" — extremely common in Mexico |
| Fue sin querer | fweh seen keh-REHR | Casual | "It was an accident / I didn't mean to" |

That **una disculpa** row is worth memorizing: in Mexico you'll constantly hear **te ofrezco una disculpa** ("I offer you an apology") or just **una disculpa** on its own — from shop clerks, in official announcements, between colleagues. It's a distinctly Mexican formula that you rarely hear in Spain.

Another very Mexican softener: **qué pena** — "how embarrassing / I feel bad." In Mexico *pena* means embarrassment more than sorrow, so *¡Ay, qué pena, perdón!* is the perfect reaction when you realize you've made a small social blunder.

## Hear it in context

**Dialog 1 — Arriving late (casual)**

> — **¡Perdón por llegar tarde! Había un tráfico horrible.**
> — No te preocupes, acabamos de llegar también.
>
> — Sorry for arriving late! Traffic was horrible.
> — Don't worry, we just got here too.

**Dialog 2 — A real apology (serious)**

> — **Oye, perdóname por lo que dije ayer. Estuvo mal.**
> — Está bien, gracias por decírmelo. Todos tenemos días malos.
>
> — Hey, forgive me for what I said yesterday. It was wrong.
> — It's okay, thanks for telling me. We all have bad days.

Notice the pattern: light situation → *perdón*; heavy situation → *perdóname* or *lo siento*. The reply **no te preocupes** ("don't worry") is the standard gracious response to a small apology, along with **no pasa nada** ("it's nothing").

## Common mistakes

1. **Using "lo siento" for tiny things.** The classic gringo tell. Bumped someone's elbow? *Perdón.* Save *lo siento* for when you actually feel something.

2. **Translating "I'm sorry?" (didn't hear you) as "lo siento."** If you didn't catch what someone said, the Mexican phrase is **¿Mande?** or **¿Cómo?** — never *lo siento*, which would just confuse people.

3. **Saying "estoy triste" to apologize.** *Estoy triste* means "I'm sad" — it describes your mood, it doesn't apologize. English speakers sometimes reach for it because "sorry" covers both in English. In Spanish, they're separate.

4. **Forgetting the formal/informal split.** *Discúlpame* to your friend, *discúlpeme* to your boss or an elderly stranger. One vowel, big difference in respect.

5. **Over-apologizing.** Mexican small talk is warm and forgiving — one sincere *perdón* plus a smile closes the matter. Repeating "sorry sorry sorry" American-style reads as anxious rather than polite.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "lo siento" and "perdón"?**
Weight. *Perdón* is for small everyday slip-ups; *lo siento* expresses genuine feeling — sympathy, regret, bad news. Both are correct; the situation decides.

**How do I say "I'm so sorry for your loss" in Spanish?**
**Mi más sentido pésame** (formal condolences) or the warmer **lo siento mucho, te acompaño en tu dolor** — "I'm so sorry, I'm with you in your grief."

**How do Mexicans respond to an apology?**
Most often **no te preocupes** ("don't worry"), **no pasa nada** ("it's nothing"), or **está bien** ("it's okay").

**Is "mi culpa" a real phrase?**
Yes — **fue mi culpa** means "it was my fault" and works well paired with an apology: *Perdón, fue mi culpa.*
