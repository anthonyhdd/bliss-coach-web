Good night in Spanish is **buenas noches** — and here's the twist English speakers never expect: it's not just a farewell. In Spanish, *buenas noches* is also how you **greet** someone after dark. Walk into a restaurant at 9 p.m. and the host says *buenas noches* as a hello; leave two hours later and everyone says *buenas noches* again as a goodbye. One phrase, both directions.

## The main way to say it

**Buenas noches** — pronounced **BWEH-nahs NOH-chehs**.

Pronunciation pointers:

- The **ch** is exactly like English "church" — no tricks there.
- Both words carry their stress on the first syllable: **BWEH**-nahs **NOH**-chehs.
- Like *buenos días*, it's plural by convention: "good nights." Don't ask why — just enjoy that Spanish wishes you several.

Grammar check: *noche* is **feminine**, so the adjective is *buen**as***. Compare masculine *buen**os** días*. If you keep mixing them up, remember that *días* is the odd one out — *tardes* and *noches* both take *buenas*.

**When does the night start?** Roughly at sunset or nightfall. In Mexico, *buenas tardes* often stretches to 7 or 8 p.m., and once it's dark, *buenas noches* takes over — as a greeting first, and as a send-off when you leave or head to bed.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning | When to use it |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| Buenas noches | Good evening / good night | Universal — greeting **and** farewell after dark |
| Que descanses | "Rest well" | Warm send-off to someone going to bed (informal *tú*) |
| Que descanse | "Rest well" | Same, formal *usted* — elders, guests |
| Dulces sueños | "Sweet dreams" | Affectionate — kids, partners, close friends |
| Que sueñes con los angelitos | "Dream of the little angels" | Classic tender phrase for children, very Mexican |
| Que pases buena noche | "Have a good night" | Friendly farewell when the person isn't going to sleep yet |
| Hasta mañana | "See you tomorrow" | Between people who'll meet again the next day |
| ¡Buenas! | Clipped greeting | Casual hello in the evening (not used for goodbye) |
| Bonita noche | "(Have a) lovely night" | Very Mexican sign-off, common in texts and from staff |

Two of these are worth flagging as *especially* Mexican in flavor: **que descanses**, which is the default thing to say to anyone actually heading to bed, and **bonita noche** — you'll hear *¡que tengas bonita noche!* from waiters, Uber drivers, and friends signing off a chat. It has a warmth that "good night" alone doesn't carry.

You may also hear the singular **buena noche** in Mexico. It's common in speech, but the standard form — and the safe one to produce — is the plural *buenas noches*.

## Hear it in context

**Leaving a dinner party (friendly):**

> — Bueno, ya nos vamos. Gracias por todo, estuvo delicioso.
> — ¡Gracias a ustedes por venir! Buenas noches, que descansen.
> — Buenas noches, hasta mañana.

*Well, we're heading out. Thanks for everything, it was delicious. — Thank *you* for coming! Good night, rest well. — Good night, see you tomorrow.*

**Putting a child to bed (affectionate):**

> — Ya es hora de dormir, mi amor.
> — ¿Me cuentas un cuento?
> — Uno cortito. Y luego, a dormir. Dulces sueños, que sueñes con los angelitos.

*Time to sleep, sweetheart. — Will you tell me a story? — A short one. Then off to sleep. Sweet dreams — dream of the little angels.*

## Common mistakes

- **"Buenos noches."** Wrong gender — *noche* is feminine, so it's *buen**as*** noches. This is the mirror image of the *buenos días* trap.
- **Thinking it only means goodbye.** If someone greets you with *buenas noches* at 8 p.m., they're saying hello. Answer with *buenas noches* back — not with confusion.
- **Using "buenas tardes" after dark.** Once night has clearly fallen, *tardes* sounds off. When in doubt in the evening, *buenas noches* is the safer pick.
- **Translating "sweet dreams" word-for-word incorrectly.** It's *dulces sueños* (plural), not *dulce sueño*.
- **Saying "que descansas" instead of "que descanses."** The wish uses the subjunctive: *que descans**es***. The -es ending is what makes it a wish ("may you rest") rather than a statement.

## FAQ

**Does "buenas noches" mean good evening or good night?**
Both. Spanish doesn't split evening and night the way English does — *buenas noches* covers "good evening" (as a greeting) and "good night" (as a farewell).

**How do I say good night to someone I love?**
*Buenas noches, dulces sueños* or *que descanses, te quiero* ("rest well, I love you"). For kids, *que sueñes con los angelitos* is the classic.

**Is "buena noche" (singular) wrong?**
You'll hear it in Mexico, but plural *buenas noches* is the standard. As a learner, stick to the plural.

**What do I reply when someone says "que descanses"?**
*Igualmente* ("likewise") or simply *gracias, tú también* ("thanks, you too").
