Congratulations in Spanish is **¡felicidades!** — at least in Mexico, where it's the all-purpose cheer for weddings, graduations, new jobs, new babies, and birthdays alike. You'll also meet two siblings: **¡felicitaciones!**, preferred in much of South America, and **¡enhorabuena!**, the Spain classic. All three are correct Spanish; knowing which one to reach for (and when) is what this guide is for. One thing that is *never* correct: "congratulaciones." That word doesn't exist.

## The main way to say it

**¡Felicidades!** — pronounced **feh-lee-see-DAH-dehs**.

What's going on inside the word:

- It's the plural of *felicidad* (happiness) — so you're literally showering someone with "happinesses." Spanish congratulates in the plural, the same instinct behind *buenos días* and *muchas gracias*.
- In Mexican Spanish the **c** before i/e is a clean **s** sound: feh-lee-**s**ee-DAH-dehs. No Castilian lisp needed.
- Stress on **DAH**: the -dades ending follows the regular rule (words ending in -s stress the second-to-last syllable).

To say what you're congratulating someone **for**, use *por*: *¡Felicidades **por** tu nuevo trabajo!* (congratulations on your new job), *¡Felicidades **por** el bebé!* And to make it personal, switch to the verb **felicitar**: *¡Te felicito!* ("I congratulate you!") — which in Mexico often carries even more sincerity than the noun.

## Variations

| Phrase | Meaning / flavor | Where and when |
|--------|------------------|----------------|
| ¡Felicidades! | Congratulations! | The Mexican default — any good news, plus birthdays |
| ¡Muchas felicidades! | Big congratulations! | Warmer, extremely common in Mexico |
| ¡Felicitaciones! | Congratulations! | More common in South America; understood in Mexico |
| ¡Enhorabuena! | Congratulations! (lit. "in good hour") | Spain's go-to; sounds noticeably peninsular in Mexico |
| ¡Te felicito! / ¡Lo felicito! | I congratulate you (informal / formal) | Personal and sincere — great for real achievements |
| ¡Qué buena noticia! | What great news! | Reacting to an announcement |
| ¡Bien hecho! | Well done! | For a job well executed |
| ¡Órale, qué bien! | Wow, that's great! | Pure Mexican enthusiasm, casual |
| ¡Eso! / ¡Así se hace! | That's it! / That's how it's done! | Cheering someone on, casual |
| ¡Muy merecido! | Well deserved! | The classy add-on after any of the above |

**Felicidades vs. felicitaciones — the real difference.** Both are correct. The practical Mexican rule: *felicidades* covers everything, including birthdays and general joy (*¡felicidades a los novios!* at a wedding), while *felicitaciones* leans toward formal recognition of a specific achievement and is heard more in Argentina, Chile, Peru, and Colombia. In Mexico, when in doubt, say **felicidades** — it is never wrong.

**Enhorabuena** is lovely and you should recognize it, but it's strongly associated with Spain. Congratulate your Mexican friend with *¡enhorabuena!* and they'll understand perfectly — and possibly ask if you learned Spanish in Madrid.

## Hear it in context

**A friend announces a new job (casual, Mexican):**

> — ¿Ya les conté? ¡Me dieron el puesto en Monterrey!
> — ¡¿En serio?! ¡Órale, qué bien! ¡Muchas felicidades!
> — Gracias, todavía no me la creo.
> — Te lo mereces. Esto hay que celebrarlo.

*Did I tell you guys? They gave me the job in Monterrey! — Seriously?! Wow, that's great! Congratulations! — Thanks, I still can't believe it. — You deserve it. This calls for a celebration.*

**At a graduation (warm-formal):**

> — Licenciada Torres, la felicito. Muy merecido este título.
> — Muchas gracias, profesor. No lo habría logrado sin sus consejos.
> — Felicidades también a la familia. Deben estar muy orgullosos.

*Ms. Torres, congratulations. This degree is well deserved. — Thank you so much, professor. I couldn't have done it without your guidance. — Congratulations to the family as well. You must be very proud.*

## Common mistakes

- **"Congratulaciones."** The false friend to end all false friends. It doesn't exist in Spanish. It's *felicidades* or *felicitaciones*.
- **"Felicidades por" vs. "felicidades para."** It's *por*: *felicidades **por** tu graduación*. (*Para* would aim the congratulations at a recipient: *felicidades **para** tu mamá* = "congratulations *to* your mom.")
- **Treating "felicidades" as birthday-only.** It's the opposite — it works for every occasion. If anything, learners under-use it for promotions, engagements, and good news generally.
- **"Enhorabuena" in Mexico expecting it to sound native.** It's correct Spanish, but in Mexico it flags Spain. Match your variety: this site teaches Mexican Spanish, so lead with *felicidades*.
- **Forgetting the exclamation energy.** A flat, murmured *felicidades* reads as sarcasm. This word comes with enthusiasm included — deliver it that way.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between felicidades and felicitaciones?**
Both mean congratulations. *Felicidades* is the Mexican all-rounder (achievements *and* birthdays); *felicitaciones* is more common in South America and leans toward formal recognition of achievements.

**Can I say "felicidades" for a birthday?**
Absolutely — in Mexico *¡muchas felicidades!* is one of the most common birthday greetings, alongside *¡feliz cumpleaños!*

**How do I respond to "felicidades"?**
*¡Muchas gracias!* is all you need. Warmer options: *gracias, significa mucho* ("thanks, it means a lot") or *gracias, aún no me la creo* ("thanks, I still can't believe it").

**Is "enhorabuena" wrong in Latin America?**
Not wrong — just marked. It's understood everywhere but strongly associated with Spain; in Mexico, *felicidades* is the natural choice.
