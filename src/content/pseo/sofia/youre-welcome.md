You're welcome in Spanish is **de nada** — literally "of nothing," as in "it was nothing." It's the universal reply to *gracias*, understood from Tijuana to Buenos Aires. But here's what most learners miss: native speakers rotate through half a dozen replies, and in Mexico some of them — *con gusto*, *para servirle* — are so warm they make plain *de nada* sound almost flat. If you only ever answer *de nada*, you're leaving charm on the table.

## The main way to say it

**De nada** — pronounced **deh NAH-dah**.

Quick notes:

- Both **d** sounds are softer than English d's — between vowels, Spanish d drifts toward the *th* in "this": deh NAH-thah. A hard d is fine and fully understood; the soft one just sounds more native.
- The logic of the phrase: someone thanks you, and you wave it off — "(you're thanking me) for nothing." The same humble-deflection logic runs through almost every variation below, which is a genuinely charming corner of the language.

One thing to get out of the way immediately: **"you're welcome" does NOT translate as *eres bienvenido*.** *Bienvenido* means welcome *to a place* — a house, a city, a hotel. Reply to *gracias* with *eres bienvenido* and you've just word-for-word translated English in a way no native speaker ever would. This is the single most common mistake with this phrase, so we're flagging it before the table, not after.

## Variations

| Phrase | Literal meaning | When to use it |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| De nada | "Of nothing" | Universal default, any situation |
| Por nada | "For nothing" | Casual cousin of *de nada*, common in Mexico |
| No hay de qué | "There's nothing (to thank) for" | Polite, slightly warmer than *de nada* |
| Con gusto / Con mucho gusto | "With (much) pleasure" | Warm and gracious — a Mexican favorite |
| Para servirte | "At your service" (informal) | Friendly, helpful tone |
| Para servirle | "At your service" (formal) | Signature Mexican courtesy — shops, taxis, service |
| No es nada / No fue nada | "It's/It was nothing" | After favors, downplaying your effort |
| Es un placer | "It's a pleasure" | Polished, professional |
| A la orden | "At your command" | Common in Colombia, Venezuela, the Caribbean |
| ¡No, gracias a ti! | "No — thank *you*!" | When the thanks really goes both ways |

The distinctly Mexican gem here is **para servirle**. Thank a taxi driver, a market vendor, or a receptionist in Mexico and odds are you'll hear it — old-school courtesy that's still completely alive. You don't have to produce it yourself (it can sound over-formal from a friend), but you should absolutely recognize it.

Between friends, the smoothest replies are **con gusto** ("happily"), **no es nada**, or the affectionate deflection **para eso estamos** — "that's what we're here for," i.e., that's what friends are for.

## Hear it in context

**At a market stall (formal courtesy):**

> — Aquí están sus aguacates. ¿Algo más?
> — No, es todo. Muchas gracias.
> — Para servirle, güerita. ¡Que le vaya bien!

*Here are your avocados. Anything else? — No, that's everything. Thank you very much. — At your service, miss. Have a good one!*

**Between friends after a favor (casual):**

> — Oye, gracias por prestarme tu coche ayer. Me salvaste.
> — ¡No fue nada! Para eso estamos.
> — En serio, te debo una.
> — Con gusto. Cuando quieras.

*Hey, thanks for lending me your car yesterday. You saved me. — It was nothing! That's what friends are for. — Seriously, I owe you one. — Happily. Anytime.*

## Common mistakes

- **"Eres bienvenido" as a reply to gracias.** Worth repeating: *bienvenido* welcomes people to places. The reply to thanks is *de nada* (or any phrase in the table above).
- **Confusing "de nada" with "nada."** Answering *gracias* with just *nada* sounds clipped, almost dismissive. Keep the *de*.
- **Thinking "por nada" is rude.** In Mexico it's a perfectly friendly, casual *de nada*. (In some other countries it's less common — but you're learning Mexican Spanish, so use it freely.)
- **Overusing "no hay problema."** It exists and it's fine, but English speakers lean on it because it mirrors "no problem." Natives distribute their replies across *de nada*, *con gusto*, *no hay de qué* — vary yours too.
- **Saying nothing at all.** In Mexican culture, *gracias* almost always gets acknowledged. Silence where a *de nada* should be reads as cold.

## FAQ

**What's the most common way to say you're welcome in Mexico?**
*De nada* is the default; *con gusto*, *no hay de qué*, and *para servirle* (in service settings) are the everyday alternatives you'll hear most.

**Is "de nada" formal or informal?**
Both — it's register-neutral and safe in any situation, from a friend to a government office.

**What does "no hay de qué" literally mean?**
"There's nothing for which (to thank me)" — a slightly fuller, warmer deflection than *de nada*.

**How do I say "no problem" in Spanish?**
*No hay problema* or the very casual Mexican *no te preocupes* ("don't worry about it"). Both work — just don't let them crowd out *de nada* and *con gusto*.
