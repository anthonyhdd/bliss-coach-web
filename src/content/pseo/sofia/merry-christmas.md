"Merry Christmas" in Spanish is **¡Feliz Navidad!** — pronounced *feh-LEES nah-vee-DAHD* — and yes, it's exactly the phrase from the José Feliciano song you've heard every December of your life. It literally means "Happy Christmas," and it's the standard greeting across Mexico and the entire Spanish-speaking world.

Knowing the phrase is easy. Knowing *when* Mexicans actually say it — and what they say on December 24th, which is the real main event — is where you level up. Let's cover both.

## The main way to say it

**¡Feliz Navidad!** — *feh-LEES nah-vee-DAHD*

Pronunciation notes:

- **Feliz** ends in a soft "s" sound in Mexican Spanish (*feh-LEES*), not the "th" you'd hear in Spain.
- **Navidad** stresses the last syllable: nah-vee-**DAHD**. The final **d** is very light — let it almost disappear.
- Watch the vowels: every one is short and pure. *Nah-vee-dahd*, not "navi-dad" with an English "a."

A quick spelling note, because you'll write this in cards and texts: it's **feliz** with no accent mark (a very common misspelling is "felíz"), and **Navidad** is capitalized because it's the name of the holiday, while *feliz* stays lowercase mid-sentence: *Te deseo una feliz Navidad.*

## Variations

| Spanish | Pronunciation | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| ¡Feliz Navidad! | feh-LEES nah-vee-DAHD | The standard, works with everyone |
| ¡Feliz Nochebuena! | feh-LEES noh-cheh-BWEH-nah | Christmas Eve (Dec 24) — the big night in Mexico |
| ¡Felices fiestas! | feh-LEE-sess FYES-tahs | "Happy holidays!" — covers the whole season |
| Feliz Navidad y próspero Año Nuevo | ... PROHS-peh-roh AH-nyoh NWEH-voh | The full formal wish: Christmas + a prosperous New Year |
| Que pases una feliz Navidad | keh PAH-sess... | "Have a merry Christmas" — warm and personal (informal *tú*) |
| Que pase una feliz Navidad | keh PAH-seh... | Same wish, formal *usted* — for elders, bosses, clients |
| ¡Feliz Año! / ¡Feliz Año Nuevo! | feh-LEES AH-nyoh | "Happy New Year!" — takes over after the 25th |

A cultural key: in Mexico, **Nochebuena (December 24th) is the heart of Christmas**. That's when families gather for the huge late-night dinner — tamales, pozole, romeritos, bacalao — hug at midnight, and open presents. December 25th is mostly for recovering and eating *recalentado* (leftovers, which many Mexicans will tell you taste even better). So on the 24th, *¡Feliz Nochebuena!* is perfectly natural, and hugs at midnight come with *¡Feliz Navidad!*

Also worth knowing: the season starts early. From December 16th to the 24th, many neighborhoods hold **posadas** — nightly processions and parties reenacting Mary and Joseph's search for shelter, complete with piñatas and ponche. If you're invited to one, congratulations: you've been fully adopted.

## Hear it in context

**Midnight hugs on Nochebuena:**

> — ¡Ya son las doce! ¡Feliz Navidad, mamá!
> — ¡Feliz Navidad, mi amor! Que Dios te bendiga.
> — ¿Ahora sí ya podemos abrir los regalos?
> — Primero un abrazo a tu abuela.

*— It's midnight! Merry Christmas, Mom!*
*— Merry Christmas, sweetheart! God bless you.*
*— NOW can we open the presents?*
*— First give your grandmother a hug.*

**Saying goodbye to a coworker before the break:**

> — Bueno, nos vemos hasta enero. Que pase una feliz Navidad con su familia.
> — Igualmente, ¡felices fiestas! Y próspero Año Nuevo.
> — Gracias, descanse mucho.

*— Well, see you in January. Have a merry Christmas with your family.*
*— Same to you, happy holidays! And a prosperous New Year.*
*— Thanks, get lots of rest.*

## Common mistakes

- **Flipping the word order.** It's *Feliz Navidad*, never "Navidad Feliz." Unlike English, the adjective comes first here in the fixed greeting.
- **Writing "Felíz."** No accent on *feliz* — it's a one-syllable-stress word that follows the default rule. This typo is everywhere, even among natives, but now you know better.
- **Saying "Felices Navidades."** You'll hear the plural in Spain, but in Mexico it sounds imported. Stick with the singular *Feliz Navidad*.
- **Treating December 25th as the main event.** Show up with your greetings and appetite on the **24th**. Wishing someone a great "Christmas dinner" for the 25th tells everyone you're new here.
- **Confusing nochebuena the night with nochebuena the flower.** In Mexico, *la nochebuena* is also the poinsettia — the red Christmas plant, which is native to Mexico. Context makes it clear, and it's a fun fact to drop at the dinner table.

## FAQ

**Do Mexicans say "Feliz Navidad" before December 24th?**
Yes — from mid-December on, it's the default sign-off with anyone you won't see again before the holiday, often paired as *Feliz Navidad y próspero Año Nuevo*.

**What do I write in a Christmas card in Spanish?**
A safe, warm classic: *Les deseo una feliz Navidad y un próspero Año Nuevo* ("I wish you all a merry Christmas and a prosperous New Year").

**Is "Felices fiestas" the politically neutral option like "Happy Holidays"?**
It covers the whole season (Christmas + New Year), but it doesn't carry the same secular-vs-religious weight as in English. In Mexico, *Feliz Navidad* is the norm and nobody blinks.

**How do I answer when someone wishes me Feliz Navidad?**
**¡Igualmente!** ("likewise!") or **¡Feliz Navidad para ti también!** ("Merry Christmas to you too!").
