When someone who ghosted you resurfaces, the right text is short, unbothered, and puts the explaining on them — something like "Hey, good to hear from you. What happened?" You don't owe warmth, you don't owe coldness, and you definitely don't owe an instant reply; you owe yourself a response that doesn't pretend the disappearing act never happened.

## What's actually going on

People come back after ghosting for a handful of reasons, and only some of them are good. Sometimes life genuinely blew up — a family emergency, a breakup they were mid-way through when they met you, a mental health dip — and they're returning embarrassed but sincere. More often, though, the return is about them, not you: they're bored, another option fell through, or they want the validation of knowing you'd still answer. The classic 11pm "hey stranger" with no acknowledgment of the gap is usually that second category.

Here's the useful test: does their return message contain an actual acknowledgment? "I'm sorry I disappeared, that was unfair to you, here's what happened" is a person taking responsibility. "heyyy how have you been 😊" after six weeks of silence is a person hoping you'll skip the accountability step for them. Your reply should be calibrated to which one you got — and you get to decide whether the explanation, once offered, is good enough.

## What NOT to send

- **The paragraph of hurt.** A long message about how much the ghosting affected you hands them proof they still occupy your head rent-free. Whatever you felt, don't lead with the essay.
- **The instant enthusiastic reply.** Responding in ninety seconds like nothing happened teaches them that disappearing costs nothing. If they're worth talking to, they'll survive waiting a few hours.
- **The fake-cool "who is this?"** Everyone knows you know. It reads as a performance of indifference, which is the opposite of indifference.
- **The rage text.** If you're angry enough to unload, you're angry enough that the better move is not replying at all.
- **Immediately agreeing to plans.** "Drinks Friday?" after a ghost is a test of whether you're available on demand. Slow it down until you've heard an actual explanation.

## Copy-ready texts

**Playful**

> Well well well. Back from the dead. What's the story?

> I was about to file a missing person report. You get one dramatic explanation — make it good.

> Oh hey! I remember you. Vaguely 😄 What happened?

**Direct**

> Hey, good to hear from you. Honestly, the disappearing act wasn't great. What happened?

> I'll be straight with you — I liked where things were going, and then you vanished. If there's a real reason, I'm open to hearing it.

> Glad you reached out. Before we pick anything back up, I'd want to know what happened, because I'm not up for round two of that.

**Low-key**

> Hey. Been a while — what's up?

> Good to hear from you. I'll be honest, I moved on from expecting a reply. What's going on?

> Hey! Life's been good on my end. What prompted this?

## Timing

Don't reply on their clock. If the message lands at 11pm, morning is soon enough — a late-night ghost-return answered instantly at 11:04pm tells them exactly what they hoped. A few hours to a day is the natural window: long enough to signal your life kept moving, short enough that you're not playing a punishment game. If you need a day to figure out whether you even want to re-engage, take it. Someone who vanished for weeks has zero standing to be impatient about twenty-four hours.

And if they offer an explanation that works for you, let the pace rebuild gradually. One good apology doesn't reset the thread to where it was; consistency over the next couple of weeks does.

## When to stop

Some returns don't deserve a runway. End it — politely, once — if:

- **They dodge the question.** You ask what happened and get "haha yeah I'm bad at texting." That's not an explanation, it's a preview.
- **This is the second ghost.** One disappearance can be circumstance. Two is a pattern, and patterns don't apologize their way out.
- **The return is purely logistical.** Straight to "wyd tonight" with no acknowledgment usually means you're an option being re-checked, not a person being missed.
- **You feel worse after replying.** If the re-engagement is all anxiety and no enjoyment, your gut already voted.

A clean exit line: "I appreciate you reaching out, but the vanishing thing isn't something I want to build on. Wishing you well." Then mute the thread. ## FAQ

**Should I even reply to someone who ghosted me?**
Only if part of you is genuinely open to hearing them out. If you already know you're done, silence is a complete answer — you don't owe closure to someone who didn't give you any.

**What if their excuse is actually good?**
Real emergencies exist. If the explanation is specific, sincere, and comes with an actual apology, it's fine to give it another shot — just let their consistency over the next few weeks do the convincing, not the apology alone.

**They ghosted, came back, and now they're mad I'm being distant. Is that fair?**
No. Trust rebuilds on the timeline of the person who was ghosted, not the ghoster. Anyone who returns and demands instant warmth is telling you the apology was transactional.

**How long should I wait before replying?**
A few hours to a day. Not calculated to the minute — just enough to reflect the truth that your day no longer revolves around their name lighting up your phone.
