Short answer: send something specific to *their* profile — one detail, one playful observation or question — within a day of matching. The bar is genuinely low: most first messages are "hey," so one sentence of actual attention already puts you in the top ten percent.

## What's actually going on

A match is not interest — it's permission. Swiping costs half a second and people do it in bulk, half-distracted, so what you've earned is a chance to *create* interest, not proof it exists. That reframe matters, because it explains both why "hey" fails (it asks the other person to generate the interest for you) and why you shouldn't overinvest (a paragraph essay to someone who spent 0.4 seconds on your photo is a mismatch of stakes).

On the other side of the screen, an active profile — especially a woman's — is a wall of near-identical openers: hey, heyy, how's your week, you're gorgeous. The competition isn't clever versus more clever; it's *generic versus specific*. A message that could only have been sent to them — because it references their dog's judgmental face, their third photo in Lisbon, their claim that pineapple belongs on pizza — signals two things at once: you actually looked, and you're capable of a conversation.

One more truth nobody likes: openers decay. A match from twenty minutes ago is warm; a match from three weeks ago barely remembers swiping. Speed is a real variable, not a style choice.

## What NOT to send

- **"Hey" / "Hi" / "How's your week going?"** — not offensive, just invisible. It competes with forty identical messages.
- **"You're stunning"** — a compliment on the photos they already know they posted. It gives them nothing to reply to except "thanks."
- **The pickup-line special** — "Are you a parking ticket?" They've seen it. It got old in 2019.
- **The interview opener** — "So what do you do for work?" You're not screening a candidate; you're starting a spark.
- **Anything negative** — "you probably won't reply but," "I hate these apps," "bold of me to match with someone out of my league." Self-deprecation as an opener is just handing them a reason to agree.
- **A triple text before they've replied once** — one opener. Let it breathe.

## Copy-ready texts

Swap in their actual details — these are patterns, not scripts to paste blind.

**Playful** — the default mode for app openers:

> Your dog looks like he disapproves of me already. What did you tell him?

> Third photo: are you lost in Lisbon or posing like you're lost in Lisbon? Important distinction.

> You said pineapple on pizza is elite, so this is me matching with you *despite* my values.

> Ok the bio made me laugh and now the pressure's on me. Great system you've built.

**Direct** — when their profile gives you a real hook and you want to skip the bit:

> You climb? I just got into it and my forearms are filing complaints. What grade are you on?

> Matched with you for the hiking photos, staying for the book in photo four — what is it?

> Your taste in music is dangerously close to mine. Best concert you've been to?

**Low-key** — calm, warm, zero performance:

> Genuinely curious about the story behind the tiny boat photo.

> Your profile reads like you actually enjoy your life. Rare on here. What's the thing you're most into lately?

> Hi — I'd rather ask about your ramen ranking than say something clever. Where's first place?

Every one of these ends with an easy door to walk through. That's the real test of an opener: not "is it impressive," but "is replying effortless."

## Timing

Message within 24 hours of matching — same day is better, and if you matched while both online, a few minutes later is completely fine (the "wait three hours to look busy" rule is folklore). Matches are warmest at the start and cool fast; most conversations that ever happen start within the first day or two.

Time of day matters less than people think, but evenings (7–10pm) are when most people actually sit with their apps, so replies come faster and conversations get momentum. Avoid the 1am opener to a new match — even a good line reads differently at 1am.

If a match has gone stale for a week or two, you get one revival attempt, and it should be shameless about the gap: "Reviving this match like it's a Tamagotchi I forgot in a drawer. How's your week actually going?"

## When to stop

One opener, and if it dies: one follow-up a few days later, ideally a fresh angle rather than a "did you see my message?" After that, stop — an unanswered match isn't a rejection with a face, it's someone who was barely there to begin with. The economics of apps reward moving on cheerfully, not litigating silence.

Stop mid-conversation, too, when you're doing all the lifting: if three exchanges in they've asked you nothing and answer in single words, close the tab. Effort in the first week is the highest-signal data you'll ever get.

## FAQ

**Should I use a line from a list of "best openers"?**
Lists are fine for structure, fatal for copy-paste — popular lines get recognized. Steal the *shape* (observation + question, playful accusation, specific detail) and fill it with their profile. There's a longer breakdown of what makes openers work in our guide to [dating app openers](/charm/blog/dating-app-openers/).

**What if their profile is empty — three photos, no bio?**
Pick the most interesting photo and ask the most specific question it allows. "Where was the mountain photo taken? Looks unreal" beats "hey" even against a blank bio. If there's truly nothing, that emptiness is itself information about effort.

**They replied — now what?**
Keep the ratio: match their message length roughly, ask a real question every couple of messages, and aim to suggest meeting within a handful of good exchanges. An opener's only job is to start the conversation that leads off the app.

**Do GIF openers work?**
Occasionally, if the GIF is genuinely on-theme for their profile. As a default move, it reads as "I couldn't think of anything."
