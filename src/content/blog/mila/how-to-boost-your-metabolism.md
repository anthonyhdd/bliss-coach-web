The only things that meaningfully boost your metabolism are building muscle, moving more throughout the day, eating enough protein, and sleeping properly — and the biggest lever by far is movement, not any food or supplement. Metabolism-boosting pills, teas, and "fat-burning" foods either do nothing or nudge the number by an amount smaller than a single bite of a sandwich.

That's a less exciting answer than the supplement aisle offers, but it's the honest one — and the honest version actually works. Here's how metabolism really operates, what moves it, and how to spot the scams.

## What Your Metabolism Actually Is

Your total daily calorie burn has four parts:

- **Resting metabolic rate (RMR)** — roughly 60–70% of your burn: the energy cost of being alive (brain, heart, liver, temperature). Mostly determined by body size, muscle mass, age, sex, and genetics.
- **Non-exercise activity (NEAT)** — around 15–30% and wildly variable between people: walking, fidgeting, standing, stairs, carrying groceries. This is the most changeable piece of the whole equation.
- **Digestion (TEF)** — about 10%: the energy cost of processing food, highest for protein.
- **Exercise** — for most people, a smaller slice than they'd guess: 5–10% unless you train a lot.

Two people the same size can differ by hundreds of calories a day — and NEAT, not some mysterious "fast metabolism," explains most of it. That's good news, because NEAT is the part you control this afternoon.

## What Actually Raises Your Burn

### 1. Move more all day (the biggest lever)

The difference between a sedentary day and an active-ish day — walking to errands, taking stairs, pacing on calls, a lunchtime walk — can easily be 300–500+ calories. No supplement on Earth comes close. If you change one thing after reading this, make it a daily step habit rather than anything you swallow.

### 2. Build muscle

Muscle is metabolically active tissue: each pound burns roughly 6–10 calories a day at rest, versus about 2 for fat. That per-pound number is modest — but strength training pays twice, because the workouts themselves burn calories and the muscle raises your baseline permanently. Over a year or two of consistent lifting, adding a few kilos of muscle nudges your RMR up meaningfully and, more importantly, changes what your body does with the food you eat.

### 3. Eat enough protein

Protein has the highest thermic effect of any macronutrient — your body spends 20–30% of protein's calories just digesting it, versus 5–10% for carbs and 0–3% for fat. Shifting to a higher-protein diet can add on the order of 80–100 calories a day of burn, and it protects muscle (see lever 2) while keeping you fuller. Targets and food lists are in [how much protein per day](/mila/blog/how-much-protein-per-day/).

### 4. Sleep 7–9 hours

Short sleep doesn't slash your metabolic rate dramatically, but it wrecks the system around it: hunger hormones shift so you eat more, energy drops so you move less, and your body preferentially holds onto fat when losing weight. Fixing chronic 5-hour sleep is worth more than every metabolism food on the internet combined.

### 5. Don't crash-diet

Aggressive, prolonged calorie cutting is one of the few things proven to genuinely suppress metabolism — the body adapts to famine by downshifting and burning off muscle. Slow, moderate changes with adequate protein and strength training preserve the engine you're trying to rev. This is a core reason crash diets produce rebound weight gain so reliably.

## The Stuff That Barely Works (Sold as if It Does)

These have measurable effects in studies — measurable, and tiny:

- **Caffeine** — genuinely raises metabolic rate ~3–5% for a couple of hours. Real, temporary, and worth maybe 50–100 calories a day for regular [coffee](/mila/foods/coffee/) drinkers, less as tolerance builds.
- **Green tea** — the caffeine-catechin combo shows effects so small that reviews describe them as unlikely to matter clinically.
- **Spicy food** — capsaicin bumps burn by single-digit calories per meal. Eat chili because you like it.
- **Cold exposure** — cold does force extra burn, but the practical effect of cold showers is minor and mostly compensated by eating or moving more afterward.

None of these are harmful. The problem is the marketing math: a 40-calorie effect gets packaged as a "fat-burning secret" while the 400-calorie lever (daily movement) goes unmentioned because nobody can sell it to you.

## The Stuff That's Just a Scam

- **"Metabolism booster" pills and fat burners** — no over-the-counter supplement has been shown to raise metabolism enough to cause meaningful fat loss. Several have caused genuine harm (heart issues, liver injury); regulators have repeatedly pulled products with hidden stimulants. The best case is an expensive caffeine pill.
- **Detox teas and "flat tummy" products** — typically laxatives and diuretics. The scale drops because you lost water and, bluntly, used the bathroom. Nothing metabolic happened.
- **"Negative-calorie foods"** — the idea that celery burns more than it contains is a myth. Digestion never costs more than the food provides.
- **Eating six small meals to "stoke the fire"** — thoroughly tested, thoroughly debunked. Meal frequency doesn't change total daily burn; digestion's cost scales with total food, not number of sittings. Eat 2, 3, or 5 meals — whatever helps you eat well overall.

A useful filter: anything claiming to boost metabolism *without* muscle, movement, protein, or sleep being involved is selling you a rounding error.

## "Do I Have a Slow Metabolism?"

Probably not in the way you fear. True metabolic differences between similar-sized people are usually modest, and clinical conditions like hypothyroidism — which genuinely lowers metabolic rate and is worth a blood test if you have symptoms like persistent fatigue, cold intolerance, and unexplained weight gain — are the exception, not the norm.

What usually hides behind "slow metabolism" is quieter: a job that got more sedentary, NEAT that shrank, muscle lost over years without strength training, portions that crept, and sleep that eroded. Each is fixable — which is far better news than a broken metabolism would be. Metabolism also declines with age less than legend says: research shows it's remarkably stable from roughly age 20 to 60 when adjusted for body composition. What declines is typically movement and muscle — both reversible.

## A Realistic Metabolism Plan

1. **Walk more, structurally.** Attach steps to existing habits — calls, commutes, lunch. Aim for a noticeable increase over your current baseline rather than a magic number.
2. **Strength train 2–3 times a week.** Compound movements, progressively heavier. Muscle is the only tissue you can add that burns calories while you watch TV.
3. **Protein at every meal.** 25–40 g per meal covers the thermic bonus and protects muscle.
4. **Protect sleep like it's a workout.** Because metabolically, it functions like one.
5. **Skip the pills, keep the coffee.** Enjoy caffeine for the focus; treat its metabolic effect as a small tip, not a strategy.

Nothing on that list is purchasable in capsule form, which is exactly why the capsule industry needs you not to know it.

## FAQ

**What actually speeds up metabolism?**

In order of impact: moving more throughout the day (NEAT), building muscle through strength training, eating adequate protein, and sleeping 7–9 hours. Everything else — teas, spices, supplements, meal timing — is either a tiny effect or none at all.

**Do metabolism booster pills work?**

No over-the-counter pill has been shown to raise metabolism enough to drive real fat loss, and some have caused genuine harm. The active ingredient doing anything at all is usually just caffeine, which you can get from coffee for a fraction of the price.

**Does eating small frequent meals boost metabolism?**

No — this one has been tested repeatedly and debunked. The calorie cost of digestion scales with how much you eat in total, not how many times you eat it. Choose a meal pattern you can sustain; the metabolic effect is identical.

**Does metabolism really slow down with age?**

Much less than commonly believed. Large studies show metabolic rate is quite stable from about 20 to 60 after accounting for body size and composition. Midlife weight gain is driven mostly by declining movement and gradual muscle loss — both of which respond to walking and strength training.

**Which foods increase metabolism?**

Honestly: protein is the only food category with a meaningful effect, via its high digestion cost (20–30% of its calories). Caffeine adds a small temporary bump. Green tea, chili, celery, and "fat-burning" foods have effects too small to matter.
