The foods most consistently associated with lower inflammation in research are fatty fish, berries, leafy greens, extra virgin olive oil, nuts, whole grains, beans, and colorful vegetables — essentially, a Mediterranean-style pattern. No single food is a magic eraser; it's the overall pattern, eaten consistently, that studies link with lower markers of chronic inflammation.

Below is the full list by category, what to gently eat less of, and a realistic way to shift your plate without overhauling your life.

## What "Anti-Inflammatory" Actually Means

Inflammation itself isn't the villain — it's how your body heals cuts and fights infections. The concern in nutrition research is **chronic, low-grade inflammation**: a background hum that's been associated with how we eat, move, sleep, and manage stress over years.

Two honest caveats before the list:

- **Food is one lever, not a cure.** Research links certain eating patterns with lower inflammatory markers (like CRP). That's meaningful — but no food treats or cures a disease, and anyone managing an inflammatory condition should work with their doctor or a registered dietitian.
- **Patterns beat superfoods.** A daily turmeric shot on top of an ultra-processed diet misses the point. A mostly-plants, olive-oil-and-fish pattern is where the evidence actually sits.

## The Anti-Inflammatory Food List by Category

### Fatty fish (the omega-3 heavyweights)

Omega-3 fats (EPA and DHA) are among the best-studied nutrients in inflammation research. The richest everyday sources:

| Food | Serving | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Salmon | 4 oz cooked | Wild or farmed both count |
| Sardines | 1 can | Cheap, shelf-stable, bones add calcium |
| Mackerel | 4 oz cooked | Strong flavor, great smoked |
| Anchovies | A few fillets | Melt into pasta sauces |
| Herring | 3 oz | Common pickled or smoked |

A common target in dietary guidance is about two servings of fatty fish per week. If you're building fish into your routine for the first time, [salmon](/mila/foods/salmon/) is the easiest on-ramp — mild, forgiving to cook, and available frozen or canned.

### Berries and colorful fruit

Berries are rich in polyphenols — plant compounds researchers keep finding in diets associated with lower inflammation.

- Blueberries, strawberries, raspberries, blackberries (frozen counts, and it's cheaper)
- Cherries, especially tart cherries
- Oranges, grapefruit, and other citrus
- Pomegranate, grapes, plums

### Leafy greens and vegetables

- Spinach, kale, arugula, Swiss chard
- Broccoli, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower, and cabbage
- Peppers, tomatoes, carrots, beets, red onion

The rule of thumb: more color, more variety. Cruciferous vegetables like [broccoli](/mila/foods/broccoli/) show up over and over in diet-pattern research, and they're inexpensive year-round.

### Healthy fats

- **Extra virgin olive oil** — the backbone fat of the Mediterranean pattern; use it as your default for cooking and dressing
- **Avocado** — fiber plus monounsaturated fat
- **Nuts** — walnuts (which also carry plant omega-3s), almonds, pistachios; a small handful most days is the pattern seen in studies
- **Seeds** — chia, flax (ground), hemp

For a full breakdown of which fats to lean on, see our [healthy fats list](/mila/blog/healthy-fats-list/).

### Whole grains and legumes

- Oats, barley, brown rice, quinoa, whole grain bread and pasta
- Lentils, chickpeas, black beans, kidney beans

Their main contribution is fiber, which feeds gut bacteria that produce anti-inflammatory compounds — one of the reasons gut health and inflammation research overlap so much (more on that in our guide to the [best foods for gut health](/mila/blog/best-foods-for-gut-health/)).

### Herbs, spices, and drinks

- **Turmeric** (with black pepper, which improves absorption of its active compound), **ginger**, **garlic**, **cinnamon**
- **Green tea** and **coffee** — both polyphenol-rich in moderation
- **Dark chocolate** (70%+) — a square, not a slab

These are supporting actors. They add flavor and plant compounds, but they can't carry a diet on their own.

## What to Eat Less Of (Not Fear)

Diets associated with higher inflammatory markers tend to be heavy in:

- **Ultra-processed foods** — packaged snacks, instant meals, processed meats
- **Refined carbs and added sugar** — sodas, sweets, white bread as the default grain
- **Deep-fried foods** and heavily charred meats
- **Excess alcohol**

Notice the framing: eat *less* of, not *never*. Cutting out entire food groups isn't required by the research and usually backfires. The most reliable move is crowding out — adding so many vegetables, beans, fish, and olive oil that the other stuff naturally shrinks to an occasional role.

## A Day of Anti-Inflammatory Eating

Nothing exotic required:

- **Breakfast:** oatmeal with blueberries, ground flaxseed, and walnuts
- **Lunch:** big salad with chickpeas, roasted peppers, feta, and an olive oil–lemon dressing
- **Snack:** an orange and a handful of almonds, or green tea and dark chocolate
- **Dinner:** baked salmon, roasted broccoli and sweet potato, all tossed in olive oil with garlic

That day covers fatty fish, four-plus servings of vegetables and fruit, legumes, whole grains, nuts, seeds, and olive oil — the entire evidence-backed pattern, from a normal grocery store.

## How to Actually Shift Your Diet (Without an Overhaul)

- **Swap your default fat.** Make extra virgin olive oil the bottle that lives next to the stove.
- **Add fish twice a week.** Canned salmon or sardines count — zero cooking skill needed.
- **Put a plant in every meal.** Berries at breakfast, greens at lunch, two vegetables at dinner.
- **Upgrade the grain, keep the meal.** Same stir-fry, brown rice instead of white; same sandwich, whole grain bread.
- **Snack on nuts and fruit first.** Keep them more visible than the packaged stuff.
- **One change per week.** Sustainable beats dramatic. The research is about years of pattern, not a 10-day reset.

## FAQ

**What is the number one anti-inflammatory food?**

There isn't one, honestly — and any list claiming a single winner is overselling. Fatty fish and extra virgin olive oil are probably the two most-studied individual foods in this space, but the benefit researchers observe comes from the whole pattern: fish, vegetables, fruit, legumes, nuts, whole grains, and olive oil together.

**How long until an anti-inflammatory diet makes a difference?**

Some studies observe changes in inflammatory markers within weeks of a dietary shift, but the meaningful associations come from eating this way over months and years. Treat it as your new normal rather than a short program, and judge it by how you feel and how sustainable it is.

**Are eggs or dairy inflammatory?**

For most people, the research doesn't support fearing either. Fermented dairy like yogurt is actually a regular feature of eating patterns associated with lower inflammation, and eggs in normal amounts fit fine. Individual tolerances vary — if a food consistently disagrees with you, that's worth discussing with a professional.

**Do I need turmeric or fish oil supplements?**

Not to eat an anti-inflammatory pattern. Whole foods come first: fatty fish twice a week outperforms guessing at capsule doses, and spices are best used generously in cooking. If you're considering supplements for a specific health reason, run it by your doctor — especially since some interact with medications.

**Is coffee anti-inflammatory or inflammatory?**

Moderate coffee consumption is generally associated with neutral-to-favorable inflammatory markers in research, likely thanks to its polyphenols. The caveats are the add-ons (heavy sugar and cream change the math) and sleep — coffee late in the day can undermine the rest that also matters for inflammation.
