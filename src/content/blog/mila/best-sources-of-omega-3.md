The best sources of omega-3 are fatty fish — salmon, sardines, mackerel, herring, and anchovies — which deliver 1–2+ grams of the most useful forms (EPA and DHA) per serving. The best plant sources are walnuts, chia seeds, ground flaxseed, and hemp seeds, which provide a different form (ALA) that your body only partially converts.

Two servings of fatty fish a week covers what major health organizations recommend for most adults. Here's the full ranked list with real amounts, the EPA/DHA vs ALA distinction that most articles blur, and what to do if you don't eat fish.

## Why Omega-3s Matter (Without the Hype)

Omega-3s are essential fats — your body can't manufacture them, so they have to arrive in food. They're structural components of every cell membrane, concentrated especially in the brain and eyes, and they play a role in regulating inflammation.

The well-supported claims: populations eating fish regularly show better heart health outcomes, DHA is critical for brain and eye development (which is why it's emphasized in pregnancy guidance), and omega-3s modestly improve triglyceride levels. The overhyped claims: that omega-3s are a cure-all for mood, joints, focus, and longevity — evidence there ranges from mixed to thin. They're a genuinely important nutrient, not a miracle.

## EPA, DHA, and ALA: The Distinction That Matters

All omega-3s are not interchangeable:

- **EPA and DHA** — the "marine" omega-3s, found in fish, seafood, and algae. These are the forms your body uses directly and the forms behind most of the heart and brain evidence.
- **ALA** — the plant omega-3, found in walnuts, flax, chia, and hemp. Your body converts ALA to EPA and DHA, but inefficiently — commonly cited conversion rates are under 10% for EPA and just a few percent for DHA.

This doesn't make plant sources pointless — ALA has value of its own, and plant omega-3 foods are excellent foods overall. It just means a tablespoon of flax and a salmon fillet are not equivalents, and vegetarians need a deliberate strategy (covered below).

## The Best Fish and Seafood Sources, Ranked

Approximate EPA+DHA per typical serving (values vary by species and season — treat as good estimates):

| Food | Serving | EPA + DHA |
|---|---|---|
| Mackerel (Atlantic) | 4 oz cooked | ~2.5–3 g |
| Salmon (farmed) | 4 oz cooked | ~2–2.5 g |
| Herring | 4 oz cooked | ~2 g |
| Salmon (wild) | 4 oz cooked | ~1.5–2 g |
| Canned sardines | 1 can (3.75 oz) | ~1–1.5 g |
| Anchovies | 2 oz | ~1 g |
| Rainbow trout | 4 oz cooked | ~1 g |
| Canned salmon | 3 oz | ~1 g |
| Oysters and mussels | 3 oz | ~0.5–1 g |
| Canned light tuna | 1 can (5 oz) | ~0.2–0.5 g |
| White fish (cod, tilapia) | 4 oz cooked | ~0.1–0.3 g |

Three practical notes:

- **Canned counts.** Sardines, canned salmon, and anchovies are among the cheapest omega-3 per euro available, shelf-stable, and require zero cooking. The tinned-fish renaissance is nutritionally justified.
- **Lean fish is still good food** — cod and tilapia are great proteins — they're just not meaningful omega-3 sources. "Fish" and "fatty fish" aren't synonyms.
- **Small fish, low mercury.** Sardines, anchovies, herring, and salmon are low on the food chain and low in mercury, which is why they're the recommended defaults, especially in pregnancy. [Salmon](/mila/foods/salmon/) in particular is the rare food that's simultaneously an omega-3 heavyweight and a 25+ g protein source.

## The Best Plant (ALA) Sources, Ranked

| Food | Serving | ALA |
|---|---|---|
| Flaxseed oil | 1 tbsp | ~7 g |
| Chia seeds | 2 tbsp (1 oz) | ~5 g |
| Ground flaxseed | 2 tbsp | ~3 g |
| Walnuts | 1 oz (~14 halves) | ~2.5 g |
| Hemp seeds | 2 tbsp | ~2 g |
| Edamame | 1 cup shelled | ~0.6 g |
| Canola oil | 1 tbsp | ~1.3 g |

Usage notes that actually matter:

- **Flaxseed must be ground.** Whole flax seeds mostly pass through undigested; buy ground (or grind it) and keep it in the fridge, as the fats oxidize.
- **[Chia seeds](/mila/foods/chia-seeds/)** need no grinding — stir into yogurt, oatmeal, or make chia pudding.
- **[Walnuts](/mila/foods/walnuts/)** are the snackable option: a daily handful covers the commonly cited ALA target on its own.
- **Flaxseed oil** is for cold use only (dressings, smoothies) — it degrades with heat.

## How Much Omega-3 Do You Need?

- **The mainstream anchor: two servings of fish per week, at least one fatty.** This is the recommendation from major heart-health organizations, and it works out to roughly 250–500 mg of EPA+DHA per day on average.
- **For ALA**, adequate intake is set around 1.1 g/day for women and 1.6 g/day for men — one serving of walnuts, chia, or ground flax clears it.
- **Pregnancy:** DHA needs rise; guidance typically emphasizes 2–3 low-mercury fish servings weekly (salmon, sardines, trout) and avoiding high-mercury species like swordfish and king mackerel. Confirm specifics with your midwife or doctor.

If you eat sardines on toast once and salmon for dinner once in a week, you're done. This is one of the easier nutrition targets to hit once you know where it lives.

## No Fish? Here's the Strategy

For vegetarians, vegans, or fish-haters:

1. **Make ALA foods daily defaults.** Ground flax or chia in breakfast, walnuts as a snack, hemp seeds on bowls. Consistency matters more here because of the conversion loss.
2. **Consider algae oil.** Algae is where fish get their omega-3 in the first place — algae-based supplements provide EPA and DHA directly, no fish involved. It's the one supplement with a genuinely solid rationale for people who eat no seafood; typical doses are 200–300 mg DHA(+EPA) daily.
3. **Use omega-3-friendly oils.** Canola and walnut oil quietly contribute ALA where olive oil (excellent for other reasons) contributes almost none.

For fish-eaters, standard fish oil capsules are largely unnecessary — food covers it, tastes better, and brings protein and other nutrients along. Large trials of fish oil supplements in generally healthy people have been mostly disappointing; the food pattern, not the capsule, is where the evidence lives. ## Easy Ways to Eat More Omega-3 This Week

- Sardines on buttered toast with lemon — a 90-second lunch with a full gram-plus of EPA/DHA
- Salmon (fresh or canned) once a week as the default fish dinner
- Chia pudding or overnight oats with ground flax for breakfast
- A handful of walnuts as the afternoon snack instead of crisps
- Anchovies dissolved into pasta sauce — flavor bomb, omega-3 bonus
- Smoked mackerel flaked over a salad — no cooking at all

None of this requires new skills; most of it comes out of a tin.

## FAQ

**What food is highest in omega-3?**

Per serving, fatty fish lead: mackerel and salmon top the list at roughly 2–3 g of EPA+DHA per 4 oz. Among plants, flaxseed oil and chia seeds are highest in ALA — though ALA converts to the active forms at low rates, so fish and plant sources aren't gram-for-gram equivalent.

**How can I get omega-3 without eating fish?**

Eat ALA sources daily — ground flaxseed, chia seeds, walnuts, hemp seeds — and consider an algae oil supplement, which supplies EPA and DHA directly from the same source fish get it. That two-part strategy is the standard recommendation for vegans and vegetarians.

**Do I need a fish oil supplement if I eat fish?**

Generally no. Two fish servings a week (at least one fatty) meets mainstream targets, and large supplement trials in healthy people have shown little added benefit. Supplements make sense mainly for people who eat no seafood or have specific medical guidance to take them.

**Are eggs and grass-fed beef good omega-3 sources?**

They're minor ones. Omega-3-enriched eggs add roughly 100–250 mg per egg and grass-fed beef slightly outdoes grain-fed, but both are an order of magnitude below fatty fish. Nice bonuses in an overall diet — not a strategy on their own.

**Is canned fish as good as fresh for omega-3?**

Yes — canned sardines, salmon, and mackerel retain their omega-3 content well and are usually far cheaper per gram than fresh fillets. Canned light tuna is the weak spot: it's lower in fat and delivers only a fraction of what salmon or sardines do.
