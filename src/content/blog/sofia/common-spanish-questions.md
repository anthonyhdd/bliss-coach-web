The most common Spanish questions you'll be asked are *¿cómo estás?* (how are you?), *¿cómo te llamas?* (what's your name?), and *¿de dónde eres?* (where are you from?) — and each has a two-word answer you can learn in a minute. This guide covers the nine question words, the 25 questions real conversations actually throw at you, and exactly how to answer each one.

## The 9 Question Words

Every Spanish question is built from a small set of interrogatives. Notice they all carry a written accent — that's how Spanish marks "this is a question word":

| Spanish | English | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ¿Qué? | What? | ¿Qué es esto? (What is this?) |
| ¿Quién? / ¿Quiénes? | Who? | ¿Quién es ella? (Who is she?) |
| ¿Cuándo? | When? | ¿Cuándo llegas? (When do you arrive?) |
| ¿Dónde? | Where? | ¿Dónde vives? (Where do you live?) |
| ¿Adónde? | Where to? | ¿Adónde vas? (Where are you going?) |
| ¿Por qué? | Why? | ¿Por qué estudias español? (Why do you study Spanish?) |
| ¿Cómo? | How? | ¿Cómo estás? (How are you?) |
| ¿Cuánto/a/os/as? | How much / many? | ¿Cuánto cuesta? (How much does it cost?) |
| ¿Cuál? / ¿Cuáles? | Which? | ¿Cuál prefieres? (Which do you prefer?) |

Two quirks worth knowing early:

- **¿Cuál es...?** where English says "what is": *¿Cuál es tu nombre?* (What's your name?), *¿Cuál es tu número?* (What's your number?). Spanish uses *cuál* before *es* when picking from possibilities.
- **¿Cómo?** by itself means "what did you say?" — the polite way to ask someone to repeat. Handy constantly.

**Pronunciation:** the upside-down ¿ warns you a question is coming, and your voice should rise at the end just like in English. *Qué* = "keh," *quién* = "kyehn," *cuándo* = "KWAHN-doh."

## The 25 Questions You'll Actually Be Asked

Organized by situation, each with the question, what it means, and an answer you can deploy as-is. Casual (*tú*) forms are shown, with formal (*usted*) noted where it changes.

### Meeting people (1–8)

**1. ¿Cómo te llamas?** (formal: ¿Cómo se llama?) — What's your name?
→ *Me llamo Sam.* Or just *Sam, ¿y tú?* (Sam, and you?)

**2. ¿Cómo estás?** (formal: ¿Cómo está?) — How are you?
→ *Bien, gracias, ¿y tú?* (Fine, thanks, and you?) In Mexico you'll also hear **¿Qué onda?** (what's up?) and **¿Qué tal?** — answer both with *todo bien* (all good).

**3. ¿De dónde eres?** (formal: ¿De dónde es?) — Where are you from?
→ *Soy de Estados Unidos / Canadá / Inglaterra.*

**4. ¿Dónde vives?** — Where do you live?
→ *Vivo en Chicago.* (I live in Chicago.)

**5. ¿A qué te dedicas?** — What do you do for a living?
→ *Trabajo como ingeniera.* (I work as an engineer.) / *Soy estudiante.* This phrasing is more common in conversation than *¿cuál es tu trabajo?* — expect it.

**6. ¿Cuántos años tienes?** — How old are you?
→ *Tengo veinticinco años.* Remember: age uses *tener* (to have), never *ser*.

**7. ¿Estás casado/a? ¿Tienes hijos?** — Are you married? Do you have kids?
→ *Sí, estoy casado.* / *No, soltero/a.* (single) / *Tengo dos hijos.* Common friendly small talk in Mexico, not prying.

**8. ¿Tienes WhatsApp?** — Do you have WhatsApp?
→ *Sí, claro. Es el...* (Yes, of course. It's...) In Mexico, WhatsApp *is* the phone system.

### As a Spanish learner (9–12)

**9. ¿Hablas español?** — Do you speak Spanish?
→ *Un poco. Estoy aprendiendo.* (A little. I'm learning.) The single most useful learner answer — honest, and it sets expectations kindly.

**10. ¿Dónde aprendiste español?** — Where did you learn Spanish?
→ *Estoy aprendiendo con una app y practicando mucho.* (I'm learning with an app and practicing a lot.)

**11. ¿Cuánto tiempo llevas estudiando?** — How long have you been studying?
→ *Llevo tres meses.* (Three months now.)

**12. ¿Me entiendes?** — Do you understand me?
→ *Sí, te entiendo.* / *Más o menos.* (more or less) / *No entendí, ¿puedes repetir?* (I didn't catch that, can you repeat?)

### Getting around (13–17)

**13. ¿Adónde vas?** — Where are you going?
→ *Voy al centro / al hotel / a la playa.* (Downtown / to the hotel / to the beach.)

**14. ¿Dónde está...?** — Where is...? (you'll ask this; you'll also get asked by lost tourists)
→ Useful answer vocab: *aquí* (here), *allá* (over there), *cerca* (near), *lejos* (far), *a la derecha/izquierda* (to the right/left), *derecho* (straight ahead).

**15. ¿Cuánto cuesta?** — How much does it cost?
→ The answer is a number — which is why numbers deserve extra ear-training. *Cuesta doscientos pesos.* (It costs 200 pesos.)

**16. ¿A qué hora...?** — At what time...?
→ *A las tres.* (At three.) *A las siete y media.* (At seven thirty.)

**17. ¿Efectivo o tarjeta?** — Cash or card?
→ *Efectivo.* / *Tarjeta, por favor.* Asked at nearly every register in Mexico.

### Eating and shopping (18–21)

**18. ¿Qué le traigo?** / **¿Qué va a querer?** — What can I bring you? / What'll you have?
→ *Me trae unos tacos, por favor.* (Bring me some tacos, please.) / *Quiero una limonada.*

**19. ¿Algo más?** — Anything else?
→ *No, es todo, gracias.* (No, that's all, thanks.) / *Sí, también...* (Yes, also...)

**20. ¿Pica?** you ask — **¿Le pongo salsa?** they ask — Shall I put salsa on it?
→ *Sí, poquita.* (Yes, just a little.) / *Sin salsa, por favor.* (Without, please.)

**21. ¿Está buscando algo en especial?** — Looking for something in particular? (in shops)
→ *Solo estoy viendo, gracias.* (Just looking, thanks.) — the browser's shield, works worldwide.

### Making plans and chatting (22–25)

**22. ¿Qué haces?** — What are you doing? / What's up?
→ *Nada, aquí descansando.* (Nothing, just relaxing.) A super-common casual opener on texts.

**23. ¿Quieres ir a...?** — Do you want to go to...?
→ *¡Sí, claro!* / *Me encantaría.* (I'd love to.) / *No puedo hoy, ¿mañana?* (I can't today — tomorrow?)

**24. ¿Te gusta México / la comida / la ciudad?** — Do you like Mexico / the food / the city?
→ *¡Me encanta!* (I love it!) Warmer than *me gusta*, and in Mexico, always the right answer about the food.

**25. ¿Verdad?** / **¿No?** — Right? (tagged onto statements: *Está rico, ¿no?* — It's tasty, right?)
→ Not a real question, just agreement-fishing. *Sí, sí* and a nod is a full answer.

## Three Rules That Make Answering Easier

**1. The question contains your answer.** Spanish questions hand you the verb: *¿Dónde **vives**?* → *​**Vivo** en...* — just flip the ending from *-es/-as* (you) to *-o* (I). *¿**Tienes** hijos?* → *​**Tengo** dos.* Listen for the verb, flip it, done.

**2. Yes/no questions are just statements with a rising tone.** *¿Hablas inglés?* is word-for-word "you speak English?" — no word-order gymnastics like English "do you speak." Answer with *sí* or *no* plus the flipped verb: *Sí, hablo un poco.*

**3. Short answers are native answers.** *¿Quieres café?* — *Sí, gracias.* Nobody says "yes, I want coffee" in full. Two words plus a smile is fluent behavior, not cheating.

## When You Don't Understand the Question

It will happen constantly at first. Have these loaded:

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| ¿Cómo? / ¿Mande? | What was that? (*¿Mande?* is the classic Mexican version) |
| ¿Puedes repetir, por favor? | Can you repeat, please? |
| Más despacio, por favor. | Slower, please. |
| ¿Qué significa...? | What does ... mean? |
| No sé. | I don't know. |

*¿Mande?* deserves a highlight: it's the distinctly Mexican way to say "pardon?" — using it instantly signals you've learned *Mexican* Spanish, and it reliably earns a grin.

## From Reading Answers to Giving Them

Here's the gap this article can't close alone: recognizing *¿de dónde eres?* on a page is easy; catching it at full speed from a stranger and answering within two seconds is a different skill. The fix is reps under mild pressure:

1. **Drill the flip out loud.** Have the 25 questions read to you in random order and answer each in under three seconds. Speed of retrieval is the whole game.
2. **Answer, then bounce it back.** *Bien, ¿y tú?* — returning the question keeps conversations alive and buys you thinking time.

## FAQ

### Why does Spanish use upside-down question marks?

Because questions and statements can have identical word order (*hablas español* / *¿hablas español?*), the opening ¿ tells readers to apply question intonation from the first word. In texting, natives often drop it — but keep it in anything formal.

### What's the difference between ¿qué? and ¿cuál?

*¿Qué?* asks for definitions or open-ended "what" (*¿Qué es esto?*); *¿cuál?* selects among options and pairs with *es* for things like names and numbers (*¿Cuál es tu correo?* — What's your email?). When English says "what is your X," Spanish usually wants *cuál*.

### Is it rude to answer with just "sí" or "no"?

Not with the usual softeners: *sí, gracias* / *no, gracias* / *sí, claro*. A bare, flat *no* can sound curt in Mexico — add *gracias* and you're always polite.

### How do I ask a question back politely in formal situations?

Swap *¿y tú?* for **¿y usted?** — *Bien, gracias, ¿y usted?* That one substitution keeps you correctly formal with elders, officials, and anyone you'd address as señor or señora.
