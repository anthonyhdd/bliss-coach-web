The days of the week in Spanish are *lunes, martes, miércoles, jueves, viernes, sábado, domingo*, and the months run from *enero* to *diciembre* — and none of them are ever capitalized. That lowercase rule, plus the fact that the Spanish week starts on Monday and dates are written day-first, covers most of what trips up English speakers. Here are the full tables, the pronunciation, and the grammar that makes scheduling actually work.

## The Days of the Week

| Spanish | Pronunciation | English |
|---|---|---|
| lunes | LOO-nehs | Monday |
| martes | MAR-tehs | Tuesday |
| miércoles | MYEHR-koh-lehs | Wednesday |
| jueves | HWEH-behs | Thursday |
| viernes | BYEHR-nehs | Friday |
| sábado | SAH-bah-doh | Saturday |
| domingo | doh-MEEN-goh | Sunday |

Three things to lock in:

1. **The week starts on Monday.** Spanish calendars put *lunes* in the first column and the weekend at the end, where it belongs.
2. **All days are masculine**: *el lunes*, *el sábado*.
3. **Only *sábado* and *domingo* change in the plural** (*los sábados, los domingos*). The five weekdays already end in -s, so they stay identical: *el lunes* → *los lunes*.

Etymology makes these easy to remember: the days are named for celestial bodies and gods — *lunes* from *luna* (moon, like "Monday" = moon day), *martes* from Mars, *miércoles* from Mercury, *jueves* from Jupiter (Jove), *viernes* from Venus. *Sábado* comes from the Sabbath and *domingo* from Latin *dies dominicus*, "the Lord's day."

| noviembre | noh-BYEHM-breh | November |

Most months are cousins of their English names, which makes recognition easy. Production has two classic pronunciation traps: *junio* vs *julio* differ by one consonant sound (n vs l), and the Spanish *j* is a breathy English *h* — *junio* is HOO-nyoh, never "JOO-nee-oh."

## Why No Capitals?

In Spanish, days, months, seasons, languages, and nationalities are all **common nouns** — ordinary words, not proper names. So it's *lunes*, *enero*, *verano*, *español*, exactly as English writes "tomorrow" or "winter." This is an official rule of the [Real Academia Española](https://www.rae.es/espanol-al-dia/mayusculas-en-los-nombres-de-los-dias-de-la-semana-los-meses-y-las-estaciones-del), not a style preference.

They're only capitalized when any word would be: at the start of a sentence (*Enero fue frío* — January was cold) or inside a proper name like a holiday or street (*Viernes Santo* — Good Friday; *la Plaza 5 de Mayo*).

Writing "Lunes" or "Enero" mid-sentence is the written equivalent of a thick accent — instantly recognizable as an English-speaker habit. Train it out early.

## El Lunes vs Los Lunes: The Scheduling Grammar

Spanish doesn't use "on" with days. The article does all the work:

- **el + day = that specific day (this coming one, or a known one)**
  - *La reunión es el lunes.* — The meeting is on Monday.
  - *Llego el viernes.* — I arrive on Friday.
- **los + day = every one of those days, habitually**
  - *Tengo clase de español los lunes.* — I have Spanish class on Mondays.
  - *Los domingos como con mi familia.* — On Sundays I eat with my family.

So *el martes* = "on Tuesday," *los martes* = "on Tuesdays." Never say *en lunes* — the English "on" doesn't translate here.

Exception: with *ser* used to identify the day, there's no article at all — *Hoy es jueves* (Today is Thursday), *Mañana es viernes*.

Months behave differently: they take **en** and usually no article — *en julio* (in July), *en diciembre viajo a México* (in December I travel to Mexico).

### Scheduling phrases you'll actually use

- *¿Qué día es hoy?* — What day is today?
- *¿A cuántos estamos?* — What's the date? (very common in Mexico)
- *el lunes por la mañana / por la tarde / por la noche* — Monday morning / afternoon / night
- *el próximo martes* / *el martes que viene* — next Tuesday
- *el fin de semana* — the weekend; *el puente* — a long weekend ("the bridge")
- *de lunes a viernes* — Monday through Friday
- *cada dos semanas* — every two weeks
- *¿Te queda bien el jueves?* — Does Thursday work for you?

## Writing Dates: Day First, Always

Spanish dates follow **day–month–year**, connected by *de*:

> *el 15 de septiembre de 2026* — September 15, 2026

Numerically that's **15/9/2026** — so a date like 3/9 is March 9 to an American and *el 3 de septiembre* to everyone else. Double-check any numeric date crossing between the two systems; this one causes real missed flights.

Two more rules:

- **Cardinal numbers, not ordinals**: *el quince de mayo*, not "the fifteenth." The single exception is the first of the month: *el primero de enero* (in Spain you'll also hear *el uno de enero*).
- **Asking and giving the date**: *¿Qué fecha es hoy?* → *Hoy es 2 de julio.*

## The Seasons

| Spanish | Pronunciation | English |
|---|---|---|
| la primavera | pree-mah-BEH-rah | spring |
| el verano | beh-RAH-noh | summer |
| el otoño | oh-TOH-nyoh | fall / autumn |
| el invierno | een-BYEHR-noh | winter |

Also lowercase, and they take *en*: *en verano hace calor* (it's hot in summer). Note that *primavera* is the only feminine season. And remember the hemisphere flip if you're heading to Argentina or Chile: their *verano* is December through March.

## FAQ

### Are days and months capitalized in Spanish?

No — never mid-sentence. Days (*lunes*), months (*enero*), and seasons (*verano*) are common nouns in Spanish, so they stay lowercase except at the start of a sentence or inside proper names like *Viernes Santo* (Good Friday). This is an official RAE rule.

### What's the difference between "el lunes" and "los lunes"?

*El lunes* means "on Monday" — one specific Monday, usually the coming one. *Los lunes* means "on Mondays" — every Monday, as a habit. Spanish never uses *en* with days of the week; the article alone means "on."

### What day does the week start on in Spanish?

Monday (*lunes*). Spanish and Latin American calendars list *lunes* through *domingo*, putting the weekend (*el fin de semana*) at the end of the row — unlike US calendars, which start with Sunday.

### How do you write the date in Spanish?

Day first, then month, then year, joined by *de*: *el 15 de septiembre de 2026*, or 15/9/2026 in numbers. Use cardinal numbers for every day except the first of the month, which is *el primero*.
