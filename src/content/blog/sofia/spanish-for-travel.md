The Spanish travel phrases you actually need fit on one page: greetings, *¿cuánto cuesta?* (how much is it?), *la cuenta, por favor* (the check, please), *¿dónde está...?* (where is...?), and a handful of emergency lines. This guide organizes them by scenario — airport, hotel, restaurant, taxi, emergencies — so you can rehearse each situation before you're standing in it.

## Before You Go: The Universal Toolkit

These ten phrases work in every scenario below. If you learn nothing else, learn these:

| Spanish | Pronunciation | English |
|---|---|---|
| Hola / Buenos días | OH-lah / BWEH-nohs DEE-ahs | Hello / Good morning |
| Por favor | pohr fah-VOHR | Please |
| Gracias | GRAH-syahs | Thank you |
| Disculpe | dees-KOOL-peh | Excuse me (to get attention) |
| ¿Habla inglés? | AH-blah een-GLEHS | Do you speak English? |
| No entiendo | noh ehn-TYEHN-doh | I don't understand |
| ¿Puede repetir, por favor? | PWEH-deh reh-peh-TEER | Can you repeat, please? |
| Más despacio, por favor | mahs dehs-PAH-syoh | Slower, please |
| ¿Cuánto cuesta? | KWAHN-toh KWEHS-tah | How much does it cost? |
| ¿Dónde está el baño? | DOHN-deh ehs-TAH ehl BAH-nyoh | Where is the bathroom? |

**Pronunciation crash course:** Spanish vowels never change — A "ah," E "eh," I "ee," O "oh," U "oo." H is silent (*hola* = OH-lah). J sounds like a strong English H (*jugo*, juice = HOO-goh). Ñ is "ny" as in canyon. Double L sounds like "y" (*tortilla* = tohr-TEE-yah). Read those five rules twice and you can pronounce every phrase in this article.

**Cultural tip #1:** In Mexico, transactions open with a greeting — walking up to a counter and launching into your request without *buenos días* reads as rude. Greeting first, request second, always.

## At the Airport

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| ¿Dónde está el mostrador de [Aeroméxico]? | Where is the [Aeroméxico] counter? |
| Tengo una reservación. | I have a reservation. |
| Aquí está mi pasaporte. | Here's my passport. |
| ¿Cuál es la puerta de embarque? | Which is the boarding gate? |
| Mi vuelo está retrasado. | My flight is delayed. |
| Perdí mi maleta. | I lost my suitcase. |
| ¿Dónde recojo el equipaje? | Where do I pick up luggage? |
| Estoy de vacaciones. | I'm on vacation. (for immigration) |
| Me quedo dos semanas. | I'm staying two weeks. |

At Mexican immigration you'll hear **¿Motivo de su viaje?** (Purpose of your trip?) — answer *turismo* (tourism) — and **¿Cuánto tiempo se queda?** (How long are you staying?) — answer *[X] días/semanas*.

**Cultural tip:** signage in Mexican airports is bilingual, but taxi touts inside the terminal are not your friend. Look for the **taxi autorizado** (authorized taxi) booths where you prepay a fixed rate at a counter — it's the standard, safe system in Mexico City and most airports.

## At the Hotel

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| Tengo una reservación a nombre de... | I have a reservation under the name... |
| ¿Tiene habitaciones disponibles? | Do you have rooms available? |
| Una habitación para dos personas, por favor. | A room for two people, please. |
| ¿Está incluido el desayuno? | Is breakfast included? |
| ¿Cuál es la contraseña del wifi? | What's the wifi password? |
| ¿A qué hora es el check-out? | What time is check-out? |
| El aire acondicionado no funciona. | The air conditioning doesn't work. |
| No hay agua caliente. | There's no hot water. |
| ¿Me puede llamar un taxi? | Can you call me a taxi? |
| ¿Puedo dejar mis maletas aquí? | Can I leave my bags here? |

Useful vocabulary: *la llave* (the key), *la recepción* (front desk), *la alberca* (swimming pool — the Mexican word; Spain says *piscina*), *una toalla* (a towel), *la propina* (the tip).

**Cultural tip:** tipping hotel staff is customary in Mexico — small bills for housekeeping and porters are appreciated. Keep pesos on hand; leaving tips in foreign coins is a known annoyance since staff can't exchange coins.

## At the Restaurant

This is where travel Spanish pays off most — menus, ordering, and the check ritual.

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| Una mesa para dos, por favor. | A table for two, please. |
| ¿Me trae el menú, por favor? | Can you bring me the menu, please? |
| ¿Qué me recomienda? | What do you recommend? |
| Quiero los tacos al pastor, por favor. | I'd like the tacos al pastor, please. |
| Para mí, el pescado. | For me, the fish. |
| Una agua mineral / una cerveza, por favor. | A sparkling water / a beer, please. |
| Soy vegetariano/a. | I'm vegetarian. |
| Tengo alergia a los cacahuates. | I'm allergic to peanuts. |
| ¿Pica? | Is it spicy? |
| Sin picante, por favor. | Not spicy, please. |
| Está delicioso. | It's delicious. |
| La cuenta, por favor. | The check, please. |
| ¿Aceptan tarjeta? | Do you take cards? |

**The check ritual:** in Mexico (and most of Latin America), the server will **never** bring the bill until you ask — dropping the check unrequested is considered rushing the guest. When you're ready, catch the server's eye and say *la cuenta, por favor*, or make the universal air-signature gesture. Budget time for this; it's hospitality, not slow service.

**On spice:** *¿pica?* is the essential question. Mexican *salsa roja* and *salsa verde* on the table range from mild to weapons-grade, and servers will answer honestly — *pica poquito* (a little spicy) vs. *sí pica* (yes, it's hot, believe them).

**Tipping:** 10–15% is standard in sit-down restaurants in Mexico. Sometimes it's added as *propina incluida* — check the bill before doubling up.

## In the Taxi (and Rideshare)

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| Al centro, por favor. | To downtown, please. |
| A esta dirección, por favor. | To this address, please. (show your phone) |
| ¿Cuánto cuesta ir al aeropuerto? | How much to go to the airport? |
| ¿Usa taxímetro? | Do you use the meter? |
| Aquí está bien. | Here is fine. (to stop) |
| Puede parar aquí, por favor. | You can stop here, please. |
| ¿Me da un recibo? | Can I get a receipt? |
| Voy con prisa. | I'm in a hurry. |
| Derecho / a la derecha / a la izquierda | Straight / to the right / to the left |

Careful with the near-twins: **derecho** = straight ahead, **derecha** = right. *Siga derecho* (keep going straight) vs. *a la derecha* (to the right). One letter, very different turn.

**Cultural tip:** in Mexican cities, Uber and Didi are widely used and remove the negotiation entirely — many travelers default to them. With street taxis, agree on the price *before* getting in if there's no meter: *¿cuánto cuesta ir a...?* Small talk with drivers is normal and friendly; even a *¿cómo está?* and a *gracias, que le vaya bien* (thanks, have a good one — a very Mexican farewell) earn smiles.

## Emergencies and Health

Hopefully unused, absolutely worth knowing:

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| ¡Ayuda! / ¡Auxilio! | Help! |
| Llame a la policía, por favor. | Call the police, please. |
| Necesito un médico. | I need a doctor. |
| ¿Dónde está la farmacia más cercana? | Where's the nearest pharmacy? |
| Me duele aquí. | It hurts here. |
| Me duele el estómago / la cabeza. | My stomach / head hurts. |
| Estoy enfermo/a. | I'm sick. |
| Me robaron. | I was robbed. |
| Perdí mi pasaporte. | I lost my passport. |
| ¿Dónde está la embajada de Estados Unidos? | Where is the US embassy? |
| Es una emergencia. | It's an emergency. |

Practical notes: the emergency number in Mexico is **911**, same as the US. Pharmacies (*farmacias*) are everywhere, often with a doctor's office attached (*consultorio*) offering cheap walk-in consultations — for minor ailments, the pharmacy is the standard first stop. *Seguro* means insurance; *hospital* is conveniently the same word, pronounced *ohs-pee-TAHL*.

## Make It Stick Before the Flight

Reading phrase lists the night before works about as well as reading about swimming. A better plan for the two weeks before your trip:

1. **Rehearse by scenario, out loud.** Run the restaurant script start to finish — greeting, ordering, check — as one continuous role-play, not isolated lines.
2. **Prioritize hearing, not just speaking.** The phrases you say are predictable; the replies are the hard part. Train your ear on numbers (prices!) and the follow-up questions above.
3. **Simulate the pressure.** Give yourself the answer-in-three-seconds test. However far you get, lead with Spanish. Even a bumpy *buenos días, ¿habla inglés?* changes the whole interaction — in Mexico especially, the attempt is what counts, and people will meet you more than halfway.

## FAQ

**How much Spanish do I need for a trip to Mexico?**
Less than you think. The ~50 phrases in this guide cover greetings, ordering, directions, transport, and emergencies. Rehearsed out loud by scenario, that's enough to handle a normal tourist day without switching to English.

**Should I learn Mexican Spanish or Spain Spanish for travel?**
Learn the vocabulary of where you're going. The core phrases are identical, but everyday words differ — in Mexico you'll hear *¿mande?* instead of *¿qué?*, and *camión* can mean city bus. This guide leans Mexican because that's where most travelers use Spanish first.

**What's the most useful single phrase for travelers?**
*"¿Me puede ayudar?"* (Can you help me?) opens almost any interaction politely, and *"No entiendo, ¿puede repetir más despacio?"* (I don't understand, can you repeat more slowly?) rescues you when the reply comes too fast.

**How early should I start practicing before the trip?**
Two to three weeks of 10–15 minutes a day is enough to make the core scenarios automatic. The goal isn't fluency — it's that the restaurant script and the numbers don't require thinking when you're jet-lagged.
