To order coffee in Spanish, you need one polite formula and one drink name: **"Me da un café americano, por favor"** (meh dah oon kah-FEH ah-meh-ree-KAH-noh, pohr fah-VOHR) — "I'll have an americano, please." Swap in any drink — *un latte*, *un capuchino*, *un espresso* — and you've handled 90% of every café interaction from Mexico City to Madrid.

The other 10% is what this guide covers: the follow-up questions the barista will fire at you, size and milk modifications, the drinks that don't translate, and the regional quirks that make a coffee order in Oaxaca different from one in Barcelona.

## The Three Ordering Formulas

Spanish has several polite ways to order, all interchangeable at a café counter:

- **Me da... por favor** (meh dah) — literally "you give me..." Standard, friendly, what most Mexicans actually say: *Me da un latte grande, por favor.*
- **Quisiera...** (kee-SYEH-rah) — "I would like..." Slightly more formal, always safe: *Quisiera un café con leche.*
- **¿Me puede dar...?** (meh PWEH-deh dahr) — "Could you give me...?" The question form, extra polite: *¿Me puede dar un americano para llevar?*

What you should *not* do is translate "Can I get...?" as *¿Puedo tener...?* — it's grammatical but instantly marks you as translating from English. And skip the direct *quiero* (I want) — not rude exactly, but *me da* and *quisiera* are what the room says.

One word unlocks everything to-go: **para llevar** (PAH-rah yeh-VAHR) — "to take away." For here is **para tomar aquí**. The barista will usually ask: **¿Para tomar aquí o para llevar?**

## Coffee Drinks: What to Call What You Want

### The international standards

- **un espresso** (ehs-PREH-soh) — also *un café solo* in Spain
- **un americano** (ah-meh-ree-KAH-noh) — espresso plus hot water; the closest thing to US drip coffee
- **un café con leche** (kah-FEH kohn LEH-cheh) — coffee with milk, roughly half and half; the default breakfast coffee of the Spanish-speaking world
- **un latte** (LAH-teh) — in modern cafés everywhere; more milk than a café con leche
- **un capuchino** (kah-poo-CHEE-noh) — cappuccino
- **un cortado** (kohr-TAH-doh) — espresso "cut" with a splash of milk
- **un mocha / un moka** — chocolate plus espresso and milk
- **un café descafeinado** (dehs-kah-fay-NAH-doh) — decaf; shorten to *descafeinado* and every barista understands
- **un café helado / un café frío** — iced coffee; specify **con hielo** (with ice) if you want it over ice

### The Mexican specialties

- **café de olla** (KAH-feh deh OH-yah) — coffee simmered in a clay pot with cinnamon and *piloncillo* (raw cane sugar). Order it wherever you see it; it's Mexico in a cup.
- **café lechero** (leh-CHEH-roh) — a Veracruz institution: strong coffee in a tall glass, hot milk poured theatrically from height. Tap the glass with a spoon for a refill.
- **café de chiapas / de veracruz / de oaxaca** — menus often name the growing region; all mean good local beans.

### Sizes

Chains use *chico* (small), *mediano* (medium), *grande* (large) — **CHEE-koh, meh-DYAH-noh, GRAHN-deh**. Starbucks sizes stay in Starbucks-speak (*un venti*), so don't overthink it there. In a traditional café, drinks come in one size and nobody asks.

## Milk, Sugar, and Modifications

The barista's follow-ups come fast. Here's the vocabulary to catch them:

- **¿Con leche entera o deslactosada?** — whole milk or lactose-free? (Deslactosada is everywhere in Mexico.)
- **leche de almendra / de avena / de soya** — almond / oat / soy milk: *¿Tiene leche de avena?* (Do you have oat milk?)
- **el azúcar** (ah-SOO-kahr) — sugar: *con azúcar* / *sin azúcar* (with / without)
- **la miel** (myehl) — honey
- **un sobre de azúcar** — a sugar packet; sweeteners are *edulcorantes*, but asking for *Splenda* works fine
- **caliente / tibio / extra caliente** — hot / lukewarm / extra hot
- **doble** (DOH-bleh) — double shot: *un americano doble*
- **con poca leche / con más leche** — with a little milk / with more milk

The pattern for all modifications is beautiful and simple: **con** (with), **sin** (without), placed after the drink. *Un capuchino grande, sin azúcar, con leche de avena, para llevar.* You just built a Starbucks-grade order with three prepositions.

## The Full Dialogue: Hear It Before You Live It

**Barista:** Hola, ¿qué le doy? — *Hi, what can I get you?*
**You:** Me da un café con leche mediano, por favor. — *A medium café con leche, please.*
**Barista:** ¿Para tomar aquí o para llevar? — *For here or to go?*
**You:** Para llevar. — *To go.*
**Barista:** ¿Algo más? — *Anything else?*
**You:** Sí, un pan dulce. ¿Cuánto es? — *Yes, a pastry. How much is it?*
**Barista:** Son setenta y cinco pesos. — *That's 75 pesos.*
**You:** Aquí tiene. Gracias. — *Here you go. Thanks.*
**Barista:** ¿A nombre de quién? — *What name?* (for the cup)
**You:** De Ana. — *Ana.*

Two curveballs worth pre-loading: **¿Algo más?** (anything else?) gets *No, es todo, gracias* — "no, that's all, thanks" — and the price will come at you as a number, fast. ## Café Small Talk and Survival Lines

- **¿Me regala una servilleta?** — Mexican politeness for "could I get a napkin?" (*Regalar* = "to gift"; nobody expects a free napkin ceremony, it's just the idiom.)
- **¿Tienen wifi? ¿Cuál es la contraseña?** — Do you have wifi? What's the password? (The real reason half of us are at the café.)
- **¿Dónde están los baños?** — Where are the bathrooms?
- **Disculpe** (dees-KOOL-peh) — excuse me, for getting the barista's attention; more on when to use it in [how to say excuse me in Spanish](/sofia/how-to-say/excuse-me/)
- **Está muy rico el café.** — The coffee's great. Baristas everywhere run on this sentence.

Tipping: in Mexican cafés, dropping coins in the *propinas* jar or adding 5–10% at table service is appreciated; in Spain, rounding up is plenty.

## Regional Differences in 30 Seconds

- **Mexico**: *me da* ordering, *café de olla* and *lechero* as local stars, deslactosada milk everywhere, pesos.
- **Spain**: a plain coffee is *un solo*; *café con leche* rules breakfast; a *cortado* mid-afternoon; ask for *un café con hielo* and you'll get hot espresso plus a glass of ice to pour it over — trust the system.
- **Argentina**: a *lágrima* ("teardrop") is mostly milk with a splash of coffee; a *cortado* is the standard.
- Everywhere: *un café, por favor* plus pointing at the menu is a complete, dignified order. Fluency is a bonus, not an entry requirement.

Ordering coffee is the single best beginner speaking mission there is: it's a 30-second interaction, the script is 90% predictable, the stakes are zero, and you get a reward at the end. ## FAQ

**What's the most natural way to order coffee in Spanish?**

"Me da un [drink], por favor" is what native speakers actually say at the counter — friendly and standard across Latin America. "Quisiera un [drink]" is the slightly more formal textbook version, and both beat translating "can I get" literally as *¿puedo tener?*, which sounds off in Spanish.

**How do I ask for coffee to go in Spanish?**

Add "para llevar" (to take away) to the end of your order: *Un americano grande, para llevar, por favor.* If the barista asks first, the question will be *¿Para tomar aquí o para llevar?* — "for here or to go?" Answer with whichever half applies.

**What is café de olla?**

Mexico's traditional coffee, simmered in a clay pot (*olla*) with cinnamon and piloncillo, a raw cane sugar. It comes pre-sweetened and spiced — don't add anything, and don't ask for it with oat milk. You'll find it at traditional cafés, markets, and grandmothers' kitchens rather than espresso bars.

**How do I ask for milk alternatives like oat milk?**

Ask *¿Tiene leche de avena?* (Do you have oat milk?) — almond is *leche de almendra*, soy is *leche de soya* (*soja* in Spain). In Mexico, also know *leche deslactosada*, lactose-free milk, which even small cafés stock. Then order: *un latte con leche de avena*.

**Is "café con leche" the same as a latte?**

Close but not identical. A café con leche is roughly equal parts strong coffee and hot milk — the traditional breakfast drink. A latte is espresso with much more steamed milk and a foam cap, and it's what you'll get at any third-wave café. If a menu has both, the latte will be the bigger, milkier, pricier one.
