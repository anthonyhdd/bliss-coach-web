The Spanish subjunctive is a verb mood used when a sentence expresses something other than plain fact — a wish, an emotion, a doubt, a demand, or something that hasn't happened yet. You'll recognize it by its "flipped" endings (*hablar* → *hable*, *comer* → *coma*) and by the word **que** ("keh") that almost always introduces it: *Quiero que **vengas*** (I want you to come).

That's the whole idea. The subjunctive has a scary reputation because textbooks bury it under fifty rules, but it boils down to one question: **is the speaker stating reality, or reacting to it?** Stating reality → indicative (the normal tenses you already know). Wanting it, doubting it, feeling something about it → subjunctive.

## What the Subjunctive Actually Is

Spanish verbs don't just have tenses (past, present, future) — they have **moods**, which signal the speaker's attitude:

- **Indicative** = "this is real." *Sofia **viene** a la fiesta.* (Sofia is coming to the party. Fact.)
- **Subjunctive** = "this lives in my head." *Espero que Sofia **venga** a la fiesta.* (I hope Sofia comes to the party. Not a fact yet — a hope.)

Same event, different mood, because the second sentence isn't reporting what Sofia does; it's reporting what *I hope*. English still has traces of this too — "I suggest that he **be** on time," "if I **were** you." Spanish just never stopped using it.

The structural pattern is almost always the same:

> **[trigger phrase] + que + [subjunctive verb]**

*Quiero que... Dudo que... Es importante que... Ojalá que...* — hear one of those, and the verb after **que** flips to subjunctive.

## How to Conjugate the Present Subjunctive

Good news: if you know the present indicative, you're 90% there. (If you need a refresher first, start with our guide to [Spanish present tense conjugation](/sofia/blog/spanish-present-tense-conjugation/).) The recipe has three steps:

1. Take the **yo** form of the present tense: *hablo, como, vivo, tengo, conozco*.
2. Drop the **-o**.
3. Add the **opposite** endings: -ar verbs take -e endings, -er/-ir verbs take -a endings.

| Person | hablar → hable | comer → coma | vivir → viva |
|---|---|---|---|
| yo | hable | coma | viva |
| tú | hables | comas | vivas |
| él / ella / usted | hable | coma | viva |
| nosotros | hablemos | comamos | vivamos |
| ustedes / ellos | hablen | coman | vivan |

Starting from the **yo** form is what makes irregulars free: *tengo* → **tenga**, *digo* → **diga**, *conozco* → **conozca**, *veo* → **vea**. If you can say "tengo," you can say "tenga."

Only six common verbs break the recipe:

| Verb | Subjunctive (yo) |
|---|---|
| ser | sea ("SEH-ah") |
| estar | esté ("es-TEH") |
| ir | vaya ("BAH-yah") |
| haber | haya ("AH-yah") |
| saber | sepa ("SEH-pah") |
| dar | dé ("DEH") |

Stack the first letters — **D**ar, **I**r, **S**er, **H**aber, **E**star, **S**aber — and you get **DISHES**: the six irregular dishes you have to wash by hand.

## WEIRDO: The Six Triggers

You don't decide "hmm, is this subjunctive-y?" in the abstract — you learn the **trigger phrases** that demand it. The classic mnemonic is **WEIRDO**:

### W — Wishes and Wants

Wanting, hoping, preferring, requesting — anything where one person wants another person (or the universe) to do something.

- **Quiero que me ayudes.** — I want you to help me.
- **Espero que tengas un buen día.** — I hope you have a good day.
- **Ojalá que llueva pronto.** — Hopefully it rains soon. (*Ojalá*, "oh-hah-LAH," is a gift from Arabic — "God willing" — and it *always* takes subjunctive.)

### E — Emotions

Feelings *about* something: happiness, sadness, surprise, fear, annoyance.

- **Me alegra que estés aquí.** — I'm glad you're here.
- **Me molesta que llegue tarde.** — It bothers me that he arrives late.
- **Qué lástima que no puedas venir.** — What a shame you can't come.

### I — Impersonal Expressions

*Es* + adjective + *que*: important, necessary, good, strange, possible...

- **Es importante que practiques todos los días.** — It's important that you practice every day.
- **Es posible que vayamos a Oaxaca.** — It's possible we'll go to Oaxaca.
- Exception: expressions of certainty stay indicative. *Es obvio que **tienes** razón.* (It's obvious you're right.)

### R — Recommendations

Advising, suggesting, insisting, ordering.

- **Te recomiendo que pruebes los tacos al pastor.** — I recommend you try the tacos al pastor.
- **El doctor sugiere que descanses.** — The doctor suggests you rest.

### D — Doubt and Denial

Doubting, not believing, denying.

- **Dudo que sea verdad.** — I doubt it's true.
- **No creo que venga hoy.** — I don't think she's coming today.
- Flip alert: **creo que** (I believe) takes indicative because you're asserting; **no creo que** takes subjunctive because you're doubting. *Creo que **viene** / No creo que **venga**.*

### O — Ojalá (and Other Fixed Expressions)

- **Ojalá que gane México.** — Hopefully Mexico wins.
- Other always-subjunctive phrases: **para que** (so that), **antes de que** (before), **a menos que** (unless), **cuando** + future events (*Cuando **llegues**, llámame* — When you arrive, call me).

## 10 Sentences You'd Actually Say

1. **Quiero que vengas conmigo.** — I want you to come with me.
2. **Espero que te guste.** — I hope you like it.
3. **Es mejor que salgamos temprano.** — It's better that we leave early.
4. **No creo que haya tiempo.** — I don't think there's time.
5. **Mi mamá quiere que la llame.** — My mom wants me to call her.
6. **Te sugiero que hables con ella.** — I suggest you talk to her.
7. **Me sorprende que no lo sepas.** — I'm surprised you don't know.
8. **Dile que me espere.** — Tell him to wait for me.
9. **Cuando tengas tiempo, avísame.** — When you have time, let me know.
10. **Que te vaya bien.** — Hope it goes well for you. (A daily-life Mexican goodbye — pure subjunctive, and nobody thinks of it as grammar.)

That last one is worth pausing on: everyday phrases like *que te vaya bien, que descanses* (sleep well), and *que tengas buen viaje* (have a good trip) are subjunctive with the trigger (*deseo que...*) simply dropped. You've probably already used the subjunctive without knowing it.

## The One-Subject Shortcut

The subjunctive needs **two different subjects** on either side of *que*. If the same person wants and does, skip *que* entirely and use the infinitive:

- **Quiero aprender español.** — I want to learn Spanish. (I want, I learn → infinitive.)
- **Quiero que mis hijos aprendan español.** — I want my kids to learn Spanish. (I want, they learn → subjunctive.)

This one rule prevents the classic learner error *"quiero que yo aprenda"* — a sentence no native speaker would produce.

## Common Mistakes English Speakers Make

- **Translating "I want you to..." literally.** English uses an infinitive ("I want you to come"); Spanish demands *que* + subjunctive: **quiero que vengas**, never ~~"quiero tú venir"~~.
- **Using subjunctive after "creo que."** Affirmative belief is assertion → indicative. Only the negative (*no creo que*) flips to subjunctive.
- **Forgetting the mood flip after ojalá.** *Ojalá* is never followed by indicative. Drill *ojalá que llueva*, not ~~"ojalá que llueve"~~.
- **Building the subjunctive from the infinitive instead of the yo form.** That's how you get ~~"tena"~~ instead of **tenga**. Always start from *tengo, digo, salgo, conozco*.
- **Avoiding it entirely.** Learners often reroute every sentence to dodge the subjunctive. Don't — a wrong subjunctive is still understood, and Mexicans will gently repeat the correct form back to you, which is exactly how it sticks.

## FAQ

**What is the subjunctive in Spanish in simple terms?**

It's a verb mood (not a tense) used when you're not stating a fact but expressing a wish, emotion, doubt, recommendation, or something that hasn't happened yet. It almost always appears after *que*: *Quiero que vengas* (I want you to come). The endings flip: -ar verbs take -e endings, -er/-ir verbs take -a endings.

**How do I know when to use the subjunctive?**

Learn the trigger phrases rather than reasoning from scratch. The WEIRDO mnemonic covers them: Wishes, Emotions, Impersonal expressions, Recommendations, Doubt/denial, and Ojalá. If the main clause expresses one of those and the subject changes after *que*, the second verb is subjunctive.

**Is "creo que" followed by the subjunctive?**

No — affirmative *creo que* states a belief, so it takes the indicative: *creo que viene*. The negative *no creo que* expresses doubt, so it flips to subjunctive: *no creo que venga*. The same pattern applies to *pienso que / no pienso que* and *es cierto que / no es cierto que*.

**Do native speakers really use the subjunctive every day?**

Constantly — it's not formal or literary. Everyday Mexican phrases like *que te vaya bien* (have a good one), *que descanses* (sleep well), and *cuando llegues, me avisas* (let me know when you arrive) are all subjunctive. Skipping it sounds noticeably foreign, but using it imperfectly is always understood.

**How long does it take to learn the Spanish subjunctive?**

Understanding the present subjunctive forms and WEIRDO triggers takes a week or two of study. Producing it automatically in conversation typically takes a couple of months of regular speaking practice, because you're training a reflex, not memorizing a rule. Start with the highest-frequency triggers — *quiero que, espero que, ojalá* — and the rest follows.
