To ask for directions in Spanish, the two phrases you need are **¿Dónde está…?** (Where is…?) and **¿Cómo llego a…?** (How do I get to…?). For example, **¿Dónde está el baño?** (Where is the bathroom?) or **¿Cómo llego a la estación?** (How do I get to the station?) — start with a polite **Disculpe** (Excuse me) and you're set for almost any situation.

The trickier half is *understanding the answer*, because a helpful local will fire back a stream of lefts, rights, and blocks. So this guide covers both directions: how to ask clearly, and how to decode what comes back — with the Mexican expressions you'll genuinely hear on the street.

## Start Politely: The Opener

Before any question, get someone's attention respectfully. Use the formal *usted* — you're talking to a stranger.

- **Disculpe…** (Excuse me… — formal, the standard opener)
- **Perdón…** (Pardon me… / Sorry…)
- **Disculpe, ¿me puede ayudar?** (Excuse me, can you help me?)
- **¿Me podría ayudar, por favor?** (Could you help me, please? — extra polite)

In Mexico, a warm **Disculpe** with a smile goes a long way. People are generally very willing to help a visitor find their way.

## The Core Questions

Here are the phrases that cover almost everything you'll need.

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| **¿Dónde está…?** | Where is…? |
| **¿Dónde queda…?** | Where is…? (very common in Mexico) |
| **¿Cómo llego a…?** | How do I get to…? |
| **¿Cómo puedo llegar a…?** | How can I get to…? |
| **¿Está lejos?** | Is it far? |
| **¿Está cerca?** | Is it near? |
| **¿Cuánto se hace caminando?** | How long does it take on foot? |
| **¿Voy bien para…?** | Am I going the right way to…? |

Fill in the blank with wherever you're headed:

- **¿Dónde está el metro?** (Where is the subway?)
- **¿Cómo llego al centro?** (How do I get downtown?)
- **¿Dónde queda la farmacia más cercana?** (Where's the nearest pharmacy?)

Note **al** = *a + el* (to the), a required contraction: **¿Cómo llego al hotel?**, never "a el hotel."

## Understanding the Answer: Direction Words

This is the part travelers skip and then regret. Here are the words the local will use back at you.

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| a la derecha | to the right |
| a la izquierda | to the left |
| (todo) derecho / recto | straight ahead |
| adelante | ahead / forward |
| atrás | back / behind |
| cerca | near |
| lejos | far |
| aquí / acá | here |
| allí / allá | there |
| la esquina | the corner |
| la cuadra / la manzana | the block |
| el semáforo | the traffic light |
| enfrente de | in front of / across from |
| al lado de | next to |
| entre | between |

Watch this classic trap: **derecho** (with the -o) means "straight ahead," while **a la derecha** means "to the right." One tiny difference, two opposite meanings. Mixing them up is the number-one directions mistake.

In Mexico, distance is very often counted in **cuadras** (blocks): **"a dos cuadras"** means "two blocks away."

## What a Real Answer Sounds Like

Put the direction words together and you get sentences like these:

- **Sigue derecho dos cuadras y da vuelta a la derecha.** (Go straight two blocks and turn right.)
- **Está a la izquierda, enfrente del banco.** (It's on the left, across from the bank.)
- **Camina hasta el semáforo y luego a la izquierda.** (Walk to the traffic light and then left.)
- **Está en la esquina, al lado de la farmacia.** (It's on the corner, next to the pharmacy.)

A very Mexican verb here is **dar vuelta** (to turn): **"da vuelta a la derecha"** (turn right). You'll also hear **"derechito"** — the affectionate diminutive of *derecho* — meaning "straight ahead" in a friendly, reassuring tone.

## Your Rescue Phrases

You won't catch everything the first time. That's normal — have these ready.

- **¿Puede repetir, por favor?** (Can you repeat that, please?)
- **Más despacio, por favor.** (Slower, please.)
- **No entendí bien.** (I didn't quite understand.)
- **¿Me lo puede mostrar en el mapa?** (Can you show me on the map?)
- **¿Está lejos de aquí?** (Is it far from here?)
- **Muchas gracias, muy amable.** (Thank you so much, very kind of you.)

That last one — **muy amable** — is a lovely Mexican way to thank someone warmly for their help. Always close on a gracias.

## A Full Sample Exchange

> — **Disculpe, ¿cómo llego al Zócalo?** (Excuse me, how do I get to the Zócalo?)
> — **Está cerca. Sigue derecho tres cuadras y da vuelta a la izquierda en el semáforo.** (It's close. Go straight three blocks and turn left at the traffic light.)
> — **¿Está lejos caminando?** (Is it far on foot?)
> — **No, unos diez minutos.** (No, about ten minutes.)
> — **Muchas gracias, muy amable.** (Thank you so much, very kind of you.)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Confusing *derecho* and *derecha*.** *Derecho* = straight; *a la derecha* = to the right. This is the single most common error — burn the difference into memory.

**Forgetting the *al* contraction.** It's *al centro*, *al hospital* — never "a el."

**Using *tú* with strangers.** Directions are a classic *usted* situation. Say **¿me puede ayudar?**, not *¿me puedes ayudar?*, with someone you don't know.

**Nodding along without understanding.** If you didn't catch it, say **más despacio, por favor**. Locals are happy to slow down — pretending to understand just gets you lost.

## FAQ

### How do you say "where is" in Spanish?

**¿Dónde está…?** is the standard phrase — for example, *¿Dónde está el baño?* (Where is the bathroom?). In Mexico you'll also hear **¿Dónde queda…?**, which means the same thing and is extremely common for asking where a place is located.

### What's the difference between "derecho" and "derecha"?

**Derecho** means "straight ahead," while **a la derecha** means "to the right." They look almost identical but point in different directions, so this is the most important pair to keep straight. "To the left" is *a la izquierda*.

### How do I politely get a stranger's attention to ask directions?

Start with **Disculpe** (Excuse me) or **Perdón** (Pardon me), using the formal *usted*. A full polite version is **Disculpe, ¿me puede ayudar?** (Excuse me, can you help me?). Ending with **muy amable** (very kind of you) is a warm Mexican touch.

### What does "a dos cuadras" mean?

It means "two blocks away." In Mexico, distances on foot are usually counted in **cuadras** (blocks), so an answer like *"a dos cuadras a la derecha"* means "two blocks away, on the right." *Manzana* is another word for a city block.

### What should I say if I don't understand the directions?

Use **¿Puede repetir, por favor?** (Can you repeat, please?) or **más despacio, por favor** (slower, please). You can also ask **¿me lo puede mostrar en el mapa?** (can you show me on the map?). Locals are happy to help — don't just nod and walk off lost.
