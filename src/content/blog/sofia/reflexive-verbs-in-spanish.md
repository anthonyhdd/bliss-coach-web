Reflexive verbs in Spanish are verbs where the subject performs the action *on itself*, and they use a matching reflexive pronoun — **me, te, se, nos, os, se** — placed before the conjugated verb. For example, **me lavo** means "I wash myself," while plain **lavo** would mean "I wash (something else)." The giveaway is the infinitive ending in **-se**, like *lavarse* (to wash oneself) or *levantarse* (to get up).

They sound abstract, but you already use them constantly in English — you *wake yourself up*, *get dressed*, *sit down*. Spanish just marks these more explicitly. Once you see the pattern, they're one of the most learnable parts of the language. Here's exactly how they work.

## What Makes a Verb Reflexive

A reflexive verb loops the action back onto the subject. Compare:

- **Lavo el coche.** (I wash the car.) — normal verb, acting on the car.
- **Me lavo.** (I wash myself.) — reflexive, acting on myself.

In the dictionary, reflexive verbs are listed with **-se** stuck to the infinitive: *ducharse* (to shower), *vestirse* (to get dressed), *despertarse* (to wake up). That **-se** is your signal: "this action comes back to the person doing it."

## The Reflexive Pronouns

There are six, one for each subject. You'll notice they mostly match the object pronouns you may already know.

| Subject | Pronoun | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| yo | me | myself |
| tú | te | yourself |
| él / ella / usted | se | himself / herself / yourself |
| nosotros | nos | ourselves |
| vosotros | os | yourselves |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes | se | themselves |

The key move: the pronoun must **agree with the subject**. If *yo* does the action, you use *me*; if *ellos* do it, you use *se*.

## How to Conjugate: Step by Step

Take *levantarse* (to get up). Two things happen at once — you conjugate the verb normally *and* add the matching pronoun in front.

| Subject | Pronoun + verb | English |
|---|---|---|
| yo | me levanto | I get up |
| tú | te levantas | you get up |
| él / ella / usted | se levanta | he/she gets up / you get up |
| nosotros | nos levantamos | we get up |
| vosotros | os levantáis | you all get up |
| ellos / ustedes | se levantan | they/you all get up |

So the recipe is: **pick the pronoun for your subject → conjugate the verb as usual → place the pronoun right before it.**

- **Me despierto a las siete.** (I wake up at seven.)
- **¿A qué hora te acuestas?** (What time do you go to bed?)
- **Nos vestimos rápido.** (We get dressed quickly.)

## Where the Pronoun Goes

Usually the pronoun sits **before** the conjugated verb, as above. But with infinitives, gerunds, and commands, you can attach it to the end.

| Structure | Two correct options |
|---|---|
| Infinitive | **Me voy a duchar.** / **Voy a ducharme.** (I'm going to shower.) |
| Gerund (-ing) | **Se está peinando.** / **Está peinándose.** (She's combing her hair.) |
| Affirmative command | **¡Levántate!** (Get up!) |

Both versions in the first two rows are equally correct — pick whichever feels natural. Notice that attaching the pronoun sometimes adds a written accent (*peinándose*, *levántate*) to keep the stress in the right place.

## Common Reflexive Verbs (Daily Routine)

Reflexive verbs dominate when you describe your morning and night. This is the single most useful set to memorize.

| Reflexive verb | English |
|---|---|
| despertarse | to wake up |
| levantarse | to get up |
| ducharse | to shower |
| bañarse | to bathe / shower |
| lavarse | to wash (oneself) |
| cepillarse (los dientes) | to brush (one's teeth) |
| peinarse | to comb one's hair |
| vestirse | to get dressed |
| maquillarse | to put on makeup |
| afeitarse | to shave |
| acostarse | to go to bed |
| dormirse | to fall asleep |

A full routine sounds like this: **"Me despierto, me levanto, me ducho, me visto y me voy."** (I wake up, get up, shower, get dressed, and leave.) — notice *me* repeating with each reflexive verb.

## Beyond Routine: Emotions and Change

Reflexives aren't only about washing and dressing. Many describe **changes of state or emotion**, where English would use "get" or "become."

| Reflexive verb | English |
|---|---|
| sentirse | to feel |
| enojarse | to get angry (Mexican; Spain uses *enfadarse*) |
| alegrarse | to become happy |
| preocuparse | to worry |
| aburrirse | to get bored |
| casarse | to get married |
| divertirse | to have fun / enjoy oneself |

- **Me siento bien hoy.** (I feel good today.)
- **No te preocupes.** (Don't worry.) — a phrase you'll hear all the time in Mexico.
- **Nos divertimos mucho.** (We had a lot of fun.)

A very Mexican one to know: **enojarse** (to get angry). In Mexico you'll hear **"no te enojes"** (don't get angry) far more than the Spain version *enfadarse*.

## Reflexive vs. Non-Reflexive: The Key Contrast

The same verb often exists in both forms, and the meaning shifts depending on *who receives the action*.

| Non-reflexive | Reflexive |
|---|---|
| **Lavo los platos.** (I wash the dishes.) | **Me lavo.** (I wash myself.) |
| **Despierto a mi hijo.** (I wake up my son.) | **Me despierto.** (I wake up.) |
| **Acuesto al bebé.** (I put the baby to bed.) | **Me acuesto.** (I go to bed.) |

The logic is consistent: if the action stays on the subject, it's reflexive; if it lands on someone or something else, it's not.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Forgetting the pronoun entirely.** *Levanto a las seis* is incomplete — you need **me levanto**. Dropping the pronoun changes or breaks the meaning.

**Mismatching pronoun and subject.** It's *me* with *yo* and *se* with *él/ellos*. Saying "yo se levanto" is wrong — it must be **yo me levanto**.

**Overusing the reflexive.** Not every verb is reflexive. You wash *the car* (*lavo el coche*), not "me lavo el coche" unless you literally mean washing yourself.

**Wrong pronoun placement in commands.** In affirmative commands it attaches to the end (*levántate*), but in negative commands it goes before (*no te levantes*).

## FAQ

### What is a reflexive verb in Spanish?

A reflexive verb is one where the subject does the action to itself, marked by a reflexive pronoun (*me, te, se, nos, os, se*). Its infinitive ends in **-se**, like *ducharse* (to shower). *Me ducho* means "I shower (myself)," with the action looping back to *yo*.

### What are the reflexive pronouns in Spanish?

They are **me** (myself), **te** (yourself), **se** (himself/herself/yourself formal), **nos** (ourselves), **os** (yourselves, Spain), and **se** (themselves/yourselves). The pronoun must match the subject of the sentence.

### Where do I put the reflexive pronoun?

Usually right **before** the conjugated verb: *me levanto*. With an infinitive or gerund you may attach it to the end instead: *voy a levantarme* or *estoy levantándome*. In affirmative commands it attaches to the verb (*levántate*); in negative commands it goes before (*no te levantes*).

### What's the difference between "lavo" and "me lavo"?

**Lavo** means "I wash" something else — *lavo el coche* (I wash the car). **Me lavo** means "I wash myself." The reflexive pronoun *me* signals the action returns to the subject rather than landing on an external object.

### Are all daily-routine verbs reflexive in Spanish?

Most are, because you perform them on yourself: *despertarse, ducharse, vestirse, acostarse*. But the same verbs turn non-reflexive when you do the action to someone else — *despierto a mi hijo* (I wake up my son) has no pronoun because the action lands on *my son*, not on me.
