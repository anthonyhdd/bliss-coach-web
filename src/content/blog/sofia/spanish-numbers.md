Spanish numbers follow a surprisingly logical system: memorize 1–15, learn the pattern for the rest, and watch out for about six irregular forms. This guide gives you every number from 1 to 1000 with pronunciation, the traps that catch English speakers (*quinientos*, not "cincocientos"), and how numbers actually behave in prices, phone numbers, and dates.

## Numbers 1–30: The Ones You Must Memorize

The numbers 1–15 are pure memorization. From 16 on, Spanish starts combining — and from 21 to 29, it fuses the words together into a single word.

## The Tens and the "Y" Pattern

From 31 to 99, Spanish stops fusing and writes three separate words: ten + *y* (and) + unit. So 31 is *treinta y uno*, 47 is *cuarenta y siete*, 99 is *noventa y nueve*.

| Number | Spanish | Pronunciation |
|---|---|---|
| 30 | treinta | TRAYN-tah |
| 40 | cuarenta | kwah-REHN-tah |
| 50 | cincuenta | seen-KWEHN-tah |
| 60 | sesenta | seh-SEHN-tah |
| 70 | setenta | seh-TEHN-tah |
| 80 | ochenta | oh-CHEHN-tah |
| 90 | noventa | noh-BEHN-tah |

The classic mix-up is **sesenta (60) vs. setenta (70)** — one letter apart, said fast, in a price. When in doubt, ask the vendor to repeat: *¿Puede repetir, por favor?*

## The Hundreds (Where the Irregulars Live)

One hundred alone is **cien**. The moment anything follows it, it becomes **ciento**: *ciento uno* (101), *ciento cincuenta* (150). There is no *y* after the hundred — 101 is *ciento uno*, never "ciento y uno."

| Number | Spanish | Pronunciation |
|---|---|---|
| 100 | cien | syehn |
| 101 | ciento uno | SYEHN-toh OO-noh |
| 200 | doscientos | dohs-SYEHN-tohs |
| 300 | trescientos | trehs-SYEHN-tohs |
| 400 | cuatrocientos | kwah-troh-SYEHN-tohs |
| 500 | quinientos | kee-NYEHN-tohs |
| 600 | seiscientos | says-SYEHN-tohs |
| 700 | setecientos | seh-teh-SYEHN-tohs |
| 800 | ochocientos | oh-choh-SYEHN-tohs |
| 900 | novecientos | noh-beh-SYEHN-tohs |
| 1000 | mil | meel |

Three hundreds are irregular, and they're the ones learners invent wrong forms for:

- **500 = quinientos** (not "cincocientos") — related to *quince*, not *cinco*
- **700 = setecientos** (not "sietecientos") — the *ie* drops
- **900 = novecientos** (not "nuevecientos") — same drop

And **1000 is just *mil*** — never "un mil." *Mil quinientos* = 1,500. *Dos mil veintiséis* = 2026.

## The Tricky Ones: Uno, Veintiún, and Gender Agreement

*Uno* shortens and changes gender depending on what it counts:

- Before a masculine noun, *uno* → **un**: *un peso*, *veintiún años* (note the accent on *veintiún*), *treinta y un días*.
- Before a feminine noun, *uno* → **una**: *una casa*, *veintiuna personas*.
- Standing alone (counting, answering "how many?"), it stays *uno*: *—¿Cuántos quieres? —Uno.*

The hundreds from 200 to 900 also agree in gender: *doscientos pesos* but *doscientas personas*, *quinientos dólares* but *quinientas páginas*. *Cien* and *mil* never change.

## Using Numbers in Real Life

### Prices

Prices in Latin America use the structure *cuesta* / *son* + number + currency:

- *¿Cuánto cuesta?* — How much is it?
- *Son ciento cincuenta pesos.* — That's 150 pesos.
- *Cuesta veintiún dólares con cincuenta.* — It costs $21.50. (*con* introduces the cents)

In Mexico you'll constantly hear *varo* or *lana* as slang for money, but the numbers themselves never change — which is why they're worth over-learning.

### Phone Numbers

Spanish speakers usually read phone numbers in **pairs or single digits**, not the three-four chunks English uses. The Mexican number 55 1234 5678 would be read: *cincuenta y cinco, doce, treinta y cuatro, cincuenta y seis, setenta y ocho*. If pairs overwhelm you in a live conversation, saying digits one by one (*cinco, cinco, uno, dos...*) is completely acceptable and always understood.

### Dates and Years

Dates use plain cardinal numbers — no "fourth of July" ordinals except for the 1st:

- *el quince de septiembre* — September 15th
- *el primero de enero* — January 1st (the one ordinal)
- Years are read as full numbers: 1999 = *mil novecientos noventa y nueve*, 2026 = *dos mil veintiséis*. Never split years the English way ("nineteen ninety-nine").

## Practice Drills That Actually Work

Reading a table doesn't build recall — production does. Do these out loud:

1. **The odd-count drill.** Count from 1 to 101 by twos, out loud, in under two minutes. Odd numbers force you through *veintiuno, treinta y uno, cuarenta y uno* — the forms that stall learners.
2. **The price gauntlet.** Grab any receipt and read every line's price in Spanish, cents included: *cuatro dólares con noventa y nueve*.
3. **The backwards ladder.** Count down from 100 by sevens: *cien, noventa y tres, ochenta y seis...* Doing math in Spanish forces real retrieval instead of recitation.
4. **Year sprints.** Say your birth year, this year, and the year you started learning Spanish. Then random years: 1521, 1810, 1985, 2001.
5. **Live listening.** Numbers are the #1 thing learners understand on paper but miss at full speed. ## Quick Answer Key: The Six Forms Everyone Gets Wrong

1. **veintiún años** — accented, shortened before masculine nouns
2. **quinientos** — 500, from *quince*
3. **setecientos** — 700, no *ie*
4. **novecientos** — 900, no *ue*
5. **cien vs. ciento** — *cien* alone, *ciento* + anything
6. **mil** — never "un mil"

If you own those six, everything else in the system is assembly.

## FAQ

### How do you say 100 in Spanish — cien or ciento?

Both, depending on position. Use **cien** when the number is exactly 100 or before a noun (*cien pesos*, *cien personas*). Use **ciento** the moment another number follows: *ciento dos* (102), *ciento noventa* (190).

### Why is 500 "quinientos" and not "cincocientos"?

It's an irregular form inherited from Latin *quingenti*, the same root that gives Spanish *quince* (15). The three irregular hundreds — *quinientos* (500), *setecientos* (700), *novecientos* (900) — simply have to be memorized.

### Do Spanish numbers have gender?

Only some. *Uno* becomes *un/una* before nouns (*veintiún años*, *veintiuna horas*), and the hundreds 200–900 agree in gender (*doscientas mujeres*). Everything else — *dos* through *diez*, the teens, the tens, *cien*, *mil* — never changes.

### How do you say 1,000 in Spanish?

Just **mil** — no article: *mil pesos*, *mil novecientos ochenta*. "Un mil" is always wrong. For multiples, use a number plus *mil*: *dos mil*, *diez mil*, *cien mil*.
