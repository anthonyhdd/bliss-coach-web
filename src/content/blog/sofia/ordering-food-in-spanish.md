To order food in Spanish, you need one core structure — **Me trae... por favor** ("Could you bring me..., please") or **Quisiera...** ("I'd like...") — plus the ability to understand the five questions every waiter asks. This guide walks the entire meal in order, from the host stand to the check, with the exact phrases you'll hear back, a Mexican menu decoder, and the tipping rules nobody explains.

## Getting a Table

Walk in, make eye contact with the host, and use one of these:

- *Una mesa para dos, por favor.* — A table for two, please.
- *¿Tienen mesa para cuatro?* — Do you have a table for four?
- *¿Hay que esperar mucho?* — Is there a long wait?
- *¿Podemos sentarnos afuera / adentro?* — Can we sit outside / inside?

What you'll hear back:

- *¿Cuántas personas?* — How many people?
- *Por aquí, por favor.* — This way, please.
- *En seguida les traigo el menú.* — I'll bring you the menu right away.
- *Hay una espera de veinte minutos.* — There's a twenty-minute wait.

A cultural note: in Mexico and most of Latin America, service is unhurried by design. A meal is an event, not a transaction. Nobody is ignoring you — they're giving you space.

## Ordering Drinks and Food

The waiter opens with one of these:

- *¿Qué les traigo de tomar?* — What can I bring you to drink?
- *¿Ya decidieron?* / *¿Están listos para ordenar?* — Have you decided? / Ready to order?
- *¿Qué van a querer?* — What are you going to have? (extremely common, casual)

Your toolkit, from most to least formal:

- *Quisiera el pollo, por favor.* — I'd like the chicken, please. (polite, always safe)
- *Me trae unos tacos de pastor, por favor.* — Could you bring me some pastor tacos, please. (the natural Latin American default)
- *Para mí, la sopa.* — For me, the soup. (perfect when ordering in a group)
- *¿Me da una quesadilla?* — Can I get a quesadilla? (casual, taquería-appropriate)

Notice what's missing: *"Puedo tener..."* — the word-for-word translation of "Can I have..." — is not how Spanish works. *Tener* means to possess, so *¿puedo tener un taco?* sounds like you're asking permission to own one. Use *me trae*, *me da*, or *quisiera*.

Useful add-ons:

- *¿Qué me recomienda?* — What do you recommend? (waiters light up at this)
- *¿Cuál es la especialidad de la casa?* — What's the house specialty?
- *¿Qué lleva este platillo?* — What's in this dish?
- *¿Es muy picante?* — Is it very spicy? (in Mexico, calibrate: their "not spicy" has range)
- *Sin cebolla, por favor.* — Without onion, please.
- *¿Me trae más tortillas?* — Could you bring more tortillas?
- *Así está bien, gracias.* — That's all, thanks.

## Waiter Phrases You'll Hear Mid-Meal

These five sentences account for most of what confuses learners at full speed:

1. *¿Algo más?* — Anything else?
2. *¿Todo bien? / ¿Cómo está todo?* — Everything OK? / How is everything?
3. *¿Les retiro esto?* — Shall I clear this?
4. *El plato está caliente.* — The plate is hot.
5. *Provecho.* — Enjoy your meal.

That last one matters. ***Provecho*** (or *buen provecho*) is said constantly in Mexico — by waiters, by strangers passing your table, by people leaving the restaurant. The correct response is a simple *gracias*. Saying *provecho* to other diners as you pass is a small courtesy that instantly marks you as someone who gets it.

## Decoding a Mexican Menu

Menus rarely translate themselves. Here's the vocabulary skeleton, then the staples.

**Menu sections:** *entradas* (starters), *platos fuertes* (mains), *guarniciones* (sides), *postres* (desserts), *bebidas* (drinks). Watch for *menú del día* or *comida corrida* — a fixed-price lunch of soup, main, drink, and sometimes dessert that is reliably the best value in the building.

**Cooking words:** *a la parrilla / al carbón* (grilled / charcoal-grilled), *frito* (fried), *al vapor* (steamed), *asado* (roasted), *empanizado* (breaded), *al pastor* (spit-roasted, marinated in adobo).

**Mexican staples worth knowing before you point at random:**

| On the menu | What it actually is |
|---|---|
| tacos al pastor | marinated pork carved off a vertical spit, with pineapple |
| enchiladas verdes / rojas | tortillas rolled around filling, bathed in green or red salsa |
| pozole | hominy stew with pork or chicken; a weekend institution |
| chilaquiles | fried tortilla pieces in salsa, usually breakfast |
| mole | complex sauce (often with chocolate) over chicken |
| cochinita pibil | Yucatecan slow-roasted pork in achiote |
| tlayuda | huge crispy Oaxacan tortilla with beans, cheese, meat |
| agua fresca (horchata, jamaica, tamarindo) | fruit/grain waters: rice-cinnamon, hibiscus, tamarind |
| flan | caramel custard, the default dessert |

One warning for the brave: on a Mexican menu, anything described as *con todo* ("with everything") means onion, cilantro, and salsa. And if a taquero asks *¿con todo?*, the expected answer is *sí*.

## Dietary Restrictions and Allergies

Be direct and use *soy* (I am) or *tengo alergia a* (I'm allergic to):

- *Soy vegetariano / vegetariana.* — I'm vegetarian.
- *Soy vegano / vegana.* — I'm vegan.
- *No como carne.* — I don't eat meat.
- *Tengo alergia a los cacahuates.* — I'm allergic to peanuts.
- *Soy alérgico/alérgica a los mariscos.* — I'm allergic to shellfish.
- *No puedo comer gluten.* — I can't eat gluten.
- *¿Este platillo lleva...?* — Does this dish have...?

Key allergy vocabulary: *nueces* (nuts), *cacahuates* (peanuts — Mexico; you'll see *maní* elsewhere in Latin America), *lácteos* (dairy), *huevo* (egg), *trigo* (wheat), *camarones* (shrimp).

Two traps for vegetarians: many traditional beans and tamales are made with *manteca* (lard), and some kitchens don't count chicken broth as "meat." Ask explicitly: *¿Está preparado con manteca o caldo de pollo?* — Is it made with lard or chicken broth?

## Getting the Check and Tipping

The check will not come until you ask — bringing it unrequested is considered rude in Mexico, because it rushes you out. When you're ready:

- *La cuenta, por favor.* — The check, please. (the universal air-signature gesture also works)
- *¿Aceptan tarjeta?* — Do you take cards?
- *¿Está incluida la propina?* — Is the tip included?
- *¿Nos puede dividir la cuenta?* — Can you split the check for us?

**Tipping culture (la propina):** in Mexico, 10–15% is standard at sit-down restaurants, with 15% for good service; street stands and markets expect nothing, though rounding up is appreciated. Check the bill first — some tourist-area restaurants add *servicio incluido*, and you don't tip twice. If paying by card, the waiter will often ask *¿con propina?* before running it; tell them the percentage or amount: *con el quince por ciento, por favor*.

Here's a compressed run-through you can rehearse:

## FAQ

### How do you politely order food in Spanish?

Use *Quisiera...* (I'd like...) or *Me trae... por favor* (Could you bring me..., please). Both are polite everywhere in Latin America. Avoid translating "Can I have..." as *¿Puedo tener...?* — it's a word-for-word calque that sounds wrong; use *¿Me da...?* instead.

### What does "provecho" mean and how do I respond?

*Provecho* (or *buen provecho*) means "enjoy your meal," and in Mexico it's said by waiters and even strangers passing your table. Respond with a simple *gracias* — and say it yourself to seated diners as you leave; it's a warm, expected courtesy.

### How much do you tip at restaurants in Mexico?

10–15% at sit-down restaurants, with 15% signalling good service. Street food stands don't expect tips. Always check whether the bill already includes *servicio incluido* in tourist areas, and if paying by card, expect the waiter to ask *¿con propina?* before charging.

### Why won't the waiter bring my check?

Because in Mexican dining culture, bringing an unrequested check is rude — it pressures you to leave. The table is yours as long as you want it. When you're ready, say *la cuenta, por favor* or catch the waiter's eye and mimic signing in the air.
