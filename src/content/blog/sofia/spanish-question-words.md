Spanish has eight core question words: **qué** (what), **cuál** (which), **quién** (who), **dónde** (where), **cuándo** (when), **por qué** (why), **cómo** (how), and **cuánto** (how much/many). Every one of them carries a written accent when it's asking a question — that tilde isn't decoration, it's the difference between asking *¿qué?* (what?) and stating *que* (that).

Learn these eight words plus one word-order pattern and you can ask almost anything in Spanish. Here's the full map, with pronunciation, the accent logic, and the qué-vs-cuál trap that catches every English speaker.

## The 8 Question Words at a Glance

| Spanish | Pronunciation | English | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| ¿Qué? | "KEH" | What? | ¿Qué es esto? — What is this? |
| ¿Cuál? / ¿Cuáles? | "KWAHL" | Which? / What? | ¿Cuál prefieres? — Which do you prefer? |
| ¿Quién? / ¿Quiénes? | "kee-EN" | Who? | ¿Quién habla? — Who's speaking? |
| ¿Dónde? | "DOHN-deh" | Where? | ¿Dónde está el baño? — Where's the bathroom? |
| ¿Cuándo? | "KWAHN-doh" | When? | ¿Cuándo llegas? — When do you arrive? |
| ¿Por qué? | "por KEH" | Why? | ¿Por qué estudias español? — Why do you study Spanish? |
| ¿Cómo? | "KOH-moh" | How? | ¿Cómo se dice...? — How do you say...? |
| ¿Cuánto/a/os/as? | "KWAHN-toh" | How much / many? | ¿Cuánto cuesta? — How much does it cost? |

Two mechanics before the details:

- **The upside-down ¿** opens every question in writing. In speech, questions are marked purely by rising intonation — *¿Hablas español?* and *Hablas español* use identical words.
- **Word order flips** after the question word: verb before subject. *¿Dónde **vive Sofia**?* (Where does Sofia live?) — not ~~"¿Dónde Sofia vive?"~~ There's no "does" helper word in Spanish; the question word plus the flip does all the work.

## The Accent Rule (It's Simpler Than It Looks)

Every question word has an unaccented twin that means something different:

| With accent (asking) | Without accent (connecting) |
|---|---|
| ¿**Qué** quieres? — What do you want? | Dice **que** viene. — He says **that** he's coming. |
| ¿**Dónde** vives? — Where do you live? | La casa **donde** vivo. — The house **where** I live. |
| ¿**Cómo** estás? — How are you? | **Como** tú digas. — However/as you say. |
| ¿**Por qué** lloras? — Why are you crying? | **Porque** estoy feliz. — **Because** I'm happy. |

The rule: **accent = the word is doing the asking** — even inside a statement. *No sé **dónde** está* (I don't know **where** it is) keeps the accent because "where" is still an embedded question. If the word merely connects two ideas, no accent.

Note the *por qué* family, a favorite exam trap: **¿por qué?** (two words, accent) = why; **porque** (one word, no accent) = because. The other two — *el porqué* (the reason) and *por que* (rare, "for which") — you can safely ignore as a beginner.

## Qué vs Cuál: The One That Trips Everyone

English "what" sometimes translates to **qué** and sometimes to **cuál**, and picking wrong is the most common question-word mistake. Two rules cover it:

1. **Before a noun, use qué.** *¿**Qué** libro lees?* (What book are you reading?) Cuál directly before a noun sounds off in most of Latin America.
2. **Before es/son, "qué" asks for a definition; "cuál" asks for a selection.**
   - *¿**Qué** es tu correo?* = "What *is* an email?" (asking you to define the concept)
   - *¿**Cuál** es tu correo?* = "What's your email address?" (asking you to pick yours out of all possible emails)

So all these everyday questions take **cuál**, even though English says "what": *¿Cuál es tu nombre? ¿Cuál es tu número? ¿Cuál es la fecha? ¿Cuál es tu color favorito?* If the honest answer is one item from a set, it's cuál.

**Cuál** also has a plural, *cuáles*: *¿**Cuáles** son tus platillos favoritos?* (Which are your favorite dishes?)

## Quién, Cuánto: The Ones That Change Form

Most question words are frozen, but two agree with what they refer to:

- **Quién → quiénes** (plural): *¿**Quién** viene?* (Who's coming? — one person expected) vs *¿**Quiénes** vienen?* (Who all is coming?). With prepositions it stays glued: *¿**De quién** es esto?* (Whose is this?), *¿**Con quién** vas?* (Who are you going with?).
- **Cuánto → cuánta / cuántos / cuántas** agrees in gender and number: *¿**Cuánto** cuesta?* (How much does it cost?), *¿**Cuánta** agua?* (How much water?), *¿**Cuántos** años tienes?* (How old are you? — literally "how many years do you have"), *¿**Cuántas** personas?* (How many people?).

One more shape-shifter: **dónde** combines with direction. *¿**Adónde** vas?* ("ah-DOHN-deh" — where are you going *to*?) for movement, *¿**De dónde** eres?* (where are you *from*?) for origin. Plain *dónde* is for location: *¿Dónde estás?*

## 15 Questions You'll Actually Ask

Real questions for real situations, Mexican-flavored:

1. **¿Qué hora es?** — What time is it?
2. **¿Qué significa "chido"?** — What does "chido" mean? (Mexican slang for "cool" — you'll hear it constantly.)
3. **¿Cuál es tu nombre?** — What's your name?
4. **¿Cuál me recomiendas?** — Which one do you recommend?
5. **¿Quién es ella?** — Who is she?
6. **¿De quién es esta mochila?** — Whose backpack is this?
7. **¿Dónde está la estación del metro?** — Where's the metro station?
8. **¿De dónde eres?** — Where are you from?
9. **¿Adónde van?** — Where are you all going?
10. **¿Cuándo es tu cumpleaños?** — When is your birthday?
11. **¿Por qué no vamos mañana?** — Why don't we go tomorrow?
12. **¿Cómo se llama tu perro?** — What's your dog's name? (Literally "how is your dog called.")
13. **¿Cómo llego al centro?** — How do I get downtown?
14. **¿Cuánto cuesta esto?** — How much does this cost?
15. **¿Cuántos hermanos tienes?** — How many siblings do you have?

Notice #12: Spanish often asks "how" where English asks "what." *¿Cómo te llamas?* (What's your name?), *¿Cómo es tu ciudad?* (What's your city like?). And a solo **¿Cómo?** is the polite Mexican way to say "sorry, what?" when you didn't catch something — far more common than *¿qué?*, which can sound brusque on its own.

## Common Mistakes English Speakers Make

- **Dropping the accents.** *¿Donde está?* is a spelling error every time. If it asks, it wears an accent — including in indirect questions like *no sé cuándo llega*.
- **Using qué for selection.** ~~"¿Qué es tu nombre?"~~ asks for a philosophical definition of your name. Selection questions before *es* take **cuál**: *¿Cuál es tu nombre?*
- **Keeping English word order.** ~~"¿Dónde tú vives?"~~ → *¿Dónde vives (tú)?* Verb comes right after the question word.
- **Translating "how old" literally.** Age uses *tener* + *cuántos años*: *¿Cuántos años tienes?* — never ~~"¿Cómo viejo eres?"~~
- **Confusing porque and por qué.** Question = *¿por qué?* (two words, accent). Answer = *porque* (one word, no accent). *¿Por qué estudias? — Porque me gusta.*
- **Forgetting to make cuánto agree.** *¿Cuántas personas?*, not ~~"¿cuánto personas?"~~ — it behaves like an adjective before nouns.

## FAQ

**What are the question words in Spanish?**

The eight core interrogatives are qué (what), cuál/cuáles (which), quién/quiénes (who), dónde (where), cuándo (when), por qué (why), cómo (how), and cuánto/cuánta/cuántos/cuántas (how much/how many). All of them carry a written accent when used to ask a question, and questions open with an inverted ¿ in writing.

**What's the difference between qué and cuál?**

Use qué directly before nouns (*¿qué libro?*) and for definitions (*¿qué es esto?*). Use cuál before *es/son* when the answer is a selection from a set — name, number, email, favorite: *¿cuál es tu nombre?* If English "what" expects one item picked from many, Spanish wants cuál.

**Why do Spanish question words have accents?**

The accent distinguishes the asking word from its unaccented twin that merely connects clauses: *¿qué?* (what?) vs *que* (that), *¿dónde?* (where?) vs *donde* (where, relative). The accent stays even in indirect questions: *no sé dónde está* (I don't know where it is).

**What's the difference between por qué and porque?**

*¿Por qué?* — two words with an accent — asks "why." *Porque* — one word, no accent — answers "because." *¿Por qué llegaste tarde? Porque había tráfico.* (Why were you late? Because there was traffic.)

**Do I have to flip the word order in Spanish questions?**

After a question word, yes: verb before subject, as in *¿dónde vive Sofia?* But yes/no questions need no changes at all — just rising intonation: *¿Hablas español?* uses exactly the same words as the statement. There is no Spanish equivalent of "do/does."
