The colors in Spanish (los colores) start with **rojo** (red), **azul** (blue), **verde** (green), **amarillo** (yellow), and **negro** (black). Below you'll find the full list with pronunciation, plus the one rule beginners always miss: in Spanish, colors change their ending to match the noun they describe.

Colors are one of the most rewarding early vocab sets — useful every day, and a perfect place to learn how Spanish adjectives really work. Let's dive in.

## The Essential Colors

Here are the core colors you'll use constantly, with a pronunciation guide:

| Spanish | English | Pronunciation |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| rojo | red | "RO-ho" |
| azul | blue | "ah-SOOL" |
| verde | green | "VER-deh" |
| amarillo | yellow | "ah-ma-REE-yo" |
| naranja | orange | "na-RAN-ha" |
| morado | purple | "mo-RA-do" |
| rosa | pink | "RO-sa" |
| café / marrón | brown | "ka-FEH" / "ma-RRON" |
| negro | black | "NEH-gro" |
| blanco | white | "BLAN-ko" |
| gris | gray | "GREES" |

A quick Mexican-Spanish note: in Mexico, brown is almost always **café** (yes, the same word as coffee), while **marrón** is more common in Spain and South America. And **anaranjado** is a fancier word for orange, but everyone just says **naranja**.

## The Rule Everyone Forgets: Agreement

Here's what makes Spanish colors more than a vocab list. In Spanish, a color is an **adjective**, and adjectives must **agree** with the noun in gender and number. There are two things happening at once.

**1. Gender.** Colors ending in **-o** switch to **-a** for feminine nouns:

- el carro **rojo** (the red car — *carro* is masculine)
- la casa **roja** (the red house — *casa* is feminine)

**2. Number.** Add **-s** (or **-es**) to make a color plural:

- los zapatos **rojos** (the red shoes)
- las flores **rojas** (the red flowers)

So **rojo** has four forms: rojo, roja, rojos, rojas. The color has to match whatever it's describing.

## The Good News: Not All Colors Change for Gender

This is where beginners breathe a sigh of relief. Only colors ending in **-o** change for gender. Colors ending in **-e** or a consonant stay the same for masculine and feminine — they only add **-s** for plural.

| Color | Changes for gender? | Example |
|-------|---------------------|---------|
| rojo | Yes (rojo/roja) | libro rojo / mesa roja |
| verde | No | libro verde / mesa verde |
| azul | No | libro azul / mesa azul |
| gris | No | libro gris / mesa gris |
| amarillo | Yes | plátano amarillo / flor amarilla |

So **verde**, **azul**, and **gris** are easy: one form for gender, add **-s**/**-es** for plural (verdes, azules, grises).

## Colors That Never Change: The Fun Exception

Some colors come from real-world objects, and these are **invariable** — they never change at all, no matter the noun. The most common is **rosa** (pink, literally "rose") and **naranja** (orange, literally "orange fruit").

- unas flores **rosa** (some pink flowers — not *rosas*, which would mean "roses"!)
- unos calcetines **naranja** (some orange socks)

Technically, purists keep these frozen. In real, casual Mexican speech, though, you'll often hear people add the **-s** anyway (**calcetines naranjas**). Don't stress about it — you'll be understood either way.

## Describing Shades

To fine-tune a color, add these:

- **claro** = light: **azul claro** (light blue)
- **oscuro** = dark: **verde oscuro** (dark green)
- Add **-ish** feeling with **-oso** or by combining, but for beginners, **claro** and **oscuro** cover most of what you need.

When you use **claro** or **oscuro**, the base color often stops agreeing: **una camisa azul claro** (a light blue shirt) — the color stays frozen as a unit.

## Where the Color Goes in the Sentence

Here's another habit English speakers have to unlearn: in Spanish, the color usually comes **after** the noun, not before it.

- English: "the **red** car"
- Spanish: "el carro **rojo**" (literally "the car red")

So you say **la casa blanca** (the white house), **los zapatos negros** (the black shoes), **un vestido azul** (a blue dress). The noun leads, the color follows. This word order feels backwards at first, but it becomes automatic quickly once you say a few phrases out loud.

This is true for most Spanish adjectives, so colors are a perfect, low-stakes place to build the habit. Get comfortable putting the color second, and a huge chunk of Spanish description will already feel natural.

## Colors in the Real World

A few everyday contexts where colors show up constantly:

- **Traffic and safety**: el semáforo está en **rojo** (the light is red), **verde** (green), **amarillo** (yellow).
- **Describing people**: ojos **cafés** (brown eyes), cabello **rubio** (blond hair), cabello **castaño** (chestnut/brown hair).
- **Shopping**: ¿lo tienen en **azul**? (do you have it in blue?), me gusta el **morado** (I like the purple one).
- **Nature**: el cielo **azul** (the blue sky), el mar **verde** (the green sea), las hojas **cafés** (the brown leaves).

Two bonus hair words worth knowing: **rubio** (blond) and **castaño** (chestnut brown, for hair specifically). They behave like regular **-o** adjectives, changing to **rubia** and **castaña** for feminine.

## Put It to Use Right Away

Try building a few phrases out loud:

- Mi color favorito es el **azul**. (My favorite color is blue.)
- Quiero la manzana **roja**, por favor. (I want the red apple, please.)
- Tengo ojos **cafés** y cabello **negro**. (I have brown eyes and black hair.)
- ¿De qué color es tu carro? — Es **blanco**. (What color is your car? — It's white.)

That last question, **¿de qué color es...?** ("what color is..."), is a great one to memorize — it opens up endless practice with everything around you.

## FAQ

### How do you say "color" in Spanish?

**Color** (pronounced "ko-LOR"), and the plural is **colores**. "The colors" is **los colores**. It's a masculine noun: **el color**.

### Why does "rojo" sometimes become "roja"?

Because colors are adjectives that agree with their noun. Feminine nouns take the **-a** ending: **la manzana roja** (the red apple). Masculine nouns keep **-o**: **el carro rojo**.

### What's the difference between "café" and "marrón"?

Both mean brown. **Café** is the standard word for brown in Mexico and much of Latin America; **marrón** is more common in Spain and parts of South America. Use **café** to sound natural in Mexican Spanish.

### Do pink and orange really never change?

As object-derived colors, **rosa** and **naranja** are traditionally invariable and don't change for gender or number. In casual speech many Mexicans add a plural **-s** anyway, and you'll be understood perfectly either way.
