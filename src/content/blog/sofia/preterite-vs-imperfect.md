Spanish has two simple past tenses and English has one, which is why "I ate" could be *comí* or *comía* depending on what you mean. The distinction is aspect, not time: the **preterite** reports a completed event — it happened, it's over — while the **imperfect** describes an ongoing scene, habit, or background state in the past. Here's the mental model, the conjugations, and 20 paired sentences that make the choice automatic.

## The Camera Analogy: Photo vs. Video

Think of yourself as a filmmaker telling a story about the past.

- The **preterite is a photo**: a snapshot of an event with edges. It started, it finished, click. *Ayer comí tacos.* (Yesterday I ate tacos — done, digested, over.)
- The **imperfect is a video**: the camera rolls over a scene with no visible start or end. It sets the stage, describes what was going on, what things were like, what used to happen. *Cuando era niño, comía tacos todos los domingos.* (When I was a kid, I used to eat tacos every Sunday — the camera pans across years of Sundays.)

In real storytelling the two work together constantly: **the imperfect paints the background, the preterite delivers the events.**

> *Llovía* (video: rain falling, scene-setting) *cuando salí de casa* (photo: the moment I stepped out) *y me di cuenta de que no tenía paraguas* (photo: the realization; video: the ongoing state of umbrella-lessness).

If you can ask "**what happened?**" — preterite. If you're answering "**what was going on / what was it like / what used to happen?**" — imperfect.

## When to Use Each

**Preterite (the photo):**
- Completed single events: *Compré el boleto.* (I bought the ticket.)
- Actions with a stated number or duration seen as a whole: *Viví en Bogotá tres años.* (I lived in Bogotá for three years — a closed chapter.)
- Chains of events in a narrative: *Llegué, vi el desastre y llamé a mi mamá.*
- The beginning or end of something: *Empezó a llover. La película terminó a las diez.*

**Imperfect (the video):**
- Habitual or repeated past actions ("used to," "would"): *Íbamos a la playa cada verano.*
- Descriptions and background: weather, scenery, appearance: *Hacía frío y las calles estaban vacías.*
- Age, time, and dates in the past: *Tenía ocho años. Eran las dos de la mañana.*
- Ongoing states, emotions, and mental activity: *Estaba cansada. Quería ayudar. Pensaba en ti.*
- An action in progress interrupted by another: *Dormía cuando sonó el teléfono.* (Video interrupted by a photo.)

## Trigger Words

These aren't ironclad rules, but they're right often enough to lean on:

| Preterite triggers | Imperfect triggers |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| ayer (yesterday) | siempre (always) |
| anoche (last night) | todos los días / cada año (every day/year) |
| la semana pasada (last week) | a menudo, con frecuencia (often) |
| una vez / dos veces (once/twice) | a veces (sometimes) |
| de repente (suddenly) | mientras (while) |
| el año pasado (last year) | de niño/a, cuando era joven (as a kid) |
| en 2019, en ese momento | generalmente, normalmente |
| primero... luego... después | todos los domingos (every Sunday) |

The word **cuando** goes both ways: *cuando llegué* (photo — the moment I arrived) vs. *cuando era niño* (video — the era of my childhood).

## Conjugation Tables

### Regular preterite

| Person | hablar | comer | vivir |
|--------|--------|-------|-------|
| yo | hablé | comí | viví |
| tú | hablaste | comiste | viviste |
| él/ella/usted | habló | comió | vivió |
| nosotros | hablamos | comimos | vivimos |
| ustedes/ellos | hablaron | comieron | vivieron |

Watch the accents — they carry meaning: *hablo* (I speak) vs. *habló* (he/she spoke).

### Regular imperfect

| Person | hablar | comer | vivir |
|--------|--------|-------|-------|
| yo | hablaba | comía | vivía |
| tú | hablabas | comías | vivías |
| él/ella/usted | hablaba | comía | vivía |
| nosotros | hablábamos | comíamos | vivíamos |
| ustedes/ellos | hablaban | comían | vivían |

The imperfect is the friendliest tense in Spanish: -er and -ir verbs share one set of endings, and there are only **three irregular verbs in the entire language** — *ser* (era, eras, era, éramos, eran), *ir* (iba, ibas, iba, íbamos, iban), and *ver* (veía, veías, veía, veíamos, veían).

### The preterite irregulars you actually need

The preterite is where the irregularity lives. These stem-changers share one set of unaccented endings (-e, -iste, -o, -imos, -ieron):

| Verb | Stem | yo / él |
|------|------|---------|
| tener | tuv- | tuve / tuvo |
| estar | estuv- | estuve / estuvo |
| poder | pud- | pude / pudo |
| poner | pus- | puse / puso |
| saber | sup- | supe / supo |
| querer | quis- | quise / quiso |
| venir | vin- | vine / vino |
| hacer | hic- | hice / hizo |
| decir | dij- | dije / dijo (ellos **dijeron**) |
| traer | traj- | traje / trajo (ellos **trajeron**) |

Plus the two twins: **ser and ir share the exact same preterite** — *fui, fuiste, fue, fuimos, fueron* — and context tells you which is which (*fui al cine* = I went; *fue increíble* = it was incredible). And **dar**: *di, diste, dio, dimos, dieron*.

## 20 Paired Example Sentences

Each pair shows the same verb in both tenses so the contrast does the teaching:

1. *Anoche **comí** pozole.* (Last night I ate pozole.) / *De niña **comía** pozole cada jueves.* (As a girl I ate pozole every Thursday.)
2. ***Estudié** dos horas y me dormí.* (I studied two hours, then fell asleep.) / ***Estudiaba** cuando me llamaste.* (I was studying when you called.)
3. *Ayer **hizo** calor.* (Yesterday it got/was hot — the day, viewed whole.) / ***Hacía** calor cuando salimos.* (It was hot out when we left — background.)
4. *El concierto **fue** increíble.* (The concert was incredible — event, summed up.) / *El estadio **era** enorme.* (The stadium was huge — description.)
5. ***Vivimos** en Lima cinco años.* (We lived in Lima for five years — closed period.) / ***Vivíamos** en Lima cuando nació mi hijo.* (We were living in Lima when my son was born.)
6. ***Tuve** un accidente esta mañana.* (I had an accident this morning — event.) / ***Tenía** un coche rojo en esa época.* (I had a red car back then — state.)
7. *Mi abuela **cocinó** tamales para la fiesta.* (Grandma made tamales for the party.) / *Mi abuela siempre **cocinaba** con manteca.* (Grandma always cooked with lard.)
8. ***Leí** el libro en un día.* (I read the book in one day — finished.) / ***Leía** el libro cuando se fue la luz.* (I was reading when the power went out.)
9. ***Fuimos** a Oaxaca en marzo.* (We went to Oaxaca in March.) / ***Íbamos** a Oaxaca todos los diciembres.* (We used to go every December.)
10. *De repente, **me sentí** mal.* (Suddenly I felt sick — onset, photo.) / ***Me sentía** mal toda esa semana.* (I felt sick all that week — ongoing state.)

Pairs 1–5 are the storytelling core; pairs 6–10 show states versus events. Read them out loud — the rhythm of *comí/comía*, *fue/era*, *tuve/tenía* is half the learning.

## Verbs That Change Meaning: Sabía vs. Supe

A handful of verbs effectively translate differently in each tense, because a "snapshot" of a mental state means its *onset*:

| Verb | Imperfect (state) | Preterite (event) |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| saber | *sabía* — I knew | *supe* — I found out |
| conocer | *conocía* — I knew (a person/place) | *conocí* — I met (for the first time) |
| querer | *quería* — I wanted | *quise* — I tried to; *no quise* — I refused |
| poder | *podía* — I was able (capacity) | *pude* — I managed to; *no pude* — I failed to |
| tener | *tenía* — I had | *tuve* — I got/received |

So: ***Sabía** la verdad* (I knew the truth all along) vs. ***Supe** la verdad ayer* (I found out yesterday). ***Conocía** a María* (I knew María) vs. ***Conocí** a María en la boda* (I met her at the wedding). These aren't exceptions to the system — they're the purest proof of it: photo = the moment the state began; video = the state itself.

## FAQ

### What's the fastest way to decide between preterite and imperfect?

Ask: am I reporting **what happened** (photo → preterite) or describing **what was going on / what used to happen** (video → imperfect)? Nine times out of ten that single question resolves it.

### Is the imperfect the same as "used to" in English?

"Used to" and "would" (habitual) always map to the imperfect: *We used to swim* = *nadábamos*. But the imperfect is broader — it also covers descriptions (*era alto*), ongoing states (*estaba triste*), age (*tenía diez años*), and time (*eran las cinco*), where English just uses "was/were."

### Why does "fui" mean both "I went" and "I was"?

*Ser* and *ir* happen to share identical preterite forms — a historical accident of Latin. Context always disambiguates: *fui al mercado* (I went to the market) vs. *fui estudiante* (I was a student). Native speakers never notice the ambiguity, and soon you won't either.

### Do sabía and supe really mean different things?

Yes: *sabía* = I knew (an ongoing state), *supe* = I found out (the moment knowledge began). Same logic gives you *conocía* (knew) vs. *conocí* (met), and *no quería* (didn't want to) vs. *no quise* (refused). The preterite snapshots the moment the state started.
