Spanish direct object pronouns are the little words that replace the thing receiving the action: **me** (me), **te** (you), **lo** (him/it), **la** (her/it), **nos** (us), **los/las** (them). The catch for English speakers is placement — they go **before** the conjugated verb, not after it: *¿Viste la película?* → *Sí, **la** vi* (literally "yes, it I-saw").

That word-order flip is 90% of the battle. This guide gives you the full pronoun set, the two placement rules that cover every sentence, and the handful of traps — like *lo* vs *le* — that keep learners stuck.

## What a Direct Object Even Is

The direct object is whatever directly receives the verb's action — find it by asking "**what?**" or "**whom?**" after the verb:

- *Compré **los boletos**.* — I bought the tickets. (Bought what? The tickets.)
- *Conozco **a Sofia**.* — I know Sofia. (Know whom? Sofia.)

Once the thing is known, repeating the full noun sounds as clunky in Spanish as in English ("Did you buy the tickets?" "Yes, I bought the tickets."). Enter the pronoun: ***Los** compré.* — I bought them.

## The Full Set

| Pronoun | Pronunciation | Replaces | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| me | "meh" | me | Sofia **me** ayuda. — Sofia helps me. |
| te | "teh" | you (informal) | **Te** veo mañana. — I'll see you tomorrow. |
| lo | "loh" | him, it (masc.), you (usted, masc.) | **Lo** compré ayer. — I bought it yesterday. |
| la | "lah" | her, it (fem.), you (usted, fem.) | **La** conozco bien. — I know her well. |
| nos | "nohs" | us | Nuestra maestra **nos** escucha. — Our teacher listens to us. |
| los | "lohs" | them (masc./mixed), you all (masc.) | **Los** invité a la fiesta. — I invited them to the party. |
| las | "lahs" | them (fem.), you all (fem.) | **Las** vi en el mercado. — I saw them at the market. |

Two things to notice:

- **me, te, nos** are the same for direct and indirect objects, so you can't get them wrong. All the action is in the third person: **lo, la, los, las**.
- The pronoun matches the **grammatical gender** of the noun, not any real-world logic. *El libro* → **lo**. *La mesa* → **la**. *Los tacos* → **los**. *Las llaves* → **las**. If you're shaky on noun genders, that's the real prerequisite here.

(A note for travelers: in Spain you'll hear *le* used for "him" — called *leísmo*. In Mexico and Latin America, stick with **lo**: *lo conozco*, not *le conozco*.)

## Placement Rule 1: Before the Conjugated Verb

The default position is immediately **before** the conjugated verb — the opposite of English:

- **¿Tienes las llaves?** → **Sí, las tengo.** — Yes, I have them.
- **¿Me escuchas?** — Do you hear me?
- **No lo sé.** — I don't know (it). (Note: **no** goes before the pronoun.)
- **¿Viste a Carlos?** → **No, no lo vi.** — No, I didn't see him.

This never changes for simple tenses — present, preterite, imperfect, future: *la compro, la compré, la compraba, la compraré*. The pronoun sits in front every time.

## Placement Rule 2: Attach to Infinitives, Gerunds, and Commands

Three verb forms let (or force) the pronoun to hang on the **end** of the word:

**With infinitive or gerund constructions, you choose** — both are equally correct:

- **Voy a comprarlo.** = **Lo voy a comprar.** — I'm going to buy it.
- **Quiero verte.** = **Te quiero ver.** — I want to see you.
- **Estoy leyéndolo.** = **Lo estoy leyendo.** — I'm reading it.

(When you attach to a gerund, a written accent appears to preserve the stress: *leyendo* → *leyéndolo*.)

**With affirmative commands, attaching is mandatory:**

- **¡Cómpralo!** — Buy it!
- **¡Llámame!** ("YAH-mah-meh") — Call me!
- **¡Espérala!** — Wait for her!

**With negative commands, it flips back in front:**

- **¡No lo compres!** — Don't buy it!
- **¡No me llames después de las diez!** — Don't call me after ten!

That affirmative/negative command flip is the single most-tested detail on Spanish exams, and it follows one memorable pattern: attached when you say do, in front when you say don't.

## 20 Examples, From Noun to Pronoun

1. ¿Compraste **el pan**? — **Lo** compré esta mañana. (I bought it this morning.)
2. ¿Tienes **mi número**? — Sí, **lo** tengo. (Yes, I have it.)
3. ¿Quién hizo **la tarea**? — Yo **la** hice. (I did it.)
4. ¿Viste **la nueva película**? — **La** vi el sábado. (I saw it Saturday.)
5. ¿Dónde están **los boletos**? — **Los** dejé en casa. (I left them at home.)
6. ¿Preparaste **las maletas**? — **Las** preparé anoche. (I packed them last night.)
7. ¿Conoces **a mi hermano**? — Sí, **lo** conozco. (Yes, I know him.)
8. ¿Llamaste **a tu abuela**? — **La** llamé ayer. (I called her yesterday.)
9. ¿Invitaste **a tus primos**? — **Los** invité a todos. (I invited them all.)
10. **Me** entiendes, ¿verdad? — You understand me, right?
11. **Te** escucho. — I hear you / I'm listening.
12. Nuestros papás **nos** apoyan. — Our parents support us.
13. **Lo** necesito para mañana. — I need it for tomorrow.
14. No **la** encuentro por ningún lado. — I can't find it anywhere.
15. ¿Puedes ayudar**me**? — Can you help me?
16. Voy a extrañar**te**. — I'm going to miss you.
17. Estamos esperándo**los**. — We're waiting for them.
18. ¡Tómalo con calma! — Take it easy!
19. ¡No las olvides! — Don't forget them!
20. El café ya está listo. ¿**Lo** quieres con leche? — The coffee's ready. Do you want it with milk?

Read the answers out loud — the rhythm of *lo-vi*, *la-tengo*, *los-compré* is exactly what your mouth needs to automate. These pronouns glue onto the [most common Spanish verbs](/sofia/blog/common-spanish-verbs/) constantly, so drilling those pairs pays double.

## Lo vs Le: The Trap to Defuse Early

**Lo/la** replace the *direct* object (the thing acted on). **Le** replaces the *indirect* object (the person receiving something — usually "to/for whom"):

- ***Lo** vi.* — I saw **him**. (He's what I saw → direct.)
- ***Le** di el libro.* — I gave **him** the book. (The book is what I gave; he's the receiver → indirect.)

Quick test: if the English sentence works with "to him/to her" ("I gave the book *to him*"), it's **le**. If not, it's **lo/la**. A useful cousin rule: this is the same me/te/nos + third-person system you already half-know from [reflexive verbs in Spanish](/sofia/blog/reflexive-verbs-in-spanish/) — only the third-person forms differ.

One more heads-up for listening comprehension: the **a** in *conozco **a** Sofia* isn't "to" — it's the "personal a" Spanish requires before human direct objects. Sofia is still a direct object, so she's replaced by **la**: *la conozco*.

## Common Mistakes English Speakers Make

- **Putting the pronoun after the verb, English-style.** ~~"Vi lo"~~ → **lo vi**. Before the conjugated verb, always.
- **Defaulting to lo for everything.** *La mesa* is feminine: *¿la mesa? **la** veo*, not ~~"lo veo"~~. Match the noun's gender and number every time.
- **Skipping the pronoun entirely.** English drops objects ("Did you buy the tickets?" "I bought."✗) — actually it doesn't, and neither does Spanish. Answering *¿compraste los boletos?* with just *"compré"* sounds incomplete. Say **los compré**.
- **Forgetting the flip in negative commands.** *¡Cómpralo!* but *¡no lo compres!* — attached when affirmative, in front when negative.
- **Using le for direct objects.** In Latin America, "I know him" is **lo conozco**. Reserve *le* for indirect objects (*le dije* — I told him).
- **Missing the accent when attaching.** *Esperando* + *la* = **esperándola**. The stress stays where it was, so a written accent appears. Small detail in writing, zero effect on speech.

## FAQ

**What are the direct object pronouns in Spanish?**

They are me (me), te (you), lo (him/it masculine), la (her/it feminine), nos (us), los (them masculine/mixed), and las (them feminine). They replace the noun that directly receives the verb's action: *¿Viste la película? — Sí, la vi.*

**Where do direct object pronouns go in a Spanish sentence?**

Before the conjugated verb (*lo compré* — I bought it), including in negative sentences (*no lo compré*). With infinitives and gerunds you can attach them to the end instead (*voy a comprarlo* = *lo voy a comprar*). Affirmative commands require attachment (*¡cómpralo!*); negative commands put them back in front (*¡no lo compres!*).

**What's the difference between lo and le in Spanish?**

Lo (and la) replace direct objects — the thing or person acted on: *lo vi* (I saw him). Le replaces indirect objects — the receiver, "to/for whom": *le di el libro* (I gave him the book). In Latin American Spanish, always use lo/la for "him/her" as a direct object; *le* in that role is a Spain-specific habit called leísmo.

**Do I use lo or la for objects and things?**

Match the noun's grammatical gender: *el libro* → lo, *la mesa* → la, *los tacos* → los, *las llaves* → las. The choice has nothing to do with meaning — it's purely the gender of the noun being replaced, so solid noun-gender knowledge does half the work.

**Why is there an "a" before people, like "conozco a Sofia"?**

That's the "personal a" — Spanish requires it before human (and beloved-pet) direct objects. It doesn't make the object indirect: Sofia is still a direct object, so the pronoun is la (*la conozco*), not le. When replacing the noun with a pronoun, the personal a disappears.
