If you memorize just these 100 phrases, you can survive — and even enjoy — most everyday situations in a Spanish-speaking country. They're organized by situation, each with a rough pronunciation hint and its English meaning. All of them are natural Latin American Spanish, the kind you'll actually hear in Mexico City or Bogotá, not textbook relics.

A quick note on the pronunciation hints: they're approximations using English sounds. Stressed syllables are in CAPS. Say them out loud as you read — reading silently teaches your eyes, not your mouth.

## How to Actually Learn These (Not Just Read Them)

Here's the hard truth: reading this list once will get you almost nothing. Recognition is not recall. You can nod along to every phrase above and still freeze when a waiter looks at you expectantly.

What works:

1. **Say everything out loud.** Your mouth needs the reps, not your eyes. Spanish pronunciation is mercifully consistent — once you learn the sounds, words are pronounced exactly as written.
2. **Learn in chunks by situation.** Don't grind the whole list. Master the greetings block this week, restaurants next week. Context glues phrases into memory.
3. **Practice in fake conversations.** Order an imaginary meal in the shower. Ask your dog for directions. Silly, but it builds the retrieval pathway you'll need under pressure.
4. **Get real speaking practice early.** Flashcards teach you to recognize phrases; conversation teaches you to produce them under time pressure, which is a completely different skill. If you don't have a patient native speaker on call, [Sofia, an AI Spanish tutor you can actually talk to](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6761907539) lets you practice these exact situations in voice conversations and corrects you on the spot.
5. **Accept the awkward phase.** Every fluent speaker you've ever met once said "yo gusto tacos" to a confused stranger. The mistakes are the process, not a detour from it.

A realistic goal: 10 phrases per day, spoken aloud 5 times each, revisited the next day. In two weeks you'll have the whole list in your mouth, not just your notes app.

## One Regional Note

This list uses Latin American Spanish. If you're headed to Spain, almost everything still works, but you'll hear *vosotros* forms ("¿habláis inglés?") and words like *coche* instead of *carro* and *zumo* instead of *jugo*. Don't stress about it — the differences are roughly American vs. British English. Everyone will understand you either way.

## FAQ

### How many Spanish phrases do I need to know to get by while traveling?

Around 50–100 well-practiced phrases cover the vast majority of tourist interactions: greetings, ordering food, asking directions, shopping, and emergencies. The key word is *practiced* — 50 phrases you can say instantly beat 500 you can only recognize on paper.

### Should I learn phrases or grammar first?

Phrases first. High-frequency phrases give you immediate wins and real conversations, and they secretly teach you grammar patterns ("me gustaría," "¿dónde está?") that formal study later snaps into place. Grammar without phrases leaves you knowing rules but unable to order a coffee.

### Is Latin American Spanish different from Spain's Spanish?

Yes, but they're fully mutually intelligible — think American vs. British English. Pronunciation (Spain's "th" sound for *c/z*), some vocabulary, and Spain's *vosotros* verb forms differ. Every phrase in this article works everywhere Spanish is spoken.

### How long until I can use these phrases in a real conversation?

If you practice out loud daily, you can handle basic transactional conversations (restaurant, taxi, hotel) within 2–4 weeks. The bottleneck isn't memorization — it's recall speed under pressure, which only comes from actually speaking, whether with a person or an AI tutor.
