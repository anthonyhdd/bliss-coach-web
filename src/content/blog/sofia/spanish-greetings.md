*Hola* works everywhere, with everyone, at any hour — but native speakers rarely stop there. Real Spanish greetings stack a hello with a "how are you," shift with the time of day, and swing from a formal *buenos días* with your landlord to a *¡qué onda!* with your friends. Here are 30 greetings organized by situation, with responses and pronunciation, so you can pick the right one instantly.

## The Universal Five

These work in any Spanish-speaking country, at any register:

| Greeting | Meaning | Pronunciation hint |
|----------|---------|--------------------|
| Hola | Hello / Hi | OH-lah — the h is always silent |
| Buenos días | Good morning | BWEH-nohs DEE-ahs |
| Buenas tardes | Good afternoon | BWEH-nahs TAR-dehs |
| Buenas noches | Good evening / Good night | BWEH-nahs NOH-chehs |
| ¿Cómo estás? | How are you? | KOH-moh ehs-TAHS |

**Time-of-day rules:** *buenos días* until roughly midday; *buenas tardes* from midday until sunset (in Mexico, often until 8 p.m. or later); *buenas noches* after dark — and unlike English "good night," it works as a hello, not just a goodbye. Note the gender switch: **buenos** días but **buenas** tardes/noches, because *día* is masculine while *tarde* and *noche* are feminine.

A shortcut you'll hear constantly in Latin America: just **"¡Buenas!"** — a casual clip of buenas tardes/noches that works most of the day. Walk into a small shop in Mexico and this is the standard greeting in both directions.

## Formal Greetings

Use these with strangers, elders, officials, and in business settings — anyone you'd address as *usted*:

1. **¿Cómo está (usted)?** — How are you? (formal). The -á ending instead of -ás marks the formality.
2. **¿Cómo le va?** — How's it going for you? (formal but warm; very common in Mexico).
3. **Mucho gusto.** — Nice to meet you. Standard at first introductions.
4. **Encantado / Encantada.** — Delighted (to meet you). Match the ending to *your own* gender: a man says *encantado*, a woman *encantada*.
5. **Es un placer.** — It's a pleasure.
6. **Bienvenido / Bienvenida.** — Welcome (agrees with the gender of the person *arriving*).

## Casual Greetings With Friends

7. **¿Qué tal?** — What's up? / How's it going? Casual but safe almost anywhere — one of the most useful greetings in the language.
8. **¿Cómo te va?** — How's it going?
9. **¿Cómo van las cosas?** — How are things going?
10. **¿Qué hay de nuevo?** — What's new?
11. **¿Qué cuentas?** — What's new? (literally "what do you tell?")
12. **¿Todo bien?** — All good?
13. **¡Cuánto tiempo!** / **¡Tanto tiempo sin verte!** — Long time no see!

## Mexican Greetings You Need to Know

Since Mexican Spanish is the variety most US learners hear, these matter:

14. **¿Qué onda?** — What's up? (literally "what wave?"). *The* Mexican casual greeting. Friends only — don't open with it at a job interview. Pronounced keh OHN-dah.
15. **¿Qué pasó?** — Literally "what happened?", used simply as "what's up?" in Mexico. Nothing needs to have happened.
16. **¿Qué hubo?** — What's up? Often compressed in speech to **¡Quiubo!** (KYOO-boh).
17. **¿Qué tranza?** — Very slangy "what's up?" among young people. Use with close friends only.
18. **¡Qué milagro!** — "What a miracle!" — said when you run into someone you haven't seen in ages.

Elsewhere in Latin America you'll hear regional cousins: **¿Qué más?** (Colombia), **¿Qué bolá?** (Cuba), **¿Cómo andás?** (Argentina, with the voseo *-ás*), **¿Qué fue?** (parts of the Caribbean and Andes). You don't need to produce these — but recognizing them keeps you from freezing when someone fires one at you.

## Answering a Greeting

A greeting question expects a short, upbeat reply plus a return question:

| They say | You say |
|----------|---------|
| ¿Cómo estás? | Bien, gracias, ¿y tú? (Well, thanks, and you?) |
| ¿Cómo está usted? | Muy bien, gracias, ¿y usted? |
| ¿Qué tal? | Todo bien, ¿y tú? (All good, and you?) |
| ¿Qué onda? | Nada, aquí nomás. ¿Tú qué? (Nothing, just here. You?) |
| ¿Qué hay de nuevo? | Nada nuevo, lo mismo de siempre. (Nothing new, same as always.) |
| Mucho gusto. | Igualmente. (Likewise.) |

Other handy response upgrades: **muy bien** (very well), **más o menos** (so-so), **no me quejo** (can't complain), **aquí andamos** (hanging in there — very Mexican). One trap: **regular** in Spanish means "meh, not great," not "regular/fine" — if you answer *regular*, expect a concerned "¿qué pasó?"

## Texting and Phone Greetings

19. **¡Buenas!** — the all-purpose text opener.
20. **¿Qué onda? / ¿q onda?** — standard between Mexican friends; often typed **konda** or **q ondaa**.
21. **¿Cómo vas?** — how are you doing? (checking in mid-week).
22. **Hola, ¿cómo andas?** — hi, how are you doing?
23. **xq / xk, tqm, ntp** — not greetings themselves, but you'll see them immediately after one: *porque* (because), *te quiero mucho* (love you lots), *no te preocupes* (don't worry).
24. **¿Bueno?** — how Mexicans answer the **phone**. Not "hello" — just *¿bueno?* Elsewhere: *¿Aló?* (much of South America), *¿Diga?* (Spain).

## Greetings for Specific Situations

25. **¿Cómo amaneciste?** — literally "how did you dawn?" — a warm morning greeting asking how you woke up / slept. Common in Mexico among family. Reply: *Bien, ¿y tú?*
26. **¡Feliz lunes! / ¡Feliz viernes!** — Happy Monday / Friday! Common in messages and at work.
27. **Saludos** — "greetings"; the standard friendly sign-off/opener in emails and the word for sending hello through someone: *Saludos a tu mamá* (say hi to your mom).
28. **Un gusto saludarte** — "a pleasure to greet you" — polite way to open a message to someone you know slightly.
29. **¡Hola a todos!** — Hello everyone! (groups, meetings, videos).
30. **¿Listo? / ¿Lista?** — "Ready?" — how friends greet each other when meeting up to go somewhere.

## Cheek Kisses, Handshakes, and Other Body Language

Greetings in Latin America come with physical contact that surprises US learners. In Mexico, one cheek kiss (right cheek, really a cheek touch) is standard between women, and between men and women who know each other socially; men shake hands or share the hug-plus-backslap (*el abrazo*). In business, a handshake is always safe. Standing at arm's length and waving can read as cold — leaning in is friendly, not forward.

Pronunciation across everything above follows Spanish's reliably phonetic rules — every letter (except h) says its one sound, which is why Spanish pronunciation ranks among the easiest for English speakers ([here's a full guide to the Spanish sound system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_phonology)). ## FAQ

### What's the most common way to say hello in Spanish?

*Hola* — it's universal and never wrong. But natives almost always pair it with a follow-up: *Hola, ¿qué tal?* or *Hola, ¿cómo estás?* Learning the pairs, not just the single word, is what makes you sound natural.

### What does ¿qué onda? mean and is it rude?

It literally means "what wave?" and functions as "what's up?" in Mexico. It's not rude, but it is casual — perfect with friends and people your age, wrong with your boss, elders, or officials. When in doubt, use *¿qué tal?* instead.

### Do I say buenas noches when arriving or leaving?

Both. Unlike English "good night," *buenas noches* works as a greeting when you arrive somewhere after dark and as a farewell when you leave or go to bed. Context makes the meaning obvious.

### How do you respond to mucho gusto?

Say **igualmente** ("likewise") or **el gusto es mío** ("the pleasure is mine"). Repeating *mucho gusto* back is also perfectly acceptable.
