Spanish indirect object pronouns — **me, te, le, nos, les** — mark who receives the action or benefits from it: *Le escribí un mensaje* (I wrote **him** a message). They answer "to whom?" or "for whom?", they go before the conjugated verb, and they come with one famous twist: **le** and **les** transform into **se** whenever they land next to lo, la, los, or las — which is why "I gave it to her" is *se lo di*, never "le lo di."

If you've already worked through [Spanish direct object pronouns](/sofia/blog/spanish-direct-object-pronouns/), the good news is you know most of this system: me, te, nos are identical. Only the third person changes — and the third person is where all the interesting stuff happens.

## The Five Pronouns

| Person | Pronoun | English |
|---|---|---|
| yo | **me** | to/for me |
| tú | **te** | to/for you |
| él / ella / usted | **le** | to/for him, her, you (formal) |
| nosotros | **nos** | to/for us |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes | **les** | to/for them, you all |

Notice what's missing: gender. Direct objects distinguish *lo* from *la*, but indirect objects don't care — **le** covers him, her, and formal you alike. *Le hablé* can mean "I spoke to him" or "I spoke to her"; context or a clarifying phrase (*le hablé a María*) settles it.

## Spotting the Indirect Object

The direct object is what gets acted on; the indirect object is who it's directed at. In "I sent my mom flowers": flowers = direct (what got sent), my mom = indirect (who received them).

**The test:** if the English works with "to" or "for" in front of the person — I sent flowers *to my mom*, I bought a coffee *for you* — that person is the indirect object. In Spanish:

- **Te compré un café.** (teh kohm-PREH oon kah-FEH) — I bought you a coffee.
- **Le mandé las fotos.** — I sent him/her the photos.
- **Nos dieron la llave.** — They gave us the key.
- **Les expliqué el problema.** — I explained the problem to them.

Verbs of giving, telling, sending, showing, and asking almost always carry an indirect object: *dar, decir, mandar, enviar, mostrar, enseñar, preguntar, pedir, contar, escribir, traer, prestar* — a healthy chunk of the [most common Spanish verbs](/sofia/blog/common-spanish-verbs/).

## Placement: Same Rules, No New Grammar

Indirect object pronouns sit exactly where direct ones do:

- **Before a conjugated verb:** *Le escribo cada semana.* (I write to her every week.)
- **Attached to an infinitive — or before the helper verb:** *Voy a decirte algo* = *Te voy a decir algo.* (I'm going to tell you something.) Both correct, both common.
- **Attached to a gerund — or before estar:** *Estoy explicándole* = *Le estoy explicando.* (Note the accent when you attach.)
- **Attached to affirmative commands:** *¡Dime!* (Tell me!) *¡Escríbele!* (Write to him!)
- **Before negative commands:** *No me digas.* (Don't tell me — also the standard Spanish "no way!")

If these positions already feel familiar from [reflexive verbs in Spanish](/sofia/blog/reflexive-verbs-in-spanish/), that's because Spanish reuses one placement system for all its object pronouns. Learn it once, apply it three times.

## The Redundant Le: Say It Twice, It's Not a Mistake

Here's the feature English speakers resist: Spanish routinely uses **le/les even when the recipient is named in the sentence**.

- **Le di el libro a Marta.** — I gave the book to Marta. (Literally: "I gave *her* the book to Marta.")
- **Les mandé un correo a mis colegas.** — I sent an email to my colleagues.

That "extra" pronoun isn't optional politeness — with recipients, native speakers include it nearly always, and dropping it (*"Di el libro a Marta"*) sounds stiff and foreign. Flip your instinct: the pronoun is the grammar; the *a Marta* part is the clarification.

This also solves le's ambiguity problem. Since *le* alone could be him, her, or usted, the **a + person** phrase specifies: *Le escribí a mi abuela.* (I wrote to my grandmother.)

## Le + Lo = Se Lo: The Golden Rule

When a sentence has both pronouns, the order is fixed: **indirect before direct** — "who before what."

- **¿Me prestas tu pluma?** → **¿Me la prestas?** — Will you lend it to me?
- **Te traigo el café.** → **Te lo traigo.** — I'll bring it to you.

But when *le* or *les* would bump into *lo, la, los, las*, Spanish refuses to say "le lo" — two L-words in a row — and swaps in **se**:

- Le di el libro (a Marta). → **Se lo di.** — I gave it to her.
- Les mandé las fotos. → **Se las mandé.** — I sent them to them.
- Le voy a contar la historia. → **Voy a contársela.** / **Se la voy a contar.** — I'm going to tell it to him.

This *se* has nothing to do with reflexive *se* — it's just *le/les* wearing a disguise for pronunciation's sake. And because *se* is even more ambiguous than *le* (him? her? them? you?), the clarifying phrase does overtime: *Se lo di a ella.*

Drill the pattern until it's a reflex: **who before what, and le becomes se before lo.**

## Gustar and Friends: Where Le Runs the Show

The single biggest consumer of indirect object pronouns is the *gustar* family. Spanish doesn't say "I like coffee" — it says "coffee is pleasing **to me**":

- **Me gusta el café.** — I like coffee. (Coffee pleases me.)
- **Le encantan los tacos.** — He loves tacos. (Tacos enchant him.)
- **¿Te interesa la historia?** — Are you interested in history?
- **Nos falta tiempo.** — We're short on time. (Time is lacking to us.)
- **Les duele la cabeza.** — Their heads hurt.

The structure is always: indirect pronoun + verb agreeing with the *thing* (gusta if singular, gustan if plural). *Gustar, encantar, interesar, importar, molestar, faltar, doler, parecer* — all wired the same way. If *le* has been mystifying you in sentences like *le gusta*, this is why: the liker is an indirect object, not a subject.

## 10 Sentences to Say Out Loud

1. **Me dijeron la verdad.** — They told me the truth.
2. **¿Te mando la dirección?** — Should I send you the address?
3. **Le compré un regalo a mi mamá.** — I bought my mom a gift.
4. **Nos explicó todo.** — She explained everything to us.
5. **Les pedí un favor.** — I asked them for a favor.
6. **¿Me lo puedes repetir?** — Can you repeat it for me?
7. **Se la di ayer.** — I gave it to her yesterday.
8. **Voy a mostrárselo.** — I'm going to show it to him.
9. **Le encanta la música mexicana.** — She loves Mexican music.
10. **No me digas que se lo contaste.** — Don't tell me you told him about it.

Read them aloud twice: once for meaning, once purely for the rhythm of the pronoun clusters — *me-lo, se-la, se-lo* — because that rhythm is what your mouth will reach for mid-conversation.

## The Mistakes That Mark a Learner

- **"Le lo di."** Never. Le + lo/la/los/las always becomes **se lo / se la / se los / se las**.
- **Dropping the redundant le.** *"Di el regalo a Juan"* is understood but unnatural. Say **Le di el regalo a Juan.**
- **Using lo/la for people after verbs of telling.** "La dije que..." is a common error (and a real dialect feature in parts of Spain called *laísmo*, but not standard). Telling is done *to* someone: **Le dije que...**
- **Making the liker the subject of gustar.** "Yo gusto tacos" — no. **Me gustan los tacos.** The tacos do the pleasing; you receive it.
- **Freezing over se's ambiguity.** *Se lo dije* could mean I told him/her/them/you — and that's fine. Add *a ella* or *a ellos* only when context genuinely doesn't cover it. Native speakers leave it ambiguous constantly.

## FAQ

**What are the Spanish indirect object pronouns?**

Me, te, le, nos, les — to/for me, you, him/her/usted, us, and them/ustedes respectively. Unlike direct object pronouns, they don't mark gender: *le* serves for both "to him" and "to her." (Spain also uses *os* for informal plural "you all"; Latin America uses *les*.)

**When does le become se in Spanish?**

Whenever le or les would sit directly before lo, la, los, or las. Spanish blocks the "le lo" sound sequence, so "I gave it to her" is *se lo di*, not "le lo di." This *se* is just le/les in disguise — it's unrelated to reflexive se.

**What's the difference between le and lo?**

Lo (or la) is the direct object — the thing or person directly acted on: *Lo vi* (I saw him). Le is the indirect object — the person something is given, said, or sent to: *Le hablé* (I spoke to him). Quick test: if English needs or accepts "to" before the person, use le.

**Why does Spanish say "le di el libro a Juan" — isn't that redundant?**

Yes, deliberately. Spanish requires (in practice) the pronoun even when the recipient is named — the *a Juan* phrase clarifies who *le* refers to, not the other way around. Omitting the le is grammatically tolerated but sounds unnatural to native speakers, so build the doubled version into your habit from day one.

**Why is it "me gusta" and not "yo gusto"?**

Because *gustar* means "to be pleasing," not "to like." The thing you like is the subject, and you are the indirect object it pleases: *Me gustan los tacos* = "tacos are pleasing to me." The verb agrees with the tacos (gustan, plural), and the liker is always me/te/le/nos/les.
