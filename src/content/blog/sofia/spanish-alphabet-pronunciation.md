The Spanish alphabet (el abecedario) has 27 letters: the same 26 as English plus **ñ** (eñe). The best news for anyone learning is that Spanish is almost perfectly phonetic — once you learn the sound of each letter, you can read practically any word out loud correctly, even one you've never seen.

That's the opposite of English, where "through," "though," and "tough" all break the rules. In Spanish, the letters keep their promises. Let's go through all 27, then the handful of rules that trip people up.

## The Vowels Are Your Anchor

Spanish has only **five vowel sounds**, and they never change. Say them out loud: **a** (ah), **e** (eh), **i** (ee), **o** (oh), **u** (oo). English vowels wander all over the place; Spanish vowels are steady and pure.

This is the single most important thing to master. Keep every vowel short, clean, and consistent, and you'll instantly sound more Spanish. The most common beginner mistake is stretching vowels into English diphthongs — saying "noh-oo" instead of a clean "no."

## The Rules That Actually Matter

A few letters change their sound depending on the letters around them. These are the rules worth memorizing.

- **C** sounds like "k" before a, o, u (**casa**, **como**) but like "s" before e, i (**cena** = dinner, **cine** = cinema).
- **G** is a hard "g" before a, o, u (**gato**, **gota**) but a throaty "h" before e, i (**gente** = people, **gis** = chalk).
- **J** is always that same throaty "h" sound (**jamón** = ham). It comes from the back of the throat, a bit stronger than the English "h."
- **H** is completely silent. **Hola** is "OH-la," **hora** (hour) is "OH-ra." Never pronounce it.
- **Ñ** adds a "y" glide: **año** (year) is "AH-nyo." Don't confuse it with plain **n** — **año** (year) vs. **ano** means very different things, so the ñ matters.
- **LL** together usually sounds like "y": **llave** (key) is "YAH-veh," **pollo** (chicken) is "PO-yo."
- **QU** is always "k," and the u is silent: **queso** is "KEH-so," not "kweh-so."

## The Two R Sounds

Spanish has a **single tapped r** and a **rolled rr**. A single r between vowels (**pero** = but) is a quick flick of the tongue, close to the "tt" in the American "butter." The double **rr** (**perro** = dog) is a full roll — and the r at the start of a word (**rojo**, **ratón**) rolls too.

Yes, **pero** (but) and **perro** (dog) differ only by the roll. Don't stress if the trill takes weeks to land — plenty of native speakers' kids take a while too. Practice by relaxing your tongue and pushing air across it; force never helps.

## Where the Stress Falls

Spanish tells you which syllable to emphasize, with two simple rules and one shortcut.

1. If a word ends in a **vowel, n, or s**, stress the **second-to-last** syllable: **CA-sa**, **JO-ven** (young), **za-PA-tos**.
2. If it ends in **any other consonant**, stress the **last** syllable: **ciu-DAD** (city), **ha-BLAR** (to speak).
3. If there's a **written accent** (á, é, í, ó, ú), forget the rules — just stress that letter: **ca-**fé, **rá-**pido, **a-**diós.

The accent mark is a gift: it removes all guesswork about where to push.

## A Quick Mexican-Spanish Note

In Mexico (and most of Latin America), **z** and soft **c** are both pronounced as plain "s" — **zapato** and **casa** share that sound. You'll only hear the "th" lisp (the theta) in parts of Spain. So if you're learning the accent Sofia uses, you have one fewer sound to worry about. Con mucho gusto — you're already ahead.

## FAQ

### How many letters are in the Spanish alphabet?

27. The 26 English letters plus **ñ**. You may see old textbooks count **ch** and **ll** as separate letters, but the Real Academia Española dropped them from the official alphabet in 2010, so 27 is the current count.

### Is Spanish pronunciation really easier than English?

For reading aloud, yes. Spanish spelling is highly consistent, so once you know the letter sounds and the stress rules, you can pronounce almost any written word correctly on sight. English spelling is far less predictable.

### What's the hardest sound for English speakers?

Usually the rolled **rr** and, to a lesser degree, the throaty **j**. Both come with practice. Keep your vowels pure first — that fixes more of your accent than perfecting the trill.

### Do I need to pronounce the accent marks?

You don't pronounce the mark itself, but it tells you where to put the emphasis, and that changes the word. **Papá** (dad) and **papa** (potato) are the same letters with a different stressed syllable — so the accent genuinely matters.
