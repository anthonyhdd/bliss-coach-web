Family vocabulary in Spanish (**la familia**) starts with the core pairs: **madre / padre** (mother / father), **hijo / hija** (son / daughter), **hermano / hermana** (brother / sister), and **abuelo / abuela** (grandfather / grandmother). Nearly every family word comes in a masculine form ending in **-o** and a feminine form ending in **-a**, so once you learn one, you usually get the other for free.

Family is at the heart of Mexican culture, so these are among the most useful words you can learn. Below you'll find a complete chart covering immediate family, extended relatives, in-laws, and the affectionate everyday terms Mexicans actually use at home.

## The Core: Immediate Family

These are the words you'll use most. Notice the tidy -o / -a pattern.

| Spanish (m.) | Spanish (f.) | English |
|---|---|---|
| padre | madre | father / mother |
| papá | mamá | dad / mom |
| hijo | hija | son / daughter |
| hermano | hermana | brother / sister |
| abuelo | abuela | grandfather / grandmother |
| nieto | nieta | grandson / granddaughter |
| esposo | esposa | husband / wife |

A couple of everyday notes:

- **papá / mamá** are the warm, casual words (like "dad" and "mom"), while **padre / madre** are slightly more formal. In Mexican homes you'll hear *papá* and *mamá* constantly.
- Watch the accents: **papá** (dad) versus **papa** (potato), and **mamá** (mom) versus **mama** (a verb form). That little accent is the whole difference — a classic source of accidental comedy.

## Extended Family

Beyond the household, the vocabulary keeps its predictable pattern.

| Spanish (m.) | Spanish (f.) | English |
|---|---|---|
| tío | tía | uncle / aunt |
| primo | prima | male cousin / female cousin |
| sobrino | sobrina | nephew / niece |
| bisabuelo | bisabuela | great-grandfather / great-grandmother |
| bisnieto | bisnieta | great-grandson / great-granddaughter |

The prefix **bis-** simply means "great-" — so *bisabuela* is your great-grandmother. Easy once you spot the pattern.

## In-Laws (Los Suegros)

In-laws break the neat -o / -a rule a little, so these are worth memorizing on their own.

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| suegro / suegra | father-in-law / mother-in-law |
| yerno | son-in-law |
| nuera | daughter-in-law |
| cuñado / cuñada | brother-in-law / sister-in-law |
| concuño / concuña | your spouse's sibling's spouse |

Note that **yerno** and **nuera** are irregular — they don't follow the -o / -a pattern, so they need a little extra attention. Collectively, your in-laws are **los suegros**.

## Step-Family and Half-Relatives

Modern families need modern words, and Spanish has them.

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| padrastro | stepfather |
| madrastra | stepmother |
| hijastro / hijastra | stepson / stepdaughter |
| hermanastro / hermanastra | stepbrother / stepsister |
| medio hermano / media hermana | half-brother / half-sister |

The suffix **-astro / -astra** signals a step-relationship. And godparents matter enormously in Mexican culture:

- **padrino** — godfather
- **madrina** — godmother
- **ahijado / ahijada** — godson / goddaughter
- **compadre / comadre** — the relationship between your child's parents and their godparents (a genuinely important bond in Mexico, often used affectionately between close friends too)

## Making Words Plural (Including the Mixed-Group Rule)

Spanish has one charming quirk: the **masculine plural covers mixed groups**. So:

- **los hijos** = the sons, *or* the children (sons and daughters together)
- **los padres** = the fathers, *or* the parents (mother and father)
- **los abuelos** = the grandfathers, *or* the grandparents
- **los tíos** = the uncles, *or* the aunts and uncles
- **los hermanos** = the brothers, *or* the siblings

So if someone says **"Voy a visitar a mis abuelos"** (I'm going to visit my grandparents), they almost certainly mean both grandma and grandpa, not two grandfathers.

## Mexican Terms of Affection

Here's where the language gets its warmth. Mexicans rarely stop at the plain words — everyday speech is full of affectionate nicknames.

- **mami / papi** — sweet, casual versions of mom and dad (also used affectionately with partners and kids).
- **abuelito / abuelita** — grandpa / grandma with the *-ito / -ita* diminutive, which adds tenderness. Mexicans adore diminutives.
- **mijo / mija** — a contraction of *mi hijo / mi hija* ("my son/my daughter"), used lovingly toward children, and even toward friends or strangers as a warm "sweetie" or "dear."
- **carnal** — Mexican slang for "brother," but in the sense of a very close friend ("bro").

You'll hear an *abuelita* call a grandchild **mijo** with a warmth that no textbook can quite capture. Sprinkling in diminutives like *-ito / -ita* is one of the fastest ways to sound genuinely Mexican.

## Useful Family Phrases

Put the vocabulary to work:

- **Esta es mi familia.** (This is my family.)
- **Tengo dos hermanos y una hermana.** (I have two brothers and one sister.)
- **Mi mamá se llama Rosa.** (My mom's name is Rosa.)
- **¿Cuántos primos tienes?** (How many cousins do you have?)
- **Vivo con mis abuelos.** (I live with my grandparents.)
- **Te presento a mi esposo.** (Let me introduce you to my husband.)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Confusing *padres* with "priests."** *Padres* means "parents," but *padre* singular can also mean "priest" (or, in Mexican slang, "cool" — **¡Qué padre!** means "how cool!"). Context sorts it out.

**Forgetting the accent on papá / mamá.** *Papá* is dad; *papa* is potato. *Mamá* is mom; *mama* is a verb. The accent is essential.

**Assuming every word follows -o / -a.** Most do, but *yerno*, *nuera*, and a few others don't. Memorize the exceptions.

**Over-literal in-law translations.** There's no single word for "in-law" as a category — you name the specific relative (*suegra*, *cuñado*, etc.). And *los suegros* means "the in-laws" collectively.

## FAQ

### How do you say "family" in Spanish?

**La familia**. It's a feminine noun, so it takes *la*. "My family" is **mi familia**, and "family members" are **los miembros de la familia** or simply **los familiares**.

### What's the difference between "padre" and "papá"?

**Papá** is the warm, everyday word for "dad," while **padre** is slightly more formal for "father." In casual Mexican speech, *papá* and *mamá* dominate at home. Careful with the accent — *papá* (dad) is not the same as *papa* (potato).

### Why does "los hermanos" mean both "brothers" and "siblings"?

Spanish uses the masculine plural to cover mixed-gender groups. So *los hermanos* means "the brothers" when the group is all male, but "the siblings" when it includes at least one sister. The same rule gives you *los padres* (parents) and *los abuelos* (grandparents).

### What does "mijo" mean?

**Mijo** is a contraction of *mi hijo* ("my son") — and *mija* comes from *mi hija* ("my daughter"). In Mexico it's used affectionately toward children, and often toward friends or even strangers as a warm "sweetie" or "dear."

### How do I talk about in-laws in Spanish?

Name the specific relative: **suegro / suegra** (father/mother-in-law), **cuñado / cuñada** (brother/sister-in-law), **yerno** (son-in-law), **nuera** (daughter-in-law). Collectively, "the in-laws" are **los suegros**. Note that *yerno* and *nuera* are irregular and don't follow the usual -o / -a pattern.
