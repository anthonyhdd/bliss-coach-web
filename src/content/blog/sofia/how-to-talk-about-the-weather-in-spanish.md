To talk about the weather in Spanish, you use three little patterns: **hace** + noun for general conditions (*hace calor* — it's hot), **está** + adjective or gerund for current states (*está nublado* — it's cloudy; *está lloviendo* — it's raining), and **hay** + noun for things that are present (*hay viento* — it's windy). The question that unlocks all of them is **¿Qué tiempo hace?** ("keh tee-EM-poh AH-seh") or, more casually in Mexico, **¿Cómo está el clima?** — what's the weather like?

Weather talk is the world's favorite small talk, and Spanish is no exception — master these three patterns and you have an opener for every elevator, taxi, and market stall from Tijuana to Buenos Aires.

## Why Three Patterns? (English Only Has One)

English throws "it's" at everything: it's hot, it's cloudy, it's raining, it's windy. Spanish splits the job by what kind of word follows:

| Pattern | Used with | Literal logic | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **hace** + noun | calor, frío, sol, viento, buen/mal tiempo | "it makes heat" | Hace frío. — It's cold. |
| **está** + adjective / gerund | nublado, despejado, lloviendo, nevando | "the sky is [state]" | Está nublado. — It's cloudy. |
| **hay** + noun | niebla, tormenta, nubes, hielo | "there is fog" | Hay niebla. — It's foggy. |

Yes, there's overlap — *hace viento* and *hay viento* are both fine, and Mexicans say both. Don't stress the edges; learn the classic pairings below and you'll always sound natural. (Wondering why weather uses *está* and not *es*? Weather is a current state, not an identity — the full story is in our [ser vs estar guide](/sofia/blog/ser-vs-estar/).)

## Hace: The Workhorse

**Hace** ("AH-seh") comes from *hacer* (to make/do), and for weather it's always third person singular — no conjugating to worry about:

- **Hace calor.** ("AH-seh kah-LOR") — It's hot.
- **Hace frío.** ("FREE-oh") — It's cold.
- **Hace sol.** — It's sunny.
- **Hace viento.** ("bee-EN-toh") — It's windy.
- **Hace fresco.** — It's cool/crisp.
- **Hace buen tiempo.** — The weather's nice.
- **Hace mal tiempo.** — The weather's bad.

To crank up the intensity, use **mucho** — not *muy*, because *calor* and *frío* are nouns here:

- **Hace mucho calor.** — It's really hot.
- **Hace muchísimo frío.** — It's freezing.
- **En Mérida hace un calorón.** — In Mérida it's brutally hot. (The -ón ending is a very Mexican intensifier.)

## Está: States and Right-Now Weather

**Está** ("es-TAH") covers the sky's current condition (adjectives) and precipitation in progress (gerunds):

- **Está nublado.** ("noo-BLAH-doh") — It's cloudy.
- **Está despejado.** — It's clear.
- **Está soleado.** — It's sunny (out).
- **Está lloviendo.** ("yoh-vee-EN-doh") — It's raining.
- **Está nevando.** — It's snowing.
- **Está lloviznando.** — It's drizzling.

The plain verbs work too: **llueve** (it rains / it's raining), **nieva** (it snows). *Llueve mucho en septiembre* (it rains a lot in September) states the pattern; *está lloviendo* reports what's happening out the window right now.

And for temperature-as-number: **La temperatura está a 30 grados** or simply **estamos a 30 grados** — we're at 30 degrees. (Celsius, always — 30°C is a hot day, not a cold one.)

## Hay: What's Out There

**Hay** ("AH-ee") means "there is/are," so it handles weather *things* you could point at:

- **Hay niebla.** ("nee-EH-blah") — It's foggy.
- **Hay nubes.** — There are clouds.
- **Hay tormenta.** — There's a storm.
- **Hay hielo en la carretera.** ("YEH-loh") — There's ice on the road.
- **Hay un arcoíris.** ("ar-koh-EE-rees") — There's a rainbow.

## Mini-Dialogues You Can Steal

**At the taco stand:**

> — ¡Qué calor hace hoy! (It's so hot today!)
> — Sí, hace muchísimo calor. Dicen que mañana llueve. (Yeah, it's super hot. They say it'll rain tomorrow.)
> — Ojalá. Ya necesitamos agua. (Hopefully. We need the rain.)

**Planning a trip:**

> — ¿Cómo está el clima en Guadalajara? (What's the weather like in Guadalajara?)
> — Está nublado y hace fresco. Lleva una chamarra. (It's cloudy and cool. Bring a jacket.)
> — ¿Va a llover? (Is it going to rain?)
> — El pronóstico dice que sí, por la tarde. (The forecast says yes, in the afternoon.)

**Small talk in the elevator:**

> — Está lloviendo fuertísimo, ¿no? (It's raining so hard, right?)
> — Sí, y no traje paraguas. (Yeah, and I didn't bring an umbrella.)

Note **la chamarra** — that's Mexican for jacket (Spain says *chaqueta*), and **el paraguas** ("pah-RAH-gwahs") is your umbrella — literally "stops-waters."

The exclamation pattern from the first dialogue is gold for small talk: **¡Qué + noun!** — *¡Qué frío!* (So cold!), *¡Qué calorón!* (So hot!), *¡Qué bonito día!* (What a nice day!). ## Talking About Weather in Other Tenses

You only need three extra forms to gossip about weather like a local:

- **Future (going to):** *Va a llover.* — It's going to rain. *Va a hacer frío mañana.* — It'll be cold tomorrow.
- **Past (completed):** *Ayer llovió toda la noche.* — Yesterday it rained all night. *Hizo mucho calor el fin de semana.* — It was really hot over the weekend.
- **Past (ongoing):** *Estaba lloviendo cuando salí.* — It was raining when I left.

## Common Mistakes English Speakers Make

- **Translating "I'm hot" literally.** ~~"Soy caliente"~~ or ~~"estoy caliente"~~ does *not* mean you're warm — it reads as "I'm attractive/aroused." For your body temperature, use **tener**: **Tengo calor** (I'm hot), **tengo frío** (I'm cold).
- **Using muy with hace calor.** *Calor* is a noun, so it takes *mucho*: **hace mucho calor**, never ~~"hace muy calor"~~.
- **Saying "es lloviendo."** Progressive always uses estar: **está lloviendo**. And plain *llueve* works fine too.
- **Mixing up es/está with weather adjectives.** Current sky conditions take está: **está nublado**. Climate as a permanent trait takes ser: *el clima de Cancún **es** húmedo* (Cancún's climate is humid).
- **Forgetting Celsius.** If a Mexican tells you *estamos a 35 grados*, don't reach for a sweater — that's 95°F.

## FAQ

**How do you ask "what's the weather like?" in Spanish?**

The textbook version is *¿Qué tiempo hace?* In Mexico and most of Latin America you'll more often hear *¿Cómo está el clima?* or *¿Cómo está el tiempo?* For the forecast, ask *¿Cuál es el pronóstico para mañana?* (What's the forecast for tomorrow?)

**When do I use hace, está, or hay for weather?**

Use hace with nouns for general conditions (*hace calor, hace frío, hace sol*), está with adjectives or gerunds for current states (*está nublado, está lloviendo*), and hay with things that are present (*hay niebla, hay tormenta*). Some overlap is normal — *hace viento* and *hay viento* are both correct.

**What's the difference between "hace calor" and "estoy caliente"?**

*Hace calor* means the weather is hot. To say *you* feel hot, use *tengo calor* (literally "I have heat"). *Estoy caliente* means "I'm aroused" in most contexts — one of the classic false-friend traps in Spanish, so drill *tengo calor / tengo frío* early.

**Is it "el clima" or "el tiempo" for weather?**

Both. *El tiempo* is the traditional word (also means "time"), favored in Spain; *el clima* technically means "climate" but is the everyday word for weather across Mexico and Latin America. *¿Cómo está el clima?* will sound perfectly natural anywhere in the Americas.

**How do I say "it's raining" vs "it rains" in Spanish?**

*Está lloviendo* reports rain happening right now; *llueve* states a fact or habit (*llueve mucho en verano* — it rains a lot in summer). In casual speech *llueve* can cover both, so if in doubt, *llueve* is never wrong.
