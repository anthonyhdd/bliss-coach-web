Food vocabulary in Spanish starts with a handful of anchor words: **la comida** (koh-MEE-dah) means both "food" and "meal," **el desayuno** is breakfast, **el almuerzo** (or **la comida** in Mexico) is lunch, and **la cena** is dinner. From there, the fastest way to build a working food vocabulary is by category — produce, proteins, drinks, and the menu words that glue them together — which is exactly how this list is organized.

Food words repay study faster than almost any other vocabulary set. You use them three times a day, they're printed on every menu, and they're the vocabulary of markets, taquerías, and dinner tables — the places you actually want to speak Spanish.

## The Meals of the Day

- **el desayuno** (deh-sah-YOO-noh) — breakfast; *desayunar* is the verb: *Desayuno huevos.* (I have eggs for breakfast.)
- **el almuerzo** (ahl-MWEHR-soh) — lunch. Heads up: in Mexico, the big midday meal is usually **la comida**, eaten around 2–3 pm.
- **la cena** (SEH-nah) — dinner, typically lighter and later.
- **la botana** (boh-TAH-nah) — snack in Mexico; Spain says **el aperitivo** or **la merienda** for the afternoon snack.
- **el antojito** (ahn-toh-HEE-toh) — literally "little craving": Mexican street food like tacos, quesadillas, and elotes.

- **el plátano** (PLAH-tah-noh) — banana
- **la naranja** (nah-RAHN-hah) — orange
- **el limón** (lee-MOHN) — lime in Mexico (the small green one); lemon in Spain
- **la fresa** (FREH-sah) — strawberry
- **la piña** (PEE-nyah) — pineapple

- **la sandía** (sahn-DEE-ah) — watermelon
- **el aguacate** (ah-gwah-KAH-teh) — avocado (botanically a fruit, culturally a food group)
- **el coco** (KOH-koh) — coconut
- **el durazno** (doo-RAHS-noh) — peach (Spain: *el melocotón*)

- **la cebolla** (seh-BOH-yah) — onion
- **el ajo** (AH-hoh) — garlic
- **la zanahoria** (sah-nah-OH-ree-ah) — carrot

- **el chile** (CHEE-leh) — chili pepper: jalapeño, serrano, habanero, poblano

- **el maíz** (mah-EES) — corn; on the cob it's **el elote** in Mexico

- **el cerdo / el puerco** (SEHR-doh / PWEHR-koh) — pork

## Staples, Sides, and Basics

- **el arroz** (ah-RROHS) — rice; roll that double r
- **el pan** (pahn) — bread; **el bolillo** is the Mexican crusty roll
- **la tortilla** (tohr-TEE-yah) — in Mexico, the corn or flour flatbread; in Spain, a potato omelet. Same word, two completely different foods.
- **la sal** (sahl) — salt · **la pimienta** (pee-MYEHN-tah) — pepper
- **el azúcar** (ah-SOO-kahr) — sugar
- **el aceite** (ah-SAY-teh) — oil
- **la mantequilla** (mahn-teh-KEE-yah) — butter
- **la salsa** (SAHL-sah) — sauce; in Mexico, ask **¿pica?** (PEE-kah) — "is it spicy?" — before committing
- **la sopa** (SOH-pah) — soup
- **la ensalada** (ehn-sah-LAH-dah) — salad

## Drinks (Las Bebidas)

- **el agua** (AH-gwah) — water; **agua natural** = still, **agua mineral** = sparkling
- **las aguas frescas** — fruit waters: horchata, jamaica, tamarindo. A whole food group in Mexico.
- **el café** (kah-FEH) — coffee
- **el té** (teh) — tea
- **el jugo** (HOO-goh) — juice (Spain: *el zumo*): *jugo de naranja*
- **la cerveza** (sehr-VEH-sah) — beer
- **el vino tinto / blanco** — red / white wine
- **el refresco** (reh-FREHS-koh) — soda

## Dessert and Sweets (Los Postres)

- **el postre** (POHS-treh) — dessert
- **el helado** (eh-LAH-doh) — ice cream
- **el pastel** (pahs-TEHL) — cake (Spain: *la tarta*)
- **el flan** (flahn) — the caramel custard you should always say yes to
- **el chocolate** (choh-koh-LAH-teh) — chocolate
- **la galleta** (gah-YEH-tah) — cookie
- **los churros** (CHOO-rrohs) — fried dough sticks, ideally with hot chocolate

## The Words That Make It a Sentence

Vocabulary lists don't feed you; sentences do. These verbs and phrases activate everything above:

- **Tengo hambre.** (TEHN-goh AHM-breh) — I'm hungry. · **Tengo sed.** — I'm thirsty.
- **Quisiera...** (kee-SYEH-rah) — I would like... The polite ordering verb: *Quisiera los tacos de pollo.*
- **¿Me trae...?** (meh TRAH-eh) — Could you bring me...? *¿Me trae más salsa, por favor?*
- **probar** (proh-BAHR) — to try/taste: *Quiero probar el mole.* (I want to try the mole.)
- **¡Está delicioso! / ¡Está muy rico!** — It's delicious! *Rico* is what Mexicans actually say.
- **La cuenta, por favor.** (KWEHN-tah) — The check, please.
- **soy vegetariano/a** — I'm vegetarian; **soy alérgico/a a...** — I'm allergic to... (*a los cacahuates* — peanuts, *a los mariscos* — shellfish)

## Mexican Menu Words Worth Knowing

Since Sofia teaches Mexican Spanish, a bonus round you'll see on every menu and street corner:

- **el taco** — needs no introduction; **al pastor** (spit-roasted pork), **de asada** (grilled beef), **de carnitas** (slow-cooked pork)
- **la quesadilla** (keh-sah-DEE-yah) — tortilla with melted cheese (usually — in Mexico City, famously, the cheese is optional)
- **el guacamole** (gwah-kah-MOH-leh) — you know this one; now you can pronounce it
- **los tamales** (tah-MAH-lehs) — steamed corn dough in husks; one is *un tamal*, no final "e"
- **el mole** (MOH-leh) — the complex chili-chocolate sauce family
- **el pozole** (poh-SOH-leh) — hominy stew, weekend food
- **la torta** (TOHR-tah) — a Mexican sandwich on a bolillo, not a cake

## How to Actually Memorize 100 Words

Don't drill the list top to bottom. Instead:

1. **Label your kitchen.** Sticky notes on la sal, el aceite, el refresco. You'll see each word ten times a day in context.
2. **Narrate your meals.** Once a day, say what you're eating out loud: *Desayuno huevos con pan y café.* Ten seconds, real grammar, real vocabulary.
3. **Order in Spanish in your head.** Next time you're at any restaurant, silently build your order: *Quisiera el pollo con arroz y una cerveza.*
4. **Learn by menu, not by alphabet.** Group words the way meals group them — protein + side + drink — because that's how you'll retrieve them.

Twenty minutes a week of this beats an hour of flashcards, because food vocabulary has a superpower most word lists don't: you already interact with the referents three times a day.

## FAQ

**What are the most important Spanish food words for beginners?**

Start with the meal anchors (desayuno, comida/almuerzo, cena), ten foods you personally eat weekly (pollo, arroz, huevo, pan, queso, manzana...), the drinks (agua, café, cerveza), and the ordering phrases (quisiera, la cuenta, por favor). Thirty words you'll use daily beat a hundred you won't.

**Is it "el agua" or "la agua"?**

El agua — but the word is feminine. Spanish uses *el* before feminine nouns that start with a stressed "a" sound purely for pronunciation, so it's *el agua fría* (with a feminine adjective) and *las aguas* in the plural.

**What's the difference between "pescado" and "pez"?**

*El pez* is a fish swimming in the water; *el pescado* is fish as food — literally "that which has been fished." Order *pescado* at a restaurant. The same logic doesn't apply to other animals: chicken is *pollo* whether it's clucking or on your plate.

**Do food words change between Mexico and Spain?**

Constantly — it's the vocabulary area with the most regional variation. Potato is *papa* (Mexico) vs. *patata* (Spain), juice is *jugo* vs. *zumo*, peach is *durazno* vs. *melocotón*, and *tortilla* names two entirely different dishes. Learn the Mexican set first if Latin America is your goal; Spaniards will still understand you.

**How do I say I have a food allergy in Spanish?**

*Soy alérgico* (or *alérgica*) *a...* plus the food: *Soy alérgica a los cacahuates* (peanuts), *al gluten*, *a los mariscos* (shellfish). For severity, add *Es una alergia seria, ¿este plato lo contiene?* — "It's a serious allergy, does this dish contain it?"
