Spanish present tense conjugation works by removing a verb's ending (-ar, -er, or -ir) and adding a new ending that matches who's doing the action: *hablar* (to speak) becomes *hablo* (I speak), *hablas* (you speak), *habla* (he/she speaks). Learn three ending patterns plus ten common irregular verbs and you can say most of what a beginner needs to say.

## Why Conjugation Feels Hard (and Why It Isn't)

English barely conjugates: I speak, you speak, she speak**s**. Spanish changes the ending for every person, which looks like six times the work. But there's a payoff: because the ending tells you *who*, Spanish usually drops the pronoun entirely. *Hablo español* — no "yo" needed, the -o already means "I."

And there are really only three patterns to learn, because every Spanish verb ends in one of three ways: **-ar**, **-er**, or **-ir**. That's the whole system.

## The Three Regular Patterns

Take the infinitive (the dictionary form), chop the last two letters, and attach the ending for the person. Here are the three patterns side by side with a model verb for each: *hablar* (to speak), *comer* (to eat), *vivir* (to live).

| Person | -AR → hablar | -ER → comer | -IR → vivir |
|---|---|---|---|
| yo (I) | habl**o** | com**o** | viv**o** |
| tú (you) | habl**as** | com**es** | viv**es** |
| él / ella / usted (he, she, you-formal) | habl**a** | com**e** | viv**e** |
| nosotros (we) | habl**amos** | com**emos** | viv**imos** |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes (they, you-all) | habl**an** | com**en** | viv**en** |

Three things make this table smaller than it looks:

1. **The yo form is always -o.** All three patterns. One thing to remember, not three.
2. **-ER and -IR are nearly twins.** They differ only in the nosotros form (*comemos* vs. *vivimos*). Learn -er and you've basically learned -ir.
3. **In Latin America, you don't need vosotros.** That's the informal "you all" used in Spain (*habláis, coméis, vivís*). Mexico and all of Latin America use *ustedes* for any group of "you," which conjugates exactly like *ellos*. This guide follows Latin American usage — one less row to memorize.

### Try the pattern on new verbs

Once you own the endings, every regular verb is free:

- *trabajar* (to work) → *trabajo, trabajas, trabaja, trabajamos, trabajan*
- *aprender* (to learn) → *aprendo, aprendes, aprende, aprendemos, aprenden*
- *escribir* (to write) → *escribo, escribes, escribe, escribimos, escriben*

**Pronunciation note:** regular present tense forms stress the second-to-last syllable: *ha-BLA-mos*, *a-PREN-des*. Keep the vowels pure — *hablo* is *AH-bloh*, never "hab-low."

## The 10 Irregular Verbs Actually Worth Learning First

Here's the honest part: the most *frequent* Spanish verbs are irregular. That's not sadism — it's true in every language (think English: to be → am/is/are). The good news is that ten verbs cover an enormous share of daily speech, and even their irregularities follow mini-patterns.

### The Big Four (learn these cold)

**1. ser — to be (permanent: identity, origin, traits)**
*soy, eres, es, somos, son*
→ *Soy de México.* (I'm from Mexico.) *Eres muy amable.* (You're very kind.)

**2. estar — to be (temporary: location, feelings, states)**
*estoy, estás, está, estamos, están*
→ *Estoy cansado.* (I'm tired.) *¿Dónde está el baño?* (Where's the bathroom?)

**3. tener — to have**
*tengo, tienes, tiene, tenemos, tienen*
→ *Tengo hambre.* (I'm hungry — literally "I have hunger.") *Tengo treinta años.* (I'm 30.)

**4. ir — to go**
*voy, vas, va, vamos, van*
→ *Voy al mercado.* (I'm going to the market.) Bonus: **ir + a + infinitive is the beginner's future tense.** *Voy a comer.* (I'm going to eat.) One irregular verb unlocks talking about the future — that's why *ir* punches above its weight.

### The Next Six

**5. hacer — to do/make** — irregular only in yo: *hago, haces, hace, hacemos, hacen*
→ *Hago ejercicio.* (I exercise.) *¿Qué haces?* (What are you doing?)

**6. poder — to be able to / can** — *puedo, puedes, puede, podemos, pueden*
→ *¿Puedes ayudarme?* (Can you help me?) *No puedo.* (I can't.)

**7. querer — to want** — *quiero, quieres, quiere, queremos, quieren*
→ *Quiero un café.* (I want a coffee.) Attach an infinitive for instant sentences: *Quiero aprender español.*

**8. decir — to say/tell** — *digo, dices, dice, decimos, dicen*
→ *¿Cómo se dice "table" en español?* (How do you say "table" in Spanish?) — the single most useful learner question.

**9. venir — to come** — *vengo, vienes, viene, venimos, vienen*
→ *Vengo de Estados Unidos.* (I come from the US.) *¿Vienes a la fiesta?* (Are you coming to the party?)

**10. saber — to know (facts/how to)** — irregular only in yo: *sé, sabes, sabe, sabemos, saben*
→ *No sé.* (I don't know.) You will say *no sé* constantly as a learner; make peace with it early.

### The hidden patterns inside the irregulars

Look again and the "irregular" verbs organize themselves:

- **The -go club:** tener → ten**go**, hacer → ha**go**, decir → di**go**, venir → ven**go**, plus *salir → salgo* (I leave) and *poner → pongo* (I put). Irregular yo, mostly regular elsewhere.
- **Stem changers:** poder (o→ue: p**ue**do), querer (e→ie: qu**ie**ro), decir (e→i: d**i**ces). The vowel in the stem shifts in every form *except nosotros* (*podemos, queremos, decimos*). Once you spot this "boot pattern," hundreds of verbs like *dormir* (duermo), *pensar* (pienso), and *pedir* (pido) come along free.
- **Truly wild:** only *ser* and *ir* refuse all patterns. Two verbs. You can brute-force two verbs.

## A Practice Method That Actually Works

Drilling conjugation tables in silence produces people who ace worksheets and freeze in conversation. Do this instead:

### 1. Learn in sentences, not tables (Week 1)

For each of the ten irregulars, memorize one real sentence per form you'll actually use: *Tengo hambre. ¿Tienes tiempo? No puedo hoy. Quiero agua.* Your brain stores chunks far better than grids.

### 2. Run the 3-verb morning drill (Weeks 1–4)

Every morning, pick three verbs and say all five forms **out loud** for each — 90 seconds total. Speed matters more than ceremony: conjugation has to become reflex, and reflexes are built by frequency, not marathon sessions.

### 3. Narrate your day in yo-form (Weeks 2+)

The yo form is the one you'll use most, so narrate as you go: *Hago café. Como cereal. Trabajo. Voy al gimnasio.* Sounds silly, works absurdly well — you're conjugating dozens of times a day with zero study time.

### 4. Ask and answer (Weeks 3+)

Conjugation lives in the tú↔yo flip: someone asks *¿tienes...?* and you answer *tengo...*. Practice question-answer pairs: *¿Dónde vives? — Vivo en Denver. ¿Qué quieres? — Quiero un taco.* This flip is exactly what real conversation demands and exactly what tables never teach.

### 5. Get live reps with correction

Nothing cements conjugation like producing it under mild pressure and being corrected in the moment. ## FAQ

### How many tenses do I need before speaking?

One — this one. Present tense plus *ir a + infinitive* (future) and *me gustaría* (I'd like) handles weeks of real conversation. Speak now; add the past tenses when the present feels automatic.

### What's the difference between ser and estar? Both mean "to be."

Rough rule: **ser** for what something *is* (identity, origin, profession, traits — *soy maestra, es de México*), **estar** for how or where it *is right now* (location, mood, condition — *estoy en casa, está cansado*). It's a deep topic, but that rule covers most beginner sentences.

### Do I always drop the pronoun?

Usually, yes. *Hablo español* beats *yo hablo español*. Keep the pronoun for emphasis or contrast: *Yo cocino y tú lavas los platos.* (I cook and *you* wash the dishes.)

### Is Mexican conjugation different from Spain's?

The verbs conjugate identically — the difference is that Spain uses *vosotros* for informal "you all" while Mexico uses *ustedes* for everyone. If you're learning Latin American Spanish, you can politely ignore *vosotros* forever.
