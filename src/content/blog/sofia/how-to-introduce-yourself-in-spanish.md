To introduce yourself in Spanish, say *Hola, me llamo...* (Hello, my name is...) followed by *Mucho gusto* (Nice to meet you). That two-line exchange covers 90% of first meetings — everything else in this guide builds on it, from the casual version you'd use at a party in Mexico City to the formal one for a job interview.

## The Core Script

Here's a complete self-introduction you can learn today and use anywhere:

> **Hola, me llamo Ana. Soy de Estados Unidos, de Chicago. Tengo veintiocho años y trabajo como diseñadora. Estoy aprendiendo español. ¡Mucho gusto!**
>
> "Hi, my name is Ana. I'm from the United States, from Chicago. I'm 28 years old and I work as a designer. I'm learning Spanish. Nice to meet you!"

Swap in your own details and you have a working introduction. Let's break down each piece so you can adapt it.

### Saying your name: three options

| Spanish | Literal meaning | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Me llamo Ana. | I call myself Ana. | The default. Works everywhere. |
| Soy Ana. | I'm Ana. | Casual and quick — friends of friends, parties. |
| Mi nombre es Ana. | My name is Ana. | Correct but slightly stiff; common on forms and phone calls. |

All three are fine. If you only learn one, learn *me llamo* — it's the one Spanish speakers use most in face-to-face introductions.

### Saying where you're from

- **Soy de...** — "I'm from..." → *Soy de Canadá.* / *Soy de Londres.*
- **Soy estadounidense / americano(a).** — "I'm American." In Mexico you'll also hear the friendly *soy gringo/gringa* used self-deprecatingly by foreigners, but let locals offer that word first.
- **Vivo en...** — "I live in..." → *Soy de Texas, pero vivo en Guadalajara.* (I'm from Texas, but I live in Guadalajara.)

### Age, work, and why you're there

- **Tengo... años.** — literally "I have... years." Spanish uses *tener* (to have) for age, never *ser* (to be). Saying *soy veintiocho* is a classic beginner error.
- **Trabajo como...** — "I work as a..." → *Trabajo como maestro.* (I work as a teacher.) Or use *soy* + profession with no article: *Soy enfermera.* (I'm a nurse.)
- **Estudio...** — "I study..." → *Estudio medicina.*
- **Estoy aprendiendo español.** — "I'm learning Spanish." Say this early. It buys you patience, slower speech, and instant goodwill.

## Formal vs. Casual: Tú and Usted

Spanish has two ways to say "you," and your introduction changes slightly depending on which one the situation calls for.

**Use *tú* (casual)** with people your age, friends of friends, classmates, and generally in relaxed social settings. Mexico leans friendly — among younger people, *tú* is the default almost immediately.

**Use *usted* (formal)** with older people, officials, your partner's grandparents, and in professional settings like interviews or client meetings. When in doubt, start with *usted*; the other person will say *háblame de tú* ("speak to me with tú") if formality isn't needed.

| Casual (tú) | Formal (usted) | English |
|---|---|---|
| ¿Cómo te llamas? | ¿Cómo se llama? | What's your name? |
| ¿De dónde eres? | ¿De dónde es? | Where are you from? |
| ¿A qué te dedicas? | ¿A qué se dedica? | What do you do for a living? |
| Encantado/a de conocerte. | Encantado/a de conocerlo/la. | Delighted to meet you. |

A formal introduction sounds like this:

> **Buenos días. Mi nombre es Ana García. Es un placer conocerlo.**
> "Good morning. My name is Ana García. It's a pleasure to meet you."

And a casual one:

> **¡Qué onda! Soy Ana. Mucho gusto.**
> "What's up! I'm Ana. Nice to meet you."

*¿Qué onda?* (literally "what wave?") is quintessential Mexican Spanish — friendly, informal, and everywhere. Don't open a business meeting with it, but at a taquería with new friends it lands perfectly.

## Responding When Someone Meets You

Introductions are two-way. When someone says *mucho gusto*, you have several natural replies:

| Spanish | English | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mucho gusto. | Nice to meet you. | Fine to echo it right back. |
| Igualmente. | Likewise. | The smoothest reply. |
| El gusto es mío. | The pleasure is mine. | A touch more charming/formal. |
| Encantado. / Encantada. | Delighted. | Ends in -o if you're male, -a if you're female. |

Note that *encantado/encantada* agrees with **your** gender (the speaker), not the other person's. A man says *encantado* even when meeting a woman.

## Pronunciation Notes

Spanish spelling is phonetic — once you know the rules, you can pronounce anything you can read. The introduction phrases hit a few key sounds:

- **ll** in *me llamo* sounds like English "y": *meh YAH-moh*. In Mexico it's a clean "y" sound (in Argentina it shifts toward "zh," but stick with "y").
- **The vowels never change.** A = "ah," E = "eh," I = "ee," O = "oh," U = "oo" — always, in every word. *Mucho gusto* is *MOO-choh GOOS-toh*, not "myoo-cho."
- **H is silent.** *Hola* is *OH-lah*. Never "hoh-la."
- **Años needs the ñ.** *AH-nyohs*, like the "ny" in "canyon." This one matters: *años* means years, while *anos* (no tilde) means... anuses. *Tengo treinta años*, always with the ñ sound.
- **Qué** is just "keh" — the u is silent after q.
- **Stress the right syllable.** *Trabajo* is *tra-BAH-hoh* (the j sounds like a strong English h).

## The Follow-Up Questions You'll Actually Hear

The scary part of introductions isn't your script — it's what comes back. These are the questions Spanish speakers most commonly ask right after meeting you, with ready-made answers:

| You'll hear | It means | Easy answer |
|---|---|---|
| ¿Y tú? / ¿Y usted? | And you? | Repeat their question's answer format back. |
| ¿De qué parte (de Estados Unidos)? | What part (of the US)? | De California. / Del norte. (From the north.) |
| ¿Cuánto tiempo llevas aquí? | How long have you been here? | Llevo dos semanas. (I've been here two weeks.) |
| ¿Te gusta México? | Do you like Mexico? | ¡Me encanta! (I love it!) |
| ¿Dónde aprendiste español? | Where did you learn Spanish? | Estoy aprendiendo, apenas empiezo. (I'm learning, just starting.) |
| ¿A qué te dedicas? | What do you do? | Trabajo como... / Estudio... |
| ¿Estás casado/a? ¿Tienes hijos? | Are you married? Kids? | Común in Mexico and friendly, not nosy. *Sí/No, tengo...* |
| ¿Cuál es tu número? / ¿Tienes WhatsApp? | What's your number? Do you have WhatsApp? | WhatsApp is how Mexico communicates. *Sí, claro.* |

Two survival phrases for when a follow-up loses you:

- **¿Puedes repetir, por favor?** — Can you repeat that, please?
- **Más despacio, por favor.** — Slower, please.

Nobody minds. Asking someone to slow down *in Spanish* signals effort, and effort is what people warm to.

## Cultural Notes (Mexico Especially)

**Greetings are physical.** In Mexico, a handshake is standard between men; between a man and a woman or two women, one light kiss on the cheek (the right cheek, one kiss — not two like Spain) is normal even at first meeting in social settings. In business, a handshake is safe for everyone.

**Names run long.** Many Mexicans introduce themselves with a first name only, but full names carry two surnames (father's, then mother's): *Ana García López*. Address people by the name they gave you.

**Titles matter in formal settings.** *Señor / Señora* plus surname, or profession-based titles like *licenciado* (college graduate/professional) and *ingeniero* (engineer), are used seriously in Mexican business culture.

**Small talk comes first.** Jumping straight to business without a *¿cómo estás?* exchange reads as cold. Budget two minutes of pleasantries; it's not wasted time, it's the culture.

## How to Practice Until It's Automatic

Reading a script isn't the same as saying it while someone looks at you. Close that gap in three steps:

1. **Write your personal script** — name, origin, work, why you're learning — using the patterns above. Keep it to five sentences.
2. **Say it out loud daily for a week.** Out loud is non-negotiable; your mouth needs the reps, not your eyes.
3. **Do it live with follow-ups.** The real test is handling *¿y tú? ¿de qué parte? ¿te gusta México?* without freezing. ## FAQ

### Is "me llamo" or "mi nombre es" more natural?

*Me llamo* wins in conversation. *Mi nombre es* isn't wrong — it's just what forms and receptionists say. In speech, natives overwhelmingly use *me llamo* or simply *soy*.

### Do I say "yo soy" or just "soy"?

Just *soy*. Spanish verbs already encode the subject, so *yo* is usually dropped. *Yo soy Ana* isn't incorrect, but it adds emphasis ("**I** am Ana"), which a plain introduction doesn't need.

### How do I introduce someone else?

*Te presento a...* (casual) or *Le presento a...* (formal): *Te presento a mi amiga Laura.* — "This is my friend Laura." The other person responds *mucho gusto*, same as always.

### What if I have a name Spanish speakers find hard to pronounce?

Say it slowly once, then offer the closest Spanish-friendly version if you have one: *Me llamo Heather... o "Éder", más fácil.* People appreciate the flexibility, and you'll hear the Spanish version of your name more than you expect.
