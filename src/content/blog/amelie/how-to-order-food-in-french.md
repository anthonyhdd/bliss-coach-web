To order food in French, the golden formula is ***Je voudrais…*** (zhuh voo-DREH — "I would like…") followed by what you want, plus a *s'il vous plaît* at the end: *Je voudrais un café, s'il vous plaît.* Say *bonjour* before anything else, order with *je voudrais* or *je vais prendre*, and ask for the bill with *l'addition, s'il vous plaît* — that's 80% of every café and restaurant interaction in France.

The rest is scripts and etiquette, and both are wonderfully predictable. French service follows a ritual with fixed lines on both sides — which means you can rehearse the whole scene before you ever sit down. Here's the full performance, course by course.

## Before you order: the entrance ritual

Walking into a café or restaurant, the sequence never changes:

1. **Say bonjour.** Non-negotiable — service in France starts only after the greeting is exchanged. (This is the famous "bonjour contract" from [French greetings](/amelie/blog/french-greetings/).) After 6 p.m., it's *bonsoir*.
2. **State your party size.** *Une table pour deux, s'il vous plaît.* (ewn TAH-bluh poor DUH seel voo PLEH) — "A table for two, please." Or simply: *Nous sommes deux.* (noo sum DUH) — "We are two."
3. **Wait to be seated** in restaurants. In cafés, you generally seat yourself — terrace or inside, your pick.

Useful early lines:

- *Vous avez une table en terrasse ?* (voo za-vay ewn TAH-bluh ah(n) teh-RAHSS) — "Do you have a table on the terrace?"
- *La carte, s'il vous plaît.* (lah KART) — "The menu, please."
- *Vous avez un menu en anglais ?* — "Do you have a menu in English?" (Fine to ask — but ordering in French, however imperfect, is always warmly received.)

**Teacher's tip:** *la carte* is the menu you order from; *le menu* (luh muh-NEW) is a fixed-price set (starter + main + dessert). *Le menu du jour* is usually the best deal in the building.

## Ordering: the three magic structures

When the server arrives — *Vous avez choisi ?* (voo za-vay shwah-ZEE — "Have you decided?") — you have three polite ways to answer, from most to least formal:

- ***Je voudrais…*** (zhuh voo-DREH) — "I would like…" The classic, always correct.
- ***Je vais prendre…*** (zhuh vay PRAH(N)-druh) — "I'll have…" What French people themselves most often say.
- ***Pour moi, … s'il vous plaît.*** (poor MWAH) — "For me, … please." Casual and natural, great in groups.

Never *je veux* ("I want") — grammatically fine, socially a bulldozer. And close every order with *s'il vous plaît*; if politeness formulas need a refresh, my guide to [saying thank you in French](/amelie/how-to-say/thank-you/) pairs perfectly with this one.

A complete café order:

> — **Bonjour ! Je vais prendre un café et un croissant, s'il vous plaît.**
> — Un café, un croissant. Tout de suite. ("Right away.")
> — **Merci !**

Café vocabulary worth knowing: *un café* is an espresso — if you want milk, ask for *un café crème* (kah-FAY KREHM); *un déca* (day-KAH) is a decaf; *une carafe d'eau* (ewn kah-RAHF DOH) is the free jug of tap water, and asking for it is completely normal.

## Restaurant script: from starter to dessert

French meals run in courses: *l'entrée* (lah(n)-TRAY — the starter, a famous false friend: it is *never* the main), *le plat* or *le plat principal* (luh PLAH — the main course), then *le dessert* (luh deh-SEHR).

The server's questions, and your lines:

> — **Vous avez choisi ?** — "Have you decided?"
> — **Oui. En entrée, je vais prendre la soupe à l'oignon, et en plat, le poulet rôti, s'il vous plaît.** — "As a starter, the onion soup, and for the main, the roast chicken, please."
> — **Et comme boisson ?** (ay kum bwah-SO(N)) — "And to drink?"
> — **Un verre de vin rouge et une carafe d'eau, s'il vous plaît.** (uh(n) VEHR duh veh(n) ROOZH) — "A glass of red wine and a jug of tap water, please."

For steak, one more question is coming — *Quelle cuisson ?* (kehl kwee-SO(N) — "How cooked?"):

| French | Pronunciation | English |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| bleu | BLUH | very rare |
| saignant | seh-NYAH(N) | rare |
| à point | ah PWEH(N) | medium(-rare, by French standards) |
| bien cuit | byeh(n) KWEE | well done |

Mid-meal, the server may ask *Tout va bien ?* ("Is everything all right?"). The all-purpose answer: *Très bien, merci !* — or, to compliment properly, *C'est délicieux !* (say day-lee-SYUH — "It's delicious!").

Special needs, stated simply:

- *Je suis végétarien / végétarienne.* (vay-zhay-tah-RYEH(N) / -RYEHN) — "I'm vegetarian." (masculine/feminine)
- *Je suis allergique aux noix / au gluten.* (ah-lehr-ZHEEK oh NWAH / oh glew-TEHN) — "I'm allergic to nuts / gluten."
- *Sans fromage, s'il vous plaît.* (sah(n) froh-MAHZH) — "Without cheese, please."

## Getting the bill (they won't bring it unless you ask)

Here's the cultural point that confuses every visitor: in France, bringing the bill unrequested is considered rude — as if the restaurant were pushing you out. The table is yours for the evening, so when you're ready, *you* must ask:

- ***L'addition, s'il vous plaît.*** (lah-dee-SYO(N) seel voo PLEH) — "The bill, please." The universal line; a subtle air-signature gesture works from across a noisy terrace too.
- *On peut payer, s'il vous plaît ?* (oh(n) puh pay-YAY) — "Can we pay, please?"
- *On peut payer séparément ?* (say-pah-ray-MAH(N)) — "Can we pay separately?" Common and accepted, though some places prefer one payment.
- *Vous prenez la carte ?* (voo pruh-NAY lah KART) — "Do you take cards?" (Almost always yes, including contactless.)

**Tipping:** service is legally included in French prices (*service compris*). Nobody expects an American-style 20%. Rounding up or leaving a euro or two for good service is a kind gesture, not an obligation.

Then the exit ritual mirrors the entrance: *Merci, au revoir ! Bonne soirée !* — thank you, goodbye, have a good evening. ## The bakery quick-script

Because half your meals in France will honestly come from a *boulangerie*:

> — **Bonjour ! Je voudrais une baguette et deux croissants, s'il vous plaît.**
> — Et avec ceci ? (ay ah-vek suh-SEE) — "Anything else?" (bakery ritual phrase)
> — **C'est tout, merci.** (say TOO) — "That's all, thanks."
> — Ça fait quatre euros cinquante. — "That's €4.50."
> — **Voilà. Merci, bonne journée !**

Four lines, endlessly reusable. *Et avec ceci ?* is so ritualized that hearing it should trigger your *c'est tout, merci* automatically.

## FAQ

**What is the most polite way to order food in French?**

*Je voudrais…* ("I would like…") followed by the item and *s'il vous plaît* is polite in every setting: *Je voudrais le poulet, s'il vous plaît.* French speakers themselves often say *je vais prendre…* ("I'll have…"), which is equally acceptable. Avoid *je veux* ("I want") — it sounds demanding.

**How do you ask for the bill in French?**

*L'addition, s'il vous plaît* (lah-dee-SYO(N) seel voo PLEH). Crucially, you must ask — French servers deliberately don't bring the bill until requested, because rushing guests is considered poor manners. Catch the server's eye, say the phrase, and it arrives.

**Do you tip in French restaurants?**

Service is included by law in the listed prices (*service compris*), so tipping is optional. Locals typically round up or leave small change — a euro or two at a café, perhaps five for excellent service at a nice restaurant. No one will chase you down for not tipping.

**What does "entrée" mean in France?**

The starter — not the main course. It comes from *entrer* (to enter): the dish that opens the meal. The main course is *le plat* or *le plat principal*. Ordering an *entrée* expecting an American-sized main is the most classic menu misunderstanding in France.

**Is it rude to order in English in France?**

Not rude, but starting with *bonjour* and attempting even one line of French changes the whole interaction — servers soften visibly and will often meet you halfway in English. The worst move is launching into English with no greeting; the *bonjour* matters more than the language that follows.
