The essential French question words are *qui* (KEE — who), *que/quoi* (KUH/KWAH — what), *où* (OO — where), *quand* (KAH(N) — when), *pourquoi* (poor-KWAH — why), *comment* (koh-MAH(N) — how), *combien* (koh(m)-BYEH(N) — how much/many), and *quel* (KEHL — which). Learn these eight and you can ask for directions, prices, times, reasons, and names — which is to say, you can survive.

What makes French questions feel tricky isn't the words — it's that French has **three different ways to build a question** with them. The secret every learner deserves to hear early: you only need the easy way to be understood everywhere. Here's the full toolkit, from survival level to polish.

## The eight question words, with pronunciation

| French | Pronunciation | English | Example |
|--------|---------------|---------|---------|
| qui | KEE | who | *Qui est-ce ?* — "Who is it?" |
| que / quoi | KUH / KWAH | what | *Tu fais quoi ?* — "What are you doing?" |
| où | OO | where | *Où sont les toilettes ?* — "Where are the toilets?" |
| quand | KAH(N) | when | *Tu arrives quand ?* — "When do you arrive?" |
| pourquoi | poor-KWAH | why | *Pourquoi pas ?* — "Why not?" |
| comment | koh-MAH(N) | how | *Comment ça marche ?* — "How does it work?" |
| combien | koh(m)-BYEH(N) | how much / many | *C'est combien ?* — "How much is it?" |
| quel(le)(s) | KEHL | which / what | *Quelle heure est-il ?* — "What time is it?" |

Pronunciation flags: *qui* and *quand* start with a clean hard "k" — the *qu* is never "kw" as in English "quick." *Où* is just "oo" (the accent distinguishes it from *ou*, "or" — a spelling detail, not a sound change). And *quand*'s nasal "ah(n)" means the *d* is silent — until liaison: *quand est-ce que…* sounds like "kah(n)-TESS-kuh," the *d* surfacing as a *t*.

## The three ways to ask any question

Take one question — "Where do you live?" — and watch it wear three outfits:

- **Intonation (spoken, casual):** *Tu habites où ?* (tew ah-BEET OO) — statement word order, question word at the end, rising voice. This is how French people actually talk.
- **Est-ce que (neutral, always safe):** *Où est-ce que tu habites ?* (oo ess-kuh tew ah-BEET). The magic phrase *est-ce que* (literally "is it that") glues a question word to a normal sentence. Works everywhere, spoken or written.
- **Inversion (formal, written):** *Où habites-tu ?* (oo ah-beet-TEW) — verb and pronoun swap places, joined by a hyphen. Elegant, standard in writing, and slightly stiff in casual speech.

**Teacher's tip:** as a learner, build your questions with **est-ce que** by default. It's never wrong, never rude, and it lets you keep normal word order — question word + *est-ce que* + plain sentence. *Pourquoi est-ce que tu ris ?* ("Why are you laughing?"), *Quand est-ce qu'on mange ?* ("When do we eat?"). Fluency in casual intonation questions comes free later, just from listening.

## Que vs quoi: the "what" problem

French splits English "what" in two, and the rule is about position:

- ***Que*** (or *qu'* before a vowel) goes at the **front**: *Que fais-tu ?* / *Qu'est-ce que tu fais ?* — "What are you doing?"
- ***Quoi*** goes at the **end** (or stands alone): *Tu fais quoi ?* — "You're doing what?" And solo: *Quoi ?!* — "What?!"

The single most useful chunk in all of spoken French is built from this pair: ***Qu'est-ce que…*** (kess-kuh) — literally "what is it that…" — the all-purpose "what" opener. *Qu'est-ce que c'est ?* (kess-kuh SAY) — "What is that?" — deserves to be memorized as one welded-together sound, because you will say it constantly.

One refinement for politeness: *quoi* alone as "what?/pardon?" is fine among friends but brusque with strangers — the polite versions are *pardon ?* or *comment ?* (Yes, "how?" doubles as "pardon?" — very French.)

## Quel: the "which" that agrees

*Quel* means "which" or "what" before a noun — and, being an adjective, it agrees with that noun's gender and number:

| Form | Used with | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| quel | masculine singular | *Quel jour ?* — "Which day?" |
| quelle | feminine singular | *Quelle heure est-il ?* — "What time is it?" |
| quels | masculine plural | *Quels livres ?* — "Which books?" |
| quelles | feminine plural | *Quelles chaussures ?* — "Which shoes?" |

All four are pronounced identically — KEHL — so this is purely a writing concern. The pattern to internalize: when English "what" sits directly before a noun (*what time, what color, what name*), French wants *quel*, not *que*: *Quel est ton nom ?* — "What is your name?"

## The questions you'll actually ask

Survival French is mostly these, ready to deploy:

- *Où sont les toilettes ?* (oo soh(n) lay twah-LEHT) — "Where are the toilets?"
- *C'est combien ?* (say koh(m)-BYEH(N)) — "How much is it?" — see [French numbers](/amelie/blog/french-numbers/) to survive the answer.
- *Quelle heure est-il ?* (kehl uhr eh-TEEL) — "What time is it?"
- *Comment tu t'appelles ?* (koh-mah(n) tew tah-PELL) — "What's your name?" (literally "how do you call yourself" — French asks *how*, not *what*)
- *Comment dit-on… en français ?* (koh-mah(n) dee-TOH(N)) — "How do you say… in French?" — the learner's best friend.
- *Qui est-ce ?* (kee ESS) — "Who is it?"
- *Quand est-ce que ça ouvre ?* (kah(n) tess-kuh sah OO-vruh) — "When does it open?"
- *Pourquoi ?* — "Why?" — and its charming cousin *Pourquoi pas ?* — "Why not?"

Notice how many everyday questions are also greetings and small talk — *Comment ça va ?* ("How's it going?") is question words in action, covered from the social angle in [French greetings](/amelie/blog/french-greetings/). And most answers will lean on the same dozen verbs, so keep [the most common French verbs](/amelie/blog/common-french-verbs/) within reach.

## A rapid-fire dialogue

Two friends planning dinner — every question word earning its keep, in the casual register:

> — **On mange où ce soir ?** — "Where are we eating tonight?"
> — Chez Luigi. — "At Luigi's."
> — **C'est quoi, Chez Luigi ?** — "What's Chez Luigi?"
> — Un italien. — "An Italian place."
> — **On y va comment ?** — "How do we get there?"
> — À pied. — "On foot."
> — **Et on y va quand ?** — "And when are we going?"
> — À vingt heures. — "At 8 p.m."
> — **C'est combien, à peu près ?** — "How much is it, roughly?"
> — Vingt euros. **Pourquoi ?** — "Twenty euros. Why?"
> — Pour rien. **Qui** d'autre vient ? — "No reason. Who else is coming?"

Six question words in eleven lines — that's a normal French conversation. Note how every question uses the casual end-position pattern (*où*, *quoi*, *comment*, *quand*, *combien* after the verb): pure spoken French.

## FAQ

**What are the main question words in French?**

Eight cover nearly everything: *qui* (who), *que/quoi* (what), *où* (where), *quand* (when), *pourquoi* (why), *comment* (how), *combien* (how much/many), and *quel/quelle* (which/what before a noun). With these plus *est-ce que* to build the sentence, you can form any everyday question.

**What does "est-ce que" literally mean, and do I need it?**

Literally "is it that…" — but treat it as a question-making particle with no real translation. You don't strictly need it (intonation alone works in speech), but it's the learner's safest tool: put a question word before it, a normal sentence after it, and the grammar takes care of itself.

**What's the difference between que and quoi?**

Position. *Que* starts a question (*Que veux-tu ?* — "What do you want?"); *quoi* ends one or stands alone (*Tu veux quoi ?* / *Quoi ?*). Same meaning, different slots. In casual speech, the *quoi*-at-the-end pattern dominates; in writing, *que* and *qu'est-ce que* take over.

**Why do the French say "comment" for "what's your name"?**

French asks *how* you are called, not *what* your name is: *Comment tu t'appelles ?* (literally "how do you call yourself?"). English and French simply chose different question words for the same idea. If you want the *quel* version, it exists too: *Quel est ton nom ?* — grammatical, just less common in conversation.

**Is "quoi ?" rude when I didn't hear someone?**

With friends, it's fine — the equivalent of "what?" With strangers or in formal settings, it lands as abrupt. The polite options are *pardon ?* (pahr-DOH(N)) or *comment ?* — and if you need a full sentence: *Vous pouvez répéter, s'il vous plaît ?* ("Can you repeat, please?").
