The most common French verbs are *être* (to be), *avoir* (to have), *aller* (to go), and *faire* (to do/make) — four irregular workhorses that appear in a huge share of everything French people say. Master those four plus the twenty-one verbs below, and you can build the majority of everyday sentences: what you want, where you're going, what you're doing, what you like.

This guide gives you the full present-tense conjugation of the big four, the pattern that unlocks regular verbs, and the complete top-25 list with pronunciation and a real example for each. A register note before we start: in spoken Parisian French, *nous* is largely replaced by *on* (oh(n)) — *on va au cinéma* ("we're going to the movies") — which conveniently takes the same verb form as *il/elle*. One less ending to memorize.

## The big four: être, avoir, aller, faire

These four are irregular, unavoidable, and — bonus — they're also the machinery behind other tenses (*j'ai mangé*, *je vais partir*). Learn them cold.

### Être — to be (EH-truh)

| Person | Form | Pronunciation |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | suis | zhuh SWEE |
| tu | es | tew EH |
| il/elle/on | est | eel EH |
| nous | sommes | noo SOHM |
| vous | êtes | voo ZEHT |
| ils/elles | sont | eel SOH(N) |

*Je suis fatigué* (zhuh swee fah-tee-GAY) — "I'm tired," the most Parisian sentence in existence. Note that *es* and *est* sound identical: "eh."

### Avoir — to have (ah-VWAHR)

| Person | Form | Pronunciation |
|--------|------|---------------|
| j' | ai | zhay |
| tu | as | tew AH |
| il/elle/on | a | eel AH |
| nous | avons | noo zah-VOH(N) |
| vous | avez | voo zah-VAY |
| ils/elles | ont | eel ZOH(N) |

*Avoir* hides a trap for English speakers: French *has* hunger, thirst, and age rather than *being* them. *J'ai faim* (zhay FEH(N)) — "I'm hungry." *J'ai soif* — "I'm thirsty." *J'ai trente ans* — "I'm thirty." Saying *je suis faim* is the classic beginner error — it literally means "I am hunger."

### Aller — to go (ah-LAY)

| Person | Form | Pronunciation |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | vais | zhuh VAY |
| tu | vas | tew VAH |
| il/elle/on | va | eel VAH |
| nous | allons | noo zah-LOH(N) |
| vous | allez | voo zah-LAY |
| ils/elles | vont | eel VOH(N) |

*Aller* does double duty: movement (*je vais à Paris*) and the near future (*je vais manger* — "I'm going to eat"), which is how French people actually talk about future plans in conversation. It's also the verb inside *ça va* — literally "it goes."

### Faire — to do / to make (FEHR)

| Person | Form | Pronunciation |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | fais | zhuh FEH |
| tu | fais | tew FEH |
| il/elle/on | fait | eel FEH |
| nous | faisons | noo fuh-ZOH(N) |
| vous | faites | voo FEHT |
| ils/elles | font | eel FOH(N) |

*Faire* is everywhere idioms live: *il fait beau* (the weather's nice), *faire du sport* (to exercise), *faire les courses* (to do the grocery shopping), *ça fait combien ?* (how much is it?). When in doubt about which verb an activity takes, *faire* is a solid bet.

## The -er pattern: one template, thousands of verbs

Roughly 90% of French verbs end in **-er** and conjugate exactly like *parler* (to speak):

| Person | Ending | Example | Pronunciation |
|--------|--------|---------|---------------|
| je | -e | parle | zhuh PARL |
| tu | -es | parles | tew PARL |
| il/elle/on | -e | parle | eel PARL |
| nous | -ons | parlons | noo par-LOH(N) |
| vous | -ez | parlez | voo par-LAY |
| ils/elles | -ent | parlent | eel PARL |

Here's the wonderful secret the table hides: **four of the six forms sound identical.** *Parle, parles, parle, parlent* — all simply "PARL," because those endings are silent (the *-ent* verb ending is always, 100% silent — more on that in my guide to [French pronunciation](/amelie/blog/french-pronunciation/)). Out loud, a regular -er verb has only three forms to learn: PARL, par-LOH(N), par-LAY. French conjugation is dramatically easier spoken than written.

Every -er verb in the list below — *aimer, parler, donner, trouver, penser* and friends — follows this exact template.

Three usage notes worth their weight in croissants:

- **Savoir vs connaître** both mean "to know," but *savoir* is for facts and skills (*je sais nager* — I know how to swim) while *connaître* is for people and places (*je connais Marie*). English merges them; French never does.
- **Prendre is the food verb.** At a café you *take* your order: *je prends un croissant*. It sounds odd in English and perfectly natural in French — you'll hear it at every table around you.
- **Trouver means "to think" in opinions.** *Je trouve ça cher* — "I find that expensive" — is how Parisians deliver judgments, and they deliver many.

## How to actually learn these (without conjugation tables haunting your dreams)

- **Learn je/tu/on forms first.** Real conversation is overwhelmingly "I," "you," and "we/one." *Nous* forms can wait — spoken French barely uses them.
- **Drill out loud, not on paper.** Since four written forms of -er verbs share one sound, writing drills overstate the difficulty. Say full sentences: *je parle, tu parles, on parle*.
- **Steal the sentence, not the verb.** Memorize *je prends un café*, not "prendre = to take." Verbs stick when they're attached to situations you'll actually be in.
- **Use each verb in a real exchange within 24 hours.** A verb you've deployed in conversation is a verb you own; a verb you've only conjugated is a verb you'll relearn. ## FAQ

**What are the most common French verbs?**

The top four by frequency are *être* (to be), *avoir* (to have), *aller* (to go), and *faire* (to do/make) — all irregular, all essential. Rounding out the top ten: *dire* (to say), *pouvoir* (can), *vouloir* (to want), *savoir* (to know), *voir* (to see), and *devoir* (must). These ten appear constantly in everyday speech, which is exactly why they've stayed irregular — words used that often resist simplification.

**How many French verbs do I need to know to have a conversation?**

Around 25–50 well-chosen verbs cover basic daily conversation, and the 25 in this guide are the core. Combined with the near-future trick (*aller* + infinitive: *je vais manger*), you can talk about present and future plans without learning any new conjugations. Depth beats breadth: knowing *faire* and its twenty idioms outperforms knowing a hundred rare verbs.

**Why are the most common French verbs irregular?**

High-frequency words get worn smooth by centuries of daily use and resist the regularizing pressure that tidies up rarer verbs — the same reason English kept "is/was/been" and "go/went." The practical upshot: memorize the big four individually, then relax, because the overwhelming majority of remaining verbs follow the regular -er pattern.

**Do I need to learn all six conjugation forms?**

On paper yes, out loud barely. For regular -er verbs, *je/tu/il/ils* forms are pronounced identically, and spoken French replaces *nous* with *on* (which uses the *il* form) — so in conversation you're really juggling three sounds per verb. Prioritize *je*, *tu*, and *on*: they carry almost all real dialogue.

**What's the difference between savoir and connaître?**

Both translate as "to know," but they split the territory. *Savoir* covers facts, information, and skills: *je sais où c'est* (I know where it is), *elle sait conduire* (she knows how to drive). *Connaître* covers familiarity with people, places, and things: *je connais Paul*, *tu connais Montmartre ?* Rule of thumb: if you could replace "know" with "be familiar with," use *connaître*.
