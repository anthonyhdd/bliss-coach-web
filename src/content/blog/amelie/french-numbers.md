French numbers from 1 to 69 work much like English — learn 1–16, then build the tens. Then, at 70, French famously goes rogue: 70 is *soixante-dix* ("sixty-ten"), 80 is *quatre-vingts* ("four-twenties"), and 90 is *quatre-vingt-dix* ("four-twenties-ten"), so 99 comes out as *quatre-vingt-dix-neuf* — literally "four-twenty-ten-nine."

Yes, really. And yes, every learner has stood frozen at a market stall doing that arithmetic while the vendor waits. The good news: the system is completely regular once you see the logic, and this guide walks you from *un* to *cent* with pronunciation for every step.

## Numbers 1–16: the ones you simply memorize

These sixteen are the foundation — everything above them is assembled from these parts.

| # | French | Pronunciation | # | French | Pronunciation |
|---|--------|---------------|---|--------|---------------|
| 1 | un | uh(n) | 9 | neuf | NUHF |
| 2 | deux | DUH | 10 | dix | DEES |
| 3 | trois | TRWAH | 11 | onze | OH(N)Z |
| 4 | quatre | KAH-truh | 12 | douze | DOOZ |
| 5 | cinq | SAH(N)K | 13 | treize | TREHZ |
| 6 | six | SEES | 14 | quatorze | kah-TORZ |
| 7 | sept | SEHT | 15 | quinze | KAH(N)Z |
| 8 | huit | WEET | 16 | seize | SEHZ |

A pronunciation quirk worth knowing early: **six, huit, and dix change their sound depending on what follows.** Alone or at the end of a phrase, the final consonant is pronounced (*j'en ai six* — "SEES"). Before a noun starting with a consonant, it goes silent: *six croissants* = "see krwah-SAH(N)." Before a vowel, it liaises: *six ans* = "see-ZAH(N)." The nasal "(n)" respellings mean the vowel goes through your nose and the *n* itself is never pronounced — the full mechanics are in my guide to [French pronunciation](/amelie/blog/french-pronunciation/).

## 17–69: the regular zone

From 17, French starts compounding, and it stays pleasantly logical until 69.

**17–19** are "ten-seven," "ten-eight," "ten-nine":

- 17 *dix-sept* (dee-SEHT), 18 *dix-huit* (deez-WEET), 19 *dix-neuf* (deez-NUHF)

**The tens:**

| # | French | Pronunciation |
|---|--------|---------------|
| 20 | vingt | VAH(N) |
| 30 | trente | TRAH(N)T |
| 40 | quarante | kah-RAH(N)T |
| 50 | cinquante | sah(n)-KAH(N)T |
| 60 | soixante | swah-SAH(N)T |

**Combining** works like English, with two rules:

1. **21, 31, 41, 51, 61 insert "et"** (and): *vingt et un* (vah(n) tay UH(N)), *trente et un*, *soixante et un*. No hyphen-and-no-et for anything else.
2. **Everything else just hyphenates:** 22 *vingt-deux* (vah(n)t-DUH), 35 *trente-cinq* (trah(n)t-SAH(N)K), 47 *quarante-sept*, 58 *cinquante-huit*, 69 *soixante-neuf*.

Note that *vingt* alone is "VAH(N)" — the *gt* is fully silent — but in 22–29 the *t* wakes up: *vingt-trois* = "vah(n)t-TRWAH."

So far, so sensible. Now take a breath.

## 70–99: welcome to French math

Here's the system that launched a thousand memes. Instead of inventing words for 70, 80, and 90, standard French builds them from arithmetic:

| # | French | Literally | Pronunciation |
|---|--------|-----------|---------------|
| 70 | soixante-dix | sixty-ten | swah-sah(n)t-DEES |
| 71 | soixante et onze | sixty and eleven | swah-sah(n) tay OH(N)Z |
| 75 | soixante-quinze | sixty-fifteen | swah-sah(n)t-KAH(N)Z |
| 80 | quatre-vingts | four-twenties | kah-truh-VAH(N) |
| 81 | quatre-vingt-un | four-twenty-one | kah-truh-vah(n)-UH(N) |
| 85 | quatre-vingt-cinq | four-twenty-five | kah-truh-vah(n)-SAH(N)K |
| 90 | quatre-vingt-dix | four-twenties-ten | kah-truh-vah(n)-DEES |
| 91 | quatre-vingt-onze | four-twenty-eleven | kah-truh-vah(n)-OH(N)Z |
| 95 | quatre-vingt-quinze | four-twenty-fifteen | kah-truh-vah(n)-KAH(N)Z |
| 99 | quatre-vingt-dix-neuf | four-twenty-ten-nine | kah-truh-vah(n)-deez-NUHF |

The trick that makes it click: **70–79 recycles the teens on top of sixty** (70 = 60+10, 71 = 60+11, 75 = 60+15... up to 79 = 60+19 *soixante-dix-neuf*), and **90–99 recycles the teens on top of eighty** (91 = 80+11, 95 = 80+15). Once your brain accepts "the seventies and nineties are teen-numbers stacked on a base," the whole block becomes two patterns instead of thirty words.

Three details for correctness:

- **71 takes "et"** (*soixante et onze*) but **81 and 91 don't** (*quatre-vingt-un*, *quatre-vingt-onze*). French giveth logic, French taketh away.
- **Quatre-vingts has an -s only when nothing follows:** *quatre-vingts* (80) but *quatre-vingt-trois* (83). Purely a spelling matter — you can't hear it.
- Why does this system exist? It's a leftover from an old **base-20 counting system** (likely Celtic in origin) that medieval French used widely — Paris still has the *Hôpital des Quinze-Vingts*, the "Fifteen-Twenties" hospital, named for its 300 beds.

**Teacher's tip:** Belgium and Switzerland quietly fixed this — *septante* (70), *nonante* (90), and in much of Switzerland *huitante* (80). Every French person understands these words, but in France using them marks you as Belgian or Swiss. For Parisian French, do the math like the locals.

## 100 and beyond, briefly

100 is **cent** (SAH(N)), and life becomes easy again: 101 *cent un*, 150 *cent cinquante*, 200 *deux cents*, 1000 *mille* (MEEL). The 70/80/90 logic carries into big numbers — 1999 was famously *mille neuf cent quatre-vingt-dix-neuf*, a small poem of arithmetic.

## Where numbers ambush you in real life

Knowing the list is one thing; catching numbers at native speed is another. These are the four situations to train for:

- **Prices.** *Ça fait dix-neuf quatre-vingt-quinze* — "that's 19.95." French drops the word "euros" mid-price, exactly as English drops "dollars." The killer combo is prices in the 70–99 range: 95 € = *quatre-vingt-quinze euros*.
- **Phone numbers.** French phone numbers are read in **pairs**: 06 12 84 71 93 comes out as *zéro six, douze, quatre-vingt-quatre, soixante et onze, quatre-vingt-treize*. Every pair from 70–99 forces the arithmetic — this is the final boss of French numbers, and it's worth drilling on its own.
- **Times and dates.** *Le train part à dix-huit heures quarante-cinq* — the train leaves at 18:45. France uses the 24-hour clock in all official contexts, so hours run to *vingt-trois* (23).
- **Ages and years.** *J'ai vingt-huit ans* ("I'm 28" — with *avoir*, never *être*), *en deux mille vingt-six* (in 2026).

Once you can produce a price and understand a phone number, you're functionally numerate in French — which pairs nicely with the café and shopping lines in my list of [the 100 most common French phrases](/amelie/blog/common-french-phrases/).

## How to practice (so 90 stops requiring a pause)

- **Drill the two hard blocks separately.** Spend a week just on 70–79 and 90–99. Count aloud: *soixante-dix, soixante et onze, soixante-douze...* until the stacking feels mechanical, not mathematical.
- **Read numbers around you in French.** License plates, prices, page numbers, your phone's clock. Ten micro-reps a day beat one long session.
- **Practice hearing, not just saying.** Have someone (or an app, or a French YouTube video) fire random prices at you and write down the digits. Production and recognition are separate skills — most learners can *say* *quatre-vingt-quinze* long before they can *catch* it at speed.
- **Say your own phone number in French until it's automatic.** It's the number sequence you'll produce most often in real life, and it forces the pair-reading habit.

Numbers are also a perfect early milestone: they're a closed system you can fully master in a couple of weeks, which does wonders for momentum — a theme I expand on in [how long it takes to learn French](/amelie/blog/how-long-to-learn-french/).

## FAQ

**Why is 80 "quatre-vingts" in French?**

It's a fossil of a base-20 (vigesimal) counting system, probably inherited from Celtic languages, in which people counted in scores: one-twenty, two-twenties, four-twenties. Medieval French used many such forms — Paris's Quinze-Vingts hospital ("fifteen-twenties" = 300) preserves one. Over the centuries, French standardized on base-10 words for most numbers but kept the old forms for 70, 80, and 90.

**Do all French speakers say soixante-dix and quatre-vingt-dix?**

No — Belgium and Switzerland use the simpler *septante* (70) and *nonante* (90), and much of Switzerland says *huitante* (80). All French speakers understand these variants, but in France they immediately mark you as Belgian or Swiss. If your target is the French of Paris, learn the *soixante-dix / quatre-vingts / quatre-vingt-dix* system — it's what you'll hear in every price and phone number there.

**What's the hardest part of French numbers for English speakers?**

Not saying them — hearing them. Numbers 70–99 arrive as multi-word arithmetic at full native speed (*quatre-vingt-quatorze* = 94), and French phone numbers, read in pairs, are a relentless stream of exactly these. The fix is dedicated listening practice: have random prices and phone numbers read to you and transcribe the digits until decoding becomes instant.

**When do six, huit, and dix change pronunciation?**

Three cases. Alone or at the end of a sentence, pronounce the final consonant: *j'en veux six* ("SEES"). Before a consonant, drop it: *six mois* = "see MWAH." Before a vowel, it liaises — s and x become a z sound: *six ans* = "see-ZAH(N)," *dix heures* = "dee-ZUHR." The same three-way rule covers *huit* (WEET / wee / wee-t).

**Is there an easy trick for 70–99?**

Think of it as two patterns rather than thirty numbers. The seventies are "sixty + teens": 72 = *soixante-douze* (60+12), 79 = *soixante-dix-neuf* (60+19). The nineties are "four-twenties + teens": 92 = *quatre-vingt-douze* (80+12), 99 = *quatre-vingt-dix-neuf* (80+19). The eighties are just "four-twenties + units" like a normal decade. Two stacking rules, one memorized base — that's the entire hard zone.
