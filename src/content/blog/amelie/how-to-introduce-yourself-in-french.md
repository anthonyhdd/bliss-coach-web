To introduce yourself in French, say *Je m'appelle...* (zhuh mah-PEHL — "My name is...") followed by your name, or simply *Je suis...* (zhuh SWEE — "I am..."). Add *Enchanté* (ah(n)-shah(n)-TAY — "Nice to meet you") — or *Enchantée* if you're a woman — and you've completed a perfectly correct French introduction.

That's the two-second version. The full version — the one that carries you through a dinner party, a hotel check-in, or a first conversation with a Parisian — needs about fifteen phrases and one crucial decision: formal or casual. Let's build it properly.

## The three ways to say your name

| French | Pronunciation | Literally | When to use |
|--------|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Je m'appelle Marie | zhuh mah-pehl mah-REE | "I call myself Marie" | The universal default — works everywhere |
| Je suis Marie | zhuh swee mah-REE | "I am Marie" | Neutral, slightly quicker |
| Moi c'est Marie | mwah say mah-REE | "Me, it's Marie" | Casual — parties, peers, very Parisian |

*Je m'appelle* is the form every textbook teaches, and rightly so: it's never wrong. But listen to actual Parisians at a *soirée* and you'll mostly hear **moi c'est** — someone says *"Moi c'est Léa,"* you answer *"Moi c'est Tom."* It's warm, symmetrical, and instantly casual.

To ask the question back:

- **Comment vous appelez-vous ?** (koh-mah(n) voo zah-play VOO) — formal
- **Comment tu t'appelles ?** (koh-mah(n) tew tah-PEHL) — casual
- **Et vous ? / Et toi ?** (ay VOO / ay TWAH) — "And you?" — the effortless way to return any question

**Teacher's tip:** *Enchanté(e)* covers "nice to meet you" in every register. The extra *-e* is purely written — *enchanté* and *enchantée* sound identical, so speak freely and only worry about the spelling in texts.

## The full self-introduction toolkit

After your name, French small talk follows a predictable script: where you're from, what you do, and — for learners — the inevitable conversation about your French. Here's each block.

### Where you're from

| French | Pronunciation | English |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| Je viens des États-Unis | zhuh vyeh(n) day zay-tah-zew-NEE | I come from the United States |
| Je suis américain(e) | zhuh swee zah-may-ree-KEH(N) | I'm American |
| Je suis anglais(e) / canadien(ne) | zhuh swee zah(n)-GLEH / kah-nah-DYEH(N) | I'm English / Canadian |
| J'habite à Paris | zhah-beet ah pah-REE | I live in Paris |
| Je suis de passage | zhuh swee duh pah-SAHZH | I'm just visiting |

Note the feminine forms: a woman says *américaine* (ah-may-ree-KEHN — the final *n* is pronounced), *anglaise* (ah(n)-GLEHZ), *canadienne*. This is that silent-final-consonant rule doing its gender-marking work — the mechanics are in my guide to [French pronunciation](/amelie/blog/french-pronunciation/).

### What you do

| French | Pronunciation | English |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| Je travaille dans... | zhuh trah-VY dah(n) | I work in... (a field) |
| Je suis ingénieur / prof / étudiant(e) | zhuh swee... | I'm an engineer / teacher / student |
| Qu'est-ce que vous faites dans la vie ? | kess-kuh voo FEHT dah(n) lah VEE | What do you do for a living? (formal) |
| Tu fais quoi dans la vie ? | tew feh KWAH dah(n) lah VEE | What do you do? (casual) |

A lovely quirk: French drops the article before professions. It's *je suis médecin* — "I am doctor," not "I am *a* doctor." Saying *je suis un médecin* marks you as a learner immediately, so enjoy the shortcut.

### Talking about your French

These four lines are your survival kit — they buy you patience, slower speech, and goodwill in every conversation:

- **J'apprends le français** (zhah-PRAH(N) luh frah(n)-SEH) — I'm learning French
- **Je parle un peu français** (zhuh parl uh(n) puh frah(n)-SEH) — I speak a little French
- **Vous pouvez parler plus lentement ?** (voo poo-vay par-lay plew lah(n)-tuh-MAH(N)) — Can you speak more slowly?
- **Comment dit-on... en français ?** (koh-mah(n) dee-TOH(N)... ah(n) frah(n)-SEH) — How do you say... in French?

More of these conversation-rescue lines live in my list of [the 100 most common French phrases](/amelie/blog/common-french-phrases/).

## Formal introduction: the complete dialogue

Picture a professional context — a meeting, a language exchange with someone older, checking in with a host. Everything runs on *vous*.

> — **Bonjour, je me présente : je m'appelle Sarah Miller.**
> (bohn-ZHOOR, zhuh muh pray-ZAH(N)T : zhuh mah-pehl...)
> "Hello, let me introduce myself: my name is Sarah Miller."
>
> — Enchanté, madame. Moi, c'est Laurent Dubois. Vous êtes américaine ?
> "Nice to meet you. I'm Laurent Dubois. Are you American?"
>
> — **Oui, je viens de Chicago, mais j'habite à Paris depuis deux mois. Et vous, vous êtes d'ici ?**
> "Yes, I'm from Chicago, but I've lived in Paris for two months. And you, are you from here?"
>
> — Oui, parisien depuis toujours. Qu'est-ce que vous faites dans la vie ?
> "Yes, Parisian all my life. What do you do?"
>
> — **Je travaille dans le marketing. Et j'apprends le français — soyez indulgent !**
> (swah-yay zeh(n)-dewl-ZHAH(N)) "I work in marketing. And I'm learning French — be kind!"

That last line — *soyez indulgent* — always gets a laugh, and a French person who laughs with you will slow down for you.

Note *je me présente* (zhuh muh pray-ZAH(N)T), "allow me to introduce myself": a polished opener for professional settings, emails, and any moment you're introducing yourself unprompted.

## Casual introduction: the party version

Same information, completely different music. Everything runs on *tu*, sentences shrink, and *moi c'est* replaces *je m'appelle*.

> — **Salut ! Moi c'est Tom.** (sah-LU ! mwah say TOM)
> "Hi! I'm Tom."
>
> — Salut Tom, moi c'est Chloé. Ça va ?
> "Hi Tom, I'm Chloé. How's it going?"
>
> — **Ça va ! T'es d'où ?** (sah VAH ! tay DOO)
> "Good! Where are you from?"
>
> — De Lyon, mais j'habite ici maintenant. Et toi, t'es américain ?
> "From Lyon, but I live here now. And you, are you American?"
>
> — **Ouais, de New York. Je suis là pour six mois.** (WEH, duh noo-YORK)
> "Yeah, from New York. I'm here for six months."
>
> — Trop bien ! Tu parles super bien français !
> "Cool! Your French is really good!"

Two very Parisian details hiding in there: *t'es* (TAY) is the spoken contraction of *tu es* — nobody at a party says "tu es d'où ?" — and *ouais* (WEH) is the universal casual "yeah." You don't have to produce these yet, but you absolutely need to recognize them.

**Teacher's tip:** if you've just been introduced by a friend, you can skip names entirely and open with *"Salut, ça va ?"* The name exchange happens later, if at all — very French.

## The follow-up questions to expect

Introductions are a two-way script, and freezing on the *reply* is the classic beginner failure. After you introduce yourself, expect some mix of:

- **Vous venez d'où ? / Tu viens d'où ?** — Where are you from?
- **Vous êtes en France pour longtemps ?** — Are you in France for long?
- **Ça vous plaît, Paris ?** (sah voo PLEH) — Do you like Paris? (The only correct answer is an enthusiastic *"J'adore !"* — zhah-DOR)
- **Pourquoi vous apprenez le français ?** — Why are you learning French?

Prepare a one-sentence answer to each and your first conversations stop being scary. Notice how much of this runs on just two verbs — *être* (to be) and *venir* (to come); if you want the grammar underneath, start with my guide to [the 25 most common French verbs](/amelie/blog/common-french-verbs/).

## FAQ

**What's the difference between "je m'appelle" and "je suis"?**

Both introduce your name and both are correct. *Je m'appelle* (literally "I call myself") is the standard, slightly fuller form; *je suis* is quicker and equally natural. The truly casual option is *moi c'est* + name, which is what young Parisians actually say at parties. With *je suis* you can also attach nationality or profession: *je suis canadienne*, *je suis prof*.

**Do I say enchanté or enchantée?**

They're pronounced identically (ah(n)-shah(n)-TAY), so in speech it makes no difference. In writing, a man uses *enchanté* and a woman *enchantée* — the extra *-e* is the feminine agreement. Some French people find the word slightly old-fashioned; a warm *"Bonjour !"* with a smile, or *"Ravi(e) de vous rencontrer"* (delighted to meet you) in formal settings, works just as well.

**Should I introduce myself with tu or vous?**

Default to *vous* with strangers, anyone older, and all professional contexts — it's never rude. Switch to *tu* at parties among peers, with children, or when invited (*"On se tutoie ?"* — "Shall we use tu?"). A good shortcut: if the other person opens with *salut*, you're in *tu* territory; if they open with *bonjour* plus *vous*, follow their lead.

**How do I say "Nice to meet you" without using enchanté?**

The main alternatives are *Ravi de vous rencontrer* (rah-VEE duh voo rah(n)-koh(n)-TRAY — "delighted to meet you"; *ravie* for a woman) in formal settings, and the very casual *"Ça fait plaisir"* ("it's a pleasure") among peers. In relaxed situations, French people often skip the phrase entirely — the exchange of names with a smile does the job.

**Why do French people drop the article before jobs?**

It's simply how the grammar works: professions after *être* behave like adjectives, so it's *je suis médecin*, *elle est avocate* — no *un/une*. English speakers instinctively add the article ("I am *a* doctor"), which French listeners understand perfectly but immediately recognize as a learner's reflex. Dropping it is a free, instant upgrade to your French.
