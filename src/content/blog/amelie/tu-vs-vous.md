French has two words for "you": *tu* (TEW) for one person you're close to — friends, family, children, peers — and *vous* (VOO) for strangers, professional contacts, elders, and *always* for more than one person. The safe rule is brutally simple: **when in doubt, use vous.** Being too formal reads as well-mannered; being too familiar can read as disrespect.

English dropped this distinction centuries ago (that's what "thou" was), which is why *tu* vs *vous* feels so foreign — it's not just grammar, it's a social signal baked into every sentence. The good news: the system runs on a handful of clear rules, and the French forgive learners almost everything. Let's make you confident in it.

## The rule in one table

| Situation | Use | Why |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| Friends and family | tu | Closeness is established |
| Children and teenagers (you're an adult) | tu | Adults say *tu* to kids universally |
| Fellow students, teammates, peers your age in casual settings | tu | Peer contexts default to *tu* |
| Strangers, shopkeepers, waiters, taxi drivers | vous | No established closeness |
| Your boss, clients, officials, doctors | vous | Professional respect |
| Anyone noticeably older than you | vous | Age deference — let *them* propose *tu* |
| Online gaming, hobby forums, social media replies | tu | Internet culture defaults to *tu* |
| **Any group of two or more people** | **vous** | *Vous* is always the plural, formal or not |

That last row trips people up: *vous* does double duty. Addressing your two best friends is *vous* — not out of politeness, but because they're plural. Context makes it unambiguous to French ears.

**Pronunciation while we're here:** *tu* uses the pinched French *u* — say "ee" and round your lips as for "oo." *Vous* is a plain "voo," final *s* silent. Both vowels are covered properly in my guide to [French pronunciation](/amelie/blog/french-pronunciation/).

## Why it matters more than you think

*Tu* and *vous* don't just change one word — they conjugate the entire sentence:

- *Tu as faim ?* (tew ah FEH(N)) — "Are you hungry?" (informal)
- *Vous avez faim ?* (voo za-vay FEH(N)) — same question, formal or plural
- *Comment t'appelles-tu ?* vs *Comment vous appelez-vous ?* — "What's your name?"
- *Ça va ?* works in both registers, which is one reason [asking "how are you?"](/amelie/how-to-say/how-are-you/) is such a safe opener.

The choice also carries real social weight. French even has verbs for it: ***tutoyer*** (tew-twah-YAY) — to address someone as *tu* — and ***vouvoyer*** (voo-vwah-YAY) — to address someone as *vous*. When a language needs dedicated verbs for a pronoun choice, you know the choice matters. Using *tu* with the wrong person can feel like an uninvited arm around the shoulder; clinging to *vous* with a close friend feels like shaking hands with your sister.

## The decision guide, in 30 seconds

Run these three questions in order — the first "yes" decides:

1. **Is it more than one person?** → *vous*, always, end of story.
2. **Would this person plausibly be a friend?** Same generation, casual context, first names in use, or a child? → *tu*.
3. **Everything else** — strangers, service staff, professional contacts, elders, anyone whose title you'd use? → *vous*.

A reliable shortcut: **match the register of the greeting.** If the situation calls for *bonjour* with a stranger, it calls for *vous*; if *salut* feels right, *tu* almost certainly does too. The greeting and the pronoun live in the same register — as covered in [French greetings](/amelie/blog/french-greetings/).

## Switching from vous to tu: the little ceremony

Relationships in France often begin in *vous* and graduate to *tu* — and the switch is a small, genuinely warm ritual. The standard invitation:

> — **On peut se tutoyer ?** (oh(n) puh suh tew-twah-YAY) — "Shall we switch to *tu*?"
> — **Oui, bien sûr, avec plaisir !** — "Yes, of course, with pleasure!"

Or, offered as a statement: *On se tutoie ?* / *Tu peux me tutoyer.* ("You can call me *tu*.")

The etiquette of who proposes it:

- **The senior person offers.** The older person, the boss, the professor — the one with more standing extends the invitation. Proposing *tu* upward is presumptuous.
- **Accepting is near-mandatory.** Refusing the offer is technically possible (*Je préfère qu'on se vouvoie* — "I'd rather we stay with *vous*") but it's a firm boundary-setting move.
- **Once switched, never go back.** Reverting to *vous* after *tutoiement* signals a relationship rupture — it's how French speakers put someone coldly at arm's length.

**Teacher's tip:** in many modern workplaces — startups, creative fields, among colleagues of similar level — *tu* is immediate and automatic; imposing *vous* there would feel stiff. Traditional corporations, law, medicine, and government still run on *vous*. On day one, listen for how colleagues address *you*, and mirror it.

## The mistakes, ranked by damage

**Barely matters — using vous when tu was expected.** Worst case, you sound charmingly formal, and someone smiles and says *on peut se tutoyer !* This is why *vous* is the default: the failure mode is polite.

**Noticeable — using tu with service staff or strangers.** Saying *tu* to a waiter, taxi driver, or shopkeeper you don't know reads as condescending, even though foreigners get a wide benefit of the doubt. A quick *pardon, vous…* self-correction fixes everything.

**Actually costly — using tu in formal professional settings.** With a client, an interviewer, a French administration official, or an older business contact, an unearned *tu* can genuinely undercut you. These are exactly the rooms where the *vous* reflex pays for all the practice.

**The learner's classic — mixing them mid-sentence.** *Vous pouvez me passer ton stylo ?* ("Can you [formal] pass me your [informal] pen?") French people find this endearing rather than rude, but consistency is the mark of control: pick the pronoun, and make every verb and possessive follow it (*ton/ta/tes* with *tu*, *votre/vos* with *vous*).

And one reassurance, because learners genuinely fear this: **no French person is offended by a struggling foreigner's pronoun choice.** The rules above are for sounding polished — not for avoiding disaster. The French know their system is hard; the attempt is what earns the goodwill.

## FAQ

**What's the basic difference between tu and vous?**

Both mean "you." *Tu* addresses one person informally — friends, family, kids, peers. *Vous* is for one person formally — strangers, professionals, elders — and for any group of people, formal or not. The sentence's verbs change with the choice: *tu as* but *vous avez*.

**When in doubt, should I use tu or vous?**

*Vous*, always. Excess formality is read as good manners and, at worst, gently corrected with an invitation to switch. Excess familiarity is the direction that can offend. There is no situation where a polite *vous* to a single stranger gets you in trouble.

**What do tutoyer and vouvoyer mean?**

They're the French verbs for the choice itself: *tutoyer* = to address someone as *tu*; *vouvoyer* = to address someone as *vous*. You'll hear them constantly in the negotiation: *On peut se tutoyer ?* ("Shall we use *tu*?") is the standard, friendly way to propose switching.

**Do French people use tu with waiters and shopkeepers?**

No — service interactions run on *vous* in both directions, even when everyone involved is young and the tone is friendly. A waiter says *vous* to you; you say *vous* back. Regulars at a neighborhood café may eventually graduate to *tu* with staff they've known for years, but that's earned, never assumed.

**Can I use tu on social media and in games?**

Yes — internet French defaults to *tu* almost everywhere: gaming, Twitter/X replies, Reddit, Discord, YouTube comments. Writing *vous* to one person online sounds oddly stiff, like wearing a suit to a barbecue. The exception is professional platforms like LinkedIn, where *vous* remains standard between people who don't know each other.
