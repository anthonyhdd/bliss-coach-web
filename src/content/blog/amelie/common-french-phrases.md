If you memorize the 100 phrases below, you can survive — and even enjoy — almost any everyday situation in France: ordering a coffee, asking for directions, chatting with a stranger, or handling an emergency. Each phrase comes with a rough pronunciation guide and a note on whether it's formal (*vous*) or casual (*tu*), because using the wrong register is the most common beginner mistake in French.

A quick note on the pronunciation hints: French stresses the **last syllable** of a phrase (shown in CAPS), "zh" sounds like the *s* in "measure," and "(n)" means a nasal vowel — let the air go through your nose and don't fully pronounce the *n*.

## Tu or vous? The 30-second rule

Before the phrases, one rule that governs all of them:

- **Vous** = formal or plural. Use it with strangers, waiters, shopkeepers, older people, and anyone in a professional context. When in doubt, use *vous* — it's never rude.
- **Tu** = casual and singular. Use it with friends, children, and people who invite you to (*"On se tutoie ?"* — "Shall we switch to *tu*?").

As a visitor or beginner, you'll live in *vous* territory 90% of the time. French people will find it polite, not stiff.

## How to actually memorize these 100 phrases

Don't try to swallow the list in one sitting. Here's the approach I give my students:

- **Ten a day, out loud.** Reading silently builds recognition, not speech. Say each phrase five times, exaggerating the final-syllable stress.
- **Group by situation, not alphabetically.** Your brain retrieves language by context. Rehearse the café block before you go to a café — even an American one.
- **Practice the responses too.** Knowing how to ask *"Où sont les toilettes ?"* is useless if you freeze when someone answers *"au fond à droite"* (at the back, on the right).

## FAQ

### How many French phrases do I need to travel comfortably?

Around 50–100 well-chosen phrases cover the vast majority of tourist interactions. The greetings, café, and directions sections above (60 phrases) will handle a full trip; the rest make you conversational rather than just functional.

### Should beginners default to tu or vous?

Default to *vous* with anyone you don't know. It is never offensive, while an unearned *tu* can feel presumptuous. Switch to *tu* only when invited (*"On se tutoie ?"*) or with children and close friends.

### Is French pronunciation really that hard for English speakers?

The sounds themselves are learnable in weeks; the hard part is the ones English lacks — nasal vowels, the French *u*, and the uvular *r*. The pronunciation guides above are approximations; nothing replaces hearing and repeating phrases aloud with feedback.

### What's the fastest way to move from phrases to real conversation?

Start using the phrases in unscripted exchanges as early as possible. Memorized lines are your on-ramp: once you can deploy "Je voudrais...", "Vous pouvez répéter ?" and "Comment dit-on... ?", you can keep a genuine conversation going and learn inside it.
