The essential French greetings are *bonjour* (bohn-ZHOOR — "hello," safe with everyone), *bonsoir* (bohn-SWAHR — "good evening," after roughly 6 p.m.), *salut* (sah-LU — casual "hi" for friends), and *coucou* (koo-KOO — an affectionate "hey!" reserved for people you're close to). Which one you pick matters far more than in English: greetings are where French registers its famous formality, and choosing wrong is the single fastest way to sound either rude or weirdly stiff.

Here's the good news — the rules are few, consistent, and learnable in ten minutes. Let's go through them the way I'd teach them in a first lesson.

## The four hellos, at a glance

| French | Pronunciation | English | Who it's for |
|--------|---------------|---------|--------------|
| Bonjour | bohn-ZHOOR | Hello / Good day | Everyone — the universal default |
| Bonsoir | bohn-SWAHR | Good evening | Everyone, after ~6 p.m. |
| Salut | sah-LU | Hi / Hey | Friends, family, peers your age |
| Coucou | koo-KOO | Hey you! | Close friends, partners, kids |

One thing to notice immediately: the final letters of *salut* are silent, and the nasal "on" in *bonjour* means your tongue never actually touches for the "n." If those respellings look mysterious, my guide to [the 10 French sounds English speakers get wrong](/amelie/blog/french-pronunciation/) covers nasal vowels and the French *u* in detail.

## Bonjour: the non-negotiable greeting

*Bonjour* is not just "hello" — in France it's a social contract. Walking into a bakery, a pharmacy, a small shop, or up to any stranger and launching into your request **without saying bonjour first** is genuinely considered rude. The transaction hasn't started until both parties have said it. Say *bonjour*, get a *bonjour* back, and *then* ask for your croissant.

Three rules govern it:

- **One bonjour per person per day.** If you greet the same colleague or shopkeeper a second time, saying *bonjour* again quietly implies you forgot the first meeting. The fix is *re-bonjour* (ruh-bohn-ZHOOR) — literally "re-hello" — usually said with a smile, or a simple *"re !"* among younger Parisians.
- **It works at any hour before evening.** French has no everyday "good morning" — *bon matin* is not a thing in France (Québec, yes; Paris, no). From 7 a.m. to early evening, it's *bonjour* all the way.
- **Add the title in formal settings.** *Bonjour madame* (bohn-zhoor mah-DAHM), *bonjour monsieur* (bohn-zhoor muh-SYUH). In shops and offices this tiny addition instantly reads as polite and well-brought-up.

## Bonsoir and the time-of-day switch

Around **6 p.m.** — earlier in winter when night falls, later in summer — *bonjour* hands over to *bonsoir* (bohn-SWAHR). There's no official cutoff; French people themselves hesitate at 5:45 p.m. and nobody will blink if you get it "wrong" by an hour. The switch is about arriving: *bonsoir* is how you greet someone in the evening.

Leaving is different. When you part ways, you wish the person a good rest-of-the-day:

- **Bonne journée** (bun zhoor-NAY) — "have a good day," said when *leaving*, never when arriving
- **Bonne soirée** (bun swah-RAY) — "have a good evening"
- **Bonne nuit** (bun NWEE) — "good night," strictly for someone actually going to bed. Saying *bonne nuit* to your waiter at 9 p.m. is a classic — and charming — beginner mistake.

**Teacher's tip:** the arrival/departure distinction is the whole system. *Bonjour/bonsoir* = hello. *Bonne journée/bonne soirée* = goodbye-with-good-wishes. Mixing them up is understood, but keeping them straight is what makes you sound like you've actually lived in France.

## Salut and coucou: the casual register

**Salut** (sah-LU — the *t* is silent) is the everyday "hi" between friends, classmates, family, and colleagues you're on *tu* terms with. It conveniently works for both hello *and* goodbye, like the Italian *ciao*. The rule of thumb: if you'd use *tu* with someone, *salut* is fine; if you're in *vous* territory, stick to *bonjour*. (Unsure about *tu* vs *vous*? The 30-second rule is in my guide to [the 100 most common French phrases](/amelie/blog/common-french-phrases/).)

**Coucou** (koo-KOO) is a notch warmer and more playful — think "hey you!" It's what you text a good friend, what parents say to children, what couples say to each other. Parisians in their twenties and thirties use it constantly in messages: *"Coucou, ça va ?"* Use it with your boss and you'll get a raised eyebrow; use it with a friend and you'll get a smile.

Two more you'll hear in Paris:

- **Ça va ?** (sah VAH) — "how's it going?" — often functions as the greeting itself. The beautiful thing is that the answer is the same words: *Ça va !* ("I'm good"). *"Ça va ?" "Ça va, et toi ?"* is a complete, perfectly natural exchange.
- **Quoi de neuf ?** (kwah duh NUHF) — "what's new?" — casual, friendly, expects an actual (short) answer.

## La bise: the cheek-kiss protocol

No guide to French greetings survives without *la bise* — the light cheek-to-cheek kiss (it's really a cheek touch with a kiss sound, not a kiss planted on skin) exchanged between friends, family, and often friendly acquaintances.

What you actually need to know:

- **In Paris, it's two kisses**, usually starting on the left cheek (their right). Other regions do one, three, even four — the French joke about this confusion themselves, so nobody expects a foreigner to get it right.
- **Who does it:** friends and family greeting each other; women with women, women with men, and increasingly men with men among younger generations (though many men still shake hands with each other).
- **Who doesn't:** strangers in shops, professional contexts with people you've just met, and any *vous* relationship where a handshake fits. In offices, colleagues who know each other well may *faire la bise* on arrival — follow the room.
- **As a foreigner, you have a free pass.** If someone leans in, go with it — cheek touch, kiss sound, switch sides. If you'd rather not, a warm handshake and *bonjour* is never wrong. Since 2020, plenty of French people themselves have downgraded to a wave.

**Teacher's tip:** never initiate *la bise* with someone you just met in a formal setting. Let the French person set the protocol — they always will, usually within the first two seconds.

## Saying goodbye

A quick tour of exits, from formal to casual:

| French | Pronunciation | English | Register |
|--------|---------------|---------|----------|
| Au revoir | oh ruh-VWAHR | Goodbye | Neutral — works everywhere |
| Bonne journée / soirée | bun zhoor-NAY / swah-RAY | Have a good day / evening | Neutral, very common in shops |
| À bientôt | ah byeh(n)-TOH | See you soon | Neutral |
| À demain | ah duh-MEH(N) | See you tomorrow | Neutral |
| À tout à l'heure | ah too tah LUHR | See you later (same day) | Neutral |
| À plus / À plus tard | ah PLUS / ah plew TAR | See ya / Later | Casual — texted as "A+" |
| Salut ! | sah-LU | Bye! | Casual |

The one nuance worth flagging: *à tout à l'heure* promises you'll see the person again **later the same day**. If you won't, use *à bientôt* or *à plus*.

## Putting it together

A complete polite interaction in a Parisian bakery sounds like this:

> — **Bonjour madame !** (bohn-zhoor mah-DAHM)
> — Bonjour monsieur. (the greeting contract is now fulfilled)
> — Une baguette, s'il vous plaît. (ewn bah-GEHT seel voo PLEH)
> — Voilà. Autre chose ? ("Anything else?")
> — Non merci, c'est tout. **Bonne journée !** (say TOO — bun zhoor-NAY)
> — Bonne journée, au revoir !

Greeting on arrival, good wishes on departure — that's the full ritual, and it's 80% of sounding polite in France. ## FAQ

**What is the most common French greeting?**

*Bonjour* — by an enormous margin. It works with strangers, friends, shopkeepers, and officials, at any time from morning until early evening. If you learn one French word before a trip, make it this one, and say it before every request: in France, skipping the *bonjour* is read as rudeness, not efficiency.

**What's the difference between salut and bonjour?**

Register. *Bonjour* is neutral-to-formal and safe with everyone; *salut* is casual and belongs with friends, family, and peers — the same people you'd address as *tu*. Using *salut* with a stranger or a waiter sounds overly familiar, while using *bonjour* with close friends just sounds a touch stiff, never rude. When in doubt, *bonjour* wins.

**How do you say good morning in French?**

You say *bonjour*. Metropolitan French has no separate everyday "good morning" — *bon matin* is used in Québec but sounds wrong in France. *Bonjour* covers the entire day until roughly 6 p.m., when *bonsoir* takes over for the evening.

**How many kisses is la bise in Paris?**

Two — one on each cheek, typically starting on the left. The number varies by region (one in parts of Brittany, three in the southeast, four in pockets of the north), which the French themselves find confusing. As a visitor you're not expected to know the local count; just follow the other person's lead.

**Is it rude to say coucou to someone you don't know well?**

It's not rude so much as jarringly intimate — like greeting a new colleague with "hey sweetie." *Coucou* is for close friends, partners, children, and warm text messages. With anyone you'd address as *vous*, stick to *bonjour*; with casual acquaintances, *salut* is the safe informal choice.
