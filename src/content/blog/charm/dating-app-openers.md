The openers that get replies do one thing: they make replying *easy and fun*. That means reacting to something specific in their profile, adding a little friction or tease, or asking a question with an obvious fun answer. Below: why the generic stuff dies on arrival, and 30 openers organized by style with the logic behind each one.

## Why "Hey" Dies Every Time

Put yourself in the inbox of anyone moderately attractive on a dating app. They have 15 to 50 unread messages. Eleven of them say "hey," "hi," "hey :)," or "how's your week going?"

"Hey" fails for three reasons:

1. **It's zero-effort, and effort is the first filter.** A one-word opener signals you sent the same thing to 40 people. People match energy — zero in, zero out.
2. **It transfers all the work.** "Hey" hands them the job of starting the conversation you initiated. The people worth talking to don't pick up other people's slack.
3. **It gives them nothing to react to.** A reply needs a handle — something to grab. "Hey" is a smooth wall.

Same graveyard: "how was your weekend," "wyd," and any compliment about their looks. "You're gorgeous" is information they already have, delivered by people they hear it from daily. It doesn't distinguish you; it files you.

The bar is genuinely low. A first message that shows you read their profile and have a personality clears 90% of the inbox instantly.

## Rule One: The Profile Is the Cheat Sheet

Every photo and prompt in a profile is something they *chose* to show you. That's a menu of conversation starters they pre-approved. The strongest openers pick one item off that menu and do something with it — react, tease, ask, or challenge. Not "nice dog" (an observation), but a message with a hook that demands an answer.

## Profile-Based Openers (10 Examples)

These are your highest-percentage plays. Adapt the specifics.

1. **"Okay, the [location] photo — planned that shot or just naturally photogenic in front of waterfalls?"** — Compliments the photo while teasing them about it. Answerable either way.
2. **"You said you make the best [dish they mention]. Bold claim. What's your evidence?"** — Playfully challenges a claim they made. People love defending their own hill.
3. **"Ranking your photos: dog first, you second, the hiking one third. The dog carried."** — Cheeky, structured, and shows you actually looked. Teasing beats flattery.
4. **"Your prompt says you're 'weirdly competitive.' Define weirdly. Board games or elbows-out at brunch?"** — Takes their own words and asks for the incriminating details.
5. **"[Band/artist on their profile]? Respect. But I need to know your most embarrassing music opinion to balance it out."** — Validates their taste, then asks for the fun answer instead of the résumé answer.
6. **"That's a [city] skyline in photo three. Did you live there or is this vacation-photo propaganda?"** — Specific observation plus a light accusation. Accusations (playful ones) get replies.
7. **"You listed 'good at parallel parking' as a skill. I'm going to need this notarized."** — Amplifies the small funny detail they included. They put it there hoping someone would bite.
8. **"Two truths and a lie is on your profile but you didn't play. Go — I'll guess."** — Turns their prompt into an actual game. Games get replies because they're fun to answer.
9. **"The pasta in photo four looks better than any date either of us has been on. Recipe or restaurant?"** — Self-aware humor plus an easy question. Food is the safest common ground on earth.
10. **"You said your ideal Sunday is a farmers market. Counterpoint: staying in bed. Convince me."** — Sets up a friendly debate. Debates create back-and-forth, which is the entire goal.

## Playful Teasing Openers (10 Examples)

Teasing works because it flips the default dynamic — instead of auditioning for them, you're playfully making *them* qualify. Keep it warm; you're poking, not negging.

11. **"I was going to open with something smooth but your profile says you like honesty, so: your dog is the reason I swiped."** — The bait-and-switch structure is funny, and "honesty" makes it charming instead of rude.
12. **"You seem like trouble. The fun kind, but still. Confirm or deny."** — Vague accusation + binary question. Nearly everyone confirms.
13. **"Matching with you was easy. Now the real test — do you actually reply, or do you collect matches like Pokémon?"** — Playfully calls out app behavior everyone's guilty of. Replying becomes proving you wrong.
14. **"I give this match two weeks before you're annoyed by how often I win at [game/activity from their profile]."** — Fake confidence about a future together is flirty without being heavy.
15. **"Not to be dramatic, but if you don't like [food/show they mention], this can't work. I need answers upfront."** — Mock-ultimatum. High energy, zero pressure, obvious invitation to banter.
16. **"You have big 'orders for the whole table' energy. Am I right or am I right?"** — Assigning them a persona invites correction or confession. Both are conversations.
17. **"Photo two says gym person. Photo five says pizza person. I'm here for the contradiction."** — Noticing a tension between photos proves attention and gives them a fun defense to mount.
18. **"I'd say you have great taste, but you did swipe right on me, so the jury's out."** — Self-deprecating with a compliment buried inside. Disarming.
19. **"Warning: I take [thing from their profile — trivia, mini golf, karaoke] extremely seriously. Hope you can handle losing."** — A challenge and a first-date premise in one message.
20. **"You look like you have a strong opinion about pineapple on pizza and I'm scared to hear it."** — Low-stakes controversy. Everyone has an answer loaded and ready.

## Direct Openers (10 Examples)

Sometimes the strongest move is skipping the routine entirely. Directness works because it's rare — but it has to be specific-direct, not generic-direct.

21. **"No games: your profile is the first one this week that made me stop scrolling. The [specific detail] did it."** — Directness earns its power from the specific detail. Without it, this is just another compliment.
22. **"You seem like someone worth getting a drink with. Thursday or Saturday?"** — Confident and calendar-specific. High risk, high reply-quality — the people who say yes actually show up.
23. **"I'll be honest, I'm bad at opener small talk. What's something you're excited about right now?"** — Admitting the game is tired, then asking a real question. Refreshing in a sea of bits.
24. **"Your [prompt answer] made me laugh out loud on the subway. That's rare. Hi, I'm [name]."** — A concrete compliment about their *humor* lands ten times harder than one about their face.
25. **"Let's skip the 'hey, how are you' arc. Coffee this week — best case we hit it off, worst case you get a good coffee spot rec."** — Names the boring script, then removes the risk from saying yes.
26. **"I read your whole profile, which by app standards is basically a love letter. The [detail] sold me."** — Self-aware about app culture, and the effort claim is backed with proof.
27. **"Genuine question, no bit attached: what does a great first date look like for you?"** — Signals intentionality. Filters for people who actually want to meet, which is the point.
28. **"You mention [hobby]. I've always wanted to try it. Pitch me on it — and maybe teach me sometime?"** — Curiosity plus a built-in date idea. People light up explaining what they love.
29. **"I'm not going to pretend I have a clever line. You seem interesting and I'd like to find out if that's true in person."** — The anti-opener. It works precisely because everyone else is performing.
30. **"Odds you're free for a drink this week? Give me a number, be honest."** — The odds framing turns a date ask into a game. "60%" is a yes with plausible deniability — take it.

## The Opener Is Only Move One

A great opener earns you a reply, not a date. The follow-up is where most conversations flatline: they answer your question, and you respond with "haha nice." Rule of thumb — every reply should either add something (a story, an opinion, a tease) or advance something (toward a plan). If you're three messages in and it feels like a polite interview, change the game or make the ask. ## FAQ

### What's the best opener for Hinge specifically?

Comment on their prompt answer, not their photos. Hinge prompts are written to be responded to — take their claim and challenge it, extend it, or ask for the story behind it. Photo comments on Hinge should target details (the place, the dog, the food), never their looks.

### Should I use pickup lines?

Canned lines get canned results — most people have seen the popular ones five times. A mediocre observation about *their actual profile* beats a brilliant line they've already screenshotted to their group chat. If you use one, commit to the bit fully and be ready to be clever after it lands.

### How long should a first message be?

One to three sentences. Long enough to show effort and give them a handle, short enough that replying feels light. A paragraph on message one reads as intense, no matter how good the paragraph is.

### They read my opener and didn't reply. Should I double text?

On apps, one follow-up after 3-4 days is fine — make it a fresh angle, not "hello??". If the second message also dies, move on. Unlike texting someone you've met, app matches carry near-zero investment; silence usually means the match was idle, not that you failed.
