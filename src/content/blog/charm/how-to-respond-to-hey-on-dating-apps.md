The best way to respond to a plain "hey" is to skip the reflexive "hey" back and send one specific, low-pressure question that gives them something real to answer. You're not trying to be clever — you're trying to move the conversation from a dead greeting to an actual exchange, and a single sentence does that better than a paragraph.

## Why "Hey" Is Such a Conversation Killer

A one-word "hey" isn't rude — it's just lazy, and usually nervous. The person opened the app, liked your profile, and defaulted to the safest possible message. It carries zero information, so if you mirror it back with "hey" or "hi," you've now stacked two dead ends on top of each other and someone has to do all the work of restarting.

That someone should be you, but only for one message. The goal isn't to rescue a lazy opener forever — it's to hand them an easy on-ramp and see if they take it. If they do, great. If they respond to your effort with another single word, you have your answer.

It also helps to remember that "hey" is not a rejection or a test. On busy apps people are often messaging several matches at once and hitting the fastest possible opener on each. Your specific reply is what makes you stand out in that pile — it's the difference between being one of ten "heys" and being the one message they actually stop to answer. So don't read hostility into it, and don't punish them for it. Just raise the level and watch what comes back.

## The Core Move: Reply With a Specific Hook

Look at their profile before you reply. Almost every profile has one detail worth grabbing: a photo at a specific place, a prompt answer, a hobby, a pet, a weird flex. Pull that thread instead of talking about the "hey" itself.

- **They have a hiking photo:** "Hey yourself. Okay, real question — is that a real trail or a 'stand at the trailhead for the photo' situation?"
- **Their prompt says they make the best tacos:** "Bold taco claim in your profile. I need evidence. What's the move — al pastor or nothing?"
- **They have a dog in three photos:** "Hey! Immediate priority: what's the dog's name and does it know it's the main character of your profile?"

Notice the pattern. You acknowledge the "hey" in two words, then pivot fast to something they can only answer if they're actually paying attention. It's warm, it's playful, and it filters out people who won't put in even a sentence of effort.

## Templates You Can Adapt

The last row matters. Even when someone gives you a bare profile and a bare "hey," you can call it out lightly and turn the emptiness into the joke. That reads as confident, not annoyed.

## Match the Energy — But Set the Floor

Here's the balance. You want to lift the energy above "hey," but you don't want to write three paragraphs to someone who wrote three letters. That imbalance makes you look overinvested and makes them feel like they can coast.

One or two sentences with one clear question is the sweet spot. It signals: *I'm interested and I'm easy to talk to, but I'm also not going to carry this alone.* That's an attractive frame because it's honest. You're offering effort and quietly asking for a little back.

### What about flirty replies?

Flirting off a cold "hey" is high-risk this early — you have no rapport yet, so a heavy line can land as try-hard. A light, teasing question does more work than an overt compliment. "Hey! You look like trouble in the third photo, explain yourself" is playful and specific. "Hey gorgeous, you're stunning 😍" is a wall — there's nothing to reply to, and it reads as something they've gotten fifty times.

## When to Just Let It Go

Not every "hey" deserves a saved conversation, and chasing every one is a fast route to burnout. Send your one good reply. If they come back with another single word, or take two days and then say "nm you?", the effort mismatch is your signal. You're allowed to stop there.

This isn't about being harsh. It's about protecting your own energy so the people who *do* engage get the best version of you instead of a you that's exhausted from reviving twelve dead threads.

## A Quick Checklist Before You Hit Send

- Did you read their profile for a specific detail? (Not "you seem cool" — an actual thing.)
- Is there exactly one clear question they can answer?
- Is it short? Two sentences max on a cold open.
- Is the tone warm and light, not interrogating or over-the-top?
- Would *you* enjoy getting this message? If not, rewrite it.

Run that filter and you'll almost never send a boring reply again.

## FAQ

### Should I ever just reply "hey" back?

Rarely. It technically continues the conversation but it puts all the work back on them, and most people won't pick it up. If you genuinely have nothing — no profile to reference, no energy — a low-effort mirror is better than nothing, but a single specific question will almost always outperform it.

### How long should I wait to respond to a "hey"?

Don't overthink timing. Replying within a few hours is normal and reads as interested-but-not-glued-to-your-phone. There's no strategic advantage to making someone wait days off a first message — that just kills momentum before there's any to kill.

### What if they only ever send one-word replies?

That's your compatibility data. Some people are texters and some aren't, and neither is wrong — but if you like a real back-and-forth and they consistently give you three words, the mismatch is real. Try one more genuinely open question; if it still flatlines, it's fine to move on.

### Is it okay to use humor with someone I don't know yet?

Yes, and it's usually your strongest tool. Light, self-aware humor lowers the stakes and shows personality faster than any compliment. Keep it teasing rather than sarcastic, and never at their expense — the joke should include them, not target them.
