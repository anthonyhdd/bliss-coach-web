Text conversations die from interview energy, not from a shortage of topics. Question, answer, question, answer — until one of you feels like they're renewing a passport and stops replying. The fix is structural: lead with statements, build inside jokes fast, and keep more than one thread alive so no single dead end kills the whole thing.

## Why Questions Kill Conversations

A question hands the other person homework. One question is interest. Five questions in a row is an intake form, and the reply quality drops with every one because answering homework is work.

There's a status problem too. The person asking all the questions is auditioning; the person answering is being auditioned. That dynamic gets uncomfortable fast — even when the answers keep coming — because nobody flirts with their dentist's intake form.

The 80/20 rule fixes it: roughly 80% statements, reactions, and stories; 20% actual questions. You're not banning questions. You're demoting them from engine to seasoning.

### Statements pull harder than questions

Counterintuitive but true: a statement with a gap in it generates better replies than a question, because responding to it is a choice, not an obligation — and people are more invested in what they chose to do.

- Interview mode: "How was your weekend?"
- Statement mode: "I need you to know I lost a bar trivia tiebreaker on a question about YOUR favorite show."

The second one contains a story, a callback, and an implied invitation. She can grab any of the three. That's the pattern: **react, relate, then maybe ask.** Take whatever they just said, have a genuine reaction to it, add something of yours that connects — and only then, if it still needs one, attach a question.

## Callback Humor: Your Compounding Asset

A callback is a reference to something from earlier in your conversation — a joke, a confession, a weird detail. It's the highest-leverage move in texting because it does three things at once: proves you actually listen, creates in-group language that exists only between you two, and gives you infinite material that never feels random.

She mentions her sister steals her fries. Two days later you're at a restaurant: "Ordered extra fries in case your sister shows up." That message took four seconds and lands harder than the wittiest cold open, because it's *yours and hers*.

Building the asset is deliberate work: when they tell you something specific — a fear of pigeons, a terrible ex named Kyle, an unhinged opinion about cereal — flag it mentally (or literally scroll back before texting). Two or three running jokes are worth more than fifty facts about their job.

## The Thread-Splitting Trick

Most people run conversations single-threaded: one topic, mined until it's exhausted, then the awkward silence where somebody has to invent a new one from nothing. That invention moment is where conversations go to die.

Thread-splitting means keeping two or three open loops running at once, so no single topic ever bears the whole weight.

### How to split

When they send a message with multiple things in it, don't respond to just one — respond to one *and* plant a flag on another:

> Her: "Ugh, work was chaos, my boss scheduled a 5pm Friday meeting. At least I have my trip to Austin next month."
>
> You: "A 5pm Friday meeting is a crime and your boss knows it. Also — Austin? We're coming back to that, I have opinions."

Now two threads live: boss slander (active) and Austin (parked). When the boss thread runs dry, you don't need to invent anything — "Okay, Austin. First trip or are you a regular?" The conversation *continues* instead of restarting, and restarts are where the energy leaks out.

### The parked-thread revival

Parked threads are also your best re-openers after a day of silence. "Wait, you never told me the Austin story" beats "hey, how's your week" every single time, because it resumes something instead of launching a cold start. ## 20 Revival Lines by Context

Dead conversations are usually revivable — if you come back with content instead of a pulse-check. "Hey" and "how's your week" are pulse-checks. These are content.

### It faded naturally a few days ago

1. **"Important update on the [their thing] situation: I have thoughts."** — Resumes a thread instead of cold-starting. Fill in their actual thing.
2. **"Saw [specific thing they mentioned] in the wild today and thought of you. This is your fault."** — Callback + blame-as-flirtation.
3. **"You crossed my mind at a weirdly specific moment. Ask me about it."** — Open loop. Curiosity does the heavy lifting.
4. **"Quick, settle a debate: [genuinely fun either/or]."** — Zero-effort entry point with a built-in opinion slot.
5. **"I owe you a conversation. Life got loud. Where were we — I think you were being wrong about [callback]?"** — Owns the fade in one clause, restarts mid-flow.

### They left you on read

6. **"You've been quiet. I'm choosing to believe you got recruited by the CIA."** — Names the silence without weight. One send, then done.
7. **"No pressure, but the group chat needs to know if you survived [thing they had coming up]."** — References their life, invents a stakesless reason.
8. **"This is your reminder that you never finished the [X] story and I think about it weekly."** — Flattering pressure on an open loop.
9. **"I'll allow the ghosting if the excuse is good."** — Playful frame that makes replying fun and not-replying an answer. Last card you play, not first.

### After a great first date

10. **"Made it home. Confirming you're as fun in person as advertised."** — Warm, evaluative, zero games. Send it that night.
11. **"Still thinking about [specific moment from the date]. That's on you."** — Specificity is the whole message. Generic 'had fun' is wallpaper.
12. **"Round two needs to happen before you get a chance to remember my flaws."** — Escalates to the next date with a self-deprecating wink.
13. **"You mentioned [thing] last night and I already found the place. This is not a drill."** — Turns a date detail into a concrete plan. Strongest move on the list.

### The conversation is alive but flatlining

14. **"New topic, that one's finished: what's your most defendable bad opinion?"** — Kills the dying thread on purpose instead of letting it wheeze.
15. **"Be honest, are we in the boring part of the conversation? Say something unhinged."** — Names the flatline. Self-aware beats slow death.
16. **"I just remembered you said [callback] and I have follow-up questions."** — Reaches into the archive when the present is dry.
17. **"Voice memo or it didn't happen — I need the full story with tone."** — Switches medium. Energy that died in text often lives in audio.

### You dropped the ball (you didn't reply)

18. **"I owe you a reply from three days ago. In my defense: no defense."** — Owns it in one line. No paragraph of excuses; excuses make it bigger.
19. **"Resurfacing like I didn't leave you on read. Anyway — [actual content or question]."** — Acknowledge then immediately give them something. The content is the apology.
20. **"I'm back. Punish me accordingly and then tell me about [their parked thread]."** — Hands them a free shot at you, which resets the balance faster than sorry does.

## When Letting It Die Is the Correct Move

Not every conversation deserves CPR. Reviving the wrong one costs you time, dignity, and attention that belongs on people who actually reply.

- **You've sent two revival attempts with zero real response.** Two is the limit. A third message into silence isn't persistence, it's evidence.
- **Every reply is effortless.** "Haha," "nice," "omg fr" — with no questions, no threads, no callbacks — means they're not building anything with you. You can't thread-split with someone who only sends punctuation.
- **You're the only one who ever restarts.** Scroll up. If the last five conversation-starts are all yours, you don't have a conversation, you have a subscription they forgot to cancel.
- **It only lives after midnight.** A conversation that's dead for six days and electric at 1 a.m. Saturday isn't a texting problem. It's an answer, and you already know the question.
- **You're bored too and performing otherwise.** Sometimes the honest read is that neither of you is feeling it and you're both being polite. Ending that one is a gift to you both.

Letting it die isn't losing. Spending three weeks of prime effort on someone at 20% interest — that's losing.

## FAQ

### How do I keep a conversation going without asking questions?

React to what they said with an actual opinion, share a related story of your own, and leave a gap they'll want to fill. "That's the most Leo thing you've ever said" continues a conversation harder than "what's your sign?" Statements with hooks invite replies; questions demand them.

### How long should I wait before reviving a dead conversation?

If it faded naturally, two to four days is the sweet spot — long enough not to hover, short enough that the callbacks still breathe. If they left you on read, give it at least two or three days and come back with content, not a "hey?". If you're past a week, only revive with something specific and good.

### Is it bad to double text?

No — one confident double text with real content is fine and often exactly right. What's bad is the *anxious* double text: "hello?", "did I say something?", "you there?". One adds value; the other requests reassurance. If the first double text gets nothing, the conversation is telling you something. Believe it.

### They reply but never start conversations. Should I keep going?

Look at reply *quality*, not who initiates. Some genuinely interested people just never text first — but their replies are long, curious, full of questions and callbacks. That's fine. Thin replies AND never initiating is the dead combination. And either way, stop measuring and suggest a date — texting patterns are a terrible way to gauge someone you could just go meet.
