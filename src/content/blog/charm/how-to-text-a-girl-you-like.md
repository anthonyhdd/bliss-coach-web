To text a girl you like, be specific instead of generic, ask questions she actually wants to answer, match her energy rather than chasing it, and steer toward meeting up before the chat goes stale. The whole thing is simpler than the internet makes it sound: text like a warm, curious person who has a life, not like someone running a strategy.

## Start With Something Only She Would Get

The fastest way to sound like every other guy in her inbox is to send "hey how's your day." It's fine, but it's forgettable. Reference something real — a thing she said, a thing from her profile, a moment you shared — and you're instantly in a different category.

- Generic: "How was your weekend?"
- Specific: "Did the disaster brunch actually happen or did your friend flake again?"

The second one proves you listened. That's the entire trick behind "being a good texter" — not wit, not word count, just paying attention and referencing it back. Attention is the flirt.

## Ask Questions She Wants to Answer

A conversation needs a ball to keep bouncing. Statements end the volley; questions keep it going. But not all questions are equal — "how are you?" is a chore, while a question with a little personality in it is a gift.

| Instead of | Try |
|---|---|
| "What do you do for fun?" | "What's the thing you'd do all weekend if adulting didn't exist?" |
| "How's work?" | "Is your job the fun kind of busy or the send-help kind of busy this week?" |
| "Do you like to travel?" | "Last place that genuinely surprised you — where was it?" |
| "What are you up to?" | "Okay important: are you a 'plans every night' person or a 'blanket and delete my calendar' person?" |

Each of these invites a story instead of a one-word answer. And when she answers, follow the thread she gives you — don't just fire the next question. Reacting to her answer ("wait, you drove *how* far for tacos?") matters more than having a list of questions ready.

## Match Her Energy — Don't Chase It

This is the balance point most people miss. If you're sending three long texts to her one short one, you're carrying the whole thing, and she can feel it. If she's giving you enthusiasm and you're playing it too cool, you'll read as uninterested and she'll fade.

The move is to roughly mirror her investment. She sends a paragraph? A paragraph back is welcome. She sends a quick line? Keep yours light too. This isn't a game — it's basic conversational rhythm, the same thing you'd do in person. It also quietly protects you: if you're always matching rather than over-giving, you'll notice fast when the energy isn't mutual.

### Being interested is good, actually

Somewhere online, guys got told that showing interest is weakness. It isn't. Genuine enthusiasm is attractive — what's unattractive is *neediness*, which is a different thing. Neediness is texting again before she's replied, fishing for reassurance, or getting weird when she's busy. Interest is warm and secure; neediness is anxious and demanding. Aim for the first and you can be as into her as you actually are.

## How to Flirt Without Being Cringe

Flirting is just teasing plus warmth. The tease shows confidence; the warmth shows you like her. Drop either one and it breaks — all tease reads as mean, all warmth reads as a Hallmark card.

- **Playful accusation:** "You seem dangerously competitive. This is either great news or a red flag, jury's out."
- **Light callback:** "Still thinking about your take on pineapple pizza. We may have irreconcilable differences."
- **Honest and direct:** "Not gonna lie, I've checked my phone way too many times waiting for you to reply."

That last one scares people because it's vulnerable. It's also the most effective, because directness is rare and it lands as confidence. You don't need to be slick. You need to be genuine and a little brave.

## Steer Toward Meeting Up

Texting is a bridge, not a destination. The best chemistry over text can quietly die if it never becomes a plan, because endless messaging turns into a low-stakes pen-pal loop that's easy to drift out of.

When the conversation is flowing and you've found some common ground, make a specific, low-pressure offer:

> "You clearly have strong coffee opinions. There's a place near me that would either confirm or destroy your whole worldview — want to go find out this week?"

Specific and easy to say yes to beats "we should hang out sometime." A real suggestion with a time frame gives her something concrete to react to, and it signals you're interested in *her*, not just in having someone to text.

## Do / Don't Cheat Sheet

**Do:**
- Reference specific things she's said
- Ask questions that invite stories
- Text at a natural, human pace
- Show real interest and a little vulnerability
- Suggest a real plan once there's rapport

**Don't:**
- Play hard to get or "leave her on read" as a tactic
- Double- or triple-text before she replies
- Send generic "hey" / "wyd" filler
- Fake a personality — the mask always slips in person
- Wait weeks to ask her out and let it fizzle

## FAQ

### How often should I text a girl I like?

Follow the conversation's natural rhythm rather than a rule. If you're mid-exchange, reply when you see it. If the chat has wound down, there's nothing wrong with reaching out the next day with something specific. What to avoid is the anxious loop of texting again and again before she's responded — that's the one habit that consistently reads as needy.

### Should I wait before replying to seem less eager?

No. Strategic delays are a game, and games create distance instead of connection. Reply when it's natural for you. If you're genuinely busy, you'll reply later anyway — but manufacturing a delay just to seem cool usually backfires by killing momentum.

### What do I text if the conversation goes dry?

Revive it with something specific and new, not "hey" again. Reference something from earlier ("random, but did you ever try that restaurant you mentioned?") or share something small from your own day that invites a reaction. If two genuine attempts flatline, it may just not be mutual, and that's useful to know.

### Is it too forward to say I like her over text?

Being direct is almost always better than hiding it, as long as it's proportional to where things are. You don't confess deep feelings in message three — but saying "I really like talking to you" or "you're kind of the highlight of my day" is warm, honest, and rare. Directness done with a light touch reads as confidence, not desperation.
