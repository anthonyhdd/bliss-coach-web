Someone who likes you over text does three things consistently: they ask you questions, they reference things you said before, and they keep conversations alive when it would be easier to let them die. Everything else — emoji counts, reply speed, who texted first — is noise compared to those three.

The frustrating truth is that most people analyze the noise and ignore the signal. Let's fix that.

## The Signals That Actually Mean Something

### They ask you questions

Not "how are you" reflexes — real questions that require having paid attention. "How did the thing with your boss end up going?" is a person holding a thread of your life in their head between conversations. That's not politeness. Politeness doesn't have a memory.

The inverse matters just as much: if you've sent fifty messages and can't find five questions from their side, you're not in a conversation, you're giving a performance to a polite audience.

### They reference the past and the future

**Callbacks** — bringing up your inside joke, your terrible movie take, the story you told last week — mean you're being *stored*, not just responded to.

**Future-casting** — "you'd love this place," "we should try that," "remind me to show you" — means you exist in their imagined future. People do not accidentally plan hypothetical outings with people they're lukewarm about.

> "Okay this menu has a fried pickle flight and I immediately thought of you and your cursed taste"

That message contains a callback, a future-shaped thought, and an unprompted "thought of you." It's a like-you signal in a trench coat.

### They keep the thread alive on purpose

Watch what happens when a conversation naturally lands. You answer their question, the topic completes, and the thread could gracefully die. Someone who likes you *reopens it* — a new question, a photo of their day, a "wait, also." Someone neutral lets it rest, every time, and only ever responds when you serve first.

This is the single most reliable signal on this list. Interest is measured in restarts.

### Their effort matches or beats yours

You send three thoughtful sentences; do you get three back, or "lol nice"? One low-effort reply means they were at dinner. A *pattern* of low-effort replies is the message. People sound like their level of interest over time, no matter how busy they are.

### They text you their unfiltered moments

Random observations, dumb memes chosen specifically for you, "you won't believe what just happened" dispatches at odd hours. When you become someone's default recipient for unremarkable moments, you've been promoted to a category most contacts never reach.

### They get specific about meeting

"We should hang out sometime" is filler. "Are you free Thursday?" is interest. The distance between those two sentences is the distance between enjoying your texts and wanting *you*.

## The Noise: What Doesn't Mean What You Think

### Reply-time myths

Reply speed is the most over-read metric in modern romance, so here's the honest version:

- **Fast replies** mean their phone was in their hand. Sometimes that's excitement; sometimes it's a boring meeting.
- **Slow replies** mean they have a job, a life, anxiety, a dead battery, or a personal rule about looking available. Slow-but-substantive beats instant-but-empty every single time.
- **The pattern is the only signal.** Someone who reliably comes back with real engagement — whether that takes four minutes or four hours — is interested. Someone whose replies are shrinking in both frequency *and* substance is fading, and the speed was never the story.

If you're calculating hours-to-reply ratios, you already know the substance isn't there — that's why you're counting.

### Emoji and punctuation forensics

The heart versus the laughing face, the period that felt cold, the "hey" versus "heyyy" — this is astrology for texters. Emoji habits vary wildly person to person. Measure someone against *their own baseline*, not a universal decoder ring: a chronic emoji user going flat is data; a dry texter being dry is Tuesday.

### They reply to your story but never text first

Story reactions are the lowest-cost social move that exists — one tap from the couch. It can be a shy person's opening move, sure, but on its own it's window shopping. See if it ever escalates into an actual conversation with questions in it. If six story reactions haven't produced one, it's not shyness, it's grazing.

### Niceness

Some people are warm, funny, and generous with everyone — that's personality, not preference. The tell is *differentiation*: do they treat you observably differently from the group chat? Interest discriminates. Charisma broadcasts.

## The Double-Texting Question

Both directions, since both get agonized over:

**When they double-text you** — sending a second message before you answered the first — that's one of the cleaner interest signals available. It means the conversation was still running in their head after they put the phone down. Nobody double-texts someone they're indifferent to.

**When you're wondering if you can double-text them** — yes, with rules. One double-text that adds something new (a thought, a link, a question — not "hello??" or "did you see my message") is confident and normal. It reads as "I have a life and I thought of you," which is exactly the energy you want. What you don't do is triple-text into silence. After one unanswered follow-up, the ball is fully, formally, in their court — and their silence is itself an answer you should respect enough to hear.

## When to Stop Reading and Make a Move

Here's the part the decoder guides skip: the entire point of detecting interest is to *act on it*. Signal-collecting past a certain point isn't caution, it's a hobby.

**Make the move when you can check most of these boxes:**

- They've restarted dead threads more than once
- Real questions flow in both directions
- There's been a callback or a "you'd love this"
- The conversation has survived at least one lull without dying

That's enough. You will never reach 100% certainty over text, because certainty isn't available in this medium — the move *is* the final test. Propose something specific:

> This conversation has been the best part of my week, which feels like something we should investigate in person. Drinks Thursday?

**And here's the reframe that kills over-analysis for good:** a specific, low-pressure invitation resolves in days what signal-reading can't resolve in months. A yes means everything you hoped. A warm no means you're free — free to stop drafting, stop decoding, stop narrating their typing bubbles to your group chat. Both outcomes beat the third option, which is six more weeks of screenshot forensics.

If they say no but keep texting warmly? Enjoy the friendship at face value or step back — but don't reinvest in the decoder ring. The question was asked and answered.

## When the Signals Are Genuinely Mixed

Sometimes it really is ambiguous — hot streaks then silence, real questions then one-word weeks. Mixed signals usually mean mixed interest: something real but not strong enough to be consistent, or you're one of several conversations, or their life is genuinely chaotic. You can't diagnose which from inside the thread, and you don't need to. The response to mixed signals is the same as the response to good ones: make the specific ask, and let their answer de-mix it.

## FAQ

### Do slow replies mean they're not interested?

Not by themselves. Judge the trend of substance, not the clock: slow and engaged is interest on a busy schedule; increasingly slow *and* increasingly hollow is a fade, at any speed.

### They text me every day — does that mean they like me?

Daily texting plus questions, callbacks, and meetup energy? Very likely. Daily texting that's all vibes and no attempt to see you, sustained for weeks, can also be someone enjoying attention with no destination. The calendar test settles it: suggest meeting and watch what happens.

### Is it a bad sign if I always text first?

One person often serves first in every dynamic — that alone is fine. What matters is what happens after: if your openers get real engagement and they sometimes restart threads too, relax. If nothing exists that you didn't start, run a quiet experiment: don't initiate for several days. A thread only you can start isn't a thread; it's a monologue with a delay.

### How long should I wait before asking them out?

Days-to-weeks of good conversation, not months. Once the boxes above are checked, extra waiting adds risk, not safety — text-only dynamics have a shelf life, and someone else may be running the same analysis on the same person with more nerve.
