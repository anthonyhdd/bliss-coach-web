Green flags are the behaviors that predict someone will be good *to* you, not just good *on paper*: consistency between words and actions, communication that doesn't require decoding, respect for your time and boundaries, and interest that shows up as effort. They matter more than red flags for one uncomfortable reason — most people are so busy scanning for danger that they can't recognize health when it's sitting across the table, and they mistake its calm for lack of chemistry.

That last part is worth saying twice. If your previous relationships ran on anxiety, a secure person can initially read as "boring" — no highs from intermittent attention, no lows to recover from. That flatness isn't absence of spark. It's absence of threat. Learn to tell the difference and your entire dating life changes.

## Texting and Early-Communication Green Flags

The first flags show up before the first date:

1. **Consistency without games.** Reply rhythm is roughly stable — not instant, just *reliable*. No three-day vanishing acts followed by "hey you" at 11pm. You never have to run their messages through the [texting red flags](/charm/blog/texting-red-flags/) checklist, because nothing needs decoding.
2. **They ask questions and remember the answers.** Interest that references your Tuesday presentation on Wednesday is worth more than a hundred compliments. Curiosity plus memory equals actual attention.
3. **Plans are specific and kept.** "Thursday, 7, that ramen place" — then they're there at 6:55. People who convert talk into calendar entries are showing you how they treat commitments generally.
4. **Effort is mutual without scorekeeping.** They initiate roughly as often as you do. You never look up and realize you've sent the last six openers.
5. **A canceled plan comes with a counter-offer.** "Work exploded, I'm so sorry — can we do Saturday instead?" Cancellation with immediate re-booking is life happening. Cancellation into vagueness is a decision happening.
6. **Directness.** They say what they mean at normal volume: "I had a great time, I want to see you again." No riddles, no strategic ambiguity, no making you earn clarity. If you recognize most of the healthy patterns from [how to tell if someone likes you over text](/charm/blog/how-to-tell-if-someone-likes-you-over-text/), you're looking at this flag.

## First-Date and Early-Dating Green Flags

7. **How they treat the waiter is how they'll treat you in year two.** Warmth to service staff, patience with delays, no performative dominance. Character leaks in low-stakes moments — watch those, not the curated ones.
8. **They listen more than they perform.** The conversation is a rally, not a TED talk. Bonus flag inside this one: they ask a follow-up question about something you said twenty minutes ago.
9. **They speak about exes without venom.** "It didn't work, we wanted different things" is emotional adulthood. Someone who narrates every ex as crazy is telling you what the story about you will be.
10. **Boundaries get respected the first time.** You say you're taking it slow, or you can't do Sunday, or you don't want a second drink — and they just... adjust. No sulking, no pressure, no discount negotiation. This one flag is worth five others, because it predicts every future disagreement.
11. **Their life has furniture in it.** Friends they actually see, work they can talk about without bitterness, something they do on Saturdays. People with full lives are choosing you as an addition, not auditioning you as a rescue.
12. **You feel *more* like yourself around them, not less.** Track your own behavior: are you funnier, more relaxed, more honest? Or are you managing, editing, performing? Your nervous system files a report after every date. Read it.

## The Deeper Flags: What Shows Up by Month Two

13. **Repair over rightness.** The first mild friction — a misread text, a late arrival, a disagreement about plans — and they engage with it directly, own their part, and move on without a three-day cold front. How someone handles the first small conflict is the trailer for how they'll handle every big one.
14. **Words and actions run on the same track.** "I want to see you more" is followed by actual invitations. "I'm serious about this" is followed by introductions to real friends. Green-flag people don't need their statements audited, because the behavior arrives on schedule.
15. **The trajectory is quietly upward.** More comfort, more depth, more integration into each other's actual lives — without whiplash. Healthy connection compounds like interest; it doesn't spike like a slot machine.

## Green Flags vs. Performed Green Flags

Here's the catch: everything above can be faked for a while, and self-aware daters increasingly *perform* green flags — therapy-speak, "communication is so important to me," boundary vocabulary deployed as decoration. Three tests separate the real from the rehearsed:

- **Time.** Performance is expensive; most people can't sustain it past six to eight weeks. Genuine patterns survive routine, stress, and the end of the impress-phase. Don't award the trophy in week two.
- **Cost.** A green flag only counts when it costs something. Being communicative when everything's fun is free; being communicative *while canceling*, *while disagreeing*, *while tired* is the real article. Watch behavior under mild adversity, not on best-behavior nights.
- **Density.** One or two flags prove nothing — charming people generate compliments and good dates effortlessly. What you're looking for is the cluster: consistency AND boundary-respect AND repair AND a full life. Manipulators can fake any single flag; faking the whole ecosystem is nearly impossible. That's also how you tell early intensity from [love bombing](/charm/blog/what-is-love-bombing/) — bombers max out the affection flags while failing the boundary and consistency ones.

## What to Do When You Find One

Green flags demand a response, and this is where people fumble keepers:

- **Match the energy.** Secure people extend effort and read the reply. If they're consistent and you play scarcity games because that's what worked on avoidant exes, you will bore *them* out of the connection. Games only work on people playing games.
- **Reciprocate the flags.** Confirm plans, communicate directly, respect their boundaries, keep your own life running. Green-flag people are pattern-matchers too — they're checking for the same list.
- **Don't manufacture drama to feel the spark.** If part of you wants to poke the calm to see if it breaks, notice that impulse — it's your old wiring asking for the anxiety it mistook for love. Sit with the calm for eight weeks before you judge it.
- **Name what's working.** "I really like that you just say things directly" reinforces the exact behavior you want more of, and green-flag people receive appreciation without weirdness. What you spotlight, you get more of.

The whole skill of dating well eventually reduces to this: get equally good at spotting the flags in both directions — walking away from the red ones early, and *not* walking away from the green ones out of boredom, fear, or the suspicion that calm means settling. It doesn't. Calm is what it feels like when nothing is wrong.

## FAQ

**What are the biggest green flags in dating?**

Consistency between words and actions, respecting a boundary the first time it's set, handling small conflict with repair instead of cold war, genuine curiosity about you (with memory to prove it), and a full life outside of dating. Any one is nice; the cluster is what predicts a healthy relationship.

**Can green flags be faked?**

Individually, yes — especially vocabulary-level ones like therapy-speak and "I value communication." What can't realistically be faked is the full cluster over time and under cost: consistency past week eight, boundaries respected when it's inconvenient, kindness when they're tired and nothing's at stake. Judge patterns under mild adversity, not performances on good nights.

**Why does a healthy relationship feel boring to me?**

If your reference point is anxious relationships, your nervous system learned to read intermittent attention as passion and stability as flatness. A secure partner produces no spikes — no panic, no relief-highs — so the early feeling is unfamiliar calm. Give it two months before judging; most people find the "boring" becomes the thing they'd never trade back.

**What's the difference between a green flag and love bombing?**

Green flags are calibrated to the timeline: warmth, consistency, and interest that grow as you actually know each other. Love bombing front-loads maximum intimacy — declarations, future plans, constant contact — before any real knowledge exists, and it collapses when you slow the pace. The test is the boundary: green-flag people adjust; love bombers punish.

**How many green flags should I look for before getting serious?**

There's no magic number, but demand the core four: consistency over at least a couple of months, boundary respect, direct communication, and clean handling of the first conflict. If those hold, most other flags follow. If any of the four is missing, no quantity of charm, chemistry, or shared playlists compensates.
