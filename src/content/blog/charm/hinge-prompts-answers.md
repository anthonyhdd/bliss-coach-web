The best Hinge prompts are the ones that give people something to respond to — a specific detail, a strong opinion, or an opening they can tease you about. A prompt answer that ends in a hook ("fight me," "guess which one," "I need a second opinion") gets comments; a prompt answer that ends in a shrug gets skipped.

That's the whole game. Hinge is built around comments, not swipes. Every prompt is a conversation starter you wrote in advance, so write it like one.

## What Makes a Prompt Answer Work

Three things separate answers that get comments from answers that get scrolled past:

**Specificity.** "I love to travel" is wallpaper. "I got scammed at a night market in Bangkok and I'd honestly do it again" is a story someone can ask about. The more specific the detail, the easier it is to reply.

**A hook.** The best answers end with an implied question or a soft challenge — something that makes replying feel effortless. If someone has to invent a comment from scratch, most won't bother.

**Actual personality.** A prompt answer is a writing sample. If yours could belong to any of the last 40 profiles someone saw, it's not doing its job. Take a position. Be a little wrong on purpose. Bland is the only real failure mode.

### The Three Answer Formulas

Almost every strong answer fits one of these:

- **Specific + unexpected.** Start normal, end somewhere surprising. "My simple pleasures: coffee in silence, clean sheets, and winning arguments in the shower two days later."
- **Confident + self-aware.** A brag with a wink. "I'll fall for you if you can parallel park on the first try. Low bar, but you'd be surprised."
- **Opinion + invitation.** A take plus a door left open. "Unpopular opinion: cereal is a soup. I'm willing to hear arguments."

## The Prompts Worth Picking (and the Ones to Skip)

**Pick prompts that reward a specific answer:**

- "The way to win me over is" — invites playful, concrete answers.
- "Unusual skills" — instant conversation material.
- "My most controversial opinion" — the highest comment rate of any prompt when the opinion is low-stakes (food, movies, habits — not politics).
- "Two truths and a lie" — people love solving things. Free comments.
- "Dating me is like" — the best canvas for humor on the app.
- "Together we could" — points the conversation toward an actual date.

**Skip or handle with care:**

- "I go crazy for" — tends to produce lists of generic likes.
- "Typical Sunday" — fine, but 90% of answers are "coffee, walk, farmers market." Only use it if your Sunday is genuinely funny or weird.
- "I'm looking for" — usually reads as either a demand list or a Hallmark card. Hard to win.

## What Kills a Hinge Profile

The bad news: one weak prompt won't sink you, but these patterns will.

**All three prompts are jokes.** One punchline is charm, three is a defense mechanism. Mix registers: one funny, one sincere, one flirty or date-forward. People need at least one signal that you're a real person who wants a real thing.

**Answers with no surface area.** "Ask me," "You'll find out," "I'm bad at these." An empty answer tells people you'll be this much effort in the conversation too. If you can't think of anything, that's the prompt telling you to pick a different prompt.

**Negativity dressed as standards.** "No hookups, no games, no drama, swipe left if..." Every line about what you don't want reads as baggage. Say what you're into; the filtering happens on its own.

**The résumé answer.** Listing achievements isn't attraction, it's LinkedIn. One flex delivered with self-awareness beats five delivered straight.

**Prompt-photo mismatch.** If your prompts are witty and warm but your photos are six mirror selfies with a dead expression, the profile contradicts itself. The prompts promise a conversation; the photos should look like someone you'd have it with — at least one genuine smile, one photo doing something you mention.

## How to Comment on Someone Else's Prompt

Since Hinge runs on comments both ways: never like without a comment when you actually care, and never comment "haha" or "so true." The formula is react + add. React to their specific answer, then add a detail, a tease, or a question of your own.

Their prompt: "My most controversial opinion: pineapple belongs on pizza."

> Correct, and I'll go further — cold pizza is better than reheated. We can build on this.

Two sentences. Specific, playful, and it hands them an easy reply. That's all an opener needs to do.

## FAQ

### How many prompts should I fill out on Hinge?

All three, always. An empty prompt slot reads as low effort, and each prompt is another chance for someone to find their opening.

### Should my prompt answers be long or short?

One to three sentences. Long enough to show personality, short enough that the hook isn't buried. If an answer needs a paragraph, it's a first-date story, not a prompt.

### Do voice prompts help?

They can — a warm, natural voice answer humanizes a profile fast. But a stiff, scripted one hurts more than no voice prompt at all. Record it like you're leaving a voicemail for a friend, not auditioning.

### How often should I update my prompts?

Whenever one stops generating comments, or roughly monthly. Fresh prompts also give existing likes a new reason to comment.
