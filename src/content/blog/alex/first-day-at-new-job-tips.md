On your first day at a new job, your goals are simple: arrive early, learn names, ask your manager what success looks like in the first 90 days, and listen far more than you talk. Nobody expects output on day one — they're forming an impression of whether you're organized, curious, and easy to work with, and that impression is fully in your control.

## Before Day One: The Night-Before Checklist

Most first-day stress is logistics stress, and logistics can be solved in advance.

**1. Confirm the basics in writing.** Start time, address or video link, who to ask for, parking or building access, and what to bring (ID and banking details for payroll paperwork are the usual asks). If HR hasn't sent this, email them — asking is organized, not needy.

**2. Do the commute math.** If it's an office, know the route and add a 20-minute buffer. Arriving 10–15 minutes early is the target; 45 minutes early is awkward for whoever has to receive you.

**3. Re-read your own interview notes.** The people you met, the problems they said they were hiring for, the team's current projects. Walking in remembering that your manager mentioned a Q3 migration makes your first conversations noticeably sharper. The same preparation instinct that got you the offer — covered in [how to prepare for an interview](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/) — applies to day one; the audience just changed.

**4. Prepare your 30-second introduction.** You'll deliver it a dozen times: name, what you'll be doing, one line of background, one human detail. "I'm Dana, joining the platform team to work on the billing system. I spent four years at a fintech doing similar work, and I just moved here from Denver." Practice it out loud once; the polished version of this skill is the same one behind [introducing yourself in an interview](/alex/blog/how-to-introduce-yourself-in-an-interview/), minus the evaluation pressure.

**5. Dress one notch above what you saw.** Recall what interviewers wore and match it or go slightly more formal. Overdressed on day one is a minor smile; underdressed is a lasting data point.

## Day One: What to Actually Do

**6. Learn names like it's your job — because today it is.** Write them down immediately after each introduction, with a hook ("Priya — data lead, marathon runner"). Using someone's name on day two, correctly, is a disproportionately powerful signal. On remote teams, keep the org chart or Slack profile open during every call.

**7. Ask your manager the three framing questions.** In your first one-on-one, get answers to: *What does success look like in my first 90 days? Who are the five people I should meet this week? How do you prefer to communicate — Slack, email, scheduled one-on-ones?* These three questions do more for your first quarter than anything else you'll do on day one, and managers consistently read them as a sign they hired well.

**8. Take notes on everything.** Systems, acronyms, names, processes. You will be told forty things; you will retain six. A visible notebook (or doc) also spares your colleagues from repeating themselves next week, which they'll notice and appreciate.

**9. Listen for the vocabulary.** Every company has an internal language — project codenames, acronyms, tools, the shorthand for teams. Collect terms and ask about them in batches rather than interrupting every sentence. A personal glossary built in week one pays off for months.

**10. Say yes to lunch.** If anyone invites you — coworkers, your manager, the team — go. The informal channel is where you learn how the place actually works. If nobody offers (common on distracted teams and remote ones), ask your manager: "Is there anyone I should grab lunch or a coffee chat with this week?"

**11. Sort out the boring infrastructure.** Laptop, accounts, payroll forms, badge, security training. Tedious, but every unfinished setup item becomes a blocker in week two. Chase IT the same day if something's broken.

**12. Manage your energy, not just your impression.** First days are a firehose of names, systems, and low-grade social performance, and the fatigue is real. Don't schedule anything demanding that evening, and don't judge the job by how drained you feel at 6 p.m. — day one exhaustion is universal and temporary.

## The First Week and Beyond

**13. Book your own onboarding meetings.** Take the list of five people from your manager and put 30 minutes with each on the calendar. Ask each the same three things: what they do, how your roles will intersect, and what they wish someone had told them when they started. You'll leave with a map of the org no document could give you.

**14. Find an early, small win.** Not a heroic one — a genuinely small one. Fix a broken link in the onboarding doc, update the stale wiki page you just struggled through, automate a tiny annoyance. It signals initiative without stepping on anyone, and improving the onboarding path is the classic new-hire contribution because you're the last person who'll ever see it with fresh eyes.

**15. Hold your suggestions for two weeks.** You will see things that look wrong. Some are; some have reasons you can't see yet. Write every observation down — the fresh-eyes list is valuable precisely because it expires — but deliver opinions as questions ("What's the history behind X?") until you understand the context. The new hire who announces "at my last company we did this better" in week one spends months recovering from it.

## Mistakes That Set the Wrong Tone

**Trying to prove yourself on day one.** Overreaching for impact before you understand the terrain produces visible, memorable errors. The strongest start is aggressive learning, not premature output.

**Talking about your old job constantly.** One reference is context; ten is a signal you haven't left. This is the employed cousin of the interview rule about [talking about why you left](/alex/blog/how-to-answer-why-are-you-leaving-your-job/) — keep it brief, keep it neutral, move forward.

**Skipping the social layer.** Eating alone, cameras off, declining the coffee chat — on a new team, invisible is indistinguishable from disengaged. You don't need to perform extroversion; you need to be findable.

**Pretending to understand.** Nodding through an explanation you didn't follow costs you double: you still don't know, and now it's awkward to ask. "Can you go one level more basic? I want to get this right" is a strength on day one and gets more expensive every week you wait.

**Complaining — about anything.** The chair, the tooling, the commute, the process. You have no credit balance yet, and early negativity is the stickiest first impression there is.

## FAQ

**What should I bring on my first day?**

Government ID and banking details for payroll (most common paperwork needs), a notebook and pen, and anything HR specifically requested. If lunch plans are unclear, bring something or bring a card. Everything else — laptop, badge, accounts — is typically provided that day.

**What if I have nothing to do on day one?**

Extremely common: your manager is busy, access isn't provisioned, and the calendar is empty. Don't sit idle and don't disappear. Read every internal doc you can access, build your glossary, and ask your manager one targeted question: "While access is sorting out, what should I read or who should I meet?" Self-directed learning during dead time is exactly what good managers hope to see.

**How early should I arrive?**

Ten to fifteen minutes before your start time for an office job. For remote starts, be logged in, camera-ready, and in the right meeting five minutes early — first-day lateness on a video call reads worse than in person because there's no traffic to blame.

**When will I stop feeling lost?**

For most roles, basic orientation takes two to four weeks, real comfort around three months, and full productivity three to six months depending on complexity — and good managers expect exactly that curve, which is why 90-day plans exist. Feeling useless in week two is not a signal you're failing; it's the normal shape of starting.

**Should I connect with new coworkers on LinkedIn right away?**

Wait until you've actually worked with someone — a week or two — then connect without a pitch. Day-one mass connection requests feel transactional. The exception is your manager and direct teammates, where an early connect is unremarkable.
