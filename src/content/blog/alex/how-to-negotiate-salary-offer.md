To negotiate a salary offer, thank the employer, ask for time to review, then come back within 24–48 hours with a specific counter — a single number backed by one or two concrete reasons. Almost every offer has room built into it, and recruiters expect a counter; candidates who ask politely and specifically are rarely penalized for asking.

## Before You Say Anything: The First Five Minutes

When the offer lands — by phone or email — your only job is to sound pleased and buy time. Never negotiate in the moment. You'll anchor low, forget a lever, or agree to something you can't unwind.

Here's the script for the offer call:

> "Thank you — I'm genuinely excited about this. I'd like to take a day or two to review the full package. Can you send everything in writing, and would it work if I come back to you by Thursday?"

That's it. You've shown enthusiasm, asked for the written offer (always get it in writing), and set a deadline you control. No recruiter thinks less of you for this. Silence after "here's the number" is normal, not rude — many recruiters will improve an offer just to fill it.

## Do the Math Before You Counter

A counter without evidence is a wish. Spend an hour building your case:

- **Market range.** Pull the band for your title, level, and city from at least two sources — job postings with posted ranges (mandatory in many US states now), levels-style comparison sites for tech, and people in your network with the same title.
- **Your position in the range.** Match your years of experience and specific skills to where you sit in that band. Mid-range is the honest default; top-of-band needs a reason you can say out loud.
- **Competing signals.** Another offer, an active process elsewhere, or a raise cycle at your current job all strengthen your hand. You don't need to bluff — a real "I'm in final rounds elsewhere" is enough.
- **Your walk-away number.** Decide it now, calmly, not later on a phone call.

Then pick **one number**, not a range. If you say "$95K–$105K," you just said $95K. Counter at the top of what you can justify — typically 5–15% above the offer for the same role and level. Bigger gaps are possible when the offer is clearly below market, but you'll need the data on the table.

## The Counter Email Script

Email is the right channel for the counter itself: you control the wording, they can forward it internally, and nobody gets put on the spot. Keep it under 150 words.

> Subject: Re: Offer — [Your Name]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you again for the offer — I'm excited about the team and the [specific project or scope you discussed], and I want to make this work.
>
> Based on my research on the market for [title] roles at this level, and given [one concrete reason: e.g., "my five years shipping exactly this kind of product" / "the range I'm seeing in my other active process"], I was expecting base compensation closer to **$X**. If you can get to that number, I'm ready to sign.
>
> Happy to jump on a call if that's easier.
>
> Best,
> [Your Name]

Notice the anatomy: enthusiasm first, one specific reason, one specific number, and a close that makes saying yes easy ("I'm ready to sign"). That last line matters — recruiters fight harder internally when they know the counter ends the negotiation instead of starting round two.

## The Phone Script

If the recruiter calls to discuss your counter, or you prefer to negotiate live:

> "Thanks for making time. I want to be straightforward: I'm excited about this role and I'd like to get to a yes. The gap for me is base salary — the offer is at $[offer], and based on [your reason], I'm looking for $[counter]. Is there flexibility there?"

Then **stop talking**. The silence after your number is where the negotiation happens. Whoever fills it first usually concedes. If they say they need to check, great: "I understand — when should I expect to hear back?"

If they come back partway — say you asked for $110K and they return with $105K — you have two clean moves:

> "I appreciate you pushing for that. If we can meet at $107, I'll sign today."

or trade the remaining gap for something else:

> "I can work with $105 on base if we can add [a $5K signing bonus / an extra week of PTO / a six-month review with a defined raise path]. Does that work?"

## Negotiate the Whole Package, Not Just Base

When base is genuinely capped — real bands exist, especially at large companies — move the negotiation sideways. Roughly in order of how easy they are to get:

- **Signing bonus.** One-time money is the easiest yes in most companies.
- **Start date.** Costs them nothing; worth a lot if you want a break.
- **Equity or additional stock grant.** At startups and public tech companies this is often more flexible than base.
- **Early performance review.** "A compensation review at six months" turns a no into a delayed yes — get the timing in writing.
- **PTO, remote days, title, learning budget.** Small individually, meaningful together.

Pick your top two. Asking for everything signals you'll be a difficult employee; asking for the two that matter signals you know what you want.

## What Never to Say

- **"I need more because of my rent / loans / divorce."** Your expenses are not their problem, and personal-need arguments invite personal-judgment answers. Argue market value, not lifestyle.
- **"That's insulting."** Even if the offer is bad. Say "that's further from market than I expected — here's what I'm seeing" and share your data.
- **A bluffed competing offer.** If they say "we understand, good luck with the other company," your negotiation is over and so is your credibility.
- **"Sorry to ask, but..."** Apologizing frames the ask as unreasonable. It isn't.
- **Your current salary, if you can avoid it.** In many places they can't legally ask. Redirect: "I'm focusing on the market rate for this role, which I'm seeing at $X."
- **"Yes" on the spot to a verbal offer.** You can't negotiate what you've already accepted.
- **An ultimatum you won't keep.** "I can't accept below $X" is only usable if it's true.

## Timing: When Each Move Happens

1. **During interviews:** deflect salary questions or give a researched range with the top anchored high. Never name a firm number before they've decided they want you — your leverage peaks between "we want you" and "you signed."
2. **Offer arrives:** enthusiasm + written offer + 24–48 hours.
3. **Your counter:** one email, one number, one or two reasons.
4. **Their response:** accept, meet in the middle, or trade for non-base items. Aim to close within one round — two at most. Endless rounds sour the relationship you're about to start.
5. **Agreement:** get the final terms in a revised written offer before you resign anywhere.

## If They Won't Move at All

Sometimes the answer is a flat no. Then decide against the number you set before the emotion started. If you accept, accept gracefully and ask for the six-month review in writing. If you decline, decline warmly — recruiters change companies, and the person who couldn't pay you this year may run hiring somewhere better next year.

One more thing: the counter conversation is a performance, and most people have never once said "I was expecting closer to $110,000" out loud before the moment it counts. ## FAQ

### Can negotiating make them withdraw the offer?

A polite, evidence-based counter almost never kills an offer; companies have already spent thousands recruiting you. Offers get pulled over hostility, bluffs that get called, or endless rounds — not over one professional ask.

### Should I negotiate even if the offer is good?

Yes, once. If the number already beats your research, counter modestly or negotiate a non-base item instead. Signing without any ask leaves the built-in room on the table.

### Email or phone — which is better?

Email for the counter itself (precise, forwardable), phone for the resolution if they want to talk. Never negotiate a number you haven't already put in writing.

### How much should I counter above the offer?

5–15% is the normal band for the same role and level. Go beyond that only with strong evidence — a competing offer or clear below-market data — and be ready to show it.
