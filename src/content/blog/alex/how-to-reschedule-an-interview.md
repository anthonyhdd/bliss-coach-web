To reschedule an interview, contact the recruiter or interviewer as soon as you know there's a conflict, apologize once, give a brief honest reason, and immediately offer two or three specific alternative times. Handled this way — early, direct, with alternatives attached — rescheduling is routine and costs you nothing; recruiters juggle calendar changes daily and only remember the candidates who handled it badly.

## The Rules That Make Rescheduling Painless

**Ask the moment you know.** The damage from rescheduling scales with proximity to the interview. A week out is a non-event, and even a genuine same-day emergency is fine if communicated instantly. What burns candidates is sitting on a known conflict, hoping it resolves, and asking at the last minute — or worse, not showing up at all. A no-show ends candidacies; a reschedule almost never does.

**Match the channel to the urgency.** More than 48 hours out, email is right — it gives the coordinator a written record to work from. Within 24 hours, call or do both: phone the recruiter, then send the email as backup. If the interview is within hours, calling is mandatory; an unread email is a no-show.

**Contact the right person.** Reply to whoever scheduled the interview — usually a recruiter or coordinator — not the hiring manager or panel directly, unless the interviewer scheduled with you personally. The coordinator owns the calendars and can rebook a panel far faster than you can.

**Give a reason: brief, honest, no medical file.** One clause is enough — "a family matter," "an illness I don't want to bring into your office," "an unmovable work commitment." Vague-but-honest beats detailed, and detailed beats invented. Fabricated emergencies have a way of surfacing later, and you don't need a dramatic excuse for a routine request.

**Carry the logistics.** The core courtesy move: propose two or three concrete slots in *their* stated availability pattern, and say you'll flex if none work. "Can we find another time?" pushes work onto them; "I could do Tuesday after 2, Wednesday morning, or any time Friday" nearly closes the loop in one email.

**Apologize once, then stop.** One clean apology reads as professional. Three reads as anxious, and turns a calendar footnote into an event.

## Email Templates

### Standard reschedule (2+ days ahead)

> **Subject: Rescheduling request — [Your Name] interview on [date]**
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm looking forward to speaking with the team about the [role] position. Unfortunately, a conflict has come up on my end — [brief reason: an unmovable commitment at my current job / a family matter] — and I need to ask to reschedule our interview on [date/time].
>
> I apologize for the change. I could make any of the following work:
>
> - [Day, date — time range]
> - [Day, date — time range]
> - [Day, date — time range]
>
> If none of those fit the team's calendar, I'm happy to work around whatever is easiest on your side. Thank you for understanding — I remain very interested in the role.
>
> Best,
> [Name]
> [Phone]

### Short notice (within 24 hours — call first, then send)

> **Subject: Urgent — need to reschedule today's [time] interview**
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Following up on my voicemail — I'm very sorry for the short notice, but [I've come down with something I don't want to bring to a video call at half capacity / a family emergency has come up] and I need to move our interview today at [time].
>
> I don't want to lose momentum: I'm available [tomorrow at X], [day at Y], or [day at Z], and can flex to whatever suits the interviewers.
>
> Apologies again for the disruption, and thank you for understanding.
>
> [Name]
> [Phone]

### Sick, but playing through (offer the swap)

If you're unwell but functional, offering a format change instead of a date change sometimes serves everyone:

> Hi [Name] — I've come down with a cold ahead of our on-site on [date]. I'm fully able to interview, but I'd rather not bring it into your office. Would the team prefer to switch to video at the same time, or reschedule the on-site? Either works on my end.

### Phone script (short notice)

Keep the call to twenty seconds of content:

> "Hi [Name], it's [Your Name] — we have an interview scheduled today at [time] for the [role] position. I'm very sorry to do this on short notice, but [reason] and I need to reschedule. I'm free [option 1] or [option 2], or any time that works for the team. Again, I apologize — I'm still very interested in the role."

## Special Situations

**You have a competing offer deadline.** If you're rescheduling to *accelerate* rather than delay, say so plainly — recruiters handle this constantly and most would rather compress their process than lose you silently: "I've received an offer with a deadline of [date]. I'm very interested in this role and don't want to make a decision without completing your process — is there any way to move my interview earlier?"

**The company keeps rescheduling on you.** One company-side reschedule is life; three is data about how the team operates. Stay gracious in every reply, keep interviewing elsewhere, and weigh it as the culture signal it probably is.

**You've already rescheduled once.** A second request needs to be rarer and heavier: only for genuine emergencies, ideally by phone, and with maximum flexibility offered ("I will make any time work, including early mornings or evenings"). Two reschedules is the practical career limit per process; at three, most recruiters quietly conclude the interest isn't real.

**You actually want to withdraw, not reschedule.** If the honest reason for postponing is fading interest, don't burn the recruiter's calendar slots buying yourself time. Withdrawing gracefully preserves the relationship — recruiters move companies and remember names. Say it straight; the same directness rules from [how to decline a job offer politely](/alex/blog/how-to-decline-a-job-offer-politely/) apply one stage earlier.

## After the New Time Is Confirmed

Close the loop and protect the rebooked slot:

- **Confirm in writing** the moment the new time lands — "Confirmed, Thursday the 14th at 10 a.m., video link received. Thank you for accommodating the change." — and accept the calendar invite immediately.
- **Send a brief thank-you** to the coordinator who did the shuffling. Coordinators have long memories and real influence on how your file gets talked about.
- **Treat the new slot as immovable.** Whatever flexibility existed is spent. Build extra buffer around the rebooked time: earlier arrival, tech checked the night before, backup plans for the original conflict.
- **Over-prepare, slightly.** A rescheduled interview sometimes carries a faint question mark; the fastest way to erase it is to walk in unmistakably ready. That's ordinary [interview preparation](/alex/blog/how-to-prepare-for-an-interview/), turned up one notch — and once the interview happens, the normal [follow-up sequence](/alex/blog/how-to-follow-up-after-an-interview/) applies as if the reschedule never occurred.

## Mistakes to Avoid

**Ghosting the slot.** The only truly fatal version of this situation. Any reason, communicated at any point before the start time, beats an empty chair.

**Over-explaining.** A paragraph of medical or personal detail makes the reader uncomfortable and the request feel bigger than it is. One clause of reason; the rest of the email is logistics.

**Rescheduling to buy prep time — repeatedly.** Once, quietly, with a plausible conflict? Recruiters can't read minds. But serial postponement reads exactly like what it is, and an under-prepared interview delivered on time usually beats a polished one delivered after three delays.

**Vague availability.** "Sometime next week?" forces another round-trip and doubles the coordinator's work. Specific slots, spanning at least two different days, with a flexibility fallback.

**Skipping the re-confirmation.** Reschedules are where calendar errors breed — wrong time zones, stale invites, unupdated panelists. The candidate who confirms in writing never shows up to a meeting that moved.

## FAQ

**Will rescheduling an interview hurt my chances?**

One reschedule, requested promptly and handled with alternatives in hand, has essentially no effect — coordinators process these daily. What hurts is the handling: last-minute notice for a long-known conflict, no-showing, vague availability, or a second and third request. The event is neutral; the execution is the signal.

**How late is too late to reschedule?**

There's no "too late" that makes no-showing the better option — a call 30 minutes before start still beats an empty chair by miles. The practical rule: within 24 hours, use the phone; within 2 hours, phone plus email plus, if you have it, a text to the recruiter. Late requests spend more goodwill, so reserve them for genuine emergencies.

**Should I tell the truth if the reason is another company's interview?**

You don't owe the detail, and "a scheduling conflict I can't move" is entirely honest. If the real driver is a competing offer deadline, disclosing it usually *helps* — it signals market validation and lets the recruiter accelerate. What you shouldn't do is invent an illness when the truth is a calendar collision; simple vagueness is safer than fiction.

**What if I wake up sick on interview day?**

Call as early as humanly possible — 7 a.m. voicemail included — then email. For video interviews, consider offering to proceed if you're functional ("I'm under the weather but happy to keep our slot if the team prefers"); many interviewers appreciate the option, and it converts a cancellation into a professionalism signal either way.

**Can I ask to move an interview earlier instead of later?**

Yes, and recruiters generally love it — earlier is the rare reschedule that signals *more* interest, whether driven by a competing deadline or plain enthusiasm. The same rules apply: ask promptly, offer specific slots, and make the coordinator's job as small as possible.
