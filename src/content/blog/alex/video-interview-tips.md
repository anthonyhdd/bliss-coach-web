Video interviews reward preparation that has nothing to do with your qualifications: camera at eye level, light on your face, lens-contact instead of screen-contact, and a rehearsed plan for when the tech fails. Live Zoom calls and one-way async interviews (HireVue, Spark Hire) are different games with different rules — this guide covers both, plus what AI screening actually evaluates in 2026.

## The Setup: Camera, Light, Background

You control the first impression before you say a word. Fifteen minutes of setup fixes 90% of what goes wrong on camera.

### Camera Position

- **Lens at eye level.** The most common mistake is a laptop flat on a desk, which shoots up your nostrils and makes you loom over the interviewer. Stack the laptop on books until the lens matches your eye line.
- **Frame yourself from mid-chest up**, with a little headroom. Too close is confrontational; too far makes you a speck in your own interview.
- **Arm's length from the lens** — roughly 2 to 3 feet. Built-in webcams distort faces at close range.
- **Laptop over phone.** If a phone is your only option, mount it in landscape on a stand — never handheld.

### Lighting

- **Light your face from the front.** The best setup is free: face a window. Daylight in front of you flatters; a window *behind* you turns you into a silhouette.
- **No window? Put a lamp behind your laptop**, pointed at your face, ideally diffused. A $20 ring light works but isn't required.
- **Kill overhead-only lighting.** A ceiling light with nothing in front of you casts eye-socket shadows that read as tired.
- **Test at the actual time of your interview.** A room that's bright at 10 AM can be a cave at 4 PM.

### Background and Sound

- **Real, tidy, and boring beats virtual.** A plain wall, a bookshelf, or a neat room reads as normal. Virtual backgrounds flicker and eat your ears and hair when you move; if your real space is truly unusable, pick a subtle blur over a beach.
- **Wired earbuds or a headset beat laptop audio.** Interviewers forgive bad video far more readily than bad audio.
- **Silence the environment:** phone on do-not-disturb, desktop notifications off, pets out, household warned.

## Eye Contact: The Trick Nobody Teaches

On video, looking at the interviewer's face on screen means you appear to look *below* the camera — slightly down and away, which reads as evasive. Looking into the lens is what registers as eye contact on their side. Since you can't do both at once:

- **Look at the lens while you speak; look at the screen while you listen.** Speaking is when eye contact matters most; watching their reactions while they talk is natural and useful.
- **Shrink and move the interview window** to the top-center of your screen, directly under the camera. This closes the gap so even screen-gazing looks nearly like lens-contact.
- **Put a sticky-note arrow next to the lens.** It sounds silly and it works — a physical reminder to return your gaze when you start an answer.
- **Kill self-view once you've checked your framing.** Watching yourself is the biggest eye-contact thief on the call, and every platform lets you hide it (Zoom: right-click your video → Hide Self View).

Notes are legal on video — one page of bullets taped next to the lens, not a script below the camera. Reading is instantly visible: your eyes track left-to-right and your voice flattens.

## Live Video Interviews (Zoom, Teams, Meet)

- **Do a full dress rehearsal 24 hours out**: same room, same time of day, same platform, recording on. Watch it back once — you'll fix three things you didn't know you did.
- **Join 5 minutes early.** The buffer absorbs an update prompt or a login hiccup.
- **Dress fully, in solid mid-tones.** Fine stripes strobe on camera; pure white blows out. And wear real pants — you may need to stand up.
- **Add 10–15% more energy than feels natural.** Video compresses expressiveness. Sit slightly forward, gesture inside the frame, and nod while listening — on camera, a neutral face reads as bored.
- **Master the latency pause.** Let the interviewer fully finish, count one beat, then answer. If you talk over each other, stop first: "Sorry — please, go ahead."
- **Close every application except the meeting** (and your notes). It protects bandwidth and kills pings.

## One-Way Async Interviews: HireVue, Spark Hire, and the Rest

The one-way interview — recording answers to preset questions with no human on the other end — is now standard at large employers, especially in retail, finance, healthcare, and consulting. Candidates hate it; hiring teams keep it because it screens thousands cheaply. Complaining doesn't advance you. Strategy does.

### How the Format Actually Works

You typically get a question on screen, 30 seconds to 2 minutes of prep, then a fixed window (usually 1–3 minutes) to record. Some platforms allow one or two retakes per question; many allow **zero** — read the on-screen rules before you start, because assuming a retake exists is the most common self-inflicted wound in this format.

### The Strategy

- **Treat the prep timer as an outlining tool.** Jot a three-beat skeleton (situation → action → result) on paper during the countdown, then talk to the skeleton. Do not try to script full sentences in 30 seconds.
- **Bank your stories in advance.** One-way questions are overwhelmingly predictable: tell me about yourself, why this company, a conflict, a failure, a time you led, a time you handled a difficult customer. Prepare six STAR stories and you've covered ~90% of what any platform will throw at you.
- **Fill 80–90% of the time window, not 100%.** Getting cut off mid-sentence is the worst ending; a 15-second buffer lets you land a clean closing line. If you finish early, stop confidently — don't pad.
- **Perform for a camera, not a screen.** With no human feedback, energy decays fast. Smile before you hit record, look at the lens the entire answer, and deliberately re-inject energy at the start of every question — the fifth answer is where most recordings go flat.
- **Use the practice question seriously.** Nearly every platform offers an unscored warm-up. Use it to check framing, audio, and your own on-camera energy, not just the tech.

The hard part of one-ways is producing structured, energetic answers to a countdown clock with zero human feedback — a skill nothing in normal life trains. Doing timed mock interviews with [Alex, an AI interview coach](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6761726840) replicates exactly that pressure: preset questions, a clock, and your own voice, until talking to a lens stops feeling alien.

## AI Screening in 2026: What's Real

Separating fact from folklore matters, because folklore wastes your prep time.

**What's largely gone:** facial-expression and emotion scoring. HireVue dropped facial analysis from its assessments back in 2021 after sustained criticism, and regulatory pressure has pushed the industry the same direction since — [Illinois' AI Video Interview Act](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/ilcs3.asp?ActID=4015) requires notice and consent for AI analysis, NYC's Local Law 144 mandates bias audits of automated hiring tools, and Colorado's AI Act adds broader high-risk obligations in 2026. You are not being penalized for an eyebrow.

**What's real:** language analysis. Modern screening tools transcribe your answers and evaluate the *words* — relevance to the question, structure, concrete detail, and competency keywords — along with basic delivery signals. Some employers also run resume-style AI ranking over the transcripts.

**What this means for you, practically:**

- **Answer the question that was asked**, using some of its own vocabulary. If the question says "conflicting priorities," a transcript that never mentions priorities scores poorly no matter how charming the delivery.
- **Structure wins.** STAR answers with a clear situation, action, and quantified result are exactly what both algorithms and the humans reviewing flagged answers reward. There is no gaming trick beyond being organized and specific.
- **Speak clearly at a moderate pace.** You're generating a transcript; mumbled or rushed audio literally degrades your data.
- **You're usually entitled to know.** If AI evaluation is used, notice-and-consent requirements apply in a growing set of jurisdictions, and asking the recruiter how recordings are evaluated is a legitimate, professional question.

## Tech Failure Recovery Plans

Tech failures don't sink candidates; unprepared reactions do. Interviewers watch you handle a live problem — which is itself a signal. Build these three plans before every video interview.

### Plan A: Prevent

Restart your computer that morning (clears the update that would've auto-installed mid-call), test the specific platform link an hour ahead, plug into power, and sit close to your router — or use ethernet.

### Plan B: Degrade Gracefully

- **Choppy connection:** "My video seems unstable — I'll turn my camera off so the audio stays clear," then continue without missing a beat. Audio continuity outranks video vanity.
- **Frozen platform:** leave and rejoin the meeting. It fixes most client-side glitches in 30 seconds.
- **Audio failure:** switch to the phone dial-in — nearly every calendar invite includes one. Copy that number onto paper before the interview starts.

### Plan C: Full Failure Protocol

Have the recruiter's **phone number and email accessible from your phone** (not the frozen computer). If the meeting dies: message within two minutes — "My connection just dropped; I'm rejoining now, and I'm also reachable at [number] if it's easier to switch to a call." Calm, fast, solution-shaped. Done well, a tech failure becomes a small demonstration of exactly how you handle a production incident, which is not a bad thing for an interviewer to witness.

## FAQ

### Should I use a virtual background for a job interview?

Only if your real environment is genuinely unpresentable — and then use a subtle blur or plain preset, not a beach. Virtual backgrounds glitch around hair and hands; recruiters consistently report preferring a tidy real background.

### How do retakes work on HireVue-style one-way interviews?

It varies by employer configuration: some allow one or two retakes per question, many allow none, and the practice question is almost always unscored. The rules appear on screen before each question — read them every time, and never start recording assuming a do-over exists.

### Where should I look during a video interview?

At the lens while you speak, at the screen while you listen. Move the meeting window to top-center directly below your camera to shrink the gap, and hide self-view once your framing is set — watching yourself is what pulls your eyes away most.

### What should I do in the first minute after my internet dies mid-interview?

Rejoin immediately if anything still works; simultaneously text or email the recruiter from your phone saying you dropped and are coming back, and offer your phone number as a fallback. Speed and calm matter more than the failure itself — interviewers treat a composed recovery as a positive data point.
