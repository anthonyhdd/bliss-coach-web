A panel interview — one candidate, several interviewers at once — is won by treating it as a set of one-on-one conversations happening in the same room, not a presentation to a jury. Answer the person who asked, bring the others in with eye contact, and tune each answer to the agenda of whoever's asking, because every panelist is there to evaluate something different.

## Why Companies Use Panels

Knowing why the format exists tells you how to play it. Companies run panels for three reasons: consistency (everyone hears the same answers, so debriefs compare notes on identical data), efficiency (one hour instead of four), and pressure-testing (some roles — sales, management, consulting — need people who stay composed in front of a room).

The practical implication: your answers will be discussed *collectively* afterward. In a sequence of one-on-ones, you can shade the same story differently for each person. In a panel, one answer serves four scorecards at once — which is exactly why understanding each panelist's agenda matters more here than in any other format.

## Decode the Panel Before You Walk In

Ask the recruiter for names and titles in advance — this is a standard request and almost always granted:

> "Could you share who'll be on the panel and their roles? I'd like to prepare properly."

Then spend fifteen minutes per panelist on their public profiles, and map each one to their likely agenda:

- **Your would-be manager** is scoring competence and coachability. Give them structure, results, and evidence you take feedback well.
- **A senior peer** is scoring "will this person pull their weight, and will I enjoy working with them?" Give them technical specifics and zero arrogance.
- **A cross-functional partner** (the salesperson on an engineering panel, the designer on a PM panel) is scoring whether you respect their discipline. Address their world unprompted at least once.
- **HR or a recruiter** is scoring communication, red flags, and consistency with what you said earlier in the process.
- **A skip-level or executive** is scoring judgment and ceiling. Give them the "why" behind decisions, not just the "what."

When a question comes in, you now know which lens it's coming through — "how do you handle conflicting priorities?" from your manager is about workload; from the cross-functional partner, it's about whether *their* requests go to the bottom of your pile.

## Eye Contact: The 70/30 Rule

The single most-asked question about panels, and the mechanics are simple:

- **Start and end with the asker.** When a panelist asks a question, give them your full attention for the first sentence of your answer and return to them for the final sentence. They asked; they get the bookends.
- **Sweep the room in the middle.** During the body of the answer, move your gaze naturally to each other panelist for a few seconds at a time — roughly 70% asker, 30% distributed. This isn't theatrical scanning; it's the same thing you'd do telling a story at dinner.
- **Don't chase the note-takers.** Panelists writing while you talk is normal and usually good. Keep talking to the humans who are looking at you.
- **Don't play to the boss exclusively.** Candidates who lock onto the most senior person and ignore the junior panelists fail a test they didn't know was running. Panels compare notes, and "ignored me completely" from the peer-level interviewer is a real debrief comment with real consequences. In many loops, any single panelist can sink you.

On video panels, the same rule translates to: look at the camera when delivering key points, glance at the gallery to read reactions, and say names more often since eye contact can't do the distributing for you.

## Using Names and Threading Answers

Two tactics separate polished panel candidates from nervous ones:

**Use names sparingly but deliberately.** Note each panelist's name during introductions (write them down in seating order — nobody minds). Then use them at natural moments: "That connects to what Priya asked earlier..." A name used once per panelist across the hour feels attentive; a name in every sentence feels like a sales seminar.

**Thread your answers.** The highest-value panel move is linking a current answer back to an earlier question:

> "This is actually the flip side of the incident Marcus asked about — same project, but here's where the prioritization call came from."

Threading proves you're tracking the whole conversation, not just surviving question by question, and it quietly flatters the earlier asker in front of their colleagues.

## Handling the Hard Dynamics

**Rapid-fire or interrupting panelists.** Finish your sentence calmly, then take the new question. If two questions arrive at once: "Let me take Sarah's first, then come back to yours." Composure under crossfire is often the actual test.

**The disengaged panelist.** Someone scrolling their laptop isn't necessarily bored — they may be pulling up your resume. Direct one answer partially toward them and one of your closing questions at them by name. That's all the recovery the situation needs.

**The hostile or skeptical panelist.** Answer their challenge with evidence, not defensiveness, and resist the urge to look at friendlier faces for rescue — that reads as retreat. "Good challenge — here's what actually happened when we tested that" wins the room, including the skeptic's colleagues.

**Disagreement between panelists.** Occasionally panelists debate each other through you. Don't pick a side; answer with your own reasoning: "I've seen both approaches work — where I'd land depends on X, and here's why."

**The silence after your answer.** Panels pause while everyone checks notes. Don't fill it by weakening your answer ("...but I don't know, maybe that's wrong"). Let it sit.

## Questions to Ask a Panel

You'll usually get ten minutes at the end. Aim your questions at specific people — it's your last chance to show you registered who they are:

> To your manager: "What would a great first six months look like in this role?"

> To the peer: "What's the honest day-to-day — what would I be doing on a random Tuesday?"

> To the cross-functional partner: "Where do our two teams usually create friction, and what would you want the person in this role to do about it?"

> To the whole panel, as a closer: "You've all seen people succeed and struggle in this role — what separates the two?"

That last one is quietly powerful: every panelist answers it in their head even if only one answers out loud, and their answers tell you exactly what the debrief criteria are.

## Follow-Up After a Panel

Send individual thank-you emails, not one group message — each panelist decided something about you, and each gets their own line of reasoning reinforced. Reference the specific exchange you had with that person: the question they asked, the topic they lit up on. Three sentences each is plenty. If you don't have everyone's email, send them through the recruiter with a note asking them to forward, with each message clearly addressed.

## Practice the Format, Not Just the Answers

Panel skills are mechanical — distributing eye contact, tracking multiple threads, staying composed when questions stack up — and mechanics only improve with reps. Answers that flow one-on-one fall apart when four people are watching, purely from cognitive load. Simulate it: have a friend fire questions from your prepared list without pauses, or run timed mock interviews with [Alex, an AI interview coach](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6761726840) until the answers are automatic enough that your attention is free to manage the room instead of searching for words.

## FAQ

### How is a panel interview different from a group interview?

A panel is several interviewers and one candidate. A group interview is several candidates at once. The tactics above are for panels; group interviews are a different game about standing out among peers.

### How long are panel interviews?

Typically 60–90 minutes. Expect roughly one question-set per panelist plus follow-ups, and protect energy for the final third — the closing questions carry disproportionate weight because they're what the room remembers walking out.

### Who do I address if I'm asked a question by the moderator on behalf of everyone?

Treat the moderator as the asker — bookend with them — and distribute the middle of your answer across the panel as usual.

### Should I bring anything?

Copies of your resume (one per panelist), a notepad with their names in seating order, and your prepared questions. Handing a resume to the one panelist who arrived without it is a small moment that lands well.
