The most reliable signs an interview went well are behavioral: it ran long, the interviewer shifted from grilling you to selling you, they talked about "when" instead of "if," and next steps got specific. No single signal is proof — but clusters of them are meaningful, and knowing which ones actually predict outcomes will save you a week of replaying the conversation at 2 a.m.

## The Signals That Actually Mean Something

### 1. The interview ran over time

Interviewers are busy and protect their calendars. When a 45-minute slot stretches to 65 because the conversation kept going, that's them choosing to spend more time on you. (The inverse — ending 20 minutes early with no explanation — is one of the few genuinely bad signs.)

### 2. They switched from evaluating to selling

Somewhere mid-interview, the tone flips: fewer probing questions, more "let me tell you what I love about working here" and "you'd really like the team." Interviewers sell to candidates they want. If they spent the last third of the meeting pitching you on perks, culture, and growth, you moved into the "want" column.

### 3. "When you" replaced "if you"

Language leaks intent. "When you start, you'd be picking up the Henderson project" is a different sentence than "the person in this role would pick up..." Future-tense, second-person framing — *you'll be working with, your first project, your manager would be* — means they're mentally placing you in the seat.

### 4. Next steps got specific, unprompted

"HR will be in touch" is a form letter. "I'd like you to meet our VP — are you free Thursday or Friday?" is a decision. The more concrete the timeline, names, and scheduling got without you asking, the better.

### 5. They introduced you to people who weren't on the schedule

Being walked over to meet the team, pulled into a quick hello with a director, or shown around the office is an interviewer spending social capital on you. Nobody parades a "no" around the building.

### 6. Deep follow-up questions on your answers

An engaged interviewer digs: "Wait, how exactly did you get the churn number down?" Follow-ups mean your answers were interesting enough to explore. A disengaged interviewer just moves down their list, and you can feel the difference.

### 7. They addressed your potential objections

"I know the commute is long, but we're flexible on remote days" — an interviewer preemptively solving your reasons to say no is negotiating with someone they've already picked.

### 8. Compensation and start-date logistics came up in detail

Surface-level salary screening happens early with everyone. But detailed conversation about notice periods, start dates, or "how would you feel about..." scenarios late in an interview usually means they're costing out an offer.

### 9. Real laughter and personal tangents

Rapport isn't sufficient, but its absence is telling. If you swapped a genuine story, found a shared interest, or the interviewer volunteered something personal, you passed the "do I want to sit next to this person for a year" test that quietly decides close calls.

### 10. They asked about your other processes — with urgency

"Are you interviewing elsewhere? What's your timeline?" asked late in a good conversation isn't small talk; it's them checking how fast they need to move to not lose you.

### 11. The interviewer took notes at the right moments

Notes during your best answers means they're collecting ammunition to advocate for you in the debrief. An empty notepad usually becomes an empty recommendation.

### 12. A fast, warm follow-up

A same-day or next-day email — especially a reply to your thank-you note with actual content in it — beats almost every in-room signal. Speed after the interview correlates with enthusiasm better than anything during it.

## False Positives: Good Vibes That Mean Nothing

- **"You'd be great here" and other compliments.** Some interviewers are warm to everyone; it's professionalism, not a verdict. Skilled interviewers deliberately leave every candidate feeling good — it protects the company's reputation.
- **A friendly, easy conversation with no hard questions.** Sometimes that's rapport. Sometimes they decided "no" in the first ten minutes and coasted through the rest. An easy ride can mean you were never really tested.
- **"We'll definitely be in touch."** Everyone hears this. It's the interview equivalent of "let's grab coffee sometime."
- **A long interview that was long because *you* talked too much.** Overtime only counts when they extended it.
- **The recruiter's enthusiasm.** Recruiters are professionally enthusiastic. Weight the hiring manager's behavior 10x more.

## False Negatives: Bad Vibes That Mean Nothing

- **A tough, cold interviewer.** Some people interview like auditors — with everyone. Several of the best outcomes follow the hardest conversations, because rigorous questioning means you were worth testing.
- **Stumbling on one question.** Candidates replay their worst answer on loop; interviewers score the whole picture. One flubbed question rarely decides anything unless it was the core competency.
- **No immediate feedback or poker face.** Many companies train interviewers to give nothing away, precisely so candidates can't read the room.
- **Silence for a week.** Hiring loops stall for a hundred internal reasons — vacations, budget sign-offs, other candidates finishing loops — that have nothing to do with you.
- **They mentioned other strong candidates.** Sometimes honesty, sometimes negotiation groundwork. Not a rejection.

## What to Do If It Went Well

1. **Send the thank-you email within 24 hours** — short, specific, referencing one real moment from the conversation. Restate your interest in one sentence.
2. **Don't stop your other processes.** A great interview is not an offer. Candidates who coast on one strong signal hand away all their leverage — and their backup options.
3. **Use the momentum.** If you have other interviews mid-process, a strong loop elsewhere is legitimate urgency: "I'm in late stages with another company but you're my first choice" speeds decisions up.
4. **Follow up once** if the stated timeline passes, plus two business days. Polite, one paragraph, zero pressure.

## What to Do If It Went Badly

1. **Write down what actually went wrong within an hour**, while it's specific: the question that broke you, the story that rambled, the moment you knew. This list is your prep syllabus, not a self-flagellation device.
2. **Send the thank-you email anyway.** You lose nothing, occasionally it matters, and your read on the room might simply be wrong — candidates misjudge their own interviews constantly, in both directions.
3. **If one answer truly failed, you can repair once.** A brief note — "I didn't answer the question about X well; on reflection, here's the real example I should have given" — has saved candidacies. Use it only for a substantive miss, not general anxiety.
4. **Fix the weakness before the next loop, out loud.** Whatever broke — rambling stories, a shaky "why us," freezing on scenarios — will break again if you only prep silently. 5. **Ask for feedback if rejected.** Most companies won't answer, but the ones that do give you gold.

## How to Read the Aggregate

Score clusters, not moments. Two or three strong signals from the list above — especially overtime, selling, and specific next steps together — put you in genuinely good shape. One warm compliment surrounded by clock-watching does not. And whatever your read: behave identically. Thank-you note, keep interviewing, follow up on schedule. The candidates who get burned are the ones who let a good feeling stop their search or a bad feeling stop their effort.

## FAQ

### How long after a good interview should I hear back?

Whatever timeline they gave you, plus a few days. Absent a stated timeline, one to two weeks is normal for a decision between rounds. Silence inside that window means nothing.

### Is it a good sign if the interviewer connected on LinkedIn?

Mildly. It signals personal interest, but plenty of interviewers connect with every candidate. Treat it as one weak positive signal, not a verdict.

### The interview was cut short — am I done?

Usually bad if unexplained, neutral if explained ("I have a hard stop, but I've got what I need — you'll hear from us Tuesday"). Specific next steps delivered with the early ending cancel most of the negative signal.

### Should I mention signals I noticed in my follow-up?

No. "It seemed to go well!" reads presumptuous. Keep the follow-up about them: the conversation, the role, your interest.
