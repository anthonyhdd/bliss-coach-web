When the interviewer says "do you have any questions for me?", the interview is not over — you're still being evaluated, and this is also your only chance to evaluate them. Prepare five to eight questions, expect to ask two to four per interviewer, and choose ones that show you're already thinking like someone doing the job. Here are 27 that work, grouped by what they reveal.

## Why This Part of the Interview Matters

Hiring managers consistently read "no, I think you covered everything" as low interest, even from otherwise strong candidates. Good questions do three jobs at once: they signal that you've thought seriously about the role, they generate a real conversation the interviewer will remember, and they surface information you genuinely need before accepting an offer. Bad questions do the opposite — asking something answered on the careers page tells the interviewer you didn't prepare.

One rule governs everything below: ask questions whose answers would actually change your decision or your first months on the job. Interviewers can tell the difference between a question asked to impress and a question asked to learn.

## Questions About the Role

These show you're already picturing yourself doing the work.

1. **What does success look like in this role at 90 days? At one year?** The single best question on this list. It forces the manager to articulate real expectations, and vague answers here are informative.
2. **What's the most urgent problem you need this hire to solve first?** Cuts past the job description to the actual reason the req exists.
3. **What separates someone who's good in this role from someone who's exceptional?** You learn what the manager actually values, which is rarely fully captured in the posting.
4. **What are the biggest obstacles someone in this role runs into?** Invites honesty about the hard parts before you sign up for them.
5. **How does this role's work get prioritized — who decides what I'd work on?** Reveals autonomy level and how much of your time is spoken for.
6. **Is this a new role or a backfill? If a backfill, where did the last person go?** A promotion is a great answer. A vague answer about a short tenure is worth noting.
7. **Which teams or stakeholders would I work with most closely?** Shows systems thinking and tells you where the friction will live.

## Questions About the Team and Manager

You're interviewing your future manager as much as they're interviewing you.

8. **How would you describe your management style?** Direct, fair, and revealing — especially in how comfortably they answer.
9. **How does the team handle disagreement about technical or product decisions?** Healthy teams have a real answer. Unhealthy ones say "we don't really disagree."
10. **What's the team's biggest win in the last six months? Biggest miss?** The miss half is the test: a manager who can't name one isn't being straight with you.
11. **How is the team structured, and how has it changed in the past year?** Rapid reorgs or heavy turnover show up in this answer.
12. **How does the team communicate day to day — meetings, async, documentation?** Tells you what your actual daily experience will be.
13. **What do you enjoy most about working here — and what would you change?** Personal, disarming, and the "what would you change" half often produces the most honest sentence of the interview.

## Questions About Growth and Development

These signal you plan to stay and get better, which is exactly what managers want to hear.

14. **What does the promotion path from this role look like, and have people on the team followed it?** "Have people actually done it" is the part that matters.
15. **How do you invest in people's development — budget, mentorship, stretch projects?** Concrete answers beat "we really value learning."
16. **How are performance reviews structured, and how often do they happen?** You learn whether feedback is a system or an afterthought.
17. **Where have people who excelled in this role ended up?** Trajectory data, straight from the source.
18. **What skills do you think will matter most on this team a year from now?** Shows forward thinking and tells you where the team is heading.

## Red-Flag Detector Questions

Polite on the surface, diagnostic underneath. Watch how they answer as much as what they say.

19. **What's the biggest challenge the team or company is facing right now?** Every honest answer is useful; a claim that there are no challenges is itself a red flag.
20. **How has the team's headcount changed over the past year?** Surfaces layoffs, freezes, or churn without asking about them directly.
21. **What does a typical week look like in terms of hours and after-hours expectations?** Ask it neutrally. Hedging, laughter, or "we work hard, play hard" tells you what you need to know.
22. **How do decisions get made when leadership and the team disagree?** Reveals whether the team has real input or just receives orders.
23. **What happened with the last person who left the team, and why did they leave?** Uncomfortable but fair, and experienced managers respect it.
24. **How stable is the funding or budget for this team's roadmap?** Especially important at startups and in cost-center teams. Evasiveness here is a signal.
25. **When something goes wrong in production or with a client, what does the response look like?** You're listening for blame culture versus learning culture.

## Closing Questions

Save one of these for the end.

26. **Is there anything about my background that gives you hesitation, that I could address now?** Bold, and not for everyone, but it can convert a silent objection into a conversation while you're still in the room.
27. **What are the next steps in the process, and what's your timeline?** Always ask this last. It's expected, it's practical, and it sets up your follow-up.

## Questions to Avoid

**Anything Google answers in ten seconds.** "What does your company do?" or anything covered on the first page of the careers site signals zero preparation.

**Salary, PTO, and perks in the first interview.** These are legitimate questions with a proper venue: the recruiter screen or the offer stage. Asking the hiring manager about vacation policy in round one reads as caring about everything except the work. (If a recruiter asks about your expectations, that's a different conversation — answer it there.)

**"What's the culture like?"** Not because culture doesn't matter, but because the question is so vague it only produces the word "collaborative." Ask question 9, 13, or 25 instead — they get you the real answer.

**Interrogation stacking.** Firing off six red-flag detectors in a row turns a conversation into a deposition. Mix diagnostic questions with genuine curiosity about the work.

**Questions already answered earlier in the interview.** Asking something the interviewer explained twenty minutes ago is worse than asking nothing. Take mental notes throughout, and if your prepared question got answered, say so: "You covered my question about the roadmap earlier — instead, I'm curious about..." That one sentence demonstrates you were listening.

## How Many Questions Should You Ask?

Prepare five to eight, ask two to four per interviewer. Fewer than two reads as disengaged; more than five usually blows the schedule and dilutes your best questions. In a multi-round loop, distribute deliberately: role and expectations questions for the hiring manager, day-to-day and collaboration questions for peers, strategy and stability questions for senior leaders and skip-levels.

Order matters too. Lead with your strongest role question, use the middle slots for whatever you most need to learn, and always end with next steps.

One more thing: delivery counts. A great question asked in a flat, memorized tone lands worse than a decent question asked with genuine curiosity, and the follow-up you ask after their answer matters more than the opening question itself. ## FAQ

### What if all my questions were answered during the interview?

Never say "no questions." Acknowledge it and go deeper: "You actually covered most of what I'd planned to ask. One thing I'd love to expand on — you mentioned the migration project. What makes it the top priority this quarter?" Building a question from something they said is the strongest move available.

### Should I ask every interviewer the same questions?

You can repeat one or two on purpose — asking both the manager and a peer "what does success look like at 90 days?" and comparing answers is genuinely useful. But interviewers often debrief together, so vary most of your list per round.

### Is it okay to take notes during their answers?

Yes, and it signals seriousness. In person, ask first ("Do you mind if I jot this down?"). On video, say you're taking notes so the typing or looking down isn't misread.

### When is it appropriate to ask about salary?

With the recruiter, at the screening stage, or once an offer is in motion. Recruiters expect and often initiate the compensation conversation. The hiring manager interview is for the work; the offer stage is for the money — and your leverage is highest after they've decided they want you.
