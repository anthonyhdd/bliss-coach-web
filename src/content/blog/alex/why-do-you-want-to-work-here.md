"Why do you want to work here?" is a specificity test. The interviewer wants proof that you're pursuing this company for identifiable reasons — not spraying the same application at forty employers. A strong answer connects three points: something concrete about the company, something concrete about the role, and something concrete about you. Hit all three in under 90 seconds and you've cleared a bar most candidates miss.

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Asking

Nobody expects you to have dreamed about their company since childhood. The question does three jobs:

- **Effort check.** Did you spend even 15 minutes learning what we do? A shocking share of candidates can't describe the company's product in one sentence.
- **Retention check.** People who join for specific reasons stay longer. People who join because "it seemed fine" leave in a year, and replacing you is expensive.
- **Judgment check.** Can you connect facts about us to facts about you? That's the same skill you'll use to connect customer needs to solutions, or business goals to projects.

Hiring managers hear "I'm passionate about your mission" a dozen times a week. What they almost never hear is a candidate who names the mission, ties it to the role's actual responsibilities, and explains why their own track record makes the fit obvious. That's the whole game.

## The Company-Role-You Triangle

Every strong answer has three vertices. Miss one and the answer collapses into a cliché.

### Vertex 1: The Company

One specific, verifiable thing that genuinely draws you: a product decision, a market position, a recent launch, an engineering blog post, an earnings-call priority, how they handled a public stumble. It must be something you couldn't say about their competitor. "You're a leader in the industry" fails this test. "You were the first grocery chain to put pharmacists on the floor instead of behind glass" passes.

### Vertex 2: The Role

Why this seat, not just this building. Pull one or two responsibilities straight from the job description and explain why they excite you more than the equivalent role elsewhere. If the posting emphasizes ownership of a migration, or a new market, or a zero-to-one product, say so and say why that appeals to you specifically.

### Vertex 3: You

The evidence. One accomplishment or experience that proves you can deliver on the thing you just said excites you. This vertex converts the answer from flattery into a pitch: *you need X, I've done X, and here's the receipt.*

Then close the triangle in one sentence: "That's why this feels like the right move — you're doing [company thing], the role owns [role thing], and I've spent three years getting good at exactly that."

## Research Shortcuts: 15 Minutes, Three Sources

You don't need a weekend of research. You need three inputs.

### The 10-K or About Page (5 minutes)

For public companies, skim the 10-K annual report on [SEC EDGAR](https://www.sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar?action=getcompany) — read only Item 1 (Business) and the first page of Risk Factors. It tells you, in the company's own words, how they make money and what keeps leadership up at night. Referencing a strategic priority from the 10-K instantly separates you from every candidate who only read the careers page. For private companies, the About page, the founder's LinkedIn posts, and the most recent funding announcement do the same job.

### Recent Press and Announcements (5 minutes)

Search the company name filtered to the last 90 days. You're looking for one event: a product launch, an acquisition, an expansion, a new executive, a partnership. Recency signals you did the research *for this interview*, not three jobs ago.

### The Product Itself (5 minutes)

Use it. Download the app, walk the store, book a demo, read the docs. Firsthand observations are the rarest ingredient in these answers — "I ran your onboarding flow last night and noticed X" lands harder than any statistic, because almost nobody does it.

## 5 Example Answers

Each example follows the triangle: company → role → you. Adapt the structure, not the words.

### Tech (Software Engineer)

"Three things. First, your engineering blog — the post on how you cut checkout latency by rebuilding the payments service told me this is a team that solves problems in public and cares about craft. Second, this role owns the migration off the legacy monolith, and I've spent the last two years doing exactly that at a fintech: we moved 40 services with zero customer-facing downtime. Third, I want my next role to be somewhere infrastructure is the product, not a cost center — and your last two funding announcements make it clear that's the bet you're making. I've done this work; I want to do it here, at your scale."

### Retail (Store Manager)

"I've shopped this location for four years, and I've watched it stay fully staffed and genuinely friendly while competitors up the road cycle through employees every quarter. That doesn't happen by accident — it's a management culture, and your promote-from-within policy on the careers page confirms it. This posting emphasizes reducing shrink and improving weekend conversion, and at my current store I cut shrink 18% in a year by rebuilding the receiving process. I want to manage for a retailer that treats stores as careers, not shifts — everything I can see from the outside says that's you."

### Healthcare (Registered Nurse)

"Two reasons. Your Magnet designation and your published nurse-to-patient ratios — I've worked units at 1-to-7 and units at 1-to-4, and the difference in patient outcomes and staff burnout is enormous. You've made a structural commitment to safe staffing, and that's the single biggest factor in where I'll spend the next decade of my career. And this med-surg position mentions the new clinical ladder program: I precepted six new grads at my current hospital and found I'm at my best developing newer nurses. I want to do that somewhere the institution invests in it formally."

### Startup (Operations / Generalist)

"I've followed the company since your Series A announcement, and what convinced me was the pivot you made last year — moving from consumer to B2B when the unit economics said so. That's a leadership team that follows evidence instead of ego. At this stage you need people who ship without a playbook, and that's been my whole career: at my last company I was employee 11, built the entire fulfillment operation from a spreadsheet, and handed off a documented system to the team of eight I hired. I'm looking for one more zero-to-one build, and this is the one I want."

### Career Changer (Teacher → Customer Success)

"Honestly, your product is why I'm changing careers instead of just changing schools. I used your platform for three years as a high school teacher, I onboarded two departments onto it voluntarily, and I watched teachers who feared technology become power users. Customer success is teaching — diagnosing where someone is stuck, meeting them there, and getting them to independent mastery. I've done that with 150 students a semester for eight years. The domain knowledge your CS team needs — how schools buy, how teachers adopt, what makes a rollout die in October — I have firsthand. Nobody on the outside can fake that."

Answers like these only sound natural after you've said them out loud a few times — reading a script in your head is not the same as delivering it under pressure. ## What Kills the Answer

Five failure modes, all common, all avoidable:

- **The generic compliment.** "You're an industry leader with a great culture." If the sentence works for any company in the sector, it counts for nothing. Interviewers pattern-match this to *did not prepare*.
- **The me-first answer.** "The salary is competitive, the commute is short, and the benefits are strong." All true, all rational — and all about what the company does for you. Save it for the offer negotiation. This question is about what you do for them.
- **The desperation tell.** "I really need a job right now." Even when true, it reframes you from *chosen* to *settling*, and it hands away all your negotiating leverage in one sentence.
- **The uninformed superlative.** "I've always admired this company" — followed by an inability to name a single product. This is worse than saying nothing, because it's a claim the interviewer can puncture with one follow-up.
- **Trash-talking your current employer.** "Anywhere is better than where I am now" makes the answer about escaping, not arriving, and tells the interviewer how you'll talk about *them* in two years.

## Tailor It in 10 Minutes Per Interview

The triangle makes tailoring fast. Keep the "you" vertex — your evidence — mostly stable, and rebuild the other two per company:

1. Pull one specific fact from the 10-K, press, or product (5 min).
2. Pull one responsibility from the job description (2 min).
3. Connect both to your strongest relevant accomplishment (3 min).
4. Say it out loud twice. If it runs over 90 seconds, cut the weakest sentence.

Do this before every interview and "why do you want to work here?" stops being a trap and becomes the easiest 90 seconds of your day.

## FAQ

### How long should my answer be?

Sixty to ninety seconds — roughly 150 to 220 words spoken. Under a minute feels thin; over two minutes and you're monologuing. One company point, one role point, one proof point, one closing sentence.

### What if I mostly want the job for the money or location?

Those can be real motivators without being your *answer*. Find the secondary reasons — there's always something about the role, product, or team you genuinely prefer over the alternatives — and lead with those. You're not lying; you're choosing which true things to emphasize.

### Is "why do you want to work here?" different from "why should we hire you?"

Yes. "Why here" is about your motivation and fit — the triangle. "Why you" is about your qualifications versus other candidates — lead with evidence and differentiation. If you answer one with the other, the interviewer will usually re-ask.

### What if the company is small and there's little public information?

That's an advantage: the bar for research is lower and the payoff is higher. Study the founders' LinkedIn activity, any podcast or local press appearances, the product itself, and reviews from customers. One firsthand product observation beats any statistic you'd have pulled from a 10-K.
