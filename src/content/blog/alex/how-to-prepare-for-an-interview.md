To prepare for an interview in 48 hours, split the time into three blocks: research the company and role (day one, morning), build and rehearse your answers out loud (day one afternoon through day two), and lock down logistics the night before. Preparation beats talent in interviews because most of what gets scored — clear stories, informed questions, calm delivery — is manufactured in the two days before, not in the room.

## Hour 0–4: The Research Block

The goal of research is not trivia. It's three usable outputs: a reason you want *this* job, an opinion about their business, and a prediction of what they'll ask.

### The research checklist

**The job description — line by line.** Highlight every skill and responsibility mentioned. The top three or four are your question forecast: each one will generate at least one interview question. Note the exact vocabulary they use — mirroring their language ("client success" vs "account management") makes your answers land as relevant.

**The company, in four layers:**

1. **What they sell and to whom.** If you can't explain their business model in two sentences, keep reading.
2. **What's happening now.** Recent launches, funding, leadership changes, layoffs, expansions — their newsroom, LinkedIn page, and a news search cover this in twenty minutes. One recent development, mentioned naturally, separates you from candidates who prepared in 2019.
3. **The product itself.** Use it if humanly possible. Download the app, book a demo, walk the store, read the docs. "I tried your product and noticed..." is the single highest-value sentence available to an interviewee, and almost nobody says it.
4. **The competitive picture.** Know two or three competitors and one honest sentence about how this company is different. You'll use it when they ask "why us?" — and you'll sound like an insider instead of an applicant.

**Your interviewers, by name.** Ask the recruiter who you're meeting if you don't know. Ten minutes per person on their public profile tells you their background, their tenure, and what they'll care about. A former engineer turned PM will probe differently than a career salesperson.

**The salary range.** Research it now, not when the question ambushes you. Posted ranges, comparison sites, and your network give you a defensible band in under half an hour.

Write the three outputs down: *why this company* (two sentences, specific), *one opinion or observation* about their product or market, and *the four questions this job description predicts*.

## Hour 4–8: Build Your Story Bank

Almost every interview question is one of three types: "tell me about yourself" (your pitch), "tell me about a time when..." (behavioral), or "how would you handle..." (scenario). All three run on prepared raw material.

**Your pitch — 60 to 90 seconds.** Present, past, future: what you do now, the two career beats that led here, and why this role is the logical next step. Write it, cut it by a third, and tie the "future" part to the specific job.

**Six to ten stories.** From your last few years, pick specific events — not patterns — that cover the standard behavioral territory:

- A result you're proud of (with a number)
- A conflict or difficult person, resolved
- A failure or mistake, with what changed after
- Leading or influencing without authority
- Working under pressure or a hard deadline
- Persuading someone senior
- Learning something fast

Structure each as STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result — with the setup kept to two sentences and the result carrying a number wherever one exists. Don't script sentences; script the four beats. One page of bullets covers the whole bank.

**Your answers to the fixed questions.** Some questions are so predictable that improvising them is malpractice: "Why do you want to work here?" "Why are you leaving?" "What's your weakness?" "What are your salary expectations?" "Why should we hire you?" Draft each in three or four bullets. Your research block already wrote half of them.

**Five questions to ask them.** Interviewers score your questions. Prepare more than you need, because some get answered mid-conversation. The strongest ones probe the role's reality: "What separates people who succeed in this role from people who struggle?" — "What would the first 90 days look like?" — "What's the team's biggest challenge this quarter?"

## Hour 8 Onward: Rehearse Out Loud — This Is the Step People Skip

Reading your notes is not preparation; it's the illusion of preparation. Stories that read tight run ninety seconds too long when spoken. Answers that feel obvious in your head come out unordered the first time they pass through your mouth. The first out-loud rep is always the worst one — the entire point of rehearsal is making sure that rep doesn't happen in the interview.

**The minimum effective practice:**

1. **Say your pitch out loud three times**, timed. Under 90 seconds or keep cutting.
2. **Run every story once, out loud, against a timer.** Two minutes maximum each. Where you ramble, cut setup, not action.
3. **Do at least one full mock interview** — questions arriving in random order, no pausing, no restarting. This is the difference between knowing your material and retrieving it under pressure, and it's where the real gaps show up. A friend reading your predicted-questions list works; a realistic AI mock interviewer like [Alex](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6761726840) works when no friend is available at 10 p.m. — it plays the recruiter, asks follow-ups, and doesn't let you restart an answer.
4. **Rehearse the two questions you're dreading.** Everyone has them — the resume gap, the short stint, the layoff. Draft a two-sentence, no-apology answer and say it out loud until it's boring to you. Dread compounds in silence and dissolves with reps.

If the interview is remote, one rehearsal must happen on camera: your answers, your eye line, your framing.

## The Night Before: Logistics Checklist

Every item here prevents a failure mode that has actually killed interviews:

- **Confirm time, time zone, and format.** Reread the invitation. Calendar-app time zone errors end candidacies.
- **In person:** route planned, buffer of 30 minutes, building entry instructions read (badge? lobby check-in? which entrance?). Arrive at the *area* early; walk into the *lobby* ten minutes early, no more.
- **Remote:** test the exact link, camera, and mic. Charge the laptop *and* plug it in. Clean the visible background. Close notification-generating apps. Phone as backup hotspot if your Wi-Fi has ever betrayed you.
- **Print or open in tabs:** your resume, the job description, your story-bank page, your questions for them. In person, bring paper copies of the resume.
- **Clothes chosen tonight**, one notch above the company's daily dress code.
- **Names memorized:** interviewers' names and titles, plus how to pronounce them.
- **Sleep beats cramming.** After the logistics pass, close everything. A rested brain retrieving 90% of your prep outperforms an exhausted one holding 100%.

## Day Of: The Last 60 Minutes

- **Re-read only three things:** your pitch, your "why this company," and your questions for them. No new material — last-minute cramming adds noise, not signal.
- **Warm up your voice.** Say your pitch out loud once. First words of the day shouldn't happen in the interview.
- **Arrive with two minutes of calm**, not zero. Box breathing (four counts in, hold four, out four) is unglamorous and works.
- **Bring the mindset the prep earned you:** you're not begging for a verdict, you're two professionals checking fit. Everything above exists to make that posture true rather than performed.

## After: Close the Loop

Within an hour, write down every question they asked and where you were weak — this list is the prep file for round two, and interview questions repeat across companies far more than people expect. Within 24 hours, send a short thank-you email referencing one specific moment from the conversation. Then keep your other processes moving; preparation applies to pipelines, not just conversations.

## FAQ

### How many hours should I prepare for an interview?

For a role you want: 8–12 focused hours across two days, weighted toward out-loud practice. Research past the four-hour mark has sharply diminishing returns; rehearsal almost never does.

### What if I only have one day — or one evening?

Compress, don't skip: one hour of research (job description + company now + product), one hour building your pitch and three stories, and whatever remains on out-loud reps. If you must cut something, cut research before you cut rehearsal.

### Is it possible to over-prepare?

You can over-*script* — memorized sentences sound flat and shatter under follow-ups. Prepare structures and bullets, not paragraphs. There's no such thing as too many out-loud reps of unscripted answers.

### What should I prepare for a second-round interview?

Everything above still applies, plus: reread your notes from round one, prepare new stories (don't reuse), and expect deeper scenario questions about their specific problems. Consistency between rounds gets checked — interviewers compare notes.
