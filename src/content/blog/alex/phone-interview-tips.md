A phone screen is a 20-to-30-minute elimination round, and it filters on different things than the interviews that follow: basic qualifications, communication, salary alignment, and genuine interest. You don't win the job on a phone screen — you can only lose it. These 15 tips are organized around the one goal that matters: getting to round two.

## What Phone Screens Actually Filter For

Recruiters often run 5 to 15 screens per open role and advance a fraction of them. Knowing the rubric changes how you prepare. A screener is checking four boxes:

- **Baseline qualifications.** Do you actually have the experience the resume claims? Can you speak fluently about your own work?
- **Communication.** Can you answer a question in under two minutes without rambling or needing it repeated? This is the number one differentiator on a call with no body language.
- **Logistics and salary.** Are your expectations, location, work authorization, and start date compatible? Mismatches here end candidacies quietly and politely.
- **Signal of real interest.** Do you know what the company does? Candidates who confuse the company with another one they applied to get cut on the spot — and it happens more than you'd think.

Notice what's *not* on the list: deep technical mastery, culture fit, leadership philosophy. Those come later. The screen rewards preparation and clarity, not brilliance. That's good news — preparation and clarity are entirely controllable.

## Before the Call: The Prep Sheet (Tips 1–5)

**1. Build a one-page prep sheet and keep it in front of you.** This is the phone screen's unfair advantage — you have open notes and the interviewer can't see them. One page, large type, five sections: (a) the company in one sentence and one recent news item, (b) three bullets on why this role, (c) your 60-second "tell me about yourself," (d) two accomplishment stories with numbers, (e) your salary range and three questions to ask. One page, not five — if you're reading paragraphs aloud, it's audible.

**2. Re-read the exact job posting an hour before.** Not your memory of it. Screeners frequently ask "what interested you about this position?" and the strongest answers quote the posting's own language back — the responsibilities, the team, the scope.

**3. Rehearse your 60-second intro out loud.** "Tell me about yourself" opens nearly every screen. Present role, one quantified win, why this opportunity. If you've only rehearsed it in your head, it will come out 50% longer and 100% mushier on the call.

**4. Prepare your salary number before the phone rings.** Not during. Research the band on [levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/) or the Bureau of Labor Statistics wage data, pick a researched range, and write it on the prep sheet. More on delivery below — but deciding the number mid-call is how people anchor themselves $20K low.

**5. Control the environment.** Quiet room, closed door, phone charged, earbuds tested, a glass of water. If you're caught off guard by an unscheduled call, don't wing it in a parking lot — say "I want to give this my full attention; can we talk at 3 PM?" Recruiters respect this. A rescheduled screen beats a flustered one every time.

## During the Call: Energy and Voice (Tips 6–9)

On the phone, your voice carries 100% of the impression. Everything your posture and face normally communicate has to travel through audio.

**6. Stand up, or at least sit up straight.** This sounds like folk wisdom; it isn't. Standing opens your diaphragm and measurably adds volume and energy to your voice. Call-center trainers have used this for decades because it works.

**7. Smile while you talk.** A smile changes the shape of your vocal tract and listeners can hear it. Flat, low-energy delivery is one of the most common reasons otherwise-qualified candidates don't advance — screeners read it as low interest, and low interest is a filter criterion.

**8. Answer in 60–90 seconds, then stop.** Without visual cues, you can't see the interviewer nodding or glazing over, so candidates over-talk to fill silence. Deliver the answer, land the point, and stop. If they want more, they'll ask. A useful closer: "Happy to go deeper on any part of that."

**9. Don't fear the pause.** Two seconds of silence after a question feels endless on the phone and completely normal to the other person, who is usually typing notes. "Good question — let me think for a second" is a perfectly professional sentence. Rushed answers ramble; considered answers advance.

## Recruiter Screen vs. Hiring Manager Screen (Tips 10–12)

**10. Know which call you're on, because the rubric changes.** A *recruiter* screen is logistics and fit: walk me through your resume, why are you leaving, what's your salary expectation, when could you start, are you interviewing elsewhere. A *hiring manager* screen is a shallow-but-real competence check: how would you approach X, tell me about a time you did Y, what's your experience with the tools in the posting. Check the calendar invite for the caller's title and prep accordingly.

**11. With recruiters, be clean and consistent.** The recruiter's job is to pass along a tidy summary with no red flags. Give crisp answers on timeline, location, authorization, and compensation, and keep your story consistent — what you tell the recruiter will be repeated to the hiring manager, and discrepancies surface later. Never treat the recruiter as a formality; they hold a veto.

**12. With hiring managers, bring one great story and real questions.** You need a specific, quantified accomplishment relevant to their biggest pain point (the job posting tells you what it is), and one or two questions that prove you think about the work: "What does the first 90 days look like?" or "What's the biggest challenge for whoever takes this role?" Hiring managers advance people they can already picture on the team.

The gap between knowing these answers and delivering them smoothly under pressure is rehearsal. Running a few mock phone screens with [Alex, an AI interview coach](https://apps.apple.com/app/id6761726840) lets you practice the recruiter questions and the hiring-manager follow-ups out loud, which is the only format that actually prepares you for a voice-only call.

## The Salary Question (Tip 13)

**13. Give a researched range, not a dodge and not a single number.** The salary question is a standard screen component, and pure evasion ("I'd rather discuss that later") can read as unprepared or difficult — screens exist partly to check this alignment. The strong play:

*"Based on my research for this role and market, I'm targeting $95K to $110K, but I'm flexible for the right opportunity. Can you share the budgeted range for the position?"*

Three things happen: you demonstrate preparation, you anchor at your researched number rather than your current salary, and you flip the question — many recruiters will share the band when asked directly. Two more rules: never state your *current* salary as your expectation (you'd anchor to your old job's number, and in many US states employers can't even ask for salary history), and put the bottom of your stated range at or slightly above the number you'd actually be happy with.

## After the Call (Tips 14–15)

**14. Close by asking about next steps.** Before hanging up: "This was great — what are the next steps in the process, and what's your timeline?" You get intel, you signal interest, and you earn a legitimate reason to follow up on a specific date instead of guessing.

**15. Send a short thank-you email within 24 hours.** Three sentences: thanks for the time, one specific thing from the conversation that increased your interest, and confirmation that you're excited about next steps. For a phone screen, brevity is the feature — this is a professionalism signal, not an essay. If the stated timeline passes with no word, one polite nudge a few business days later is appropriate; then let it breathe.

## The Mindset That Ties It Together

Treat the phone screen as its own event with its own rules: open notes allowed, voice is everything, clarity beats depth, and the only objective is the next round. Candidates who prepare a one-page sheet, rehearse out loud, handle the salary question with a researched range, and close by asking about next steps advance at a dramatically higher rate than candidates with identical resumes who wing it. The screen doesn't reward the best candidate — it rewards the best-prepared caller.

## FAQ

### How long does a phone screen usually last?

Fifteen to thirty minutes for a recruiter screen; hiring manager screens sometimes run 30 to 45. If a scheduled 30-minute call ends at minute 12 with no questions about your experience, that's usually a soft rejection signal — but a full-length call that covers logistics thoroughly is a good sign even if it felt easy.

### Should I take the call if a recruiter phones without warning?

No — unless you're genuinely ready. Say you're glad they called, you're very interested, and propose a time later that day or the next. An enthusiastic reschedule reads as professional. An unprepared, distracted screen can end your candidacy, and you never get a do-over.

### What questions should I ask at the end of a phone screen?

Two or three, matched to the caller. For recruiters: process, timeline, and what the hiring manager values most. For hiring managers: the team's biggest current challenge, what success looks like at 90 days, and why the role is open. Skip compensation-and-benefits deep dives unless they raise it — that conversation belongs later in the process.

### Is it okay to use notes during a phone interview?

Yes — it's the phone screen's biggest structural advantage and you should absolutely exploit it. The one rule: notes are prompts, not scripts. Bullet points you glance at sound natural; paragraphs you read aloud are obvious within one sentence.
