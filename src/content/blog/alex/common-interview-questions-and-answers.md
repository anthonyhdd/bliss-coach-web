The most common interview questions fall into three buckets: fit ("tell me about yourself," "why this company"), behavioral ("tell me about a time…"), and self-assessment ("what's your greatest weakness"). Below are 20 questions you'll actually be asked, what each one is really testing, and a template you can adapt to your own experience in an afternoon.

## How to Prepare (Before the Question List)

Interviewers aren't looking for the "right" answer. They're looking for signal: can you do the job, will you work well with the team, and will you stick around. Every question below is a proxy for one of those three concerns.

The trick is to prepare frameworks, not scripts. Memorized answers sound flat and fall apart the moment the interviewer asks a follow-up. Learn what each question is testing, prepare a specific real example, and let the wording come out fresh in the room.

For behavioral questions — anything starting with "tell me about a time" — use the STAR structure: **Situation, Task, Action, Result.** Two sentences of context, one sentence on your specific responsibility, most of your time on what you personally did, and an outcome with a number in it.

## The Fit Questions

These open most interviews and set the tone.

### 1. "Tell me about yourself."

**Testing:** Can you frame your own story with focus, and is it relevant to this role?

**Template:** Present, then past, then future. "Right now I'm a [role] at [company], where I [one signature accomplishment]. Before that I [one line of relevant background]. I'm looking to move into [this role] because [specific reason tied to the job]." Keep it to 60–90 seconds. Do not recite your résumé top to bottom.

### 2. "Why do you want to work here?"

**Testing:** Did you research us, or are you spraying applications?

**Template:** Name something specific — a product, a value, a recent launch, the team's mission — and connect it to what you want to do. "I've followed [specific thing]. I want to work on [specific problem] because [personal reason]." Vague flattery ("great culture") signals you didn't look.

### 3. "Why are you leaving your current job?"

**Testing:** Are you running from something, or toward something? Will you badmouth us next?

**Template:** Stay positive and forward-looking. "I've learned a lot at [company], and I'm ready for [specific growth this role offers]." Never criticize a current employer, even if it's justified. It reads as a risk.

### 4. "Where do you see yourself in five years?"

**Testing:** Ambition, realism, and whether your path fits the role.

**Template:** Show direction without boxing yourself in. "I want to deepen my expertise in [area] and grow into [reasonable next level]. This role is a strong step because [reason]." Don't say "in your job" (threatening) or "I have no idea" (aimless).

## The Behavioral Questions

This is the bulk of most structured interviews. All of these use STAR.

### 5. "Tell me about a time you faced a conflict at work."

**Testing:** Emotional maturity and whether you resolve or escalate.

**Template:** Pick a real disagreement, show you sought the other person's view, and end with a functional resolution. Focus on what *you* did to de-escalate, not who was right.

### 6. "Tell me about a time you failed."

**Testing:** Accountability and whether you learn.

**Template:** Choose a real failure you owned, show what you did to fix it, and finish with the specific lesson you now apply. Avoid the humble-brag ("I work too hard").

### 7. "Describe a time you led a project or team."

**Testing:** Ownership, and whether you drive outcomes.

**Template:** Show initiative, name your specific contribution, and quantify the result. If you led without formal authority, say so — that's stronger, not weaker.

### 8. "Tell me about a time you worked under pressure or a tight deadline."

**Testing:** Composure and prioritization.

**Template:** Describe how you triaged, what you cut, and how you communicated. End with the deadline being met and what system you built so it wouldn't recur.

### 9. "Give an example of a goal you achieved."

**Testing:** Drive and follow-through.

**Template:** Pick a goal with a measurable target, describe the plan you executed, and land on the number you hit.

### 10. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager."

**Testing:** Can you push back respectfully without insubordination?

**Template:** Show you raised the concern with data, listened to their reasoning, and either changed their mind or committed to their call gracefully. "Disagree and commit" is the ideal ending.

## The Self-Assessment Questions

### 11. "What is your greatest strength?"

**Testing:** Self-awareness and relevance.

**Template:** Pick one strength the job actually needs, then prove it with a one-line example. "My strength is [X]. For instance, [specific proof]." One strength, backed by evidence, beats a list.

### 12. "What is your greatest weakness?"

**Testing:** Honesty and self-improvement — not the weakness itself.

**Template:** Name a real, non-fatal weakness, then describe the concrete steps you're taking to manage it. "I used to [weakness]. I've addressed it by [specific action], and now [improvement]." Never fake-weakness ("I'm a perfectionist").

### 13. "What are your salary expectations?"

**Testing:** Are you informed, and are we in the same range?

**Template:** Research the market band first. Give a range anchored to your research, or deflect politely early in the process: "I'd like to understand the full role first, but based on my research the range is [X–Y]. Is that in line with your budget?"

### 14. "Why should we hire you?"

**Testing:** Can you make your own case concisely?

**Template:** Match your top two or three qualifications directly to the job's key requirements. "You need someone who can [requirement]. I've done exactly that: [proof]. Plus [second qualification]."

### 15. "What motivates you?"

**Testing:** Will the day-to-day work of this role actually energize you?

**Template:** Be honest and tie it to the job. If solving hard problems drives you, say so and give an example. Don't say "money" as the primary driver.

## The Closers

### 16. "Do you have any questions for us?"

**Testing:** Genuine interest and how you think.

**Template:** Always have three ready. Ask about the team's biggest challenge, what success looks like in 90 days, or how the role has evolved. Never say "no" — it reads as disinterest.

### 17. "How do you handle feedback or criticism?"

**Testing:** Coachability.

**Template:** Give an example of feedback you received, acted on, and improved from. Show you seek it, not just tolerate it.

### 18. "Tell me about a time you went above and beyond."

**Testing:** Discretionary effort.

**Template:** Pick a moment you weren't required to act but did, and quantify the impact.

### 19. "How do you prioritize when everything is urgent?"

**Testing:** Judgment under load.

**Template:** Describe your actual method — impact vs. effort, deadlines, stakeholder input — with a real example of a tough call you made.

### 20. "Is there anything else you'd like us to know?"

**Testing:** Do you close strong?

**Template:** Restate your fit in two sentences and express clear interest. "I'll just add that I'm genuinely excited about [specific thing], and I think my [strength] is a strong match. I'd love to move forward."

## The Preparation Checklist

- Prepare **6–10 distinct STAR stories** covering conflict, failure, leadership, pressure, persuasion, and initiative. Don't reuse one project for every question.
- Research the company enough to answer "why us" specifically.
- Know your **market salary band** before you walk in.
- Have **three questions** ready for the end.
- Practice **out loud** and timed. Answers always run longer spoken than they read in your head.

That last point is where most candidates lose. You can know the perfect framework and still ramble for four minutes in the room. The only fix is rehearsal under realistic conditions — ideally a full mock interview where someone asks follow-ups. ## FAQ

### How many interview questions should I prepare for?

Focus on the 20 above — they cover the vast majority of what you'll be asked. More important than volume is having 6–10 solid STAR stories you can flex across different behavioral prompts, since interviewers ask the same underlying questions with different wording.

### Should I memorize my answers word for word?

No. Memorized answers sound robotic and break under follow-up questions. Memorize the structure and the key facts of each story, then let the exact wording come out naturally. Interviewers can tell a recited script from a real answer.

### What is the most important interview question?

"Tell me about yourself" and "Do you have any questions for us?" bracket the interview and set its tone. A sharp, focused opener earns you goodwill; three thoughtful closing questions leave a strong final impression. Both are entirely within your control to prepare.

### How do I answer a question I don't have an example for?

Bridge to the closest real experience: "I haven't done exactly that, but here's a closely related case." An adjacent true story always beats an invented perfect one, and interviewers can usually spot a fabricated example when they probe it.
