The key to a group interview is to be visibly collaborative while still being individually memorable: speak early, build on other candidates' ideas by name, contribute two or three high-quality points instead of many small ones, and never compete by interrupting or dominating. Assessors are scoring how you behave with people, not just what you say — the candidate who makes the whole group better usually wins the room.

A group interview puts multiple candidates in the same session — group discussion, a shared task or case, sometimes a rotation of activities. It's different from a [panel interview](/alex/blog/panel-interview-tips/), where you're the only candidate facing several interviewers; here your competition is sitting next to you, and that's exactly the pressure the format is designed to create.

## What Assessors Are Actually Scoring

Companies run group interviews to see the behaviors a one-on-one can't show: how you act around peers, under time pressure, with no script. Assessors typically walk around with a checklist. The dimensions on it are predictable:

- **Communication** — do you speak clearly and concisely, and do you *listen*?
- **Collaboration** — do you build on others' ideas or bulldoze them?
- **Leadership** — do you move the group forward: structuring the task, watching the clock, pulling in quiet members?
- **Composure** — how do you handle disagreement, time pressure, or your idea being rejected?
- **Presence** — after ninety minutes with eight candidates, does the assessor remember you, and for what?

Notice what's *not* on the list: talking the most. In almost every scoring rubric, dominating reads as a negative. The format has two failure modes — invisible and insufferable — and your whole strategy is the corridor between them.

## Before the Interview

**Find out the format.** Ask the recruiter what the session involves: open discussion, group exercise, case study, presentations? "What should I expect from the group session?" is a completely normal question, and knowing whether you're solving a case or debating a topic changes your prep.

**Prepare a 30-second introduction.** Group interviews almost always open with a round of intros, and it's the one moment of guaranteed individual airtime. Prepare a crisp present-past-hook version of [tell me about yourself](/alex/blog/tell-me-about-yourself/): who you are, one relevant credential, one memorable specific ("I've spent three years turning support tickets into product fixes"). Rehearse it out loud — going seventh and mumbling what everyone else said wastes your best slot.

**Learn names fast.** This is the highest-leverage cheap trick in the format. If there are name tents or an intro round, note names. Using them later ("building on Sarah's point...") is rare enough among nervous candidates that it instantly reads as socially skilled.

**Steady your nerves beforehand.** The format spikes anxiety because it feels like a competition on camera. The mechanics that work for any interview work here — see [how to calm interview nerves](/alex/blog/how-to-calm-interview-nerves/) — with one addition: remind yourself the other candidates are not the graders. The person with the clipboard is.

## During the Session: The Playbook

### Speak in the first five minutes

Not necessarily first — but early. The longer you wait, the higher the internal barrier gets, and assessors start forming their picture of the group in minutes. If you can't think of a point yet, ask a smart clarifying question about the task: "Before we start — are we optimizing for cost or speed here?" You've spoken, you've shaped the work, and you did it without needing a brilliant idea.

### Contribute quality, not volume

Aim for two or three substantive contributions per discussion rather than a running commentary. A substantive contribution does one of these: introduces a new angle, brings a concrete example or number, resolves a disagreement, or structures the mess ("we've got three options on the table — want to spend two minutes on each?"). Ten small "yeah, agreed" moments score nothing.

### Build on others by name

The single most effective phrase pattern in a group interview: **"To build on Marcus's point about pricing — that also solves the retention issue, because..."** In one sentence you've demonstrated listening, name recall, collaboration, and your own thinking. Compare that with the candidate who ignores everything said before them and delivers a prepared monologue. Assessors see the difference immediately.

### Take a facilitation role, not a dictator role

The strongest leadership signal isn't having the best idea — it's making the group function. Concretely:

- **Structure:** "We have twenty minutes — should we spend five brainstorming, ten deciding, five preparing the summary?"
- **Timekeeping:** "We're halfway through and still on option one — want to move on?"
- **Including quiet members:** "Priya, you mentioned logistics experience earlier — what's your read on this?"
- **Synthesizing:** "It sounds like we agree on A and B, and the open question is C. Is that fair?"

The include-the-quiet-person move deserves special mention: it's visible from across the room, almost nobody does it, and it's precisely the collaborative-leadership behavior the rubric rewards.

### Disagree like a colleague, not a contestant

You will sometimes think another candidate's idea is wrong, and handled well that's an opportunity: "I like the ambition of that — my worry is the timeline. Could we get the same result by piloting it in one region first?" Acknowledge, state the concern, offer an alternative. Never interrupt, never score points, never look at the assessor for approval while disagreeing. Composure under disagreement is on the checklist; smugness is its own elimination.

### If the group ignores you

It happens — you make a point, the discussion rolls over it. Don't sulk and don't repeat it louder. Re-enter by attaching to the current thread: "This connects to what I raised earlier about supply — here's how." Resilience after being overlooked is itself a scored behavior.

### Handle group exercises like a project, not a debate

For a shared task (build a proposal, solve a case, rank priorities), the winning behaviors shift slightly: clarify the goal out loud first, volunteer for a concrete piece ("I'll track our decisions and draft the summary"), and make sure the group actually finishes. Groups that debate for 25 of the 30 minutes and slap together an answer fail collectively — the candidate who kept the group on schedule is the one who stands out from the wreckage.

## After the Session

If there's a Q&A or wrap-up, ask one good question — it's a final memorability slot most candidates skip because they're exhausted. Then follow up within 24 hours like any other interview: a short note to the recruiter or assessor referencing something specific from the session ("the market-entry exercise was genuinely fun — my group's pilot-region idea is still turning in my head"). Almost no group-interview candidate follows up individually, which is exactly why you should.

## Mistakes That Quietly Eliminate Candidates

**Dominating.** Speaking twice as much as anyone else, interrupting, treating every silence as yours to fill. Assessors mark it down as "doesn't collaborate" — the fatal note in a format built to test collaboration.

**Going invisible.** The opposite failure. If the assessor's notes page next to your name is empty, you were never in contention. Two quality contributions minimum, whatever it costs your nerves.

**Attacking other candidates.** Correcting people to look smart, competitive body language, visible eye-rolls. The fastest way to fail a teamwork audition is to treat teammates as opponents.

**Talking to the assessor instead of the group.** During group discussion, your audience is the group. Playing every line to the clipboard reads as exactly what it is.

**Checking out when it's not your turn.** Assessors watch listeners as much as speakers. Phone glances, slumped disengagement, or visibly rehearsing your next line while someone else talks all get noted.

**Forgetting the individual moments.** Many group sessions include a short one-on-one or a go-around question. Candidates who spent all their energy on group dynamics get caught flat by "so, why do you want this role?" Prepare your individual answers too.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between a group interview and a panel interview?**

A group interview is several candidates interviewed together, often with shared exercises. A panel interview is one candidate facing several interviewers. The prep overlaps, but group interviews add the peer dynamic — you're scored on how you work with the people you're competing against.

**How many times should I speak in a group interview?**

Aim for two or three substantive contributions per major discussion or exercise, plus your introduction — roughly an equal share of airtime for the group size. If you notice you've spoken twice as much as anyone else, facilitate instead: invite someone else in.

**What should I wear to a group interview?**

Same rule as any interview: one notch above the company's daily dress code. Being surrounded by other candidates changes nothing except that side-by-side comparison makes sloppiness slightly more visible.

**What if another candidate dominates the whole session?**

Don't fight them for airtime — redirect the group. "I want to make sure we hear a couple of other angles — Priya, what's your take?" is a facilitation move that scores for you while neutralizing them. The dominator is failing on their own; your job is to make the group work despite them.

**Are group interviews a red flag about the company?**

Not inherently — they're standard for retail, hospitality, airlines, graduate schemes, and high-volume hiring, because they're efficient and genuinely reveal team behavior. They're only a yellow flag when used for senior individual roles where a deeper individual process would be expected.
